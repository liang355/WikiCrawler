<p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_city" title="Federal city">Federal City</a> of <b>Bonn</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈbɔn]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Bonn.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/De-Bonn.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Bonn.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> in the German state of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, with a population of over 300,000. About 24&#160;km (15&#160;mi) south-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, Bonn is in the southernmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr" title="Rhine-Ruhr">Rhine-Ruhr</a> region, Germany's largest metropolitan area, with over 11 million inhabitants.</p>
<p>Together with the capital <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, the city is the <i>de facto</i> joint seat of government of Germany. Bonn is the secondary seat of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Germany" title="President of Germany">President</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Chancellor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesrat_of_Germany" title="Bundesrat of Germany">Bundesrat</a> and the primary seat of six federal government ministries and twenty federal authorities. The title of Federal City (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Bundesstadt</span>) reflects its important political status within Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Founded in the 1st century BC as a <a href="/wiki/Germania_Inferior" title="Germania Inferior">Roman settlement</a>, Bonn is one of Germany's oldest cities. From 1597 to 1794, Bonn was the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Cologne" title="Electorate of Cologne">Electorate of Cologne</a>, and residence of the Archbishops and Prince-electors of <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Cologne" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Cologne">Cologne</a>. Composer <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a> was born in Bonn in 1770. From 1949 to 1990, Bonn was the <a href="/wiki/Capital_of_Germany" title="Capital of Germany">provisional capital</a> ("temporary seat of the Federal institutions") of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>, and Germany's present constitution, the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany">Basic Law</a>, was declared in the city in 1949. From 1990 to 1999, Bonn served as the seat of government – but no longer capital – of <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">reunited Germany</a>.</p>
<p>The headquarters of <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Post_DHL" class="mw-redirect" title="Deutsche Post DHL">Deutsche Post DHL</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Telekom" title="Deutsche Telekom">Deutsche Telekom</a>, both <a href="/wiki/DAX" title="DAX">DAX-listed</a> corporations, are in Bonn. The city is home to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bonn" title="University of Bonn">University of Bonn</a> and a total of 19 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> institutions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Situated in the southernmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr" title="Rhine-Ruhr">Rhine-Ruhr</a> region, Germany's largest metropolitan area with over 11 million inhabitants, Bonn lies within the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German state</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, close to the border with <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>. Spanning an area of more 141.2&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (55&#160;sq&#160;mi) on both sides of the river <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, almost three quarters of the city lie on the river's left bank.</p>
<p>To the south and to the west, Bonn is bordering the <a href="/wiki/Eifel" title="Eifel">Eifel region</a> which encompasses the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland_Nature_Park" title="Rhineland Nature Park">Rhineland Nature Park</a>. To the north, Bonn borders the <a href="/wiki/Cologne_Lowland" title="Cologne Lowland">Cologne Lowland</a>. Natural borders are constituted by the river <a href="/wiki/Sieg" title="Sieg">Sieg</a> to the north-east and by the <a href="/wiki/Siebengebirge" title="Siebengebirge">Siebengebirge</a> (also known as the Seven Hills) to the east. The largest extension of the city in north-south dimensions is 15&#160;km (9&#160;mi) and 12.5&#160;km (8&#160;mi) in west-east dimensions. The city borders have a total length of 61&#160;km (38&#160;mi). The geographical centre of Bonn is the Bundeskanzlerplatz <i>(Chancellor Square)</i> in Bonn-Gronau.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German state</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> is divided into five <a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">governmental districts</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Regierungsbezirk</span>), and Bonn is part of the <a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk_K%C3%B6ln" class="mw-redirect" title="Regierungsbezirk Köln">governmental district of Cologne</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Regierungsbezirk Köln</span>). Within this governmental district, the city of Bonn is an <a href="/wiki/List_of_districts_of_Germany" title="List of districts of Germany">urban district</a> in its own right. The urban district of Bonn is then again divided into four administrative municipal districts (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Stadtbezirk</span>). These are Bonn, Bonn-Bad Godesberg, Bonn-Beuel and Bonn-Hardtberg. In 1969, the independent towns of <a href="/wiki/Bad_Godesberg" title="Bad Godesberg">Bad Godesberg</a> and Beuel as well as several villages were incorporated into Bonn, resulting in a city more than twice as large as before.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bonn has an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<i>Cfb</i>). In the south of the Cologne lowland in the Rhine valley, Bonn is in one of Germany's warmest regions.</p>

<p>The history of the city dates back to Roman times. In about 12 BC, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a> appears to have stationed a small unit in what is presently the historical centre of the city. Even earlier, the army had resettled members of a Germanic tribal group allied with Rome, the <a href="/wiki/Ubii" title="Ubii">Ubii</a>, in Bonn. The Latin name for that settlement, "Bonna", may stem from the original population of this and many other settlements in the area, the <a href="/wiki/Eburones" title="Eburones">Eburoni</a>. The Eburoni were members of a large tribal coalition effectively wiped out during the final phase of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Caesar</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gallic_Wars" title="Gallic Wars">War in Gaul</a>. After several decades, the army gave up the small camp linked to the Ubii-settlement. During the <a href="/wiki/1st_century" title="1st century">1st century</a> <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">AD</a>, the army then chose a site to the north of the emerging town in what is now the section of Bonn-Castell to build a large military installation dubbed <a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">Castra</a> Bonnensis, i.e., literally, "Fort Bonn". Initially built from wood, the fort was eventually rebuilt in stone. With additions, changes and new construction, the fort remained in use by the army into the waning days of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>, possibly the mid-<a href="/wiki/5th_century" title="5th century">5th century</a>. The structures themselves remained standing well into the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, when they were called the Bonnburg. They were used by <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Franks" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of the Franks">Frankish</a> kings until they fell into disuse. Eventually, much of the building materials seem to have been re-used in the construction of Bonn's 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">city wall</a>. The Sterntor (<i>star gate</i>) in the city center is a reconstruction using the last remnants of the medieval city wall.</p>
<p>To date, Bonn's Roman fort remains the largest fort of its type known from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient world</a>, i.e. a fort built to accommodate a full-strength <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Imperial Legion</a> and its auxiliaries. The fort covered an area of approximately 250,000 square metres (62 acres). Between its walls it contained a dense grid of streets and a multitude of buildings, ranging from spacious headquarters and large officers' quarters to <a href="/wiki/Barracks" title="Barracks">barracks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stable" title="Stable">stables</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Military_prison" title="Military prison">military jail</a>. Among the legions stationed in Bonn, the "1st", i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Legio_I_Minervia" title="Legio I Minervia">Prima Legio Minervia</a>, seems to have served here the longest. Units of the Bonn legion were deployed to theatres of war ranging from modern-day <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> to what is now the Russian republic of <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a>.</p>

<p>The chief <a href="/wiki/Roman_roads" title="Roman roads">Roman road</a> linking the provincial capitals of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a> cut right through the fort where it joined the fort's main road (now, Römerstraße). Once past the South Gate, the Cologne–Mainz road continued along what are now streets named Belderberg, Adenauerallee et al. On both sides of the road, the local settlement, <i>Bonna</i>, grew into a sizeable Roman town. Bonn is shown on the <a href="/wiki/4th_century" title="4th century">4th century</a> <a href="/wiki/Peutinger_Map" class="mw-redirect" title="Peutinger Map">Peutinger Map</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Late_antiquity" title="Late antiquity">late antiquity</a>, much of the town seems to have been destroyed by marauding invaders. The remaining civilian population then took refuge inside the fort along with the remnants of the troops stationed here. During the final decades of Imperial rule, the troops were supplied by <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franci</a> chieftains employed by the Roman administration. When the end came, these troops simply shifted their allegiances to the new barbarian rulers, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Franks" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of the Franks">Kingdom of the Franks</a>. From the fort, the Bonnburg, as well as from a new medieval settlement to the South centered around what later became the <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Minster" title="Bonn Minster">minster</a>, grew the medieval city of Bonn. Local legends arose from this period that the name of the village came from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface" title="Saint Boniface">Saint Boniface</a> via <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> <i>*Bonnifatia</i>, but this proved to be a myth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_and_Early_Modern_times">Middle Ages and Early Modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Middle Ages and Early Modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Between the 11th and 13th centuries, the <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> style Bonn Minster was built, and in 1597 Bonn became the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">Archdiocese</a> of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Cologne" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Cologne">Cologne</a>. The city gained more influence and grew considerably. The city was subject to a major bombardment during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Bonn_(1689)" title="Siege of Bonn (1689)">Siege of Bonn</a> in 1689. The <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Cologne" title="Electorate of Cologne">elector</a> <a href="/wiki/Clemens_August_of_Bavaria" title="Clemens August of Bavaria">Clemens August</a> (ruled 1723–1761) ordered the construction of a series of <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> buildings which still give the city its character. Another memorable ruler was <a href="/wiki/Archduke_Maximilian_Francis_of_Austria" title="Archduke Maximilian Francis of Austria">Max Franz</a> (ruled 1784–1794), who founded the university and the spa quarter of <a href="/wiki/Bad_Godesberg" title="Bad Godesberg">Bad Godesberg</a>. In addition he was a patron of the young <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a>, who was born in Bonn in 1770; the elector financed the composer's first journey to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>.</p>
<p>In 1794, the city was seized by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> troops, becoming a part of the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">First French Empire</a>. In 1815 following the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, Bonn became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>. Administered within the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Province" title="Rhine Province">Rhine Province</a>, the city became part of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871 during the Prussian-led <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a>. Bonn was of little relevance in these years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_and_time_as_the_capital_of_West_Germany">20th century and time as the capital of West Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 20th century and time as the capital of West Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Bonn acquired military significance because of its strategic location on the Rhine, which formed a natural barrier to easy penetration into the German heartland from the west. The Allied ground advance into Germany reached Bonn on 7 March 1945, and the US <a href="/wiki/1st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Infantry Division (United States)">1st Infantry Division</a> captured the city during the battle of 8–9 March 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Following the Second World War, Bonn was in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> zone of occupation. Following the advocacy of West Germany's first chancellor, <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> Mayor and a native of that area, Bonn became the <i>de facto</i> capital, officially designated the "temporary seat of the federal institutions," of the newly formed Federal Republic of Germany in 1949. However, the Bundestag affirmed <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>'s status as the German capital. Bonn was chosen as the temporary seat of government despite the fact that <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt</a> already had most of the required facilities and using Bonn was estimated to be 95 million DM more expensive than using Frankfurt. However, Adenauer and other prominent politicians intended to make Berlin the capital of the reunified Germany, and felt that locating the capital in a major city like Frankfurt or <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> would imply a permanent capital and weaken support in West Germany for reunification.</p>
<p>In 1949, the <a href="/wiki/Parlamentarischer_Rat" title="Parlamentarischer Rat">Parliamentary Council</a> in Bonn drafted and adopted the current German constitution, the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany">Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany</a>. As the political centre of West Germany, Bonn saw six <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Chancellors</a> and six <a href="/wiki/President_of_Germany" title="President of Germany">Presidents</a> of the Federal Republic of Germany. Bonn's time as the capital of West Germany is commonly referred to as the <i>Bonn Republic,</i> in contrast to the <i>Berlin Republic</i> which followed reunification in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_German_reunification_in_1990">After German reunification in 1990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: After German reunification in 1990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a> in 1990 made Berlin the nominal capital of Germany again. This decision did not mandate that the republic's political institutions would also move. While some argued for the seat of government to move to Berlin, others advocated leaving it in Bonn — a situation roughly analogous to that of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> is the capital but <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">the Hague</a> is the seat of government. Berlin's previous history as united Germany's capital was strongly connected with the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a> and more ominously with <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>. It was felt that a new peacefully united Germany should not be governed from a city connected to such overtones of war. Additionally, Bonn was closer to <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>. Former chancellor <a href="/wiki/Willy_Brandt" title="Willy Brandt">Willy Brandt</a> caused considerable offence to the Western Allies during the debate by stating that <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> wouldn't have kept the seat of government at <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy</a> after Liberation.</p>
<p>The heated debate that resulted was settled by the <i><a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a></i> (Germany's parliament) only on 20 June 1991. By a vote of 338–320,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the Bundestag voted to move the seat of government to Berlin. The vote broke largely along regional lines, with legislators from the south and west favouring Bonn and legislators from the north and east voting for Berlin.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It also broke along generational lines as well; older legislators with memories of Berlin's past glory favoured Berlin, while younger legislators favoured Bonn. Ultimately, the votes of the <a href="/wiki/New_states_of_Germany#Culture" title="New states of Germany">eastern German</a> legislators tipped the balance in favour of Berlin.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1990 to 1999, Bonn served as the seat of government of reunited Germany. In recognition of its former status as German capital, it holds the name of Federal City (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Bundesstadt</span>). Bonn currently shares the status of Germany's seat of government with Berlin, with the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Germany" title="President of Germany">President</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Chancellor</a> and many government ministries maintaining substantial presences in Bonn. Over 8,000 of the 18,000 federal officials remain in Bonn.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A total of 19 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations (UN)</a> institutions operate from Bonn today.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_council">City council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: City council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city council</a> of Bonn used to be based in the <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo-style</a> and 1737 built <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Altes Rathaus</span></i> (old city hall) adjacent to Bonn's central market square. However, due to the enlargement of Bonn in 1969 through the incorporation of Beuel and Bad Godesberg, it moved into the larger Stadthaus facilities further up north. This was necessary for the city council to accommodate the risen number of representatives. The mayor of Bonn still sits in the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Altes Rathaus</span></i>, which is also used for representative and official purposes.</p>
<p>As of the 2014–2020 election cycle, the <a href="/wiki/CDU_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="CDU (Germany)">Christian Democrats (CDU)</a> hold the majority of mandates in the city council (27 seats), followed by the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democrats (SPD)</a> with 20 seats, the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Greens (Bündnis '90/Die Grünen)</a> with 16 seats, the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Liberals (FDP)</a> with 7 seats, the <a href="/wiki/The_Left_(Germany)" title="The Left (Germany)">Left (Die Linke)</a> with 5 seats, the local Bürgerbund Bonn with 4 seats, the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_for_Germany" title="Alternative for Germany">Alternative for Germany (AfD)</a> with 3 seats, and independent candidates with a total of 4 seats. There are currently 86 seats in the city council of Bonn.</p>

<p>Four delegates represent the Federal city of Bonn in the <a href="/wiki/Landtag" title="Landtag">Landtag</a> of <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="Landtag of North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>. The last election took place in May 2012. The current delegates are Bernhard von Grünberg <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">(SPD),</a>Renate Hendricks <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">(SPD)</a>, Joachim Stamp <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">(FDP)</a> and Rolf Beu <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">(Bündnis 90/Die Grünen)</a>.</p>
<p>The next Landtag elections take place in May 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_federal_election">German federal election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: German federal election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bonn's <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district" title="Electoral district">constituency</a> is called <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Bundeswahlkreis Bonn</span></i> (096). In the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013" title="German federal election, 2013">German federal election 2013</a>, Ulrich Kelber <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">(SPD)</a> was elected a member of <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">German Federal parliament, the Bundestag</a> by direct mandate. It is his fourth term. Katja Dörner representing <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Bündnis 90/Die Grünen</a> and Claudia Lücking-Michel of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">CDU</a> were elected <a href="/wiki/Electoral_system_of_Germany#Regional_lists_of_parliamentary_candidates_for_election_to_Federal_Parliament" title="Electoral system of Germany">from regional lists</a>.</p>
<p>The next federal election takes place on 24 September 2017.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beethoven's birthplace is located in Bonngasse near the market place. Next to the market place is the Old City Hall, built in 1737 in <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> style, under the rule of <a href="/wiki/Clemens_August_of_Bavaria" title="Clemens August of Bavaria">Clemens August of Bavaria</a>. It is used for receptions of guests of the city, and as an office for the mayor. Nearby is the <i><a href="/wiki/Electoral_Palace,_Bonn" title="Electoral Palace, Bonn">Kurfürstliches Schloss</a></i>, built as a residence for the prince-elector and now the main building of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bonn" title="University of Bonn">University of Bonn</a>.</p>

Erected in the 11th and 13th century, the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Minster" title="Bonn Minster">Minster of Bonn</a> is one of Germany's oldest churches.</div>
</div>

<p>The <i>Poppelsdorfer Allee</i> is an avenue flanked by <a href="/wiki/Chestnut" title="Chestnut">chestnut trees</a> which had the first <a href="/wiki/Horsecar" title="Horsecar">horsecar</a> of the city. It connects the <i>Kurfürstliches Schloss</i> with the <i><a href="/wiki/Poppelsdorfer_Schloss" class="mw-redirect" title="Poppelsdorfer Schloss">Poppelsdorfer Schloss</a></i>, a palace that was built as a resort for the prince-electors in the first half of the 18th century, and whose grounds are now a <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_Bonn" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanischer Garten Bonn">Botanischer Garten Bonn</a>). This axis is interrupted by a railway line and <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bonn Hauptbahnhof">Bonn Hauptbahnhof</a>, a building erected in 1883/84.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Beethoven_Monument,_Bonn" title="Beethoven Monument, Bonn">Beethoven Monument</a> stands on the Münsterplatz, which is flanked by the <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Minster" title="Bonn Minster">Bonn Minster</a>, one of Germany's oldest churches.</p>
<p>The three highest structures in the city are the <a href="/wiki/Westdeutscher_Rundfunk" title="Westdeutscher Rundfunk">WDR</a> <a href="/wiki/Radio_masts_and_towers" title="Radio masts and towers">radio mast</a> in Bonn-Venusberg (180&#160;m or 590&#160;ft), the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Post" title="Deutsche Post">Deutsche Post</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Post_Tower" title="Post Tower">Post Tower</a></i> (162.5&#160;m or 533&#160;ft) and the former building for the German members of parliament <i><a href="/wiki/Langer_Eugen" title="Langer Eugen">Langer Eugen</a></i> (114.7&#160;m or 376&#160;ft) now the location of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> Campus.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland_-_Bundeskunsthalle-9245.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland_-_Bundeskunsthalle-9245.jpg/260px-Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland_-_Bundeskunsthalle-9245.jpg" width="260" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland_-_Bundeskunsthalle-9245.jpg/390px-Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland_-_Bundeskunsthalle-9245.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland_-_Bundeskunsthalle-9245.jpg/520px-Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland_-_Bundeskunsthalle-9245.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5182" data-file-height="2204" /></a>

The <a href="/wiki/Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland" title="Kunst- und Ausstellungshalle der Bundesrepublik Deutschland">Bundeskunsthalle</a> focusses on the cultural heritage outside of Germany or Europe, at the crossroads of culture, the arts, and science.</div>

<p>Just as Bonn's other four major museums, the <i><a href="/wiki/Haus_der_Geschichte" title="Haus der Geschichte">Haus der Geschichte</a></i> or Museum of the History of the Federal Republic of Germany, is located on the so-called <i>Museumsmeile</i> ("Museum Mile")<i>.</i> The Haus der Geschichte is one of the foremost German museums of contemporary German history, with branches in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>. In its permanent exhibition, the Haus der Geschichte presents German history from 1945 until the present, also shedding light on Bonn's own role as former capital of West Germany. Numerous temporary exhibitions emphasize different features, such as Nazism or important personalities in German history.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Kunstmuseum_Bonn" title="Kunstmuseum Bonn">Kunstmuseum Bonn</a></i> or Bonn Museum of Modern Art is an art museum founded in 1947. The Kunstmuseum exhibits both temporary exhibitions and its permanent collection. The latter is focused on Rhenish <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a> and post-war German art.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> German artists on display include <a href="/wiki/Georg_Baselitz" title="Georg Baselitz">Georg Baselitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Beuys" title="Joseph Beuys">Joseph Beuys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hanne_Darboven" title="Hanne Darboven">Hanne Darboven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anselm_Kiefer" title="Anselm Kiefer">Anselm Kiefer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blinky_Palermo" title="Blinky Palermo">Blinky Palermo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Vostell" title="Wolf Vostell">Wolf Vostell</a>. The museum owns one of the largest collections of artwork by Expressionist painter <a href="/wiki/August_Macke" title="August Macke">August Macke</a>. His work is also on display in the <a href="/wiki/August-Macke-Haus" title="August-Macke-Haus">August-Macke-Haus</a>, located in Macke's former home where he lived from 1911 to 1914.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kunst-_und_Ausstellungshalle_der_Bundesrepublik_Deutschland" title="Kunst- und Ausstellungshalle der Bundesrepublik Deutschland"><i>Bundeskunsthalle</i></a> (full name: Kunst- und Ausstellungshalle der Bundesrepublik Deutschland or Art and Exhibition Hall of the Federal Republic of Germany), focusses on the crossroads of culture, arts, and science. To date, it attracted more than 17 million visitors.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> One of its main objectives is to show the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">cultural heritage</a> outside of Germany or Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Next to its changing exhibitions, the Bundeskunsthalle regularly hosts concerts, discussion panels, congresses, and lectures.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Museum_Koenig" title="Museum Koenig">Museum Koenig</a></i> is Bonn's natural history museum. Affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bonn" title="University of Bonn">University of Bonn</a>, it is also a <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoological</a> research institution housing the <i>Leibniz-Institut für Biodiversität der Tiere</i>. Politically interesting, it is on the premises of the Museum Koenig where the <a href="/wiki/Parlamentarischer_Rat" title="Parlamentarischer Rat">Parlamentarischer Rat</a> first met.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum_Bonn" title="Deutsches Museum Bonn">Deutsches Museum Bonn</a></i>, affiliated with one of the world's foremost science museums, the <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum" title="Deutsches Museum">Deutsches Museum in Munich</a>, is an interactive science museum focusing on post-war German scientists, engineers, and inventions.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Other museums include the <a href="/wiki/Beethoven_House" title="Beethoven House">Beethoven House</a>, birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Rheinisches_Landesmuseum_Bonn" title="Rheinisches Landesmuseum Bonn">Rheinisches Landesmuseum Bonn</a> (Rhinish Regional Museum Bonn),<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Women%27s_Museum" title="Bonn Women's Museum">Bonn Women's Museum</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Arithmeum" title="Arithmeum">Arithmeum</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature">Nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>There are several parks, leisure and protected areas in and around Bonn. The <i>Rheinaue</i> is Bonn's most important leisure park, with its role being comparable to what <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a> is for <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>. It lies on the banks of the Rhine and is the city's biggest park intra muros.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Rhine promenade and the <i>Alter Zoll</i> (Old Toll Station) are in direct neighbourhood of the city centre and are popular amongst both residents and visitors. The <i><a href="/wiki/Arboretum_Park_H%C3%A4rle" title="Arboretum Park Härle">Arboretum Park Härle</a></i> is an <a href="/wiki/Arboretum" title="Arboretum">arboretum</a> with specimens dating to back to 1870. The <i>Botanischer Garten</i> (Botanical Garden) is affiliated with the university and it is here where <a href="/wiki/Titan_arum" class="mw-redirect" title="Titan arum">Titan arum</a> set a world record.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The natural reserve of <i><a href="/wiki/Kottenforst" title="Kottenforst">Kottenforst</a></i> is a large area of protected woods on the hills west of the city centre. It is about 40 square kilometres (15 square miles) in area and part of the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland_Nature_Park" title="Rhineland Nature Park">Rhineland Nature Park</a> (1,045&#160;km<sup>2</sup> or 403&#160;sq&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the very south of the city, on the border with <a href="/wiki/Wachtberg" title="Wachtberg">Wachtberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>, there is an extinct <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rodderberg" title="Rodderberg">Rodderberg</a>, featuring a popular area for hikes. Also south of the city, there is the <a href="/wiki/Siebengebirge" title="Siebengebirge">Siebengebirge</a> which is part of the lower half of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Rhine" title="Middle Rhine">Middle Rhine</a> region. The nearby upper half of the Middle Rhine from Bingen to <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a> is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="List of World Heritage Sites in Europe">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a> with more than 40 castles and fortresses from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and important German vineyards.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_traffic">Air traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Air traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:184px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Koeln-Bonn-Airport11.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Koeln-Bonn-Airport11.JPG/182px-Koeln-Bonn-Airport11.JPG" width="182" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Koeln-Bonn-Airport11.JPG/273px-Koeln-Bonn-Airport11.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Koeln-Bonn-Airport11.JPG/364px-Koeln-Bonn-Airport11.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2805" data-file-height="1689" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Koeln-Bonn-Airport11.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Cologne_Bonn_Airport" title="Cologne Bonn Airport">international airport of Cologne and Bonn</a> (<a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association_airport_code" class="mw-redirect" title="International Air Transport Association airport code">IATA</a>: CGN) is Germany's <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_busiest_airports_in_Germany" title="List of the busiest airports in Germany">seventh-largest</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Named after <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>, the first post-war Chancellor of West Germany, <a href="/wiki/Cologne_Bonn_Airport" title="Cologne Bonn Airport">Cologne Bonn Airport</a> is situated 15 kilometres (9.3 miles) north-east from the city centre of Bonn. With around 10.3 million passengers passing through it in 2015, it is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_busiest_airports_in_Germany" title="List of the busiest airports in Germany">seventh-largest passenger airport in Germany</a> and the third-largest in terms of cargo operations. By traffic units, which combines cargo and passengers, the airport is in fifth position in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> As of March 2015, Cologne Bonn Airport had services to 115 passenger destinations in 35 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The airport is one of Germany's few 24-hour airports, and is a hub for <a href="/wiki/Eurowings" title="Eurowings">Eurowings</a> and cargo operators <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a> and <a href="/wiki/UPS_Airlines" title="UPS Airlines">UPS Airlines</a>.</p>
<p>The federal motorway (<i>Autobahn</i>) <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_59" title="Bundesautobahn 59">A59</a> connects the airport with the city. Long distance and regional trains to and from the airport stop at <a href="/wiki/Cologne/Bonn_Airport_station" title="Cologne/Bonn Airport station">Cologne/Bonn Airport station</a>. Other major airports within a one-hour drive by car are <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankurt International Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf_Airport" title="Düsseldorf Airport">Düsseldorf International Airport</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail_and_bus_system">Rail and bus system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Rail and bus system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:183px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2008-07-28_U-Bahnhof_Hauptbahnhof_(Bonn).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/2008-07-28_U-Bahnhof_Hauptbahnhof_%28Bonn%29.jpg/181px-2008-07-28_U-Bahnhof_Hauptbahnhof_%28Bonn%29.jpg" width="181" height="121" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/2008-07-28_U-Bahnhof_Hauptbahnhof_%28Bonn%29.jpg/272px-2008-07-28_U-Bahnhof_Hauptbahnhof_%28Bonn%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/2008-07-28_U-Bahnhof_Hauptbahnhof_%28Bonn%29.jpg/362px-2008-07-28_U-Bahnhof_Hauptbahnhof_%28Bonn%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2700" data-file-height="1800" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2008-07-28_U-Bahnhof_Hauptbahnhof_(Bonn).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bonn_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bonn Hauptbahnhof">Bonn Hauptbahnhof</a>, Bonn's busiest railway station, is connected to Bonn's partly underground <a href="/wiki/U-Bahn" class="mw-redirect" title="U-Bahn">U-Bahn</a> network.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Bonn's central railway station, <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bonn Hauptbahnhof">Bonn Hauptbahnhof</a>, serves urban (<a href="/wiki/S-train" title="S-train">S-Bahn</a>, <a href="/wiki/U-Bahn" class="mw-redirect" title="U-Bahn">U-Bahn</a>, sharing the same network with the neighbouring city of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>), regional (<a href="/wiki/Regionalbahn" title="Regionalbahn">Regionalbahn</a>), and long distance destinations (<a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">ICE</a>) such as Berlin, Hamburg, Munich, Zurich, Vienna, Brussels, Amsterdam and Paris. Daily, more than 67,000 people travel via Bonn Hauptbahnhof. In late 2016, around 80 long distance and more than 165 regional trains departed to or from Bonn every day.<sup id="cite_ref-general041116_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-general041116-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-db041116_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-db041116-32">[32]</a></sup> The other major railway station (<a href="/wiki/Siegburg/Bonn_station" title="Siegburg/Bonn station">Siegburg/Bonn</a>) lies on the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ln%E2%80%93Frankfurt_high-speed_rail_line" title="Köln–Frankfurt high-speed rail line">high-speed rail line between Cologne and Frankfurt</a>.</p>
<p>The bus system of Bonn is composed of roughly 30 lines which operate on a regular basis. During peaks, buses usually run every 5 minutes; off-peak buses run every 20 minutes. Several lines offer night services, especially during the weekends. Bonn is part of the <a href="/wiki/Verkehrsverbund_Rhein-Sieg" title="Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Sieg">Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Sieg</a> (<i>Rhine-Sieg Transport Association</i>) which is the public transport association covering the area of the <a href="/wiki/Cologne/Bonn_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Cologne/Bonn Region">Cologne/Bonn region</a>.</p>

Road network adjacent to Bonn.</div>

<p>Four German <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahns</a> run through or are adjacent to Bonn: the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_59" title="Bundesautobahn 59">A59</a> (right bank of the Rhine, connecting Bonn with <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_555" title="Bundesautobahn 555">A555</a> (left bank of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, connecting Bonn with <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_562" title="Bundesautobahn 562">A562</a> (connecting the right with the left bank of the Rhine south of Bonn), and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_565" title="Bundesautobahn 565">A565</a> (connecting the A59 and the A555 with the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_61" title="Bundesautobahn 61">A61</a> to the southwest). Three Bundesstraßen, which have a general 100 kilometres per hour (62 miles per hour) <a href="/wiki/Speed_limit" title="Speed limit">speed limit</a> in contrast to the Autobahn, connect Bonn to its immediate surroundings (Bundesstraßen B9, B42 and B56).</p>
<p>With Bonn being divided into two parts by the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, three bridges are crucial for inner-city road traffic: the Konrad-Adenauer-Brücke (A562), the Friedrich-Ebert-Brücke (A565), and the Kennedybrücke (B56). In addition, regular ferries operate between Bonn-Mehlem and <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigswinter" title="Königswinter">Königswinter</a>, Bonn-Bad Godesberg and <a href="/wiki/Niederdollendorf" class="mw-redirect" title="Niederdollendorf">Niederdollendorf</a>, and Graurheindorf and Mondorf.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Port">Port</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Port">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located in the northern sub-district of Graurheindorf, the <a href="/wiki/Inland_harbor" title="Inland harbor">inland harbour</a> of Bonn is used for container traffic as well as oversea transport. The annual turnover amounts to around 500,000&#160;t (490,000 long tons; 550,000 short tons). Regular passenger transport occurs to Cologne and Düsseldorf.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Bonn had a population of 327,913. About 70% of the population was entirely of German origin, while about 100,000 people, equating to roughly 30%, were at least partly of non-German origin. The city is one of the fastest-growing municipalities in Germany and the 18th most populous city in the country. Bonn's population is predicted to surpass the populations of <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal" title="Wuppertal">Wuppertal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bochum" title="Bochum">Bochum</a> before the year 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bonn is home of the <a href="/wiki/Telekom_Baskets_Bonn" title="Telekom Baskets Bonn">Telekom Baskets Bonn</a>, the only basketball club in Germany that owns its arena, the <a href="/wiki/Telekom_Dome" title="Telekom Dome">Telekom Dome</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The club is a regular participant at international competitions such as the <a href="/wiki/Eurocup_Basketball" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurocup Basketball">Eurocup</a>.</p>
<p>The city also has an amateur football team <a href="/wiki/Bonner_SC" title="Bonner SC">Bonner SC</a> which was formed in 1965 through the merger of <i>Bonner FV</i> and <i>Tura Bonn</i>.</p>
<p>The Headquarter of the <a href="/wiki/International_Paralympic_Committee" title="International Paralympic Committee">International Paralympic Committee</a> have since 1999 been located in Bonn</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_relations">International relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bonn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: International relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_twin_towns_and_sister_cities_in_Germany" title="List of twin towns and sister cities in Germany">List of twin towns and sister cities in Germany</a></div>
<p>Since 1983, the City of Bonn has established friendship relations with the City of <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, and since 1988 Bonn, in former times the residence of the Princes Electors of Cologne, and <a href="/wiki/Potsdam" title="Potsdam">Potsdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, the formerly most important residential city of the Prussian rulers, have established a city-to-city partnership.</p>
<p>Central Bonn is surrounded by a number of traditional towns and villages which were independent up to several decades ago. As many of those communities had already established their own contacts and partnerships before the regional and local reorganisation in 1969, the Federal City of Bonn now has a dense network of city district partnerships with European partner towns.</p>
<p>The city district of Bonn is a partner of the English university city of <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> (since 1947), of <a href="/wiki/Budafok" title="Budafok">Budafok</a>, District XXII of <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> (since 1991) and of <a href="/wiki/Opole" title="Opole">Opole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> (officially since 1997; contacts were established 1954).</p>
<p>The district of Bad Godesberg has established partnerships with <a href="/wiki/Saint-Cloud" title="Saint-Cloud">Saint-Cloud</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frascati" title="Frascati">Frascati</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Borough_of_Windsor_and_Maidenhead" title="Royal Borough of Windsor and Maidenhead">Windsor and Maidenhead</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kortrijk" title="Kortrijk">Kortrijk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>; a friendship agreement has been signed with the town of <a href="/wiki/Yalova" title="Yalova">Yalova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.</p>
<p>The district of Beuel on the right bank of the Rhine and the city district of Hardtberg foster partnerships with towns in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>: <a href="/wiki/Mirecourt" title="Mirecourt">Mirecourt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Villemomble" title="Villemomble">Villemomble</a>.</p>
<p>Moreover, the city of Bonn has developed a concept of international co-operation and maintains sustainability oriented project partnerships in addition to traditional city twinning, among others with <a href="/wiki/Minsk" title="Minsk">Minsk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulaanbaatar" title="Ulaanbaatar">Ulaanbaatar</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bukhara" title="Bukhara">Bukhara</a> in <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chengdu" title="Chengdu">Chengdu</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/La_Paz" title="La Paz">La Paz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>.</p>

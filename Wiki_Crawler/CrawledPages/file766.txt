<p>The <b>Harvard University Band</b> (HUB) is the official student <a href="/wiki/Band_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Band (music)">band</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. The <i>Harvard Wind Ensemble</i>, the <i>Harvard Summer Pops Band</i>, and the <i>Harvard Jazz Bands</i> also fall under the umbrella organization of HUB. Currently, the band plays for all football games (both home and away) as well as home men's and women's ice hockey games. Occasionally it plays at men's and women's basketball games. The uniform for both football games and formal "gigs" consists of a <a href="/wiki/Crimson" title="Crimson">crimson</a> wool HUB blazer worn over a white shirt with a black HUB logo tie, black pants (since 1961), and black shoes. In the early days of the Band, white sailor hats and khaki pants were worn. For hockey games, the band wears (over casual clothes) a custom Harvard Band hockey jersey, modeled after the home jerseys for men's hockey, which features images of Bertha (the huge bass drum) on the sleeves. Band alumni, known as <i>crusties</i>, maintain strong ties to the HUB, sometimes continuing to act as regular members well after graduating from the University. <i><a href="/wiki/Illegitimum_non_carborundum" class="mw-redirect" title="Illegitimum non carborundum">Illegitimum non carborundum</a></i> (INC) is the HUB motto. Written correspondence from HUB or HUB members is frequently signed with <i>INC</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The band was formed in 1919. By 1930 the band had become a <a href="/wiki/Scramble_band" title="Scramble band">scramble band</a>, a method that was also adopted by most other <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> marching bands (as well as the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Band" title="Stanford Band">Stanford Band</a> and the Rice <a href="/wiki/Marching_Owl_Band" title="Marching Owl Band">Marching Owl Band</a>), with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> band. While the inventor of the scramble band technique remains in debate, the HUB maintains a strong claim to the title. A scramble band simply runs (in lieu of marching) from one formation to the next on a cue, typically a starter's pistol.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The HUB office was formerly at 9 Prescott St., and moved to 74 Mt. Auburn St, in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> in 1995. The Harvard University Band's new headquarters was named the "Anderson Band Center" in 1995 in honor of <a href="/wiki/Leroy_Anderson" title="Leroy Anderson">Leroy Anderson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-leroyandersonfoundation.org_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leroyandersonfoundation.org-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Band_leadership">Band leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Band leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Band is led by a Senior Staff consisting of five officers:</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Manager</b>: Oversees finances and activity bookings. The current Manager is Diondra Dilworth '18.</dd>
<dd><b>Drill Master</b>: Writes and directs the field shows for football games; coordinates the cheers during hockey games. The current Drill Master is Changseob Lim '19.</dd>
<dd><b>Student Conductor</b>: Conducts and writes arrangements. The current StudCon is Nathan Wolfe '19.</dd>
<dd><b>Drum Major</b>: Serves as the leader for field and parade performances; assists in conducting with a mace; acts as the liaison with other Ivy Bands and coordinator of all away trips. The current Drum Major is Christine Zheng '18.</dd>
<dd><b>Schneider</b>: Coordinates the social activities of the Band and maintains and bolsters esprit de corps. The current Schneider is Brandon Kim '19.</dd>
</dl>
<p>The senior staff uniforms vary from the standard uniform. The Drum Major wears a tuxedo and carries a <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_mace" title="Ceremonial mace">mace</a>, the Drill Master wears a black trench coat, the student conductor wears a HUB bow tie, the Manager wears a black hat, and the Schneider wears a green tie.</p>
<p>Junior Staffers, who often later become Senior Staffers, work to build up band loyalty and spirit, and themselves provide the core active membership. Junior staff is composed largely of committees under each of the Senior Staff members:</p>
<dl>
<dd><b>Manager's Committee</b>: Treasurer, Mailing &amp; Merchandise Coordinator, Webmaster, Alumni &amp; Public Relations coordinator, Historian</dd>
<dd><b>Drill Master's Committee</b>: Assistant Drill Manager (ADM), Prop Crew Manager, Assistant Prop Crew Manager, Recruiting &amp; Postering Coordinator, Blogtographer, Cinematographer</dd>
<dd><b>Student Conductor's Committee</b>: Music Manager, Librarian, Music Archivist, Arrangements and Licensing Coordinator, Instruments Manager</dd>
<dd><b>Drum Major's Committee</b>: Internal, Trips Manager, Section Leaders - Saxophones, Clarinets, Trumpets, Flutes, Percussion, Low Brass</dd>
<dd><b>Schneider's Committee</b>: Weisse (3), Schwartz</dd>
<dd><b>Miscellaneous</b>: Wind Ensemble Manager, Jazz Band Manager</dd>
</dl>
<p>Other miscellaneous personnel include the Assistant Director, HUB accountant, Prop Crew (who maintains, guards, and transports show props and equipment), <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.hubalums.org/mom/index.html">MOM</a>, Crusties (band alumni), and foosball league commissioner. Local artist Alice Tondel served as MOM and began her association with the HUB in 1949 until her death in 1993.</p>
<p>The Senior Staff is selected by the previous Senior Staff. The official transition takes place annually in the HUB section of the stands after the completion of the halftime show at <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Yale_football_games_(The_Game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Yale football games (The Game)">The Game</a>.</p>

<li>The Band's <a href="/wiki/Bass_drum" title="Bass drum">bass drum</a>, depicted on the HUB logo, is towed on wheels and measures approximately 8 feet across. The HUB newsletter is also named the "Bass Drum Journal". The drum is called Bertha, and it is the largest playable natural skin bass drum in the world (made from cow's hide). In the past, Bertha was the target of thefts by the rival bands from Yale and Brown. In 1963, the giant drumstick used to sound rhythm on Bertha was spirited away during the second half of the Columbia game at Harvard's home stadium; members of the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University_Marching_Band" title="Columbia University Marching Band">Columbia University Marching Band</a> at the time, and their progeny, have no idea who may have taken the stick.
<ul>
<li>Bertha originally was purchased in 1927<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> by the Associated Harvard Clubs, when the band requested a bass drum to play at their convention. Given a blank check, the band purchased the largest drum available. The band has not been invited to play for any Associated Harvard Club conventions since. The current one was purchased in 1956 after a series of fundraising concerts when the other became too damaged to play.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>HUB owns one of the world's largest working <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tubas</a>: a 7-foot-tall (2.1&#160;m) triple B-flat <a href="/wiki/Besson_(company)" title="Besson (company)">Besson</a> that is more than 50 years old. The musician playing this instrument must experiment and relearn which valve combinations are appropriate for each note. Musicians who have played the tuba in public performances include Boston Symphony Orchestra tubist Chester Schmitz,<sup id="cite_ref-powell_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powell-4">[4]</a></sup> and Sam Pilafian.<sup id="cite_ref-spilka_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spilka-5">[5]</a></sup> The tuba last had a large accumulation of dent damage removed somewhere between 1994-2002. The Tuba is engraved <i>"Besson &amp; Sons, London England, Carl Fischer, U.S Agent, New York</i>", and is one of three sub-bass Tubas.<sup id="cite_ref-Baines_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baines-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The band owns a large wooden chair (the Throne) which is ornately decorated with the Harvard logo and HUB motto. It was a gift of the University and the Class of 1903, which the band received in 1953. Currently it is the seat of the Drill Master during drill meetings at which the show for the next week is planned.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prop_Crew">Prop Crew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prop Crew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Band "Prop Crew" is part of the Harvard Band and is an integral part of the band's spirit and performance. Prop crew members do not play instruments but generally assist with putting on the halftime show (often acting as extras in the performances) and playing "Bertha." Prop Crew also protects Bertha at games when it is not being given away by errant Band freshman. The attire for Prop Crew members is a white jump suits with "HARVARD BAND" emblazoned in red stitching on the back.</p>

<ul>
<li>1954 - On March 6, the band took to the ice for the first time to skate and play for the Yale Hockey game.</li>
<li>1968 - The band is invited to play graveside at the interment service for Senator Robert F. Kennedy at Arlington Cemetery.</li>
<li>1970 - To celebrate the integration of the (female) Racliffe students into the traditionally all-male Harvard Houses (upper class dormitories), the band suggested that language study would be particularly improved by these "cunning linguists." Ever since all half-time shows have been reviewed by administration officials.</li>
<li>1971 - Director Thomas Everett founds the Harvard Jazz Band</li>
<li>1972 - Members of the Brown University Band, posing as an ABC News crew complete with blazers, jump suits, camera and truck, persuades a band freshman to help them transport the "Big Bass Drum" down to Soldiers Field for pre-game filming whereupon they absconded with the drum. A friendly Massachusetts judge (and Band alumn) issues a bench warrant and the malefactors are soon caught by State Troopers. Future band manager Sam Coppersmith remains the only person ever to be awarded "Turkey of the Year" by two separate Ivy League Bands.</li>

<li>1976 - At the Brown game, student conductor Jack Barbash lands in a helicopter on the field dressed as <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">Leonard Bernstein</a>, and the impersonation is said to have been believed by the audience.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_alumni">Notable alumni</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable alumni">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leroy_Anderson" title="Leroy Anderson">Leroy Anderson</a> (pronounced luh-ROY) '29 (Trombone) was director of the HUB from 1931-35. He also played as an undergraduate beginning in 1926, and was student conductor from 1928-1930. Composer of <i>The Syncopated Clock</i> (used for 25 years as the theme music for "The Late Show," the WCBS late-night movie.), <i>Fiddle-Faddle</i>, <i>The Typewriter</i>, <i>Blue Tango</i>, <i>A Trumpeter's Lullaby</i> and the Christmas classic <a href="/wiki/Sleigh_Ride" title="Sleigh Ride">Sleigh Ride</a> among many others.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Harvard University Band's new headquarters was named the "Anderson Band Center" in 1995 in honor of <a href="/wiki/Leroy_Anderson" title="Leroy Anderson">Leroy Anderson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-leroyandersonfoundation.org_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leroyandersonfoundation.org-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Theodore_Kaczynski" class="mw-redirect" title="Theodore Kaczynski">Theodore Kaczynski</a> '62 (Trombone) is also known as the <i>Unabomber</i>. He briefly joined the HUB as a freshman in 1958.</li>
<li>G. Wright Briggs '31 was band director from 1953-59 was a member of the theory and composition faculty of the New England Conservatory of Music and program director for the famous Boston public radio station WBZ.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Eugene_Everhart" title="Thomas Eugene Everhart">Thomas Eugene Everhart</a> '53 (Trombone) was the president of <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">Caltech</a> from 1987-1997.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_M._Dobson" class="mw-redirect" title="David M. Dobson">David M. Dobson</a> '91 (Tuba) is the creator of the computer game <a href="/wiki/Snood_(game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Snood (game)">Snood</a>. As a Tubist, Dobson was known to play <i><a href="/wiki/Flight_of_the_Bumblebee" title="Flight of the Bumblebee">Flight of the Bumblebee</a></i> and also arranged a 3-tuba <i><a href="/wiki/Pachelbel" class="mw-redirect" title="Pachelbel">Pachelbel</a>'s Canon</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tom_McGrath_(media_executive)" title="Tom McGrath (media executive)">Tom McGrath</a> '76 (Trumpet) was a founding member of the Harvard Jazz Band and Student Conductor from 1975 to 1976. He is a Hollywood and Broadway producer. Produced the film <i>The Princess Bride</i> and the Broadway revivals of <i>West Side Story</i> and <i>Hair</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sam_Coppersmith" title="Sam Coppersmith">Sam Coppersmith</a>, 1975-76 Band Manager and victim of the Brown University stolen bass drum stunt, later became a Congressman from Arizona.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Songs">Songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><i>Fight Fiercely</i></p>
<ul>
<li>Tom Lehrer, Harvard class of 1946, undoubtedly intended his song parody, "Fight Fiercely, Harvard" to mock the normally bellicose language of football fight songs. In keeping with the irreverent spirit of the band, they have adopted the song, and it is now sung with gusto at all the football games.</li>
</ul>
<p><i>Unofficial</i></p>
<ul>
<li>Budweiser <small>(played after every gig)</small></li>
<li>Ted Kennedy Song (song in honor of the local senator, Harvard alumnus, and favorite guest conductor)</li>
<li>Sieve-Goalie (the tune of Hava Nagila played by the clarinets at hockey games to mock the opposing goalie)</li>
<li>Theme from Hawaii Five-O (played during hockey games when Harvard is winning 5-0)</li>
<li>Three Blind Mice (formerly played by the tubas when the referees emerge at hockey games, but due to accusations of unsportsmanlike conduct is relegated to a quiet rendition after particularly upsetting judgements against Harvard)</li>
<li>The Bagpipe Cheer (the saxophones play "Scotland the Brave" while others dance a traditional Scottish dance)</li>
<li><i>Underdog Theme</i> played by the trombone section.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cheers">Cheers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cheers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Black Hole Cheer</i> – Used in hockey matches against opposing goalie.</li>
<li><i>"1 2 3 4 Our team can really score, 1 1 1 1, humiliating isn't it?" OR</i> "2 4 6 8 Our team is really great, 1 1 1 1, humiliating isn't it?" <i>– Cheer for Hockey when the score is 4-1 or 8-1, respectively. Also performed in 4-0 and 8-0 versions.</i></li>
<li><i>"That's all right, That's OK, You'll all work for us some day"</i> – Cheer for when team is losing. Was banned for HUB use by Harvard Administration.</li>
<li><i>"Hey Ref, you suck, we know where you live. Hey ref, we know, where you live... sucks."</i></li>
<li><i>Repel them, Repel them, make them relinquish the ball</i> to support the football defensive plays. (Written by Tom Lehrer)</li>
<li><i>Navy Cheer</i>: "Gooooooooo Har-vard! Beeeeeeeeat ___-___" for all sporting events</li>
<li><i>Safety Cheer</i>: "Hey [opposing school], 3 points is a field goal, two points is a safety, <a href="/wiki/Safety_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Safety school">safety school</a>, <a href="/wiki/Safety_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Safety school">safety school</a>" alternately: "6 points is a touchdown, etc." (used when Harvard is winning in hockey with a score of 3 to 2 or 6 to 2). It was banned for use by Harvard administration against any <i>non-<a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a></i> opponent.</li>
<li><i>Sieve Cheer</i>: (while pointing) "Sieve! Sieve! Sieve! (etc.) It's all your fault!" (used at Hockey games against the opposing goalie at the beginning of each period and when Harvard scores; also used when in the case that any obvious mistake is made, for example a flubbed musical entry or note)</li>
<li><i>Engineers Cheer</i>: E to the x, d-y,d-x; E to the y, d-y; Cosine, Secant, Tangent, Sine; 3 point 1,4,1,5,9—Come on Harvard, Give 'em the digit!! (With appropriate accompanying hand gesture.)</li>
</ul>
<p><i>Staff cheers</i><br />
These cheers are intended for the band itself, rather than the audience</p>
<ul>
<li><i>The Flower Cheer</i> – variants: Flour Cheer, Spaghetti Cheer (or any cheer for objects thrown into the HUB section of the stands).</li>
<li><i>The Greek Cheer</i> – a rousing cheer in honor of the new freshmen members of the band.</li>
<li><i>MOM</i> cheer – a cheer for Alice Tondel, aka "MOM". The word "MOM" is spelled backward, forward, and upside-down (WOW).</li>
<li><i>the Humpty Dumpty Cheer</i>, yelled by those in the back of the stands when they can't be heard</li>
<li><i>The Bottle Cheer</i> – cheer performed during third quarter during years when drinking age was 18. Band members would rhythmically beat the bottles they had emptied and would punctuate each phrase with the word, FIGHT. Obviously no longer performed (cf. Confetti cheer and one-time only Greek cheer, performed at Yale game 1978)</li>
</ul>
<p><i>Trumpet cheers</i><br />
The Harvard Band delights in mocking the conventions of High School bands. With that in mind, where a typical High School band, or for that matter most major college bands, will have their trumpet section play traditional fanfares such as an organist might play at a baseball game, followed by shouting "fight," the Harvard Band trumpet section is famous for playing orchestral excerpts followed by shouting the same "fight." Their repertoire currently includes both network's Olympic fanfares; Eine Kleine Nachtmusik; the triumphal March from Aida; the Overture to the Marriage of Figaro; the entry of the Doge from Otello; Rule Brittania; Dies Irae from Verdi's Requiem; the trumpet solo from <i>Petrushka</i>; the slow movement of Beethoven's 7th (when losing); the finale of Stravinsky's <i>Firebird</i> and several others. The tradition is believed to have originated at Harvard under John Posner '71 and Ken Zunder '73, and has been added to by each section leader ever since.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Latin_verse">The Latin verse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Latin verse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is an old saying that, "You can always tell a Harvard Man (now Grad)....but you can't tell 'em very much." In keeping with that tradition, the main Harvard fight song, <i>Ten Thousand Men of Harvard</i> features a first verse in Latin. The verse is intended as an extended Latin pun and makes little sense when translated:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Illigitimum non Carborundum, Domine Salvum Fac,<br />
Illigitimum non Carborundum, Domine Salvum Fac.<br />
Gaudeamus Igitur,<br />
Veritas, non Sequitur,<br />
Illigitimum non Carborundum, Ipso Facto</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reunions">Reunions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reunions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HUB_75th.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/HUB_75th.jpg/200px-HUB_75th.jpg" width="200" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/HUB_75th.jpg/300px-HUB_75th.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/HUB_75th.jpg/400px-HUB_75th.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1161" data-file-height="795" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HUB_75th.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tom McGrath '76 and his father Joe McGrath '44 at the Harvard Band's 75th reunion</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Uniquely among college bands, the Harvard Band holds its own reunion every five years. It is also the oldest college band reunion in the world, with the first held at the Harvard University Band's 30th Anniversary in 1949. The Band's 90th Reunion in 2009 had almost 200 alumni return to perform in Soldier's Field. These reunions allow a member to reconnect with friends from several graduating classes. By contrast, a typical class reunion is only for people who all graduated in the same year.</p>

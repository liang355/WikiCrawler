<p>In the United States, <b>school colors</b> are the <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">colors</a> chosen by a <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">school</a> to represent it on <a href="/wiki/School_uniform" title="School uniform">uniforms</a> and other items of identification. Most schools have two colors, which are usually chosen to avoid conflicts with other schools<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> with which the school competes in sports and other activities. The colors are often worn to build <a href="/wiki/Morale" title="Morale">morale</a> among the teachers and pupils, and as an expression of <a href="/wiki/School_spirit" title="School spirit">school spirit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>School colors are often found in pairs and rarely no more than trios, though some professional teams use up to four colors in a set. The choice of colors usually follows the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_tincture" title="Rule of tincture">rule of tincture</a> from <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a>, but exceptions to this rule are known.</p>
<p>Common primary colors include <b><a href="/wiki/Orange_(colour)" title="Orange (colour)">orange</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a></b>, and <b><a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a></b>. These colors are either paired with a color representing a metal (often <b><a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Brown" title="Brown">brown</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Gray" class="mw-redirect" title="Gray">gray</a></b> (or <b><a href="/wiki/Silver_(color)" title="Silver (color)">silver</a></b>), <b><a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a></b>, or <b><a href="/wiki/Gold_(color)" title="Gold (color)">gold</a></b>), or occasionally each other, such as <b>orange/blue</b>, <b>red/green</b>, or <b>blue/yellow</b>. Pairing two metals, such as <b>black/white</b>, <b>silver/gold</b>, and especially <b>black/gold</b>, is also a common practice. Finally, some <a href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="United States of America">American</a> schools, in a display of patriotism, adopt the national colors of <b>red, white, or blue</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an effort to further establish identity and promote a standard, many institutions often decree the use of specific shades of colors. <b><a href="/wiki/Maroon_(color)" class="mw-redirect" title="Maroon (color)">Maroon</a>,</b> generally regarded as a darker shade of red, is a common primary color. Various shades of blue, from <a href="/wiki/Powder_blue" title="Powder blue">powder</a> to <a href="/wiki/Prussian_blue" title="Prussian blue">Prussian</a>, are also in use; a few schools have adopted two different shades of blue for their colors, with the darker shade serving as the primary. The shade of gold can vary greatly even within an institution, from a vivid <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">yellow</a> to a more convincing <a href="/wiki/Old_gold" title="Old gold">old gold</a>.</p>
<p>Black, white and gray are often used as neutral colors for sets that do not otherwise adopt them. This practice is especially notable in basketball (where home uniforms are often white) and professional baseball (where team colors are often used as trim for white or gray uniforms).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=School_colors&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most competitive teams keep two sets of uniforms, with one emphasizing the primary color and the other emphasizing the secondary color. In some sports, such as <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a>, the primary color is emphasized on home uniforms, while uniforms for other sports, notably <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, use the secondary or a neutral color at home. This is done to avoid confusing the two schools' colors. In addition, various groups that generate support for athletic teams, including <a href="/wiki/Cheerleading" title="Cheerleading">cheerleaders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marching_band" title="Marching band">marching bands</a>, wear uniforms with the colors of their school.</p>
<p>School colors have many non-athletic purposes as well. Members of a university's community will often display them as a sign of support or spirit for their particular institution. Likewise, during college or university ceremonies, those schools which award an <a href="/wiki/Academic_hood" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic hood">academic hood</a> to their students will generally abide by the <a href="/wiki/American_Council_on_Education" title="American Council on Education">American Council on Education</a> guidelines and use the school colors on the inside and the disciplinary colors on the outside velvet trim (regardless of the ceremony, recipients of a degree have the right to wear the hood thereafter). Some <a href="/wiki/Academic_regalia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic regalia">doctoral robes</a> will also be in the colors of the university which granted the degree. At many private schools, or more traditional state schools, "school colours" are <a href="/wiki/Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Awards">awards</a> presented for achievement in a subject or a sport - See <a href="/wiki/Sporting_colours" title="Sporting colours">Sporting colours</a>.</p>
<p>British and Irish universities traditionally have an <a href="/wiki/Academic_scarf" title="Academic scarf">academic scarf</a> in the university's colors, usually long, woollen and patterned only with lengthwise stripes of varying widths. At <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_university" title="Collegiate university">collegiate universities</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_University" title="Lancaster University">Lancaster</a>, each college has its own colors and scarf. Other non-collegiate universities such as <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University" title="Newcastle University">Newcastle</a> have scarf colors for each faculty.</p>

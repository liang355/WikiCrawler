<p><b>Henley Royal Regatta</b> (or <b>Henley Regatta</b>, its original name pre-dating Royal patronage) is a <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">rowing</a> event held annually on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> by the town of <a href="/wiki/Henley-on-Thames" title="Henley-on-Thames">Henley-on-Thames</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. It was established on 26 March 1839. It differs from the three other regattas rowed over approximately the same course, <a href="/wiki/Henley_Women%27s_Regatta" title="Henley Women's Regatta">Henley Women's Regatta</a>, Henley Masters Regatta and Henley Town and Visitors' Regatta, each of which is an entirely separate event.</p>
<p>The regatta lasts for five days (Wednesday to Sunday) ending on the first weekend in July. Races are head-to-head knock out competitions, raced over a course of 1 mile, 550 yards (2,112 m).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The regatta regularly attracts international crews to race. The most prestigious event at the regatta is the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Challenge_Cup" title="Grand Challenge Cup">Grand Challenge Cup</a> for Men's Eights, which has been awarded since the regatta was first staged.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the regatta pre-dates any national or international rowing organisation, it has its own rules and organisation, although it is recognised by both <a href="/wiki/British_Rowing" title="British Rowing">British Rowing</a> (the governing body of rowing in England and Wales) and <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_Rowing_Associations" class="mw-redirect" title="International Federation of Rowing Associations">FISA</a> (the International Federation of Rowing Associations).<sup id="cite_ref-hrr_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hrr-3">[3]</a></sup> The regatta is organised by a self-electing body of Stewards, who are largely former rowers themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-hrr_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hrr-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Coubertin" title="Pierre de Coubertin">Pierre de Coubertin</a> modelled elements of the organisation of the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> on the Henley Stewards.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The regatta is regarded as part of the English <a href="/wiki/Season_(society)" title="Season (society)">social season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As with other events in the season, certain enclosures at the regatta have strict dress codes.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Format_of_competition">Format of competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Format of competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualifying">Qualifying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Qualifying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Entries for the regatta close at 6:00&#160;pm sixteen days before the Regatta.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In order to encourage a high quality of racing, create a manageable race timetable and to ensure that most crews race only once a day, each event has a limited number of places. Qualifying races are held on the Friday before the regatta. The regatta's Committee of Management decides at its absolute discretion which crews are obliged to qualify; the Committee will examine the form and calibre of the entrants and may choose to pre-qualify some of them.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The qualifying races take the form of a timed processional race up the regatta course, with the fastest crews qualifying. Times are released for non-qualifying crews only. This does not stop an enthusiastic band of unofficial timers with synchronised watches working out how fast their first round opposition might be.</p>
<p>If it is apparent that there are a number of outstanding crews in an event, they may be 'selected' by the Stewards, to prevent them from meeting too early in the competition. The regatta insists that selection is not the same as <a href="/wiki/Single-elimination_tournament#Seeding" title="Single-elimination tournament">seeding</a>, the main difference being that there is no 'rank order' as is usually the case in, for example, a tennis tournament.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_draw">The draw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The draw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The draw is a public event that takes place in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_town_hall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Henley town hall (page does not exist)">Henley town hall</a>, normally at 3&#160;pm on the Saturday before the regatta. For each event the names of all selected crews are placed on pieces of paper which are then drawn at random from the Grand Challenge Cup. These crews are then placed on pre-determined positions on the draw chart, as far apart as possible. The remaining qualifying crews are then drawn from the cup, filling in from the top of the draw chart downwards, until all places have been filled.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Racing">Racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Qubathenley.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Qubathenley.jpg/320px-Qubathenley.jpg" width="320" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Qubathenley.jpg/480px-Qubathenley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Qubathenley.jpg/640px-Qubathenley.jpg 2x" data-file-width="649" data-file-height="372" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Qubathenley.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Two crews racing in the <a href="/wiki/Temple_Challenge_Cup" title="Temple Challenge Cup">Temple Challenge Cup</a> at Henley in 2003</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Each event in the regatta takes the form of a knockout competition, with each race consisting of two crews racing side by side up the Henley course. The course is marked out by two lines of booms (wooden bars which float on the water, secured between vertical poles), which are placed along the river to form a straight course 2,112&#160;metres long. The course is wide enough to allow two crews to race down with a few metres between them. As such it is not uncommon for inexperienced steersmen or <a href="/wiki/Coxswains" class="mw-redirect" title="Coxswains">coxswains</a> to crash into the booms, possibly costing their crew the race.</p>
<p>The race begins at the downstream end of Temple Island, where the crews attach to a pair of pontoons. The race umpire will then call out the names of the two crews and start them when they are both straight and ready. Each crew is assigned to row on either the 'Bucks' (<a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>) or 'Berks' (<a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>) side of the race course. The coxswains or steersmen are expected to keep their crew on the allocated side of the course at all times during the race, else they risk disqualification. The only exception is when a crew leads by a sizeable margin and is not deemed by the umpire to be impeding the trailing crew.</p>
<p>There are several progress markers along the course. Intermediate times are recorded at two of them – "the Barrier" and "Fawley", in addition to the time to the finish. The regatta has official commentary, which is announced at these points along the course. The commentary is renowned for being unemotional and factual, with the commentator only allowed to announce the <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_striking" class="mw-redirect" title="Rate of striking">rate of striking</a>, which crew is leading, the distance between the crews, and the progress marker which the crews are passing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_course">The course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Henley Royal Regatta has always been raced over a distance of ‘about one mile and 550 yards’ from Temple Island upstream towards Henley Bridge. However, four distinct courses have been used over the regatta’s history, with smaller changes also being made incrementally. Changes to the course have all been aimed at improving the prospects for fair and safe racing.</p>
<h4><span id="The_Old_Course_(1839–1885)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Old_Course_.281839.E2.80.931885.29">The Old Course (1839–1885)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Old Course (1839–1885)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henley_Regatta_by_Tissot_1877.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Henley_Regatta_by_Tissot_1877.jpg/320px-Henley_Regatta_by_Tissot_1877.jpg" width="320" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/28/Henley_Regatta_by_Tissot_1877.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="216" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henley_Regatta_by_Tissot_1877.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An 1877 painting by James Tissot showing the Old Course</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>This ran from a point just upstream of Temple Island. At the first regatta in 1839, the finish line was Henley Bridge itself, but it was presumably quickly realised that this had inherent problems. From 1840 onward the finish was moved downstream slightly; eventually a point opposite the lawn of the Red Lion Hotel became the standard finish line. A grandstand was erected for the Stewards and their guests outside the Red Lion. Other spectators could watch from the adjacent roadway (in front of the Little White Hart Hotel) while those with carriages surveyed the scene from a vantage point on Henley Bridge. There were three racing stations (Berkshire, Centre and Buckinghamshire). When only two crews raced, the Centre Station was not used.</p>
<p>The Old Course had a large lefthand bend in the last quarter-mile. This benefited the crew on the Berkshire side of the course not only because they raced a shorter distance but also because they avoided the worst of the river’s current. Between 1866 and 1885, 57.7% all races were won by the crew on the Berkshire station, with the Buckinghamshire and Centre crews sharing 42.3%. The course was not boomed or piled, although between 1871 and 1873, poles were roped across the bay on the Berkshire side upstream of Poplar Point, in an attempt to minimise the advantage given to the crew on the Berkshire station.</p>
<h4><span id="The_New_Course_(1886–1922)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_New_Course_.281886.E2.80.931922.29">The New Course (1886–1922)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The New Course (1886–1922)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henley_regatta_course_map_1893.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Henley_regatta_course_map_1893.jpg/320px-Henley_regatta_course_map_1893.jpg" width="320" height="193" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Henley_regatta_course_map_1893.jpg/480px-Henley_regatta_course_map_1893.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Henley_regatta_course_map_1893.jpg/640px-Henley_regatta_course_map_1893.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="619" /></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>In 1884, a sub-committee of the regatta’s Committee of Management discussed options for reducing the unfairness of the course. Their recommendation was to move the finish line downstream to Poplar Point (thus avoiding the bend) and the start to the bottom of Temple Island. This was not popular with spectators as it made previous viewing points obsolete. The sub-committee also recommending reducing the racing lanes from three to two and extending racing from two days to three. The Committee gained support from the Captains of competing Clubs and the changes were introduced for the 1886 Regatta.</p>
<p>The New Course started just downstream of Temple Island, on the Buckinghamshire side and finished opposite the upstream end of Phyllis Court, very close to the current finish line. There were two slight bends (at Remenham and just after Fawley) and a staggered start to compensate for them. The course was also piled for the first time, although not boomed. Unfortunately, it became apparent that in trying to eliminate the unfairness of the Old Course, a new problem had been introduced. Downstream of Fawley, bushes grew alongside the Buckinghamshire bank and provided shelter from the prevailing wind. The course now ran close to this bank and crew on the Buckinghamshire station gained the advantage of shelter whenever a ‘Bushes Wind’ was blowing. From 1886 to 1905, Bucks took 59% of wins against 41% on Berks.</p>
<p>To attempt to reduce the effect of the Bushes Wind, the course was narrowed and pushed further to the centre of the river. The width was 135 feet in 1887, in 120 feet in 1888 and then progressively until by 1914 it was down to 100 feet at the start tapering to 80 feet at the finish. In 1899, floating booms secured between the pilings which mark the course were also introduced along part of its length in an attempt to keep spectators from obstructing races.</p>
<h4><span id="The_Experimental_Course_(1923)_and_the_Straight_Course_(1924_onwards)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Experimental_Course_.281923.29_and_the_Straight_Course_.281924_onwards.29">The Experimental Course (1923) and the Straight Course (1924 onwards)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Experimental Course (1923) and the Straight Course (1924 onwards)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In around 1920, the Stewards carried out a survey canvassing the idea of a moving the start of the course to the Berkshire side of Temple Island. At the time this channel was a winding, shallow backwater and it would clearly not be possible to lay a course of the full Henley distance without significant alteration to the bank, the island and the riverbed.</p>
<p>For the 1923 regatta, the Stewards therefore decided to try a shorter experimental straight course which started at the top of the island. This produced 53.2% wins on Bucks and 46.8% on Berks, deemed enough of an improvement on the New Course to justify a permanent change. The consent of the landowners of the Berkshire bank and Temple island (Lord Hambleden and W.D. Mackenzie respectively) was obtained to widen and deepen the Berkshire channel; 10,000 cubic yards (7,600&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of material were excavated. The Straight Course was now ready for use.</p>
<p>The Straight Course runs from the upstream end of Temple Island to a point opposite the upstream end of the Phyllis Court. It is 80 feet (24&#160;m) wide. The Straight Course has generally addressed the problems of unfairness: for example, between 1975 and 1984, 50.52% of races were won on Bucks and 49.31% on Berks (with the remainder dead heats). However, when a strong stream is flowing, the Berks station enjoys considerable shelter from the stream, particularly in the last ¼ mile. Conversely, when there is a strong south-westerly wind it is better to be on the Bucks station because it is more sheltered from the wind. The course is now piled and boomed along its entire length, except for crossing points. The booms can present a hazard for the inexperienced coxswain or steersman.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Precise_length_of_the_course">Precise length of the course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Precise length of the course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When the start and finish positions of the Old Course had become established, the distance between them was found to be 1 mile 570 yds (2131 metres). However, boats were aligned by their sterns at the start and judged by their bows at the finish. This meant that the course was slightly longer for single sculls than for eights. The length of an eight was assumed to be twenty yards and as such the course came to be described as ‘about 1 mile and 550 yards (2112 metres)’, which was the distance covered by an eight.</p>
<p>In 1967 the start of the Straight Course was relocated exactly 1 mile 550 yd from the finish. In the same year, moving pontoons were introduced at the start which allowed all boats, from singles to eights, to be aligned by the bows precisely on the start line. Since then all crews have raced a course of exactly one mile and 550 yards (2112 metres).<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the whole regatta, during racing hours there are numerous safety boats provided by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.thelifeguards.org.uk">Colwick Park Lifeguards</a> to keep competitors safe. The regatta organisers arrange for a first aid provider to be present, who have an on site hospital-style area.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henley_Regatta,_Henley-on-Thames,_England,_1890s.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Henley_Regatta%2C_Henley-on-Thames%2C_England%2C_1890s.jpg/220px-Henley_Regatta%2C_Henley-on-Thames%2C_England%2C_1890s.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Henley_Regatta%2C_Henley-on-Thames%2C_England%2C_1890s.jpg/330px-Henley_Regatta%2C_Henley-on-Thames%2C_England%2C_1890s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Henley_Regatta%2C_Henley-on-Thames%2C_England%2C_1890s.jpg/440px-Henley_Regatta%2C_Henley-on-Thames%2C_England%2C_1890s.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3556" data-file-height="2636" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henley_Regatta,_Henley-on-Thames,_England,_1890s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Regatta participants in the 1890s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At a public meeting in Henley town hall on 26 March 1839, Captain Edmund Gardiner proposed "that from the lively interest which had been manifested at the various boat races which have taken place on the Henley reach during the last few years, and the great influx of visitors on such occasions, this meeting is of the opinion that the establishing of an annual regatta, under judicious and respectable management, would not only be productive of the most beneficial results to the town of Henley, but from its peculiar attractions would also be a source of amusement and gratification to the neighbourhood, and the public in general."<sup id="cite_ref-dodd_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dodd-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The regatta was first staged in 1839 and proved so successful that it was expanded the next year from one day to two. As the regatta's popularity has grown it has further expanded: to three days in 1886, four days in 1906 and five days in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The regatta has been known as <i>Henley Royal Regatta</i> since 1851, when <a href="/wiki/Prince_Albert_of_Saxe-Coburg-Gotha" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg-Gotha">Prince Albert</a> became the first royal patron. Since his death, every reigning monarch has agreed to be the patron.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateurism">Amateurism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Amateurism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the regatta’s inception it was intended for <a href="/wiki/Amateurism" class="mw-redirect" title="Amateurism">amateur</a> oarsmen rather than those who rowed professionally. In 1879 Henley produced its first formal definition of an amateur:</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>No person shall be considered an amateur oarsman or sculler, or coxswain:
<ol>
<li>Who has ever competed in any open competition for a stake, money, or entrance fee. (Not to apply to foreign crews.)</li>
<li>Who has ever competed with or against a professional for any prize.</li>
<li>Who has ever taught, pursued or assisted in the practice of athletic exercises of any kind as a means of gaining a livelihood.</li>
<li>Who has been employed in or about boats for money or wages.</li>
<li>Who is or has been by trade or employment for wages a mechanic, artisan or labourer.</li>
</ol>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Henley Royal Regatta, 1879</cite></div>
<p>In 1884, amateur status for overseas oarsmen was put on the same basis as for home oarsmen, thus ending the concession on racing for money prizes. By 1886 a phrase had also been added debarring any person "engaged in any menial activity".</p>
<p>These rules would become the cause of growing controversy as international entries to Henley increased; most foreign countries having a different definition of amateur. The adoption of Henley's definition of amateur by the Amateur Rowing Association of Great Britain would also cause a 66-year schism in British rowing, when in 1890 a rival <a href="/wiki/National_Amateur_Rowing_Association" title="National Amateur Rowing Association">National Amateur Rowing Association</a> was set up, with a much more inclusive definition of amateurism.</p>
<p>One well-known incident was the exclusion of future <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic</a> champion <a href="/wiki/John_B._Kelly_Sr." title="John B. Kelly Sr.">John B. Kelly Sr.</a>, who had served an apprenticeship as a bricklayer, from the 1920 regatta. According to the minutes of the regatta's Committee of Management, Kelly was excluded both because he was not eligible under the manual labour rules and because he was a member of <a href="/wiki/Vesper_Boat_Club" title="Vesper Boat Club">Vesper Boat Club</a>, which was banned in 1906 because members of its 1905 Henley crew had raised money to pay for their trip through public donations – making them professionals in the eyes of the Henley Stewards.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kelly's exclusion was widely reported in newspapers in both the UK and USA, with many seeing it as an attempt to prevent an American from winning the Diamonds. Kelly's son <a href="/wiki/John_B._Kelly_Jr." title="John B. Kelly Jr.">John B. Kelly Jr.</a> would dramatically win the 1947 Diamond Sculls, and his daughter would become the famous Academy Award winning actress turned Princess of Monaco <a href="/wiki/Grace_Kelly" title="Grace Kelly">Grace Kelly</a>, keeping the incident in the public eye for years afterwards.</p>
<p>In 1936, there was a further controversy when the Australian national eight, preparing for the Berlin Olympics, was excluded from the Grand Challenge Cup because the crew was composed of policemen, deemed to be 'manual workers'. The resulting embarrassment persuaded the Amateur Rowing Association and the Stewards of Henley Royal Regatta of the need for change. On 9 June 1937, the offending references to manual labourers, mechanics, artisans and menial duties were deleted from the ARA rules; Henley's rules were changed the following day, coming into effect from the 1938 regatta.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 1997, FISA removed all references to amateurism in its rules and in December 1998 Henley followed suit. The regatta is therefore now entirely open.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_participation">International participation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: International participation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<a href="/wiki/Columbia_College,_Columbia_University" title="Columbia College, Columbia University">Columbia College</a>, winners of the <a href="/wiki/Visitors%27_Challenge_Cup" title="Visitors' Challenge Cup">Visitors' Challenge Cup</a> in 1878</div>

<p>The first ‘overseas’ entry to the regatta was in 1870 when <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Dublin" title="Trinity College, Dublin">Trinity College, Dublin</a> entered the Grand, Ladies’, Visitors’ and Wyfold. As Dublin was at that time within the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, this was not a foreign entry. TCD won the Visitors’ and reached the final of the Ladies’.</p>
<p>The first international competitors came in 1878 when G.W. Lee of <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> and G. Lee of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> entered the Diamonds, Shoe-wae-cae-mette BC of <a href="/wiki/Monroe,_Michigan" title="Monroe, Michigan">Monroe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, a crew of French Canadian watermen, entered the Stewards’ and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_College,_Columbia_University" title="Columbia College, Columbia University">Columbia College</a> entered the Stewards’ and Visitors’.</p>
<p>Lee of Boston made little progress but Lee of New Jersey lost his heat in a very close race against <a href="/wiki/Tom_Cottingham_Edwards-Moss" title="Tom Cottingham Edwards-Moss">T.C. Edwards-Moss</a>, the eventual winner. Shoe-wae-cae-mette, rowing with then-unusual swivel rowlocks, reached the final of the Stewards’ but lost to <a href="/wiki/London_Rowing_Club" title="London Rowing Club">London Rowing Club</a>.</p>
<p>Columbia won the <a href="/wiki/Visitors%27_Challenge_Cup" title="Visitors' Challenge Cup">Visitors' Challenge Cup</a>, becoming the first foreign winners of a Henley trophy.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unfortunately, there were accusations that both G.W. Lee and the Shoe-wae-cae-mette crew were not amateurs. This led in 1879 to a new, tighter, definition of amateurism (see above) and a requirement that any entries from outside the United Kingdom must be made on or before 1 March and must be ‘accompanied by a declaration made before Notary Public with regard to the profession of each member of the crew’, and this must be certified by the British <a href="/wiki/Consul" title="Consul">Consul</a>, the mayor, or the chief authority of the locality.</p>
<p>Under these new rules, Shoe-wae-cae-mette were refused entry in 1879 as were <a href="/wiki/Hillsdale,_Michigan" title="Hillsdale, Michigan">Hillsdale</a> Boat Club of Michigan in 1882.</p>
<p>The Germania Ruder Club of <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> became the first entry from continental Europe in 1880, losing in a heat of the Grand to London Rowing Club.</p>
<p>Foreign entries grew over the next twenty years, to the consternation of some who felt that the regatta should be restricted to domestic entries only. There were also a number of disputes over amateurism and the two issues were often bound up together, as in this letter to <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a> from <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Warre" title="Edmond Warre">Edmond Warre</a>, headmaster of <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a> in 1901:</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>I most earnestly desire that our amateur oarsmanship may be preserved from the deadly inroad of professionalism, which is already making a business of so much that ought only to be pleasure, and threatens to crush the life out of the sports of “merrie England”. Let us restrict our Henley pots to the United Kingdom and set up a proper international regatta elsewhere, if that is thought desirable.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Edmond Warre, <i>The Times</i></cite></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Grenfell,_1st_Baron_Desborough" title="William Grenfell, 1st Baron Desborough">W.H. Grenfell</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_parliament" title="Member of parliament">MP</a> proposed a motion for a special meeting of the Stewards that:</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>This meeting…while fully prepared to promote the establishment of an international regatta upon a proper course and under suitable conditions, is of the opinion that Henley Regatta does not provide either a proper course or suitable conditions for international competitions.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">William Grenfell</cite></div>
<p>He proposed amendments to the rules restricting entries to the United Kingdom, and for the Goblets and Diamonds to British subjects domiciled in the UK. Warre seconded his proposals. The <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Rowing_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Amateur Rowing Association">Amateur Rowing Association</a> canvassed its member clubs on the proposal and the results were decisive: all clubs opposed the proposals save for <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Boat_Club" title="Oxford University Boat Club">Oxford University Boat Club</a> which supported them with the caveat ‘Committee decide against foreign entries provided they can row other than Henley’.</p>
<p>At a special general meeting of the Stewards late in 1901, a motion moved by Colonel Makins ‘that in the opinion of this meeting it is inexpedient that any alteration in the rules of the regatta be made at present’ was carried by 19 votes to 5.<sup id="cite_ref-dodd_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dodd-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Belgians_win_the_Grand_1907.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Belgians_win_the_Grand_1907.jpg/220px-Belgians_win_the_Grand_1907.jpg" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Belgians_win_the_Grand_1907.jpg/330px-Belgians_win_the_Grand_1907.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Belgians_win_the_Grand_1907.jpg/440px-Belgians_win_the_Grand_1907.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="728" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Belgians_win_the_Grand_1907.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sport Nautique de Gand winning the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Challenge_Cup" title="Grand Challenge Cup">Grand Challenge Cup</a> in 1907</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1906, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Club_Nautique_de_Gand" title="Royal Club Nautique de Gand">Royal Club Nautique de Gand</a> of <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> became the first foreign crew to win the Grand Challenge Cup. A different Ghent Club, Sport Nautique de Gand took the Grand in 1907.</p>
<p>In advance of 1908, with the Olympic Regatta to be held on the Henley course in mid-July, the Stewards announced a temporary rule change excluding overseas entries from the 1908 regatta (which would take place two weeks before the Olympics). This led to criticism of the Stewards in the British and American press, particularly since it would not permit the Belgians to defend the Grand. The Stewards pointed out in a letter to The Times that the decision had been taken before the 1907 regatta and after consultation with the Belgians. A letter from Oscar Gregoire, President of the Belgian Rowing Federation was quoted:</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>In a year like 1908, which will see the Olympic Regatta take place at Henley…it is not reasonable to hold an international regatta 15 days previously…the Belgian holders of the Grand Challenge Cup would not have any object in going to defend it in 1908…</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Richard Burnell, <i>Henley Royal Regatta: A celebration of 150 years</i></cite></div>
<p>Overseas entries and wins at the regatta have continued to multiply. Since the 1960s, the open events in particular have almost exclusively become the province of national squad crews. Up to 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Challenge_Cup" title="Grand Challenge Cup">Grand Challenge Cup</a> had been won by overseas crews 46 times: 12 times by crews from Germany, 11 from the USA, 9 from the USSR, 4 from Canada, 3 each from Belgium and Australia, 2 from the Netherlands, and 1 each from Switzerland, France, Bulgaria and Croatia.</p>
<p>Crews from the USA have also become frequent winners of the <a href="/wiki/Ladies%27_Challenge_Plate" title="Ladies' Challenge Plate">Ladies' Challenge Plate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temple_Challenge_Cup" title="Temple Challenge Cup">Temple Challenge Cup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princess_Elizabeth_Challenge_Cup" title="Princess Elizabeth Challenge Cup">Princess Elizabeth Challenge Cup</a>. In 1927, the <a href="/wiki/Kent_School_Boat_Club" title="Kent School Boat Club">Kent School Boat Club</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kent_School" title="Kent School">Kent School</a> in Connecticut was the first American secondary school to race at the Henley Royal Regatta.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women">Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For most of its history, Henley Royal Regatta has only been open to male competitors but this has changed more recently. Women coxswains of male crews were permitted from 1975 and as such the first female competitor in the regatta was Christine Paul, cox of <a href="/wiki/Furnivall_Sculling_Club" title="Furnivall Sculling Club">Furnivall Sculling Club</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Challenge_Cup" title="Thames Challenge Cup">Thames Challenge Cup</a> in that year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-dodd_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dodd-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was much discussion about the introduction of events for women. The Stewards felt that they faced a dilemma: on the one hand it was argued that women's rowing would never flourish while there were no women's events at Britain's premier regatta; on the other it was clear that open women’s events would be dominated by foreign competitors, while events closed to foreign competitors would not serve the desired purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell-10">[10]</a></sup> Another difficulty was the length of the course. The standard distance for international women's races was at that time 1000&#160;m. It was clear that races over the full Henley course (2112&#160;m) would not be appealing to women's crews preparing for international championships.</p>
<p>Peter Coni, who was elected as Chairman of the regatta’s Committee of Management in 1978, was supportive of the idea of introducing women's events. However, he had had little time to take any action when there was an attempt to force the issue. <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Rowing_Club" title="Kingston Rowing Club">Kingston Rowing Club</a> sent in an entry for the <a href="/wiki/Double_Sculls_Challenge_Cup" title="Double Sculls Challenge Cup">Double Sculls Challenge Cup</a> in the names of A.Hohl and P.Bird. The crew was in fact Great Britain internationals Astrid Ayling and Pauline Hart, who had made the entry under their maiden names. The regatta realised the subterfuge and Coni made a statement to the press in which he said that it was 'sad that a long-established club should think it reasonable behaviour to make a deliberately false declaration'. He added that the stewards had no fear of the <a href="/wiki/Sex_Discrimination_Act_1975" title="Sex Discrimination Act 1975">Sex Discrimination Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dodd_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dodd-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibition_races">Exhibition races</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Exhibition races">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1979, the Committee announced that there would be exhibition events for women in 1981, with entries by invitation only:</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>The Stewards announced in December 1979 that they were considering the possibility of adding certain Events for Women to the Regatta programme. As a preliminary step two invitation events are being held this year, with racing from the Barrier start used for the Special Race for Schools. These events will enable the Stewards to assess the feasibility of including races over the shorter Course during the normal Regatta programme, and the desirability of the considerable extension to the hours of racing which any full Events for Women would necessarily involve.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><i>1981 Henley Royal Regatta programme</i></cite></div>
<p>The entries were limited to four in each event (Coxed Fours and Double Sculls); two from abroad and two from the UK. The standard was good, but it was reckoned that the course from the Barrier start (over 600&#160;m longer than the international standard 1000&#160;m) contributed to there being no close verdicts.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1982, a Single Sculls Event was added and the start was moved to Fawley so that the course was closer to 1000&#160;m. As the intermediate start installations were required for the shorter distance, the races had to take place during intervals in the normal racing programme (the lunch or tea breaks) which meant that only the dedicated stayed to watch.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This was a pity, since the final of the Women's Invitation Single was a highlight of the regatta, with <a href="/wiki/Beryl_Mitchell" class="mw-redirect" title="Beryl Mitchell">Beryl Mitchell</a> of Thames Tradesmen’s Rowing Club (World Silver medallist in 1981) beating Stephanie Foster of Waiariki Rowing Club, New Zealand (World Bronze medallist in 1982) by one length. Astrid Ayling was also able to race legitimately, winning the Invitation Double Sculls event with Rosie Clugston.</p>
<p>The time taken to install the start equipment at Fawley combined with the relative lack of crowd interest meant that the Stewards took the decision not to repeat the experiment in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-dodd_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dodd-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_of_current_events">Introduction of current events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Introduction of current events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%27Royal_Regatta%27_programme_1986_-_geograph.org.uk_-_257731.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/%27Royal_Regatta%27_programme_1986_-_geograph.org.uk_-_257731.jpg/220px-%27Royal_Regatta%27_programme_1986_-_geograph.org.uk_-_257731.jpg" width="220" height="315" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/%27Royal_Regatta%27_programme_1986_-_geograph.org.uk_-_257731.jpg/330px-%27Royal_Regatta%27_programme_1986_-_geograph.org.uk_-_257731.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/%27Royal_Regatta%27_programme_1986_-_geograph.org.uk_-_257731.jpg/440px-%27Royal_Regatta%27_programme_1986_-_geograph.org.uk_-_257731.jpg 2x" data-file-width="447" data-file-height="640" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%27Royal_Regatta%27_programme_1986_-_geograph.org.uk_-_257731.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A programme of events from the 1986 regatta</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The change of the international distance to 2000&#160;m and the addition of a fifth day to the regatta in 1986 allowed the Committee of Management to revisit the decision. In 1993 the regatta introduced an open Women’s Single Sculls event and from 1993–6 this counted as a round of the FISA World Cup. The first winner was <a href="/wiki/Maria_Brandin" title="Maria Brandin">Maria Brandin</a> of Sweden and she subsequently won a further four times. Fittingly, the prizegiver in 1993 was Peter Coni. In 1996, the Stewards purchased a silver cup as a challenge trophy and named it the <a href="/wiki/Princess_Royal_Challenge_Cup" title="Princess Royal Challenge Cup">Princess Royal Challenge Cup</a>; it was presented for the first time in 1997.</p>
<p>An invitation event for women's eights was added in 1998. In 2000, this was replaced by an open women's eights event under the same rules as the Grand Challenge Cup. In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Remenham_Challenge_Cup" title="Remenham Challenge Cup">Remenham Challenge Cup</a> was donated by <a href="/wiki/Remenham_Club" title="Remenham Club">Remenham Club</a> as the trophy for this event. At the 2001 Regatta, an open event for Women's Quadruple Sculls was introduced. In 2003, this event became known as the <a href="/wiki/Princess_Grace_Challenge_Cup" title="Princess Grace Challenge Cup">Princess Grace Challenge Cup</a> in memory of Princess Grace of Monaco. Her father, John B. Kelly Sr., had been controversially excluded from the <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Challenge_Sculls" title="Diamond Challenge Sculls">Diamond Sculls</a> in 1920 because of the regattas rules on amateurism; her brother, John B. Kelly Jr. had won the Diamonds Sculls in 1947 and in 1949. Princess Grace was the Regatta prizegiver in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the Stewards' meeting in December 2016, it was agreed to equalise the number of events for Men and Women in the Open category, leading to the creation of three new events: the Women's Fours, the Women's Pairs and the Women's Double Sculls <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>. These were raced for the first time at the 2017 Regatta.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_regattas">Other regattas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other regattas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1908_Olympic_Regatta">1908 Olympic Regatta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 1908 Olympic Regatta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rowing_at_the_1908_Summer_Olympics" title="Rowing at the 1908 Summer Olympics">Rowing at the 1908 Summer Olympics</a></div>
<p>Henley hosted the rowing events of the London Olympic Games in mid-July 1908, two weeks after the Royal Regatta. The course was extended 270 yards (250&#160;m) downstream and 60 yards (55&#160;m) upstream, making it 1-mile (1.6&#160;km) and 880 yards (800&#160;m). Events for single sculls, coxless pairs, coxless fours and eights were offered but only the eights produced an international final.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1919_Peace_Regatta">1919 Peace Regatta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 1919 Peace Regatta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A meeting arranged by <a href="/wiki/Leander_Club" title="Leander Club">Leander Club</a> in January 1919 concluded that it was too soon to revive the regatta but asked the Stewards to arrange a “Peace Regatta” and a four-day regatta was duly staged. Competition was not for the usual Henley trophies but for different cups and some events were restricted to ‘armed services’ crews.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1945_Royal_Henley_Regatta">1945 Royal Henley Regatta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 1945 Royal Henley Regatta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A two-day ‘Royal Henley Regatta’ (as opposed to Henley Royal Regatta) was staged between <a href="/wiki/VE_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="VE Day">VE Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/VJ_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="VJ Day">VJ Day</a>. Crews rowed three-abreast over a shortened course beginning at the Remenham Barrier. As in 1919, different trophies to the usual regatta prizes were on offer.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1948_Olympic_Regatta">1948 Olympic Regatta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 1948 Olympic Regatta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canoeing_at_the_1948_Summer_Olympics" title="Canoeing at the 1948 Summer Olympics">Canoeing at the 1948 Summer Olympics</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rowing_at_the_1948_Summer_Olympics" title="Rowing at the 1948 Summer Olympics">Rowing at the 1948 Summer Olympics</a></div>
<p>The canoeing and rowing events for the 1948 London Olympic Games were contested in early August, a month after the Royal Regatta. The course was widened to accommodate three lanes and shortened to start at the top of Temple Island: the distance was approximately 1980 metres – 20 metres short of the standard international distance. Twenty seven nations competed and events for single sculls, double sculls, coxless pairs, coxed pairs, coxless fours, coxed fours and eights were offered.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Olympic races in 2012 were held at <a href="/wiki/Dorney_Lake" title="Dorney Lake">Dorney Lake</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectators">Spectators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Spectators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henley_regatta_temple_island.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Henley_regatta_temple_island.jpg/170px-Henley_regatta_temple_island.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Henley_regatta_temple_island.jpg/255px-Henley_regatta_temple_island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Henley_regatta_temple_island.jpg/340px-Henley_regatta_temple_island.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1359" data-file-height="1812" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henley_regatta_temple_island.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Temple_Island" title="Temple Island">Temple Island</a> — the start of the regatta course</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The racing can be viewed from a number of locations along both banks of the <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">Thames</a>. Areas open to the general public are generally on the Berkshire (<a href="/wiki/Towpath" title="Towpath">towpath</a>) side of the river. Viewing opportunities on the other side of the river (Buckinghamshire) side are limited to the <a href="/wiki/Phyllis_Court" title="Phyllis Court">Phyllis Court</a> club, private residences and areas for <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporate</a> entertainment. A channel of the river remains open throughout the regatta, hence racing can also be viewed from boats although mooring is tightly restricted.</p>
<h3><span id="Stewards'_Enclosure"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Stewards.27_Enclosure">Stewards' Enclosure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Stewards' Enclosure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The enclosure situated on the Berkshire side, adjacent to the last part of the course and the finish line. It comprises two covered grandstands, a restaurant marquee, several bars, a bandstand and so on – all set in immaculately prepared lawns. It is only open to the Stewards of the Regatta, members of the Stewards' Enclosure and their guests. Overseas competitors are also given the opportunity to purchase tickets.</p>
<p>The Stewards' Enclosure as understood today, an enclosure open to members (elected by the Committee of Management of the Regatta) and their guests, came into being in 1919 with a membership of 300. This grew to 704 in 1939 and 1,500 in 1956. In 1980 the Stewards set a ceiling of 5,000. The waiting list for membership of the Stewards Enclosure is now several years long, although preference is given to people who have previously competed at the regatta. The waiting list has grown rapidly since the 1970s, when membership could be applied for and granted on the same day.</p>
<p>The social position of the event means that some in the Stewards' Enclosure (and elsewhere along the course) may have no interest in the actual rowing.</p>
<p>The Stewards' Enclosure is also known for a strict enforcement of its dress code. Men are required to wear a "lounge suit, blazer and flannels, or evening dress, and a tie". Women are required to wear a dress or skirt that covers their knees, and are "encouraged to wear a hat" (although women wearing hats is often frowned upon in higher rowing circles). Anyone not suitably dressed can be refused entry, no matter their prestige in rowing or elsewhere. Mobile phone use is also prohibited.</p>
<p>The regatta prizegiving takes place in the Stewards' Enclosure after the conclusion of racing on Sunday.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regatta_Enclosure">Regatta Enclosure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Regatta Enclosure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Regatta Enclosure is situated immediately downstream of the Stewards Enclosure and further away from the finish. This enclosure is open to all on payment of the admission fee. Competitors gain free access to this area. There is no dresscode.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remenham_Club">Remenham Club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Remenham Club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RemenhamClub01.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/RemenhamClub01.JPG/220px-RemenhamClub01.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/RemenhamClub01.JPG/330px-RemenhamClub01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/RemenhamClub01.JPG/440px-RemenhamClub01.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RemenhamClub01.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Remenham Club</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Remenham_Club" title="Remenham Club">Remenham Club</a> is situated a little over halfway along the course on the Berkshire side of the river. It is a social club run by and for members of seven rowing clubs on the Thames (the "Founding Clubs"): <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Rowing_Club" title="Kingston Rowing Club">Kingston Rowing Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Rowing_Club" title="London Rowing Club">London Rowing Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molesey_Boat_Club" title="Molesey Boat Club">Molesey Boat Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staines_Boat_Club" title="Staines Boat Club">Staines Boat Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thames_Rowing_Club" title="Thames Rowing Club">Thames Rowing Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twickenham_Rowing_Club" title="Twickenham Rowing Club">Twickenham Rowing Club</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vesta_Rowing_Club" title="Vesta Rowing Club">Vesta Rowing Club</a>. Remenham Club is open only to its members and guests. It has a similar dresscode to the Stewards Enclosure, though the rules on women's outfits are less restrictive.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_Thames_Rowing_Club">Upper Thames Rowing Club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Upper Thames Rowing Club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The clubhouse and frontage of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Thames_Rowing_Club" title="Upper Thames Rowing Club">Upper Thames Rowing Club</a> is on the Berkshire side of the river, immediately downstream of Remenham Club. It has historically had an informal atmosphere and no dresscode. It is only open to its members and their guests. In 2011, UTRC initiated the Upper Thames Enclosure to include a fairly relaxed dress code. UTRC lease the land downstream of their clubhouse to retailers, food vendors and bars.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remenham_Farm">Remenham Farm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Remenham Farm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Remenham Farm spans the riverside on the Berkshire side of the Thames from opposite the Fawley box to a point just upstream of the Barrier (approximately the 1100 m – 700m points on the regatta course). It is owned by The Copas Partnership.</p>
<p>During the Regatta it runs hospitality enclosures at The Temple Island Enclosure and The Courtyard Enclosure, and bars including the <i>Barn Bar</i>. Remenham Farm is open to the public and entrance fees apply for certain enclosures, boat moorings are also available.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leander_Club">Leander Club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Leander Club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The clubhouse and grounds of <a href="/wiki/Leander_Club" title="Leander Club">Leander Club</a> is situated on the Berkshire side of the river beyond the finish line of the regatta course, between the boat tents and Henley Bridge. Leander Club has a large marquee constructed for the regatta week and holds a large function on the Saturday night of the Regatta. It is open only to its members and their guests and has a formal dresscode.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phyllis_Court">Phyllis Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henley_Royal_Regatta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Phyllis Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Phyllis_Court" title="Phyllis Court">Phyllis Court</a> Club is situated on the Buckinghamshire side of the river, opposite the finish line. It is open only to its members and their guests. Although it has an excellent view of the finish line, it is not generally frequented by rowers, being principally a lawn sports club.</p>

<p><b>Coburg</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈkoːbʊɐ̯k]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Town#Germany" title="Town">town</a> located on the <a href="/wiki/Itz_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Itz (river)">Itz</a> river in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Franconia" title="Upper Franconia">Upper Franconia</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, Germany. Long part of one of the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_states" title="Thuringian states">Thuringian states</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ernestine_duchies" title="Ernestine duchies">Wettin line</a>, it joined <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> by popular vote only in 1920. Until the revolution of 1918, it was one of the capitals of the <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Duchy of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld">Duchy of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld</a>. Through successful dynastic policies, the ruling princely family married into several of the royal families of Europe, most notably in the person of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Albert_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Prince Albert</a>, who married <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> in 1840. As a result of these close links with the royal houses of Europe in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Coburg was frequently visited by the crowned heads of Europe and their families.</p>
<p>Coburg is also known as the location of <i><a href="/wiki/Veste_Coburg" title="Veste Coburg">Veste Coburg</a></i>, one of Germany's largest castles. In 1530, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> lived there for six months during which he worked on translating the Bible into German.</p>
<p>Today, Coburg's population is close to 41,500. Since it was little damaged in World War II, Coburg retains many historic buildings, making it a popular tourist destination.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coburg lies about 90 kilometres (56 miles) south of <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> and about 100 kilometres (62 miles) north of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> on the river <a href="/wiki/Itz_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Itz (river)">Itz</a>. It is an <a href="/wiki/Urban_district" title="Urban district">urban district</a> and is surrounded by the <i><a href="/wiki/Coburg_(district)" title="Coburg (district)">Landkreis Coburg</a></i>. Coburg lies at the foot of the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Highland" title="Thuringian Highland">Thuringian Highland</a>. On three sides it is surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>. Until reunification in 1990 the border between Bavaria and Thuringia was also the <a href="/wiki/Inner_German_border" title="Inner German border">inner German border</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Coburg was first mentioned in a monastic document dated 1056, which marked the transfer of ownership to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop-Electorate_of_Cologne" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop-Electorate of Cologne">Archbishop-Elector of Cologne</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:16</sup> although there was a settlement at the site that predates it called <i>Trufalistat</i>. The origin of the name <i>Coburg</i> is unclear; the first element may be <i>kuh</i>, which would give a literal meaning of "cow borough".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Coburg" initially referred to a property centred on the hill where <a href="/wiki/Veste_Coburg" title="Veste Coburg">Veste Coburg</a> was later built. Its oldest remains date to the 12th or 13th century. In 1248, the castle came into possession of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Henneberg" title="House of Henneberg">House of Henneberg</a> and in 1353 it passed to the <a href="/wiki/Wettin_(dynasty)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wettin (dynasty)">House of Wettin</a><sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:16</sup> with the marriage of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III,_Landgrave_of_Thuringia" title="Frederick III, Landgrave of Thuringia">Frederick III</a> with <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Henneberg" title="Catherine of Henneberg">Catherine of Henneberg</a> and was initially regarded by them as a <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxon</a> outpost within <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Augsburg" title="Diet of Augsburg">Diet of Augsburg</a> in 1530 reformer <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> spent six months at the castle (located at the southernmost point of the Saxon duchy) while his <a href="/wiki/Lehnsherr" class="mw-redirect" title="Lehnsherr">liege lord</a>, <a href="/wiki/John,_Elector_of_Saxony" title="John, Elector of Saxony">John, Elector of Saxony</a>, attended the Diet. Luther was forbidden to attend by the Elector, who feared that he would be imprisoned and burned as a heretic. While quartered at the castle Luther continued with his translation of the Bible into German.</p>
<p>In 1547, the princely residence was moved from the Veste to a former monastery, rebuilt as a <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance palace</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ehrenburg_Palace" title="Ehrenburg Palace">Ehrenburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:16</sup></p>
<p>In 1596, Coburg was raised to the status of capital of one of the dynasty's splintered <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_states" title="Thuringian states">Saxon-Thuringian</a> territories, the newly created <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxe-Coburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Saxe-Coburg">Duchy of Saxe-Coburg</a> under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/John_Casimir,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg" title="John Casimir, Duke of Saxe-Coburg">Duke John Casimir</a> (ruled 1596–1633). From 1699 to 1826, it was one of the two capitals of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld">Duchy of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld</a>, and from 1826-1918 it was a capital of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxe-Coburg-Gotha" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Saxe-Coburg-Gotha">Duchy of Saxe-Coburg-Gotha</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ernest_Frederick,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld" title="Ernest Frederick, Duke of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld">Ernest Frederick</a>, the fourth Duke of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld, moved his capital from <a href="/wiki/Saalfeld" title="Saalfeld">Saalfeld</a> to Coburg in 1764. Coburg then became capital of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld">Duchy of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld</a> and later of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Duchy of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a>.</p>
<p>In the early 19th century, the town's medieval fortifications were demolished and replaced by parks. <a href="/wiki/Francis,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld" title="Francis, Duke of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld">The duke</a> also started the collection of copperplate engravings that is today part of the Veste Coburg museum. Under his son, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_I,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Ernest I, Duke of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Ernest</a>, the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schlossplatz_(Coburg)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schlossplatz (Coburg) (page does not exist)">Schlossplatz</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schlossplatz_(Coburg)" class="extiw" title="de:Schlossplatz (Coburg)">de</a>)</span></i> with what is today the <i><a href="/wiki/Landestheater_Coburg" title="Landestheater Coburg">Landestheater Coburg</a></i> was created. He also rebuilt the Ehrenburg in <a href="/wiki/Gothic_revival_architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic revival architecture">Gothic revival style</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:17</sup></p>
<p>In the mid-19th century, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_II,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Ernest II, Duke of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Duke Ernest II</a> supported national and liberal ideas and Coburg hosted the first meeting of the <a href="/wiki/German_National_Association" title="German National Association">German National Association</a>, the founding of the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutscher_S%C3%A4ngerbund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deutscher Sängerbund (page does not exist)">Deutscher Sängerbund</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deutscher_S%C3%A4ngerbund" class="extiw" title="de:Deutscher Sängerbund">de</a>)</span></i> and the first <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutsches_Turnfest&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deutsches Turnfest (page does not exist)">Deutsches Turnfest</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deutsches_Turnfest" class="extiw" title="de:Deutsches Turnfest">de</a>)</span></i> (national sports festival).<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:17</sup></p>
<p>During the 19th century, dynastic marriages created ties with the royal families of Belgium, Bulgaria, Portugal and Britain. This turned the ducal family from the rulers of a fairly obscure backwater duchy into one playing an influential role in European politics. The era of political influence peaked with <a href="/wiki/Leopold_I_of_Belgium" title="Leopold I of Belgium">Leopold Frederick</a>; born Prince of <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld" title="Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld">Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld</a>, becoming the King of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Belgium" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Belgium">Belgium</a> in 1831 and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Albert_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a>, born in <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Rosenau,_Coburg" title="Schloss Rosenau, Coburg">Schloss Rosenau</a>, marrying his first cousin, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria</a> in 1840. The marriage between Albert and Victoria established the present British royal house, which renamed itself <a href="/wiki/House_of_Windsor" title="House of Windsor">Windsor</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. This marriage in turn led to a union with Germany's ruling dynasty, the <a href="/wiki/Hohenzollerns" class="mw-redirect" title="Hohenzollerns">Hohenzollerns</a>, when the couple's eldest child, <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Princess_Royal_and_Empress_Frederick" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria, Princess Royal and Empress Frederick">Victoria</a>, married the future Kaiser <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_III,_German_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Friedrich III, German Emperor">Friedrich III</a>.</p>
<p>After her marriage, Queen Victoria said of Coburg:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>If I were not who I am, this would have been my real home, but I shall always consider it my second one.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Due to the royal connections among the royal houses of Europe, Coburg was the site of many royal Ducal weddings and visits. Britain's Queen Victoria made six visits to Coburg during her 63-year reign. In 1894 the wedding of <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Louis,_Grand_Duke_of_Hesse" title="Ernest Louis, Grand Duke of Hesse">Ernest Louis, Grand Duke of Hesse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princess_Victoria_Melita_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Princess Victoria Melita of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Princess Victoria Melita of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a> brought together Queen Victoria, her son <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VII of the United Kingdom">Edward (future Edward VII)</a>, her second son <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Alfred, Duke of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Alfred (Duke of Saxe-Coburg-Gotha)</a>, her daughter the German dowager Empress Friedrich (Victoria), and many of her grandchildren, such as future <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Tsar Nicholas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandra_Feodorovna_(Alix_of_Hesse)" title="Alexandra Feodorovna (Alix of Hesse)">Alexandra of Russia</a> (Alix of Hesse), Kaiser <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_II,_German_Emperor" title="Wilhelm II, German Emperor">Wilhelm II</a> of Germany, and the future <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">King George V</a> of the United Kingdom.</p>

<p>In November 1918, the last Duke of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Edward,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Charles Edward, Duke of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Charles Edward</a>, abdicated. The <i><a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Coburg" title="Free State of Coburg">Freistaat Coburg</a></i> which now came into being had to decide whether to become part of Thuringia or Bavaria. In a November 1919 referendum, the locals voted to join <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> with an 88% majority. On 1 July 1920, Coburg joined Bavaria.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:17</sup></p>
<p>In 1929, Coburg was the first German town in which the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> won the absolute majority of the popular vote during municipal elections.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1932, Coburg was the first German town to make <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> an honorary citizen.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeit_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeit-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, which Coburg survived largely undamaged, the town faced the challenge of integrating over 15,000 <a href="/wiki/Flight_and_expulsion_of_Germans_(1944%E2%80%9350)" title="Flight and expulsion of Germans (1944–50)">refugees</a>. In addition, whilst the other Saxon-Thuringian principalities were incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">German Democratic Republic</a>, Bavarian Coburg became part of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>. As a result, the town spent the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> years lying right next to the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a>, surrounded by East German territory on three sides and cut off from much of its natural back country.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:17</sup></p>
<p>In 1946, Polish ambassador <a href="/wiki/Oskar_R._Lange" title="Oskar R. Lange">Oskar R. Lange</a> alleged that Coburg was a base for the <a href="/wiki/Western_Bloc" title="Western Bloc">Western Allies</a> to organize a Polish armed insurgency led by <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Anders" title="Władysław Anders">Władysław Anders</a> against the Soviet-backed communists in <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Poland" class="mw-redirect" title="People's Republic of Poland">Poland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over two thirds of Coburg's population live in the core town of Coburg rather than in one of the <i>Stadtteile</i> merged with it in the 20th century. Some of those retain a largely rural character.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most residents of Coburg are members of the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Germany">Evangelical Church</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a>). Other Christian communities are <a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">Baptists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventists</a>, the ICF Movement, <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>, Catholics, <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Catholics">Old Catholics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Apostolic_Church" title="New Apostolic Church">New Apostolic Church</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>. There are also three communities of <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a>. Coburg had a large Jewish community until the 1940s. Jews had lived there since the 14th century. The old synagogue was a former church. Today it is used by <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Catholics">Old Catholics</a>. Coburg became Protestant after the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>. All Catholics were persecuted. A new Catholic community was founded in the 19th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1919 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Brose&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Max Brose (page does not exist)">Max Brose</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Brose" class="extiw" title="de:Max Brose">de</a>)</span> and Ernst Jüngling established the metal works <i>Max Brose &amp; Co.</i> to manufacture car parts. The company is still in operation today, as <i><a href="/wiki/Brose_Fahrzeugteile" title="Brose Fahrzeugteile">Brose Fahrzeugteile</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1950, the <i>Haftpflicht-Unterstützungs-Kasse kraftfahrender Beamter Deutschlands a. G.</i> (today <a href="/w/index.php?title=HUK-Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="HUK-Coburg (page does not exist)">HUK-Coburg</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/HUK-Coburg" class="extiw" title="de:HUK-Coburg">de</a>)</span>) relocated from <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> to Coburg. HUK is today the largest employer and largest payer of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gewerbesteuer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gewerbesteuer (page does not exist)">Gewerbesteuer</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gewerbesteuer_(Deutschland)" class="extiw" title="de:Gewerbesteuer (Deutschland)">de</a>)</span></i> (local corporate tax) in Coburg.<sup id="cite_ref-HUK_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HUK-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-InFR_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-InFR-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Kapp_Werkzeugmaschinen" title="Kapp Werkzeugmaschinen">Kapp Werkzeugmaschinen</a></i> has been a manufacturer of machines since 1953, after taking over the production assets of COMAG (Coburger Maschinenbau GmbH).</p>
<p>Coburg has an above-average share of goods-producing employees. In 2013, out of 32,962 employees 10,421 worked in the manufacturing or construction sectors (31% vs. a national average of 24%), 4,853 in trade, transport and tourism, 10,381 in professional services and 7,230 in public and private services.<sup id="cite_ref-Stat_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:9</sup></p>
<p>Hotels in Coburg counted over 61,000 overnight visitors in 2014 (of which around 53,000 were from Germany). They stayed for a total of almost 120,000 nights, or close to two nights on average.<sup id="cite_ref-Stat_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:15</sup></p>
<p>In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a> per inhabitant was €72,219 in Coburg, placing it 6th among the 96 urban and rural districts (Bavarian average: €39,691).<sup id="cite_ref-Stat2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ehrenburg_Palace" title="Ehrenburg Palace">Ehrenburg</a>, a former Franciscan convent built in 1220 and turned into a palace in 1543–1549. It was repeatedly renovated until the 19th century. Ehrenburg was gutted by fire in 1690 and rebuilt in a Baroque style, with stucco work by North Italian craftsmen that includes a "Hall of the Giants" (which contains a plaque that states it was the location of the first meeting between Britain's <a href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_I_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Franz Josef I of Austria">Franz Josef</a> Emperor of Austria in 1860). The internal decoration dates from the late 17th to early 18th centuries. Its <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic Revival">Gothic Revival</a> exterior was remodelled by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Schinkel" title="Karl Friedrich Schinkel">Karl Friedrich Schinkel</a> in the 19th century. It now also houses a museum as well as a library.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Morizkirche_(Coburg)" title="Morizkirche (Coburg)">Stadtkirche St. Moriz</a></i> ("St. Maurice", 14th-16th centuries), is a <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> edifice on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hallenkirche" class="mw-redirect" title="Hallenkirche">Hallenkirche</a></i> plan with two towers. The interior, remade in the 18th century, include the notable funerary moment of <a href="/wiki/John_Casimir,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg" title="John Casimir, Duke of Saxe-Coburg">Duke Johann Casimir</a> for his parents, a 13&#160;m-tall alabaster sculpture painted with statue and reliefs (1595–1598).</li>
<li>The medieval <a href="/wiki/Veste_Coburg" title="Veste Coburg">Veste Coburg</a>, one of the biggest castles in Germany, built starting in 1225 (upon the site of an 11th-century chapel). It was mostly rebuilt in the 19th century. It has a triple line of walls with numerous towers. Today the Veste Coburg is home to three museums. One is the <i>Fürstenbau</i> (ducal palace), with many furnished rooms of the Dukes of Coburg, including the apartment where Martin Luther lived in 1530. Probably the most notable room in the castle (unique in all of Germany) is the <i>Jagdzimmer</i> (hunting room) of 1632, which is entirely made of marquetry wood inlay, done up with over 60 marquetry panels, deeply coffered marquetry ceilings and a wood paneled floor. Another museum is the <i>Rüstkammer</i> (armory), containing the largest collection of medieval armour and weaponry in Germany, with over 10,500 items. The third is the <i>Kunstsammlungen</i>, which contains a collection of 300,000 copperplate engravings (<i>Kupferstich-Kabinett</i>), a 20,000 piece coin collection (<i>Münzkabinett</i>), a 7,000 piece documentation collection (<i>Briefe &amp; Urkunden</i>), and a 3,500 piece glassware collection (<i>Gläser-Sammlung</i>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Casimirianum_Coburg" title="Casimirianum Coburg">Gymnasium Casimirianum</a>, a Renaissance building begun in 1601</li>
<li>Arsenal (1616–21).</li>

<li><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburger_Rathaus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Coburger Rathaus (page does not exist)">Coburger Rathaus</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coburger_Rathaus" class="extiw" title="de:Coburger Rathaus">de</a>)</span></i>, the town hall, part of the ensemble of structure on the market <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marktplatz_(Coburg)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marktplatz (Coburg) (page does not exist)">market square</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marktplatz_(Coburg)" class="extiw" title="de:Marktplatz (Coburg)">de</a>)</span> that also includes the <i>Stadthaus</i> (see below), the former <i>Beyersches Haus</i>, the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hof-Apotheke_(Coburg)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hof-Apotheke (Coburg) (page does not exist)">Hof-Apotheke</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hof-Apotheke_(Coburg)" class="extiw" title="de:Hof-Apotheke (Coburg)">de</a>)</span></i>, the <i>Stadtbrunnen</i> (fountain) and the central statue of Prince Albert. The seat of the public administration of Coburg was moved here in 1438. The original Gothic building proved to be too small and after 1570 the town bought additional properties and erected a Renaissance building at the corner with Ketschengasse. In another rebuilding in 1750-2 both structures were merged. The appearance of the town hall changed, only the round bay on the corner remained. The old roofs were replaced by the current garret roof and the colourful paintings were added to the façade in the 18th century. Another renovation took place in 1903, when the balcony to the market square was added and stairwell and entrance were redesigned.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:57–8</sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stadthaus_(Coburg)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stadthaus (Coburg) (page does not exist)">Stadthaus</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadthaus_(Coburg)" class="extiw" title="de:Stadthaus (Coburg)">de</a>)</span></i>, a late Renaissance building from 1597-9 built under Duke Johann Casimir to house the ducal administration. It occupies the complete northern side of the market square.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:66–7</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rose_Garden,_Coburg" title="Rose Garden, Coburg">Rose Garden</a>, park.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Callenberg_Castle" title="Callenberg Castle">Callenberg Castle</a>, with Saxe-Coburg family art collection and National Shooting Museum.</li>
<li><i>Schloss Rosenau</i> near Coburg.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/St._Augustin,_Coburg" title="St. Augustin, Coburg">St. Augustin</a>, Catholic parish church opened in 1860.</li>
<li>The Baroque <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_the_Vierzehnheiligen" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica of the Vierzehnheiligen">Basilica of the Vierzehnheiligen</a>, 20 kilometres (12 miles) outside the town.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_culture">Arts and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Arts and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Coburg is home to two major festivals: <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samba-Festival_Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Samba-Festival Coburg (page does not exist)">Samba-Festival Coburg</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samba-Festival_Coburg" class="extiw" title="de:Samba-Festival Coburg">de</a>)</span></i> and <i>Johann Strauss Musiktage</i>. Coburg is referred to as "Europe's Capital of Samba."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a result of the large presence of the US Army prior to German re-unification, Americans and American culture are still present in Coburg and the surrounding area. This influence ranges from American-style pubs and restaurants to two sports clubs sponsoring<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> baseball teams.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trivia">Trivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Trivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Hot_Dog_and_Sausage_Council" title="National Hot Dog and Sausage Council">National Hot Dog and Sausage Council</a> asserts that <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> is traditionally credited with originating the <a href="/wiki/Hot_dog" title="Hot dog">frankfurter</a>. According to the Council, this claim is disputed and that the hot dog was created in the late 17th century by Johann Georghehner, a butcher, living in Coburg.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coburg was the first German town to elect a <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> Mayor.</p>
<p>A popular local delicacy is the Coburger <a href="/wiki/Bratwurst" title="Bratwurst">Bratwurst</a>, a sausage (the official measure of which is denoted by the Marshall's staff held by the statue of the town's patron, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Maurice" title="Saint Maurice">Sankt Mauritius</a>, located on the town hall and overlooking the square) roasted over a pine cone fire.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:58</sup> The sausage is served in a Semmel (a small bread bun, a third the size of the sausage itself), and is highly popular with locals and tourists alike.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coburg_Peak" title="Coburg Peak">Coburg Peak</a> on <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Peninsula" title="Trinity Peninsula">Trinity Peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/Graham_Land" title="Graham Land">Graham Land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> is named after the town, in connection with the Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/Royal_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal house">royal house</a> of Coburg (<a href="/wiki/Royal_House_of_Bulgaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal House of Bulgaria">Saxe-Coburg-Gotha</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Car">Car</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Car">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Coburg can be reached by car via B 303 <a href="/wiki/Schweinfurt" title="Schweinfurt">Schweinfurt</a>-Coburg-Schirnding, B 4 <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>-Coburg-<a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_73" title="Bundesautobahn 73">motorway A 73</a> <a href="/wiki/Suhl" title="Suhl">Suhl</a>-Coburg-Nuremberg.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Railway_stations_in_Coburg" title="Railway stations in Coburg">Railway stations in Coburg</a></div>
<p>Coburg has four train stations:</p>
<ul>
<li>Coburg-Neuses</li>
<li>Coburg-Nord</li>
<li>Coburg main station</li>
<li>Coburg-Creidlitz</li>
</ul>
<p>From the main station one can go to <a href="/wiki/Lichtenfels,_Bavaria" title="Lichtenfels, Bavaria">Lichtenfels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forchheim_(Oberfranken)" class="mw-redirect" title="Forchheim (Oberfranken)">Forchheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erlangen" title="Erlangen">Erlangen</a>, <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth" title="Fürth">Fürth</a> and Nuremberg, to <a href="/wiki/Neustadt_bei_Coburg" title="Neustadt bei Coburg">Neustadt bei Coburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonneberg" title="Sonneberg">Sonneberg</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Bad_Rodach" title="Bad Rodach">Bad Rodach</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Kulmbach" title="Kulmbach">Kulmbach</a>, Neuenmarkt-Wirsberg.</p>
<p>In the future, some <a href="/wiki/Intercity_Express" class="mw-redirect" title="Intercity Express">Intercity Express</a> high speed trains will stop in Coburg on the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Erfurt_high-speed_railway" title="Nuremberg–Erfurt high-speed railway">Nuremberg–Erfurt high-speed railway</a>, part of a new Berlin-<a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a>-Coburg-Nuremberg route.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Airports">Airports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Airports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Small planes can land on the two <a href="/wiki/Airfields" class="mw-redirect" title="Airfields">airfields</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airport_Coburg_Brandensteinsebene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Airport Coburg Brandensteinsebene (page does not exist)">Coburg Brandensteinsebene</a> (<a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">ICAO</a>-Code: EDQC, founded in 1912)</li>
<li>Coburg Steinrücken (ICAO-Code: EDQY)</li>
</ul>
<p>Large airports nearby are in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erfurt_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Erfurt Airport">Erfurt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Airport" title="Nuremberg Airport">Nuremberg</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_public_transport_system">Local public transport system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Local public transport system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The public transport system in Coburg is operated by SÜC (<i>Stadt- und Überlandwerke Coburg</i>) with 22 bus lines. The OVF (<i>Omnibus Verkehr Franken</i>) covers Coburg's surrounding countryside with an additional 11 bus lines.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Besides various royalty, other famous individuals associated with Coburg include <a href="/wiki/Hans_Berger" title="Hans Berger">Hans Berger</a> (graduated), <a href="/wiki/William_Frishmuth" title="William Frishmuth">William Frishmuth</a> (born), and <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Study" title="Eduard Study">Eduard Study</a> (born).</p>
<ul>
<li>In 1887, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Strauss_II" title="Johann Strauss II">Johann Strauss</a>, also known as the Waltz King, left Vienna when the Roman Catholic Church forbade his divorce from his second wife. So he moved to Lutheran Saxe-Coburg-Gotha with his future third wife Adele, where he lived the last 13 years of his life in Coburg. He was, however, buried in Vienna.</li>
<li>In 1922, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> led several hundred <a href="/wiki/Sturmabteilung" title="Sturmabteilung">stormtroopers</a> in a march through the city, fighting pitched street battles with communists. During the Nazi era, the <a href="/wiki/Coburg_Badge" title="Coburg Badge">Coburg Badge</a> (made to honor the participants) was one of the most prestigious party medals.</li>
</ul>

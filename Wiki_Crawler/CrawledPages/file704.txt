			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Raphaelite&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-Raphaelite">Pre-Raphaelite</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dante_Gabriel_Rossetti_-_Proserpine_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Proserpine_(Rossetti_painting)" title="Proserpine (Rossetti painting)">Proserpine</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Dante_Gabriel_Rossetti" title="Dante Gabriel Rossetti">Dante Gabriel Rossetti</a></div>
</div>

<p>The <b>Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood</b> (later known as the <b>Pre-Raphaelites</b>) was a group of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poets" class="mw-redirect" title="Poets">poets</a>, and critics, founded in 1848 by <a href="/wiki/William_Holman_Hunt" title="William Holman Hunt">William Holman Hunt</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Everett_Millais" title="John Everett Millais">John Everett Millais</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dante_Gabriel_Rossetti" title="Dante Gabriel Rossetti">Dante Gabriel Rossetti</a>. The three founders were joined by <a href="/wiki/William_Michael_Rossetti" title="William Michael Rossetti">William Michael Rossetti</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Collinson" title="James Collinson">James Collinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frederic_George_Stephens" title="Frederic George Stephens">Frederic George Stephens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Woolner" title="Thomas Woolner">Thomas Woolner</a> to form the seven-member "brotherhood". Their principles were shared by other artists, including <a href="/wiki/Marie_Spartali_Stillman" title="Marie Spartali Stillman">Marie Spartali Stillman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Madox_Brown" title="Ford Madox Brown">Ford Madox Brown</a>.</p>
<p>A later, medievalising strain inspired by Rossetti included <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burne-Jones" title="Edward Burne-Jones">Edward Burne-Jones</a> and extended into the twentieth century with artists such as <a href="/wiki/John_William_Waterhouse" title="John William Waterhouse">John William Waterhouse</a>.</p>
<p>The group's intention was to reform art by rejecting what it considered the mechanistic approach first adopted by <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">Mannerist</a> artists who succeeded <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>. Its members believed the <a href="/wiki/Classicism" title="Classicism">Classical</a> poses and elegant compositions of Raphael in particular had been a corrupting influence on the <a href="/wiki/Academic_art" title="Academic art">academic</a> teaching of art, hence the name "Pre-Raphaelite". In particular, the group objected to the influence of <a href="/wiki/Sir_Joshua_Reynolds" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Joshua Reynolds">Sir Joshua Reynolds</a>, founder of the English <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy of Arts</a>, whom they called "Sir Sloshua". To the Pre-Raphaelites, according to William Michael Rossetti, "sloshy" meant "anything lax or scamped in the process of painting ... and hence ... any thing or person of a commonplace or conventional kind".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The brotherhood sought a return to the abundant detail, intense colours and complex compositions of <a href="/wiki/Quattrocento" title="Quattrocento">Quattrocento</a> Italian art. The group associated their work with <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> an English critic whose influences were driven by his religious background.</p>
<p>The group continued to accept the concepts of <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history painting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mimesis" title="Mimesis">mimesis</a>, imitation of nature, as central to the purpose of art. The Pre-Raphaelites defined themselves as a reform movement, created a distinct name for their form of art, and published a periodical, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Germ_(periodical)" title="The Germ (periodical)">The Germ</a></i>, to promote their ideas. The group's debates were recorded in the <i>Pre-Raphaelite Journal</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Germ.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Germ.jpg/220px-Germ.jpg" width="220" height="422" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/67/Germ.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="226" data-file-height="433" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Germ.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Illustration by Holman Hunt of Thomas Woolner's poem "My Beautiful Lady", published in <i>The Germ</i>, 1850</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood was founded in John Millais's parents' house on <a href="/wiki/Gower_Street_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gower Street (London)">Gower Street</a>, London in 1848. At the first meeting, the painters <a href="/wiki/John_Everett_Millais" title="John Everett Millais">John Everett Millais</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dante_Gabriel_Rossetti" title="Dante Gabriel Rossetti">Dante Gabriel Rossetti</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Holman_Hunt" title="William Holman Hunt">William Holman Hunt</a> were present. Hunt and Millais were students at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy of Arts</a> and had met in another loose association, the Cyclographic Club, a sketching society. At his own request Rossetti became a pupil of <a href="/wiki/Ford_Madox_Brown" title="Ford Madox Brown">Ford Madox Brown</a> in 1848.<sup id="cite_ref-McGann_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGann-3">[3]</a></sup> At that date, Rossetti and Hunt shared lodgings in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Street,_London" title="Cleveland Street, London">Cleveland Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fitzrovia" title="Fitzrovia">Fitzrovia</a>, Central London. Hunt had started painting <i>The Eve of St. Agnes</i> based on <a href="/wiki/The_Eve_of_St._Agnes" title="The Eve of St. Agnes">Keats's poem of the same name</a>, but it was not completed until 1867.<sup id="cite_ref-hilton28_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hilton28-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an aspiring poet, Rossetti wished to develop the links between <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic</a> poetry and art. By autumn, four more members, painters <a href="/wiki/James_Collinson" title="James Collinson">James Collinson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederic_George_Stephens" title="Frederic George Stephens">Frederic George Stephens</a>, Rossetti's brother, poet and critic <a href="/wiki/William_Michael_Rossetti" title="William Michael Rossetti">William Michael Rossetti</a>, and sculptor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Woolner" title="Thomas Woolner">Thomas Woolner</a>, had joined to form a seven-member-strong brotherhood.<sup id="cite_ref-hilton28_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hilton28-4">[4]</a></sup> Ford Madox Brown was invited to join, but the more senior artist remained independent but supported the group throughout the PRB period of Pre-Raphaelitism and contributed to <i>The Germ</i>. Other young painters and sculptors became close associates, including <a href="/wiki/Charles_Allston_Collins" title="Charles Allston Collins">Charles Allston Collins</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Tupper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thomas Tupper (page does not exist)">Thomas Tupper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Munro_(sculptor)" title="Alexander Munro (sculptor)">Alexander Munro</a>. The PRB intended to keep the existence of the brotherhood secret from members of the Royal Academy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_doctrines">Early doctrines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early doctrines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The brotherhood's early doctrines, as defined by William Michael Rossetti, were expressed in four declarations:</p>
<blockquote>
<ol>
<li>to have genuine ideas to express;</li>
<li>to study Nature attentively, so as to know how to express them;</li>
<li>to sympathise with what is direct and serious and heartfelt in previous art, to the exclusion of what is conventional and self-parading and learned by rote; and</li>
<li>most indispensable of all, to produce thoroughly good pictures and statues.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
</blockquote>
<p>The principles were deliberately non-dogmatic, since the brotherhood wished to emphasise the personal responsibility of individual artists to determine their own ideas and methods of depiction. Influenced by <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>, the members thought freedom and responsibility were inseparable. Nevertheless, they were particularly fascinated by <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> culture, believing it to possess a <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spiritual</a> and creative integrity that had been lost in later eras. The emphasis on medieval culture clashed with principles of <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">realism</a> which stress the independent observation of nature. In its early stages, the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood believed its two interests were consistent with one another, but in later years the movement divided and moved in two directions. The realists were led by Hunt and Millais, while the medievalists were led by Rossetti and his followers, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burne-Jones" title="Edward Burne-Jones">Edward Burne-Jones</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a>. The split was never absolute, since both factions believed that art was essentially spiritual in character, opposing their <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealism</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialist</a> realism associated with <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Courbet" title="Gustave Courbet">Courbet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a>.</p>
<p>The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood was greatly influenced by nature and its members used great detail to show the natural world using bright and sharp focus techniques on a white canvas. In attempts to revive the brilliance of colour found in Quattrocento art, Hunt and Millais developed a technique of painting in thin <a href="/wiki/Glaze_(painting_technique)" title="Glaze (painting technique)">glazes</a> of pigment over a wet white ground in the hope that the colours would retain jewel-like transparency and clarity. Their emphasis on brilliance of colour was a reaction to the excessive use of <a href="/wiki/Bitumen" class="mw-redirect" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a> by earlier British artists, such as Reynolds, <a href="/wiki/David_Wilkie_(artist)" title="David Wilkie (artist)">David Wilkie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Robert_Haydon" class="mw-redirect" title="Benjamin Robert Haydon">Benjamin Robert Haydon</a>. Bitumen produces unstable areas of muddy darkness, an effect the Pre-Raphaelites despised.</p>
<p>In 1848, Rossetti and Hunt made a list of "Immortals", artistic heroes whom they admired, especially from literature, some of whose work would form subjects for PRB paintings, notably including <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">Keats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">Tennyson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_exhibitions_and_publications">First exhibitions and publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First exhibitions and publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first exhibitions of Pre-Raphaelite work occurred in 1849. Both Millais's <i><a href="/wiki/Isabella_(Millais_painting)" title="Isabella (Millais painting)">Isabella</a></i> (1848–1849) and Holman Hunt's <i><a href="/wiki/Rienzi_vowing_to_obtain_justice_for_the_death_of_his_young_brother,_slain_in_a_skirmish_between_the_Colonna_and_the_Orsini_factions" title="Rienzi vowing to obtain justice for the death of his young brother, slain in a skirmish between the Colonna and the Orsini factions">Rienzi</a></i> (1848–1849) were exhibited at the Royal Academy. Rossetti's <i><a href="/wiki/File:Dante_Gabriel_Rossetti_004.jpg" title="File:Dante Gabriel Rossetti 004.jpg">Girlhood of Mary Virgin</a></i> was shown at a Free Exhibition on Hyde Park Corner. As agreed, all members of the brotherhood signed their work with their name and the initials "PRB". Between January and April 1850, the group published a literary magazine, <i>The Germ</i> edited by William Rossetti which published poetry by the Rossettis, Woolner, and Collinson and essays on art and literature by associates of the brotherhood, such as <a href="/wiki/Coventry_Patmore" title="Coventry Patmore">Coventry Patmore</a>. As the short run-time implies, the magazine did not manage to achieve sustained momentum. (Daly 1989)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_controversy">Public controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Public controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Everett_Millais_-_Christ_in_the_House_of_His_Parents_(%60The_Carpenter%27s_Shop%27)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/John_Everett_Millais_-_Christ_in_the_House_of_His_Parents_%28%60The_Carpenter%27s_Shop%27%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/320px-John_Everett_Millais_-_Christ_in_the_House_of_His_Parents_%28%60The_Carpenter%27s_Shop%27%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="320" height="194" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/John_Everett_Millais_-_Christ_in_the_House_of_His_Parents_%28%60The_Carpenter%27s_Shop%27%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/480px-John_Everett_Millais_-_Christ_in_the_House_of_His_Parents_%28%60The_Carpenter%27s_Shop%27%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/John_Everett_Millais_-_Christ_in_the_House_of_His_Parents_%28%60The_Carpenter%27s_Shop%27%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/640px-John_Everett_Millais_-_Christ_in_the_House_of_His_Parents_%28%60The_Carpenter%27s_Shop%27%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4500" data-file-height="2731" /></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>In 1850, the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood became the subject of controversy after the exhibition of Millais' painting <i><a href="/wiki/Christ_in_the_House_of_His_Parents" title="Christ in the House of His Parents">Christ in the House of His Parents</a></i> was considered to be <a href="/wiki/Blasphemy" title="Blasphemy">blasphemous</a> by many reviewers, notably <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Dickens considered Millais' Mary to be ugly.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Millais had used his sister-in-law, Mary Hodgkinson, as the model for Mary in his painting. The brotherhood's medievalism was attacked as backward-looking and its extreme devotion to detail was condemned as ugly and jarring to the eye.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> According to Dickens, Millais made the Holy Family look like alcoholics and slum-dwellers, adopting contorted and absurd "medieval" poses. A rival group of older artists, <a href="/wiki/The_Clique" title="The Clique">The Clique</a>, used its influence against the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood and its principles were publicly attacked by the President of the Academy, Sir <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lock_Eastlake" title="Charles Lock Eastlake">Charles Lock Eastlake</a>.</p>
<p>After the controversy, Collinson left the brotherhood and the remaining members met to discuss whether he should be replaced by Charles Allston Collins or <a href="/wiki/Walter_Howell_Deverell" class="mw-redirect" title="Walter Howell Deverell">Walter Howell Deverell</a>, but were unable to make a decision. From that point the group disbanded, though its influence continued. Artists who had worked in the style initially continued but no longer signed works "PRB".</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Everett_Millais_-_Ophelia_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/John_Everett_Millais_-_Ophelia_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-John_Everett_Millais_-_Ophelia_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/John_Everett_Millais_-_Ophelia_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-John_Everett_Millais_-_Ophelia_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/John_Everett_Millais_-_Ophelia_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-John_Everett_Millais_-_Ophelia_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7087" data-file-height="4820" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<i><a href="/wiki/Ophelia_(painting)" title="Ophelia (painting)">Ophelia</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/John_Everett_Millais" title="John Everett Millais">John Everett Millais</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The brotherhood found support from the critic <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>, who praised its devotion to nature and rejection of conventional methods of composition. The Pre-Raphaelites were influenced by Ruskin's theories. He wrote to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> defending their work and subsequently met them. Initially, he favoured Millais, who travelled to Scotland in the summer of 1853 with Ruskin and Ruskin's wife, Euphemia Chalmers Ruskin, née Gray (now best known as <a href="/wiki/Effie_Gray" title="Effie Gray">Effie Gray</a>). The main object of the journey was to paint Ruskin's portrait.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Effie became increasingly attached to Millais,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> creating a crisis. In subsequent annulment proceedings, Ruskin himself made a statement to his lawyer to the effect that his marriage had been unconsummated. <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The marriage was annulled on grounds of non-<a href="/wiki/Consummation" title="Consummation">consummation</a>, leaving Effie free to marry Millais,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> but causing a public scandal. Millais began to move away from the Pre-Raphaelite style after his marriage, and Ruskin ultimately attacked his later works. Ruskin continued to support Hunt and Rossetti and provided funds to encourage the art of Rossetti's wife <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Siddal" title="Elizabeth Siddal">Elizabeth Siddal</a>.</p>
<p>By 1853 the original PRB had virtually dissolved,<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-14">[14]</a></sup> with only Holman Hunt remaining true to its stated aims. But the term "Pre-Raphaelite" stuck to Rossetti and others, including <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burne-Jones" title="Edward Burne-Jones">Edward Burne-Jones</a>, with whom he became involved in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> in 1857.<sup id="cite_ref-Whiteley_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whiteley-15">[15]</a></sup> Hence the term Pre-Raphaelite is associated with the much wider and long-lived art movement, including the dreamy, yearning images of women produced by Rossetti and several of his followers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_developments_and_influence">Later developments and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later developments and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Morgan_Medea.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/De_Morgan_Medea.jpg/170px-De_Morgan_Medea.jpg" width="170" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/De_Morgan_Medea.jpg/255px-De_Morgan_Medea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/De_Morgan_Medea.jpg/340px-De_Morgan_Medea.jpg 2x" data-file-width="467" data-file-height="800" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Morgan_Medea.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="/wiki/Medea" title="Medea">Medea</a></i> by Evelyn De Morgan, 1889, in <a href="/wiki/Quattrocento" title="Quattrocento">quattrocento</a> style</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Artists influenced by the brotherhood include <a href="/wiki/John_Brett_(artist)" title="John Brett (artist)">John Brett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Calderon" class="mw-redirect" title="Philip Calderon">Philip Calderon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hughes_(artist)" title="Arthur Hughes (artist)">Arthur Hughes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Moreau" title="Gustave Moreau">Gustave Moreau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_De_Morgan" title="Evelyn De Morgan">Evelyn De Morgan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-hilton202_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hilton202-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Sandys" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederic Sandys">Frederic Sandys</a> (who came into the Pre-Raphaelite circle in 1857)<sup id="cite_ref-hilton202_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hilton202-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_William_Waterhouse" title="John William Waterhouse">John William Waterhouse</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ford_Madox_Brown" title="Ford Madox Brown">Ford Madox Brown</a>, who was associated with them from the beginning, is often seen as most closely adopting the Pre-Raphaelite principles. One follower who developed his own distinct style was <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_Beardsley" title="Aubrey Beardsley">Aubrey Beardsley</a>, who was pre-eminently influenced by Burne-Jones.<sup id="cite_ref-hilton202_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hilton202-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 1856, Dante Gabriel Rossetti became an inspiration for the medievalising strand of the movement. He was the link between the two types of Pre-Raphaelite painting (nature and Romance) after the PRB became lost in the later decades of the century. Rossetti, although the least committed to the brotherhood, continued the name and changed its style. He began painting versions of femme fatales using models like <a href="/wiki/Jane_Morris" title="Jane Morris">Jane Morris</a>, in paintings such as <i>Proserpine</i>, <i>The Blue Silk Dress</i>, and <i>La Pia de' Tolomei</i>. His work influenced his friend <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a>, in whose firm <a href="/wiki/Morris_%26_Co." title="Morris &amp; Co.">Morris, Marshall, Faulkner &amp; Co.</a> he became a partner, and with whose wife Jane he may have had an affair. Ford Madox Brown and Edward Burne-Jones also became partners in the firm. Through Morris's company, the ideals of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood influenced many interior designers and architects, arousing interest in <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> designs and other crafts leading to the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a> headed by William Morris. Holman Hunt was involved with the movement to reform design through the <a href="/wiki/Della_Robbia_Pottery" title="Della Robbia Pottery">Della Robbia Pottery</a> company.</p>
<p>After 1850, Hunt and Millais moved away from direct imitation of medieval art. They stressed the realist and scientific aspects of the movement, though Hunt continued to emphasise the spiritual significance of art, seeking to reconcile religion and science by making accurate observations and studies of locations in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> for his paintings on biblical subjects. In contrast, Millais abandoned Pre-Raphaelitism after 1860, adopting a much broader and looser style influenced by Reynolds. William Morris and others condemned his reversal of principles.</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">

James Archer, <i>The Death of King Arthur</i>, 1860</div>

<p>Pre-Raphaelitism had a significant impact in Scotland and on Scottish artists. The figure in Scottish art most associated with the Pre-Raphaelites was the Aberdeen-born <a href="/wiki/William_Dyce" title="William Dyce">William Dyce</a> (1806–64). Dyce befriended the young Pre-Raphaelites in London and introduced their work to Ruskin.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> His later work was Pre-Raphaelite in its spirituality, as can be seen in his <i>The Man of Sorrows</i> and <i>David in the Wilderness</i> (both 1860), which contain a Pre-Raphaelite attention to detail.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Noel_Paton" title="Joseph Noel Paton">Joseph Noel Paton</a> (1821-1901) studied at the Royal Academy schools in London, where he became a friend of Millais and he subsequently followed him into Pre-Raphaelitism, producing pictures that stressed detail and melodrama such as <i>The Bludie Tryst</i> (1855). His later paintings, like those of Millais, have been criticised for descending into popular sentimentality.<sup id="cite_ref-Macmillan_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macmillan-19">[19]</a></sup> Also influenced by Millais was <a href="/wiki/James_Archer_(artist)" title="James Archer (artist)">James Archer</a> (1823-1904) and whose work includes <i>Summertime, Gloucestershire</i> (1860)<sup id="cite_ref-Macmillan_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macmillan-19">[19]</a></sup> and who from 1861 began a series of <a href="/wiki/Arthurian" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthurian">Arthurian</a>-based paintings including <i>La Morte d'Arthur</i> and <i>Sir Lancelot and Queen Guinevere</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The movement influenced many later British artists into the 20th century. Rossetti came to be seen as a precursor of the wider European <a href="/wiki/Symbolism_(arts)" title="Symbolism (arts)">Symbolist</a> movement. In the late 20th century the <a href="/wiki/Brotherhood_of_Ruralists" title="Brotherhood of Ruralists">Brotherhood of Ruralists</a> based its aims on Pre-Raphaelitism, while the <a href="/wiki/Stuckism" title="Stuckism">Stuckists</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Group_(artists)" title="Birmingham Group (artists)">Birmingham Group</a> have also derived inspiration from it.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Museum_%26_Art_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Birmingham Museum &amp; Art Gallery">Birmingham Museum &amp; Art Gallery</a> has a world-renowned collection of works by Burne-Jones and the Pre-Raphaelites that, some claim, strongly influenced the young <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> who wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>, with influences taken from the same mythological scenes portrayed by the Pre-Raphaelites.</p>

<a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hughes_(artist)" title="Arthur Hughes (artist)">Arthur Hughes</a>, <i>Fair Rosamund</i>, 1854</div>

<p>In the 20th century artistic ideals changed and art moved away from representing reality. Since the Pre-Raphaelites were fixed on portraying things with near-photographic precision, though with a distinctive attention to detailed surface-patterns, their work was devalued by many painters and critics. After the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, British <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">Modernists</a> associated Pre-Raphaelite art with the repressive and backward times in which they grew up. In the 1960s there was a major revival of Pre-Raphaelitism. Exhibitions and catalogues of works, culminating in a 1984 exhibition in London's <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a>, re-established a canon of Pre-Raphaelite work.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Among many other exhibitions, there was another large show at <a href="/wiki/Tate_Britain" title="Tate Britain">Tate Britain</a> in 2012–13.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections">Collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Holman_Hunt_001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/William_Holman_Hunt_001.jpg/320px-William_Holman_Hunt_001.jpg" width="320" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/William_Holman_Hunt_001.jpg/480px-William_Holman_Hunt_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/William_Holman_Hunt_001.jpg/640px-William_Holman_Hunt_001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="830" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Holman_Hunt_001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/William_Holman_Hunt" title="William Holman Hunt">William Holman Hunt</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hireling_Shepherd" title="The Hireling Shepherd">The Hireling Shepherd</a></i>, 1851</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are major collections of Pre-Raphaelite work in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Museum_and_Art_Gallery" title="Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery">Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Art_Gallery" title="Manchester Art Gallery">Manchester Art Gallery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Lever_Art_Gallery" title="Lady Lever Art Gallery">Lady Lever Art Gallery</a>, and Liverpool's <a href="/wiki/Walker_Art_Gallery" title="Walker Art Gallery">Walker Art Gallery</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Art_Gallery_of_South_Australia" title="Art Gallery of South Australia">Art Gallery of South Australia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Art_Museum" title="Delaware Art Museum">Delaware Art Museum</a> have the most significant collections of Pre-Raphaelite art outside the United Kingdom. The <a href="/wiki/Museo_de_Arte_de_Ponce" title="Museo de Arte de Ponce">Museo de Arte de Ponce</a> In Puerto Rico has a notable collection of Pre-Raphaelite works, including Sir Edward Burne-Jones' "<a href="/wiki/The_Last_Sleep_of_Arthur_in_Avalon" title="The Last Sleep of Arthur in Avalon">The Last Sleep of Arthur in Avalon</a>", <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Lord_Leighton" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederic Lord Leighton">Frederic Lord Leighton</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Flaming_June" title="Flaming June">Flaming June</a>", and works by William Holman Hunt, John Everett Millais, Dante Gabriel Rossetti, and <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Sandys" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederic Sandys">Frederic Sandys</a>.</p>
<p>There is a set of Pre-Raphaelite murals in the Old Library at the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Union" title="Oxford Union">Oxford Union</a>, depicting scenes from the <a href="/wiki/Arthurian_legend" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthurian legend">Arthurian legends</a>, painted between 1857 and 1859 by a team Dante Gabriel Rossetti, William Morris and Edward Burne-Jones. The <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a> houses at <a href="/wiki/Wightwick_Manor" title="Wightwick Manor">Wightwick Manor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/Wallington_Hall" title="Wallington Hall">Wallington Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, both have significant and representative collections. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lloyd_Webber" title="Andrew Lloyd Webber">Andrew Lloyd Webber</a> is an avid collector of Pre-Raphaelite works and a selection of 300 items from his collection were shown at an exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a> in London in 2003.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kelmscott_Manor" title="Kelmscott Manor">Kelmscott Manor</a>, the country home of <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> from 1871 until his death in 1896, owned by the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Antiquaries_of_London" title="Society of Antiquaries of London">Society of Antiquaries of London</a>, is open to the public. The Manor is featured in Morris' work <a href="/wiki/News_from_Nowhere" title="News from Nowhere">News from Nowhere</a>. It also appears in the background of <a href="/wiki/Water_Willow_(Rossetti)" title="Water Willow (Rossetti)"><i>Water Willow</i></a>, a portrait of his wife, <a href="/wiki/Jane_Morris" title="Jane Morris">Jane Morris</a>, painted by <a href="/wiki/Dante_Gabriel_Rossetti" title="Dante Gabriel Rossetti">Dante Gabriel Rossetti</a> in 1871. There are exhibitions connected with Morris and Rossetti’s early experiments with photography.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portrayal_in_popular_culture">Portrayal in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Portrayal in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The story of the brotherhood, from its controversial first exhibition to being embraced by the art establishment, has been depicted in two <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television series. The first, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Love_School" title="The Love School">The Love School</a></i>, was broadcast in 1975; the second is the 2009 BBC television drama serial <i><a href="/wiki/Desperate_Romantics" title="Desperate Romantics">Desperate Romantics</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bowker" title="Peter Bowker">Peter Bowker</a>. Although much of the latter's material is derived from <a href="/wiki/Franny_Moyle" title="Franny Moyle">Franny Moyle</a>'s factual book <i>Desperate Romantics: The Private Lives of the Pre-Raphaelites</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the series occasionally departs from established facts in favour of dramatic licence and is prefaced by the disclaimer: "In the mid-19th century, a group of young men challenged the art establishment of the day. The pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood were inspired by the real world around them, yet took imaginative licence in their art. This story, based on their lives and loves, follows in that inventive spirit."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ken_Russell" title="Ken Russell">Ken Russell</a>'s television film <i><a href="/wiki/Dante%27s_Inferno_(1967_TV_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dante's Inferno (1967 TV film)">Dante's Inferno</a></i> (1967) contains brief scenes on some of the leading Pre-Raphaelites but mainly concentrates on the life of Rossetti, played by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Reed" title="Oliver Reed">Oliver Reed</a>.</p>

<p><b>Duisburg</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈdyːsbʊɐ̯k]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Duisburg.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f7/Duisburg.ogg" class="internal" title="Duisburg.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The world's biggest <a href="/wiki/Inland_port" title="Inland port">inland port</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> it is also close to <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf_Airport" title="Düsseldorf Airport">Düsseldorf Airport</a>.</p>
<p>In the early Middle Ages it was a royal court of the Franks, first mentioned in writing in 883.</p>
<p>Duisburg is a result of numerous incorporations of surrounding towns and smaller cities. It is the fifteenth-largest city in Germany and the fifth-largest city in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, with 486,855 residents at the end of 2013. It is the central city of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr" title="Rhine-Ruhr">Rhine-Ruhr</a> metropolitan area. The city is renowned for its <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel industry</a>. All <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a> in the Ruhr are now located in Duisburg. In 2000, 49% of all hot metal and 34.4% of all <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">pig iron</a> in Germany was produced here. It also has a large brewery, <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nig_Brauerei" class="mw-redirect" title="König Brauerei">König</a>. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Duisburg-Essen" title="University of Duisburg-Essen">University of Duisburg-Essen</a>, with 39,000 students, ranks among the ten largest German universities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Duisburg is in the Lowland Rhine area at the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_(river)" title="Ruhr (river)">Ruhr</a> and near the outskirts of the <a href="/wiki/Bergisches_Land" title="Bergisches Land">Bergisches Land</a>. The city spreads along both sides of these rivers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjacent_cities">Adjacent cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Adjacent cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a> border Duisburg (clockwise starting from north-east): <a href="/wiki/Oberhausen" title="Oberhausen">Oberhausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BClheim" title="Mülheim">Mülheim an der Ruhr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ratingen" title="Ratingen">Ratingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meerbusch" title="Meerbusch">Meerbusch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krefeld" title="Krefeld">Krefeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moers" title="Moers">Moers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rheinberg" title="Rheinberg">Rheinberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dinslaken" title="Dinslaken">Dinslaken</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Duisburg" title="Timeline of Duisburg">Timeline of Duisburg</a></div>
<p>The first syllable of the name of the city could go back to the Germanic word "dheus", which means something like "wet area" or "flood plain". Duisburg therefore could mean "fortified place in the floodplain". Another interpretation assumes that the name is derived from the Old German "duis" which means "hill". Duisburg could mean something like "castle on the hill". Thus, a place on a hill overlooking the Rhine, that could refer to the area of the present Town Hall. Duisburggau (Diuspurgau) was also the name of the medieval <a href="/wiki/Gau_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gau (country subdivision)">Gau (country subdivision)</a> on the Lower Rhine.</p>
<p>A legend recorded by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Aventinus" title="Johannes Aventinus">Johannes Aventinus</a> (fl. 1525) holds that Duisburg, (along with <a href="/wiki/Deutz,_Cologne" title="Deutz, Cologne">Deutz, Cologne</a>, Duisdorf in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Doesburg" title="Doesburg">Doesburg</a> in the Netherlands, all on the Rhine's right bank) was built by the namesake <a href="/wiki/Tuisto" title="Tuisto">Tuisto</a>, mythical progenitor of Germans, ca. 2395&#160;BC. There is nothing to establish any historical basis for such an early founding of Duisburg, which would have made it among the earliest cities in Europe.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_period">Roman period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Latest archaeological studies show that the present-day market-place was already in use in the first century. It has been the major central trading place of the city since the 5th century. The city itself was located at the "<a href="/wiki/Hellweg" title="Hellweg">Hellweg</a>", an important medieval <a href="/wiki/Trade_route" title="Trade route">trade route</a>, and at a <a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">ford</a> across the Rhine. The <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Romans</a> already guarded the ford.</p>
<ul>
<li>420: The <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> usurp the Roman settlement and recolonize the old part of the town.</li>
<li>883: The <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> conquer Duisburg and stay for the winter. First historic document mentioning Duisburg.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Due to the town's favorable geographic position a <a href="/wiki/Count_palatine" title="Count palatine">palatinate</a> was built and the town was soon granted the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Charter">royal charter</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">free city</a>. Duisburg became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>. Around 1000 the river Rhine moved westward from the city. This put an end to the city's development as a trading town and it soon grew into a quiet rural city.</p>
<p>The productions of cartographer <a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Mercator" title="Gerardus Mercator">Gerardus Mercator</a> and the foundation of a university in 1655 established the city's renown as "Educated Duisburg" ("Duisburgum Doctum").</p>

<li>1279: "city charter" granted by King <a href="/wiki/Lothair_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lothair III, Holy Roman Emperor">Lothar III</a></li>
<li>1290 Duisburg becomes part of the County (after 1417 Duchy) of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cleves" title="Duchy of Cleves">Cleves</a></li>
<li>1445 attack by Archbishop-Elector <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dietrich_II_von_Moers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dietrich II von Moers (page does not exist)">Dietrich II von Moers</a> (<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietrich_II._von_Moers" class="extiw" title="de:Dietrich II. von Moers">de</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Archbishop-Electorate_of_Cologne" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop-Electorate of Cologne">Cologne</a> was thwarted</li>
<li>1566 <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Corputius" title="Johannes Corputius">Johannes Corputius</a> completes his <a href="/wiki/City_map" title="City map">city map</a> of Duisburg.</li>
<li>1666 Duisburg within the Duchy of Cleves becomes a part of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Prussia" title="Brandenburg-Prussia">Brandenburg-Prussia</a></li>
</ul>

<p>The rise of tobacco and textile industries in the 18th century made Duisburg an industrial center. Big industrial companies such as iron and steel producing firms (<a href="/wiki/Thyssen_AG" title="Thyssen AG">Thyssen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Krupp</a>) influenced the development of the city within the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Province" title="Rhine Province">Rhine Province</a>. Large housing areas near production sites were being built as workers and their families moved in.</p>
<ul>
<li>1823 a district ("Landkreis") Duisburg is established including the cities of <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BClheim" title="Mülheim">Mülheim an der Ruhr</a>.</li>
<li>1824 construction of the <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> factory Fr. W. Curtius; beginning of the industry age in Duisburg.</li>
<li>1828 Franz Haniel builds a dockyard for <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">steamships</a></li>
<li>1846 <a href="/wiki/Cologne%E2%80%93Duisburg_railway" title="Cologne–Duisburg railway">railway line to Düsseldorf</a></li>
<li>1847 <a href="/wiki/Cologne-Minden_trunk_line" title="Cologne-Minden trunk line">railway line via Dortmund to Minden</a></li>
<li>1873 Duisburg becomes an <a href="/wiki/Independent_city" title="Independent city">independent city</a> borough.</li>
<li>1904 Birth of the 100,000th resident (Ernst R. Straube)</li>
<li>1921 French Infantry occupy the city on <a href="/wiki/8_March" class="mw-redirect" title="8 March">8 March</a> to secure <a href="/wiki/War_reparations" title="War reparations">war reparation</a> payments incurred during World War I.</li>
<li>1929 The city of Hamborn and Duisburg are joined together. The new city is given the name of Duisburg-Hamborn.</li>
<li>1935 Duisburg-Hamborn is renamed Duisburg.</li>
<li>1938 (November) The <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazis</a> destroy the city's <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a>.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Duisburg_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Duisburg in World War II">Bombing of Duisburg in World War II</a></div>
<p>A major logistical center in the Ruhr and location of chemical, steel and iron industries, Duisburg was a primary target of <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bombers</a>. As such, it is considered by some historians to be the single most heavily bombed German city by the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, with industrial areas and residential blocks targeted by Allied <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">incendiary bombs</a>.</p>
<p>On the night of 12–13 June 1941, British bombers dropped a total of 445 tons of bombs in and around Duisburg. As part of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Ruhr" title="Battle of the Ruhr">Battle of the Ruhr</a>, another British raid of 577 bombers destroyed the old city between 12–13 May 1943 with 1,599 tons of bombs. During the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing" title="Strategic bombing">bombing raids</a>, 96,000 people were made homeless with countless lives lost.</p>
<p>In 1944 the city was again badly damaged as a total of 2,000 tons of bombs were dropped on 22 May. On 14 October, the tonnage was repeated with 2,018 tons when <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Halifax" title="Handley Page Halifax">Halifax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Lancaster</a>, and <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Mosquito" title="De Havilland Mosquito">Mosquito</a> bombers appeared over Duisburg as part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Hurricane_(1944)" title="Operation Hurricane (1944)">Operation Hurricane</a>. This daylight raid was followed by a night attack; over 24 hours about 9,000 tons of HE and incendiaries had been dropped on Duisburg. Numerous similar attacks followed until the end of 1944.</p>
<p>The Allied ground advance into Germany reached Duisburg in April 1945. The US <a href="/wiki/17th_Airborne_Division_(United_States)" title="17th Airborne Division (United States)">17th Airborne Division</a>, acting as regular infantry and not in a parachute role, met only scattered resistance in the vicinity and captured the city on 12 April 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 8 May 1945 the <a href="/wiki/ADSEC" title="ADSEC">ADSEC</a> Engineer Group A, led by Col. <a href="/wiki/Helmer_Swenholt" title="Helmer Swenholt">Helmer Swenholt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commanding_officer" title="Commanding officer">commanding officer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/332nd_Engineer_General_Service_Regiment_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="332nd Engineer General Service Regiment (United States)">332nd Engineer General Service Regiment</a>, constructed a <a href="/wiki/Duisburg-Hochfeld_rail_bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Duisburg-Hochfeld rail bridge">railway bridge between Duisburg and Rheinhausen</a> across the Rhine. It was 860 meters long, and constructed in six days, fifteen hours and twenty minutes, a record time. It was named the "Victory Bridge".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>A total of 299 bombing raids had almost completely destroyed the historic cityscape. 80% of all residential buildings had been destroyed or partly damaged. Almost the whole of the city had to be rebuilt, and most historic landmarks had been lost.</p>

<p>Beginning in the mid-1960s, the decline of Duisburg's steel and mining industry caused a significant loss of residents. While in 1975 approximately 590,000 people were living in Duisburg, the number has shrunk to 518,000 in 1985.</p>
<p>Duisburg celebrated its 1100th anniversary in 1983. The city's population recovered a little in the following years, up to 537,000 in 1992. It declined to 488,000 in 2011. On 19 July 2004, it was hit by a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>. The municipal theater and parts of the city center were damaged. The city hosted the 7th <a href="/wiki/World_Games" title="World Games">World Games</a> in 2005. In 2010, 21 people died because of a mass panic at the <a href="/wiki/Love_Parade_disaster" title="Love Parade disaster">Love Parade</a>; over 500 people were injured.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Links_to_the_September_11_attacks">Links to the September 11 attacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Links to the September 11 attacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, evidence surfaced that linked a mosque in the city with the <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_cell" title="Hamburg cell">Hamburg cell</a>, and also with ringleader <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_Atta" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohammed Atta">Mohammed Atta</a>. Also, the police uncovered calendars owned by people who attended the mosque, with the dates April 15, 2001 (the date when many hijackers slipped into the US to finalize preparations for the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>) inscribed with 'The program begins' and September 11, 2001 inscribed with 'The program ends' in Arabic. Further investigation revealed that <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_detention_camp" title="Guantanamo Bay detention camp">Guantanamo Bay</a> inmate Mohamed Ould Slahi was a frequent visitor to the mosque, and had helped Atta recruit conspirators for the attacks. It was also believed that Ould Slahi was present in the city on September 10 discussing the attacks with other unidentified extremists.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This evidence arose at around the same time as news broke of German Islamists being trained in Pakistani terrorist camps.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turks_in_Duisburg">Turks in Duisburg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Turks in Duisburg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Duisburg is home to 85,000 people of <a href="/wiki/Turkish_people" title="Turkish people">Turkish</a> origin.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Other estimates suggest that the Turkish population is as many as 100,000<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The new Merkez Mosque, one of the largest Muslim <a href="/wiki/Place_of_worship" title="Place of worship">place of worship</a> in Western Europe, was built with help by the way of contribution of 3.2 million euro from the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> and the state of North Rhine-Westphalia.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nur_Fettaho%C4%9Flu" title="Nur Fettahoğlu">Asiye Nur Fettahoğlu</a>, a Turkish-German actress was born in Duisburg on November 12, 1980.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duisburg_Port">Duisburg Port</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Duisburg Port">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Duisport"<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> is the largest inland <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It is officially regarded as a "seaport" because seagoing river vessels go to ports in Europe, Africa and <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">the Middle East</a>. Numerous docks are mostly located at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_(river)" title="Ruhr (river)">Ruhr</a> where it joins the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>.</p>
<p>Each year more than 40 million tonnes of various goods are handled with more than 20,000 ships calling at the port. The public harbor facilities stretch across an area of 7.4 square kilometres (2.9&#160;sq&#160;mi). There are 21 docks covering an area of 1.8&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (0.7&#160;sq&#160;mi) and 40 kilometres (25 miles) of wharf. The area of the Logport Logistic Center Duisburg stretches across an area of 2.65&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (1.02&#160;sq&#160;mi). With 2.5 million <a href="/wiki/Twenty-foot_equivalent_unit" title="Twenty-foot equivalent unit">TEU</a> it is also the largest inland container port based on 2011 figures.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A number of companies run their own private docks and 114 million tonnes of goods yearly (2010) are handled in Duisburg in total.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Duisburg is served by several <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">autobahns</a>, with 3 east–west routes and 2 north–south routes. <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_3" title="Bundesautobahn 3">A3</a> forms a bypass east of the city and mostly serves through traffic. <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_59" title="Bundesautobahn 59">A59</a> runs parallel to A3 and serves the city from north to south with 14 interchanges, much more than most other cities in the Ruhr area. The <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_40" title="Bundesautobahn 40">A40</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_42" title="Bundesautobahn 42">A42</a> are two east–west routes that serve central and northern Duisburg. Autobahn A40 also serves major through traffic from the Netherlands to Berlin and points east. A short spur, <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_524" title="Bundesautobahn 524">A524</a> serves southern Duisburg. Most Autobahns have six lanes or are upgraded to six lanes (A59).</p>
<p>Apart from the autobahns, no <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe" title="Bundesstraße">Bundesstraßen</a> serve the city directly. <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_8" title="Bundesstraße 8">B8</a> runs through the city, but uses A59's alignment. B288 runs in the extreme south of the city, and serves traffic to and from <a href="/wiki/Krefeld" title="Krefeld">Krefeld</a>. Several bridges span the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, most prominently the A40 and A42 bridges, but also the L287 <a href="/wiki/Suspension_bridge" title="Suspension bridge">suspension bridge</a> and the L237 <a href="/wiki/Arch_bridge" title="Arch bridge">arch bridge</a>, a three-lane bridge with 2 lanes per peak direction with dynamic lane usage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transport">Public transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Public transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Duisburg_Hauptbahnhof" title="Duisburg Hauptbahnhof">Duisburg Hbf</a> is served by the <a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">InterCityExpress</a> and <a href="/wiki/InterCity" title="InterCity">InterCity</a> long-distance network of the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a>, in addition lines <a href="/wiki/S1_(Rhine-Ruhr_S-Bahn)" title="S1 (Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn)"><span title="S-Bahn Rhein-Ruhr S1" style="font-weight:bold; white-space:nowrap; font-size:85%; padding:0px .4em; border: 1px solid #0B9A33;background-color: #0B9A33; -webkit-border-radius: 1ex; -moz-border-radius: 1ex; border-radius: 1ex; color:white;">S 1</span></a> and <a href="/wiki/S2_(Rhine-Ruhr_S-Bahn)" title="S2 (Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn)"><span title="S-Bahn Rhein-Ruhr S2" style="font-weight:bold; white-space:nowrap; font-size:85%; padding:0px .4em; border: 1px solid #006DB6;background-color: #006DB6; -webkit-border-radius: 1ex; -moz-border-radius: 1ex; border-radius: 1ex; color:white;">S 2</span></a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Ruhr_S-Bahn" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhein-Ruhr S-Bahn">S-Bahn</a> line connect Duisburg with other cities of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr" title="Rhine-Ruhr">Rhine-Ruhr</a> area.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Stadtbahn" title="Stadtbahn">Stadtbahn</a> <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> line, the <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Duisburg" title="Trams in Duisburg">Duisburg tramway network</a>, and a bus system, all operated by the Duisburger Verkehrsgesellschaft provide local services. Stadtbahn line U79, the so-called D-Bahn, is a connection to the neighbouring city of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> and is operated jointly with the <a href="/wiki/Rheinbahn" title="Rheinbahn">Rheinbahn</a> of Düsseldorf. All S-Bahn, Stadtbahn and bus lines operate under the umbrella of the <a href="/wiki/Verkehrsverbund_Rhein-Ruhr" title="Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Ruhr">Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Ruhr</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several newspapers reporting on local events and politics, including the "<a href="/wiki/Westdeutsche_Allgemeine_Zeitung" title="Westdeutsche Allgemeine Zeitung">Westdeutsche Allgemeine</a>" (WAZ), the "Neue Ruhr Zeitung" (NRZ) and the "<a href="/wiki/Rheinische_Post" title="Rheinische Post">Rheinische Post</a>" (RP). The local <a href="/wiki/Radio_broadcasting" title="Radio broadcasting">radio station</a> "Radio Duisburg" was the first local radio broadcaster in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German state</a> of North Rhine-Westphalia. It started broadcasting in 1990. There is a local <a href="/wiki/Television_station" title="Television station">television station</a> ("STUDIO 47"), which was the first local station to broadcast in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>. It started broadcasting in 2006. In its Duisburg studios the <a href="/wiki/Westdeutscher_Rundfunk" title="Westdeutscher Rundfunk">WDR</a> produces a local programme for the city of Duisburg and the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Rhine" title="Lower Rhine">Lower Rhine</a> region north of Düsseldorf. WDR is part of the German television and <a href="/wiki/Radio_network" title="Radio network">radio network</a> <a href="/wiki/ARD_(broadcaster)" title="ARD (broadcaster)">ARD</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duisburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Duisburg hosts a comprehensive range of cultural facilities and events. A highlight is the annual "Duisburger Akzente",<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> a festival focusing on modern social, political and cultural topics.</p>

<p>Besides <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> Duisburg is a residence of the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Oper_am_Rhein" title="Deutsche Oper am Rhein">Deutsche Oper am Rhein</a>, one of the major <a href="/wiki/Opera_house" title="Opera house">opera houses</a> in Germany. The <a href="/wiki/Duisburg_Philharmonic" title="Duisburg Philharmonic">Duisburg Philharmonic Orchestra</a> is one of Germany's orchestras with an international reputation.</p>
<p>Thanks to its history as a harbor city and a trade and industrial center Duisburg offers a variety of architectural places of interest, such as the <a href="/wiki/German_Inland_Waterways_Museum" title="German Inland Waterways Museum">German Inland Waterways Museum</a>. The spectrum goes from old churches such as "St Johann Baptist" in Duisburg-Hamborn, which was built in 900, to <a href="/wiki/Modern_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern era">modern age</a> buildings like Micro-Electronic-Centrum in Duisburg-Neudorf, built in 1995. Another subject of interest is the <a href="/wiki/Landschaftspark_Duisburg-Nord" title="Landschaftspark Duisburg-Nord">Landschaftspark Duisburg-Nord</a><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> an abandoned industrial complex open to the public and an Anchor Point of ERIH, The <a href="/wiki/European_Route_of_Industrial_Heritage" title="European Route of Industrial Heritage">European Route of Industrial Heritage</a>. The city center locates the <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Lehmbruck" title="Wilhelm Lehmbruck">Wilhelm Lehmbruck</a> Museum,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the municipal theatre<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and the shopping street known as "fountain mile".</p>
<p>The city also contains two <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical gardens</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_Duisburg-Hamborn" title="Botanischer Garten Duisburg-Hamborn">Botanischer Garten Duisburg-Hamborn</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_Kaiserberg" title="Botanischer Garten Kaiserberg">Botanischer Garten Kaiserberg</a>, as well as a number of municipal parks.</p>
<p>On 24 July 2010, 21 people were killed and hundreds injured in the city during the <a href="/wiki/Love_Parade_disaster" title="Love Parade disaster">Love Parade</a>, an electronic music procession and party.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>Duisburg is involved in many kinds of <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sports</a>. Nevertheless, most important for its inhabitants is the local <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> club <a href="/wiki/MSV_Duisburg" title="MSV Duisburg">MSV Duisburg</a>. Recently, with the new MSV Arena the city received a brand new sports stadium for various kinds of sports such as football and American football. During the summer months of 2005 the <a href="/wiki/World_Games_2005" title="World Games 2005">World Games</a> took place in Duisburg. During the <a href="/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2006 FIFA World Cup">2006 FIFA World Cup</a>, Duisburg was the stage for preparation of the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> team and the residence of the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Football_team" title="Football team">football team</a>, who won the cup in the final match against <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. Duisburg is also known for its <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Ruhr-Marathon" title="Rhein-Ruhr-Marathon">Rhein-Ruhr-Marathon</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">rowing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canoeing" title="Canoeing">canoeing</a> <a href="/wiki/Regatta" title="Regatta">regattas</a> and the world championships that take place there regularly. Other popular sports are <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">icehockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_polo" title="Water polo">water polo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">hockey</a>.</p>

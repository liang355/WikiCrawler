<p>The <b>tobacco industry</b> comprises those persons and companies engaged in the growth, preparation for sale, shipment, advertisement, and distribution of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> and tobacco-related products. It is a global industry; tobacco can grow in any warm, moist environment, which means it can be farmed on all continents except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</p>
<p>Tobacco, one of the most widely used addictive substances in the world<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, is a plant native to the Americas and historically one of the half-dozen most important crops grown by American farmers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> More specifically, tobacco refers to any of various plants of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana" title="Nicotiana">Nicotiana</a></i> (especially <i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana_tabacum" title="Nicotiana tabacum">N. tabacum</a></i>) native to <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> America and widely cultivated for their leaves, which are dried and processed chiefly for smoking in <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_pipe" title="Tobacco pipe">pipes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">cigarettes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cigar" title="Cigar">cigars</a>; it is also cut to form <a href="/wiki/Chewing_tobacco" title="Chewing tobacco">chewing tobacco</a> or ground to make <a href="/wiki/Snuff_(tobacco)" title="Snuff (tobacco)">snuff</a> or dipping tobacco, as well as other less common preparations. From 1617 to 1793 tobacco was the most valuable staple export from the English American mainland colonies and the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Until the 1960s, the United States not only grew but also manufactured and exported more tobacco than any other country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Tobacco is an agricultural <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> product, <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_economics" title="Agricultural economics">similar in economic terms</a> to agricultural <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">foodstuffs</a>: the price is in part determined by crop yields, which vary depending on local weather conditions. The price also varies by specific <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a> grown, the total quantity on the market ready for sale, the area where it is grown, the health of the plants, and other characteristics individual to product quality.</p>
<p>Since 1964 conclusive medical evidence of the deadly effects of tobacco consumption has led to a sharp decline in official support for producers and manufacturers of tobacco, although it contributes to the agricultural, fiscal, manufacturing, and exporting sectors of the economy. Laws around the world now often have some restrictions on smoking, but almost 6 trillion cigarettes are still produced each year, representing over a 12% increase since the year 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-atlas_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlas-1">[1]</a></sup> China accounts for over 40% of current world production.<sup id="cite_ref-atlas_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlas-1">[1]</a></sup> Tobacco is often heavily taxed to gain revenues for governments and as an incentive for people not to smoke.<sup id="cite_ref-whotax_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whotax-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tobacco_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_tobacco" title="History of tobacco">History of tobacco</a></div>
<p>For a history of how tobacco has been grown and marketed, see <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">smoking</a> and articles on similar topics.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Position_of_industry">Position of industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tobacco_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Position of industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phrase "tobacco industry" generally refers to the <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">companies</a> involved in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">cigarettes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cigar" title="Cigar">cigars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snuff_(tobacco)" title="Snuff (tobacco)">snuff</a>, chewing tobacco and <a href="/wiki/Smoking_pipe_(tobacco)" class="mw-redirect" title="Smoking pipe (tobacco)">pipe</a> tobacco. <a href="/wiki/China_National_Tobacco_Co." class="mw-redirect" title="China National Tobacco Co.">China National Tobacco Co.</a> has become the largest tobacco company in the world by volume. Following extensive merger and acquisition activity in the 1990s and 2000s, four firms dominate international markets - in alphabetical order:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Altria" title="Altria">Altria</a> formerly <a href="/wiki/Philip_Morris_International" title="Philip Morris International">Philip Morris International</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_American_Tobacco" title="British American Tobacco">British American Tobacco</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Imperial_Tobacco" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Tobacco">Imperial Tobacco</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Japan_Tobacco" title="Japan Tobacco">Japan Tobacco</a></li>
<li class="mw-empty-elt"></li>
</ul>
<p>Altria, formerly called the <a href="/wiki/Philip_Morris_Cos." class="mw-redirect" title="Philip Morris Cos.">Philip Morris Cos.</a> (Philip Morris Companies Inc.), still owns the Philip Morris tobacco business in the United States, but Philip Morris International has been fully independent since 2008. In most countries these companies either have long-established dominance, or have purchased the major domestic producer or producers (often a former <a href="/wiki/State_monopoly" title="State monopoly">state monopoly</a>). Until 2014 the United States had one other substantial independent firm, <a href="/wiki/Lorillard" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorillard">Lorillard</a>, which Reynolds American, Inc. acquired. India has its own major player, <a href="/wiki/ITC_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="ITC Limited">ITC Limited</a> (25.4%-owned by British American Tobacco). A small<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="This term requires quantification. (November 2015)">quantify</span></a></i>]</sup> number of state monopolies survive, as well as some small independent firms.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tobacco_advertising" title="Tobacco advertising">Tobacco advertising</a> is becoming increasingly restricted by the governments of countries around the world citing health issues as a reason to restrict tobaccos appeal<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The tobacco industry in the United States has suffered greatly since the mid-1990s, when it was successfully sued by several U.S. states. The suits claimed that tobacco causes cancer, that companies in the industry knew this, and that they deliberately understated the significance of their findings, contributing to the illness and death of many citizens in those states.</p>
<p>The industry was found to have decades of <a href="/wiki/Legacy_Tobacco_Documents_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Legacy Tobacco Documents Library">internal memos</a> confirming in detail that tobacco (which contains <a href="/wiki/Nicotine" title="Nicotine">nicotine</a>) is both <a href="/wiki/Substance_use_disorder" title="Substance use disorder">addictive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a> (cancer-causing). The industry had long denied that <a href="/wiki/Nicotine_addiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicotine addiction">nicotine is addictive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Glantz2000_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glantz2000-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The suit resulted in a <a href="/wiki/Master_Settlement_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Master Settlement Agreement">large cash settlement</a> being paid by a group of tobacco companies to the states that sued. Further, since the suit was settled, other individuals have come forth, in <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuits</a>, claiming individual damages. New suits of this nature will probably continue for a long time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Since the settlement is a heavy tax on the profits of the tobacco industry in the US, regressive against smokers, and further settlements being made only add to the financial burden of these companies, it is debatable if the industry has a money-producing long term outlook.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The tobacco industry has historically been largely successful in this litigation process, with the majority of cases being won by the industry. During the first 42 years of tobacco litigation (between 1954 and 1996) the industry maintained a clean record in litigation<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> thanks to tactics described in a R.J. Reynolds Tobacco Company internal memo as "the way we won these cases, to paraphrase <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">Gen. Patton</a>, is not by spending all of Reynolds' money, but by making the other son of a bitch spend all of his."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Between 1995 and 2005 only 59% of cases were won by the tobacco industry either outright or on appeal in the US,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> but the continued success of the industry's efforts to win these cases is questionable. In Florida, the industry has lost 77 of the 116 "Engle progeny" cases that have gone to trial.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The U.S. Supreme Court has also denied the industry's major grounds for appeal of Engle cases.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2009, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> signed into law the <a href="/wiki/Family_Smoking_Prevention_and_Tobacco_Control_Act" title="Family Smoking Prevention and Tobacco Control Act">Family Smoking Prevention and Tobacco Control Act</a> which has been called a "sweeping anti-smoking" bill.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Among other restrictions, this Act banned the use of any constituent, additive, herb or spice that adds a "characterizing flavor" to the tobacco product or smoke (Section 907)(a)(1)(A).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The aim of this ban is to prevent children and teenagers from becoming addicted to cigarettes at a young age with the <a href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="US Department of Health and Human Services">US Department of Health and Human Services</a> citing that "studies have shown that 17 year old smokers are three times as likely to use flavored cigarettes as are smokers over the age of 25".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This ban however does not apply to <a href="/wiki/Menthol_cigarette" title="Menthol cigarette">menthol cigarettes</a>, which are exempt from the bill.</p>
<p>Lawsuits against the tobacco industry are primarily restricted to the United States due to differences in legal systems in other countries. Many businesses class ongoing lawsuits as a cost of doing business in the US and feel their revenue will be only marginally affected by the activities.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Big_Tobacco" title="Big Tobacco">Large tobacco companies</a> have entered the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_cigarette#History" title="Electronic cigarette">electronic cigarette</a> market by either buying some of the small e-cigarette companies or by starting their own e-cigarette companies.<sup id="cite_ref-Orellana-BarriosPayne2015_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orellana-BarriosPayne2015-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tobacco_control">Tobacco control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tobacco_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tobacco control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 11, 2004, the U.S. became the 108th country to sign the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>'s <a href="/wiki/WHO_Framework_Convention_on_Tobacco_Control" title="WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control">Global Treaty on Tobacco Control</a>. This treaty places broad restrictions on the sale, advertising, shipment, and taxation of tobacco products. The U.S. has not yet ratified this treaty in its <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">senate</a> and does not yet have a schedule for doing so.</p>
<p>Most recently, there has been discussion within the tobacco control community of transforming the tobacco industry through the replacement of tobacco <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporations</a> by other types of <a href="/wiki/Business_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Business organization">business organizations</a> that can be established to provide tobacco to the market while not attempting to increase market demand.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 20, 2007, the <a href="/wiki/US_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="US Supreme Court">US Supreme Court</a> ruled that the <a href="/wiki/Altria_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Altria Group">Altria Group</a> (formerly Philip Morris) did not have to pay $79.5 million in punitive damages awarded to Mayola Williams in a 1999 Oregon court ruling, when she sued Phillip Morris for responsibility in the cancer death of her husband, Jesse Williams.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Supreme Court's decision overturns a ruling made by the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Supreme_Court" title="Oregon Supreme Court">Oregon Supreme Court</a> that upheld the award.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 3, 2008, The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit threw out a $800 billion class-action lawsuit filed on behalf of a group or class of people who smoked light cigarettes. The plaintiffs' lawyers were confident that they would be able to win this suit due to the success of the Schwab case <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> wherein tobacco companies were found guilty of fraud-like charges because they were selling the idea that light cigarettes were safer than regular cigarettes. The ruling by the three-judge panel will not allow the suit to be pursued as a class, but instead need proof for why individual smokers chose light cigarettes over regular cigarettes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tobacco_industry_in_popular_culture">Tobacco industry in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tobacco_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tobacco industry in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tobacco industry has had a long relationship with the entertainment industry. In silent era movies, back-lit smoke was often used by filmmakers to create sense of mystery and sensuality in a scene. Later, cigarettes were deliberately placed in the hands of Hollywood stars as an early phase of <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">product placement</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> until health regulating bodies tightened rules on tobacco advertisement and anti-smoking groups pressured actors and studio executives against such tactics. <a href="/wiki/Big_Tobacco" title="Big Tobacco">Big Tobacco</a> has since been the subject focus of films such as the <a href="/wiki/Docudrama" title="Docudrama">docudrama</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Insider_(film)" title="The Insider (film)">The Insider</a></i> (1999) and <i><a href="/wiki/Thank_You_for_Smoking_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thank You for Smoking (film)">Thank You For Smoking</a></i> (2005).</p>
<p>These issues have also constituted a recurring storyline in the <a href="/wiki/AMC_(TV_channel)" title="AMC (TV channel)">AMC</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Men" title="Mad Men">Mad Men</a></i>, from <a href="/wiki/Mad_Men_(season_1)" title="Mad Men (season 1)">season 1</a> beginning with the pilot episode (<a href="/wiki/Smoke_Gets_in_Your_Eyes_(Mad_Men)" title="Smoke Gets in Your Eyes (Mad Men)">"Smoke Gets In Your Eyes"</a>) through season 7's midseason finale, <a href="/wiki/Mad_Men_(season_7)" title="Mad Men (season 7)">"Waterloo"</a>. In another television show, the <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> original program <a href="/wiki/List_of_Orange_Is_the_New_Black_episodes#Season_2_.282014.29" title="List of Orange Is the New Black episodes"><i>Orange Is the New Black</i> season 2</a>, episode 2 ("Looks Blue, Tastes Red"), "<a href="/wiki/List_of_characters_from_Orange_Is_the_New_Black#Inmates" class="mw-redirect" title="List of characters from Orange Is the New Black">Black Cindy</a> bring[s] some <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Naderism</a> to Lichtfield (a women's federal penitentiary) when talking about <a href="/wiki/Altria_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Altria Group">Philip Morris</a>: 'Nah they ain't so bad. The people can decide for themselves if they wanna smoke. The real evil is them companies killing us without our consent. <a href="/wiki/Monsanto" title="Monsanto">Monsanto</a>. <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_Group" title="Rio Tinto Group">Rio Tinto</a>. <a href="/wiki/Big_Pharma_conspiracy_theory" title="Big Pharma conspiracy theory">Big Pharma</a>. <a href="/wiki/British_Petroleum" class="mw-redirect" title="British Petroleum">BP</a>. <a href="/wiki/Halliburton" title="Halliburton">Halliburton</a>. I've been reading there's some dark shit goin' down. Not that those motherfuckers are ever gonna hire us! The real criminals? They don't bother with small timers.' ".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

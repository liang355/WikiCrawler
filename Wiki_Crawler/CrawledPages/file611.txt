<p><b>Ralph Cudworth</b> (1617&#160;– 26 June 1688) was an <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Classicist" class="mw-redirect" title="Classicist">classicist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theologian" class="mw-redirect" title="Theologian">theologian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosopher</a>, a leading figure among the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Platonists" title="Cambridge Platonists">Cambridge Platonists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> From a family background embedded in the early <a href="/wiki/Nonconformist" title="Nonconformist">nonconformist</a> environment of <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_College,_Cambridge" title="Emmanuel College, Cambridge">Emmanuel College</a> in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>, where he studied, he became <a href="/wiki/Regius_Professor_of_Hebrew_(Cambridge)" title="Regius Professor of Hebrew (Cambridge)">Regius Professor of Hebrew</a> 1645–88, Master of <a href="/wiki/Clare_College,_Cambridge" title="Clare College, Cambridge">Clare Hall</a> 1645–54 and Master of <a href="/wiki/Christ%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Christ's College, Cambridge">Christ's College</a> 1654–88. His great work, entitled <i>The True Intellectual System of the Universe</i>, was published in 1678. He was a leading opponent of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_Cudworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_early_life">Origins and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_Cudworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ralph Cudworth's father, Dr. Ralph Cudworth (born 1572), derived from the Cudworths of <a href="/wiki/Werneth,_Greater_Manchester" title="Werneth, Greater Manchester">Werneth</a> Hall in Lancashire.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The family originated in Cudworth near <a href="/wiki/Barnsley" title="Barnsley">Barnsley</a>, Yorkshire, and came to Werneth by the marriage of John Cudworth to Margaret, daughter and coheiress of Richard Oldham, during the early 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ralph Cudworth the elder matriculated from <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_College,_Cambridge" title="Emmanuel College, Cambridge">Emmanuel College</a> in the University of Cambridge in 1588/89, and graduating B.A. (1592/93), M.A. (1596), and B.D. (1603) became a Fellow of the college.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1606 he was instituted to the Perpetual Vicarage of <a href="/wiki/Coggeshall" title="Coggeshall">Coggeshall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, at the presentation of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rich,_1st_Earl_of_Warwick" title="Robert Rich, 1st Earl of Warwick">Baron Rich</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> to replace Thomas Stoughton, who had been deprived by <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Commissioner" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord High Commissioner">High Commission</a> for <a href="/wiki/Nonconformism" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonconformism">nonconformism</a>. He was presented to the rectory of <a href="/wiki/Aller,_Somerset" title="Aller, Somerset">Aller, Somerset</a> by his college (which held the <a href="/wiki/Advowson" title="Advowson">advowson</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>) in <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1609 or 1610.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On 18 June 1611 at <a href="/wiki/Southwark" title="Southwark">Southwark</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> he married Mary Machell<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (who had been nurse to Prince <a href="/wiki/Henry_Frederick,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Henry Frederick, Prince of Wales">Henry</a> the son of <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>) and their children were christened at Aller over the following decade.</p>
<p>One of at least five children, Ralph was born at Aller in 1617. His father, having been awarded <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Divinity" title="Doctor of Divinity">Doctor of Divinity</a> in 1619, died at Aller in autumn 1624,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_Stoughton_(priest)" title="John Stoughton (priest)">John Stoughton</a> (son of Thomas Stoughton of Coggeshall), also Fellow of Emmanuel College, who succeeded him as Rector, took the widow Mary Cudworth as his own wife. Dr. Stoughton paid careful attention to the education of his stepchildren, and in 1632 when Stoughton received preferment to <a href="/wiki/St_Mary_Aldermanbury" title="St Mary Aldermanbury">St Mary Aldermanbury</a> in the City of London<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and the family left Aller, Ralph matriculated from Emmanuel to begin his university career.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> His elder brother <a href="/wiki/James_Cudworth_(colonist)" title="James Cudworth (colonist)">James Cudworth</a> married and emigrated permanently to <a href="/wiki/Scituate,_Massachusetts" title="Scituate, Massachusetts">Scituate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a> in New England in 1634.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Their mother died in 1635 and Dr. Stoughton married a daughter of John Browne of <a href="/wiki/Frampton,_Dorset" title="Frampton, Dorset">Frampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Dorset" title="Dorchester, Dorset">Dorchester</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Dr. Stoughton died in 1639.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_marriage">Career and marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_Cudworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career and marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A diligent student, Ralph graduated B.A. 1636 and M.A. 1639, whereupon he was elected Fellow and became a successful tutor. In 1642 he published <i>A Discourse concerning the true Notion of the Lord's Supper</i> and a tract entitled <i>The Union of Christ and the Church</i>. In 1645 he was appointed Master of <a href="/wiki/Clare_College,_Cambridge" title="Clare College, Cambridge">Clare Hall</a> and the same year was elected Regius professor of <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>. In 1650 he was presented to the college living of <a href="/wiki/North_Cadbury,_Somerset" class="mw-redirect" title="North Cadbury, Somerset">North Cadbury, Somerset</a>. From the correspondence of his friend <a href="/wiki/John_Worthington_(academic)" title="John Worthington (academic)">John Worthington</a> we learn that Cudworth was nearly compelled, through poverty, to leave the University,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> but in 1654 he was elected master of Christ's College.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>This appointment coincided with Cudworth's marriage, a very advantageous alliance which secured his position in the University.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> His wife was Damaris, daughter of Damaris and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Cradock" title="Matthew Cradock">Matthew Cradock</a>, a London merchant and first Governor of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts Bay Company">Massachusetts Bay Company</a> (which he never visited) who died in 1641.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Her stepmother Rebeccah Cradock in 1643 married <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Whichcote" title="Benjamin Whichcote">Benjamin Whichcote</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> eminent Platonist, of Emmanuel College, where Samuel Cradock (nephew of Mathew Cradock and pupil of Whichcote) was also undergraduate and Fellow, 1637–1656.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Damaris married first (by 1646) the London citizen Thomas Andrewes of London and Feltham (son of Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Andrewes" title="Thomas Andrewes">Thomas Andrewes</a>, Lord Mayor of London in 1649 and 1651–52, d. 1659), who predeceased his notable father in 1653 having produced several children.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> She then became the wife of Ralph Cudworth in Cambridge, and outlived him, dying in 1695.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>He now became recognized as a leader among the remarkable group known as the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Platonists" title="Cambridge Platonists">Cambridge Platonists</a>. The whole party was more or less in sympathy with the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth</a>: during the late 1650s Dr. Cudworth was consulted by <a href="/wiki/John_Thurloe" title="John Thurloe">John Thurloe</a>, Cromwell's secretary to the council of state, in regard to university and government appointments, and in other matters.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">Restoration</a> Dr. Cudworth's Mastership of his college was challenged, but he retained it until his death.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1662 he was presented to the rectory of <a href="/wiki/Ashwell,_Hertfordshire" title="Ashwell, Hertfordshire">Ashwell</a>, Herts, by the Bishop of London, Dr <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Sheldon" title="Gilbert Sheldon">Gilbert Sheldon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1665 he almost quarrelled with his fellow-<a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_More" title="Henry More">Henry More</a>, because the latter had written an ethical work which Cudworth feared would interfere with his own long-contemplated treatise on the same subject. To avoid clashing, More brought out his book, the <i>Enchiridion ethicum</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>; Cudworth's never appeared. His own majestic work, <i>The True Intellectual System of the Universe</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> was conceived in three parts of which only the first was brought to completion,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> being given its Archiepiscopal <a href="/wiki/Imprimatur" title="Imprimatur">Imprimatur</a> in 1671 but not published until 1678. Cudworth was installed prebendary of Gloucester in 1678.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> He died on 26 June 1688, and was buried in the chapel of Christ's College.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_Cudworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ralph's sons included John Cudworth (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1656–1726), who became a Fellow of Christ's College,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Charles (d. 1684), who died in India,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and probably Thomas, who obtained his M.A. at Christ's College in 1682.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> His daughter <a href="/wiki/Damaris_Cudworth_Masham" title="Damaris Cudworth Masham">Damaris</a> (1659–1708), a devout and talented woman, became the second wife of Sir Francis Masham of <a href="/wiki/High_Laver" title="High Laver">High Laver</a>, Essex.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The Lady Masham was distinguished as the friend of <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> and exchanged letters with <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" class="mw-redirect" title="Gottfried Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a>.</p>
<p>His elder brother <a href="/wiki/James_Cudworth_(colonist)" title="James Cudworth (colonist)">James Cudworth</a> (1612–1682) became an Assistant Governor of Massachusetts and a Commissioner of the United Colonies,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> whose descendants form an extensive American branch of the Cudworth family. His younger brother, John Cudworth (1622–1675),<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> of London and of <a href="/wiki/Bentley,_Suffolk" title="Bentley, Suffolk">Bentley, Suffolk</a>, became an Alderman of London and Master of the <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Girdlers" title="Worshipful Company of Girdlers">Worshipful Company of Girdlers</a> in 1667–68.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In 1675 John left four orphans of whom Thomas (1661–1726)<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and Benjamin (d. 1725)<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> both attended Christ's College.</p>
<p>His sister Elizabeth (1615–1654) became the second<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> of three wives of Josias Beacham of <a href="/wiki/Broughton,_Northamptonshire" title="Broughton, Northamptonshire">Broughton, Northamptonshire</a> (Rector of <a href="/wiki/Seaton,_Rutland" title="Seaton, Rutland">Seaton, Rutland</a> from 1627 to 1676) in 1636,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> by whom she had several children. Beacham was ejected by the Puritans in 1653 but reinstated by 1662.</p>
<p>A certain Jane or Joan Cudworth living unmarried in 1647 might also have been a sister of Ralph's, but this is not finally confirmed.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<p>His sermons, such as that preached before the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom House of Commons">House of Commons</a>, on 31 March 1647, advocate principles of religious toleration and charity. In 1678 he published <i>The True Intellectual System of the Universe: the first part, wherein all the reason and philosophy of atheism is confuted and its impossibility demonstrated</i> (<i>imprimatur</i> dated 1671). No more was published, perhaps because of the theological clamour raised against this first part. Much of Cudworth's work still remains in manuscript; <i>A Treatise concerning eternal and immutable Morality</i> was published with a preface by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Chandler_(bishop)" title="Edward Chandler (bishop)">Edward Chandler</a> in 1731;<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and <i>A Treatise of Freewill</i>, edited by <a href="/wiki/Chaplain_of_King%27s_College_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaplain of King's College London">John Allen</a>, in 1838; both are connected with the design of his <i>magnum opus</i>, the <i>Intellectual System</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <i>Intellectual System</i> arose, so its author tells us, out of a discourse refuting "fatal necessity", or <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">determinism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Enlarging his plan, he proposed to prove three matters:</p>
<dl>
<dd>(a) the <a href="/wiki/Existence_of_God" title="Existence of God">existence of God</a>;</dd>
<dd>(b) the naturalness of moral distinctions; and</dd>
<dd>(c) the reality of human <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">freedom</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<p>These three together make up the intellectual (as opposed to the physical) system of the universe; and they are opposed respectively by three false principles, atheism, religious fatalism which refers all moral distinctions to the will of God, and thirdly the fatalism of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Stoics" class="mw-redirect" title="Stoics">Stoics</a>, who recognized God and yet identified Him with nature. The immense fragment dealing with atheism is all that was published by its author. Cudworth criticizes two main forms of materialistic <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a>, the atomic, adopted by <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">Democritus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">Epicurus</a> and Hobbes; and the <a href="/wiki/Hylozoism" title="Hylozoism">hylozoic</a>, attributed to <a href="/wiki/Strato_of_Lampsacus" title="Strato of Lampsacus">Strato of Lampsacus</a>, which explains everything by the supposition of an inward self-organizing life in matter. Atomic atheism is by far the more important, if only because Hobbes, the great antagonist whom Cudworth always has in view, is supposed to have held it. It arises out of the combination of two principles, neither of which is atheistic taken separately, i.e. atomism and corporealism, or the doctrine that nothing exists but body. The example of Stoicism, as Cudworth points out, shows that corporealism may be theistic.</p>
<p>Cudworth plunges into the history of atomism with vast erudition.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> It is, in its purely physical application, a theory that he fully accepts; he holds that it was taught by <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">Empedocles</a>, and in fact, nearly all the ancient philosophers, and was only perverted to atheism by Democritus. It was first invented, he believes, before the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Trojan war">Trojan war</a>, by a <a href="/wiki/Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidonian</a> thinker named Moschus or <a href="/wiki/Mochus" title="Mochus">Mochus</a>, who is identical with the <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>. In dealing with atheism Cudworth's method is to marshal the atheistic arguments elaborately, so elaborately that Dryden remarked "he has raised such objections against the being of a God and Providence that many think he has not answered them"; then in his last chapter, which by itself is as long as an ordinary treatise, he confutes them with all the reasons that his reading could supply. A subordinate matter in the book that attracted much attention at the time is the conception of the "<a href="/wiki/Plastic_Principle" title="Plastic Principle">Plastic Medium</a>," which is a mere revival of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>'s "World-Soul," and is meant to explain the existence and laws of nature without referring all to the direct operation of God. It occasioned a long-drawn controversy between <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bayle" title="Pierre Bayle">Pierre Bayle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc" class="mw-redirect" title="Georges-Louis Leclerc">Le Clerc</a>, the former maintaining, the latter denying, that the Plastic Medium is really favourable to atheism.</p>
<p>A much more favourable judgment must be given upon the short <i>Treatise on eternal and immutable Morality</i>, which deserves to be read by those who are interested in the historical development of British moral philosophy. It is an answer to Hobbes's famous doctrine that moral distinctions are created by the state, an answer from the standpoint of Platonism. Just as knowledge contains a permanent intelligible element over and above the flux of sense-impressions, so there exist eternal and immutable ideas of morality. Cudworth's ideas, like Plato's, have "a constant and never-failing entity of their own," such as we see in geometrical figures; but, unlike Plato's, they exist in the mind of God, whence they are communicated to finite understandings. Hence "it is evident that wisdom, knowledge and understanding are eternal and self-subsistent things, superior to matter and all sensible beings, and independent upon them"; and so also are moral good and evil. At this point Cudworth stops; he does not attempt to give any list of Moral Ideas. It is, indeed, the cardinal weakness of this form of intuitionism that no satisfactory list can be given and that no moral principles have the "constant and never-failing entity," or the definiteness, of the concepts of geometry (these attacks are not uncontested&#160;— see, for example, the "Common Sense" tradition from Thomas Reid to James McCosh and the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_realism" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford realism">Oxford Realists</a> Harold Prichard and Sir William David Ross). Henry More, in his <i>Enchiridion ethicum</i>, attempts to enumerate the "<i>noemata moralia</i>"; but, so far from being self-evident, most of his moral axioms are open to serious controversy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commentary_on_Cudworth">Commentary on Cudworth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_Cudworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commentary on Cudworth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Dickson_White" title="Andrew Dickson White">Andrew Dickson White</a> wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/A_History_of_the_Warfare_of_Science_with_Theology_in_Christendom" title="A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom">A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom</a></i> (1896):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In 1678 Ralph Cudworth published his <i>Intellectual System of the Universe</i>. To this day he remains, in breadth of scholarship, in strength of thought, in tolerance, and in honesty, one of the greatest glories of the English Church... He purposed to build a fortress which should protect Christianity against all dangerous theories of the universe, ancient or modern. ...while genius marked every part of it, features appeared which gave the rigidly orthodox serious misgivings. From the old theories of direct personal action on the universe by the Almighty he broke utterly. He dwelt on the action of law, rejected the continuous exercise of miraculous intervention, pointed out the fact that in the natural world there are "errors" and "bungles" and argued vigorously in favor of the origin and maintenance of the universe as a slow and gradual development of Nature in obedience to an inward principle.</p>
</blockquote>

<p><b>Passau</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈpasaʊ]</a></span>' <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_language" title="Bavarian language">Bavarian</a>: <span lang="bar" xml:lang="bar"><i>Båssa</i></span>) is a town in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Bavaria" title="Lower Bavaria">Lower Bavaria</a>, Germany. It is also known as the <b>Dreiflüssestadt</b> or "City of Three Rivers," because the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> is joined at Passau by the <a href="/wiki/Inn_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Inn River">Inn</a> from the south and the <a href="/wiki/Ilz" title="Ilz">Ilz</a> from the north.</p>
<p>Passau's population is 50,000 of whom about 12,000 are students at the local <a href="/wiki/University_of_Passau" title="University of Passau">University of Passau</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is renowned in Germany for its institutes of economics, law, theology, computer science and cultural studies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Passau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2nd century BC, many of the <a href="/wiki/Boii" title="Boii">Boii</a> tribe were pushed north across the Alps out of northern Italy by the Romans. They established a new capital called <b>Boiodurum</b> by the Romans (from Gaulish <i>Boioduron</i>), now within the Innstadt district of Passau.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Passau was an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Roman</a> colony of ancient Noricum called <b>Batavis</b>, Latin for "for the <i>Batavi</i>." The <a href="/wiki/Batavi_(Germanic_tribe)" title="Batavi (Germanic tribe)">Batavi</a> were an ancient Germanic tribe often mentioned by classical authors, and they were regularly associated with the Suebian marauders, the <a href="/wiki/Heruli" class="mw-redirect" title="Heruli">Heruli</a>.</p>
<p>During the second half of the 5th century, <a href="/wiki/Severinus_of_Noricum" title="Severinus of Noricum">St. Severinus</a> established a <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> here. In 739, an English monk called <a href="/wiki/Boniface" class="mw-redirect" title="Boniface">Boniface</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Passau" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Passau">diocese of Passau</a>, which for many years was the largest <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="German Kingdom">German Kingdom</a>/<a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, covering territory in southern Bavaria and most of what is now <a href="/wiki/Upper_Austria" title="Upper Austria">Upper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a>. From the 10th century the bishops of Passau also exercised secular authority as <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishop">Prince-Bishops</a> in the immediate area around Passau (see <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Passau" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishopric of Passau">Prince-Bishopric of Passau</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hochstift_Passau" class="extiw" title="de:Hochstift Passau">de</a>)</span>).</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Passau" title="Peace of Passau">Treaty of Passau</a> (1552), Archduke <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand I</a>, representing Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>, secured the agreement of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> princes to submit the religious question to a <a href="/wiki/Diet_(assembly)" title="Diet (assembly)">diet</a>. This led to the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Augsburg" title="Peace of Augsburg">Peace of Augsburg</a> in 1555.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a>, Passau was one of the most prolific centres of sword and bladed weapon manufacture in Germany (after <a href="/wiki/Solingen" title="Solingen">Solingen</a>). Passau smiths stamped their blades with the Passau wolf, usually a rather simplified rendering of the wolf on the city's coat-of-arms. Superstitious warriors believed that the Passau wolf conferred invulnerability on the blade's bearer, and thus Passau swords acquired a great premium. According to the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Donau-Zeitung</span></i>, aside from the wolf, some cabalistic signs and inscriptions were added.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result, the whole practice of placing magical charms on swords to protect the wearers came to be known for a time as "Passau art". (See Eduard Wagner, <i>Cut and Thrust Weapons</i>, 1969.) Other cities' smiths, including those of Solingen, recognized the marketing value of the Passau wolf and adopted it for themselves. By the 17th century, Solingen was producing more wolf-stamped blades than Passau was.</p>
<p>In 1662, a devastating fire consumed most of the city. Passau was subsequently rebuilt in the Baroque style.</p>
<p>Passau was secularised and divided between the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Electorate of Bavaria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Salzburg" title="Electorate of Salzburg">Electorate of Salzburg</a> in 1803. The portion belonging to Salzburg became part of Bavaria in 1805.</p>

<p>From 1892 until 1894, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> and his family lived in Passau.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The city archives mention Hitler being in Passau on four different occasions in the 1920s for speeches. On November 3, 1902 <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Heinrich Himmler</a> and his family arrived from Munich. They lived at Theresienstraße 394 (currently Theresienstraße 22) until September 2, 1904. Himmler maintained contact with locals until May 1945.</p>
<p>In November 1933, the building of <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Nibelungenhalle</span></i> (Hall of the Nibelungs) was announced. Intended to hold 8,000 to 10,000 guests, and another 30,000 in front of it, in 1935 the hall also became quarters for a unit of the <i>Austrian Legion</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Beginning in 1934, these troops had occupied a building that belonged to Sigmund Mandl, a Jewish merchant. That building, in turn, was referred to as <i>SA barracks</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in 1940, Passau offered the building at Bräugasse 13 to <a href="/wiki/Volksdeutsche_Mittelstelle" title="Volksdeutsche Mittelstelle">Volksdeutsche Mittelstelle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During World War II, the town also housed three sub-camps of the infamous <a href="/wiki/Mauthausen-Gusen_concentration_camp" title="Mauthausen-Gusen concentration camp">Mauthausen-Gusen concentration camp</a>: Passau I (Oberilzmühle),<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Passau II (Waldwerke Passau-Ilzstadt) and Passau III (Jandelsbrunn).</p>
<p>On May 3, 1945, a message from Major General <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Eric_Reinhart" title="Stanley Eric Reinhart">Stanley Eric Reinhart</a>’s 261st Infantry Regiment stated at 3:15 am: "AMG Officer has unconditional surrender of PASSAU signed by Burgermeister, Chief of Police and Lt. Col of Med Corps there. All troops are to turn themselves in this morning."</p>
<p>It was the site of a post World War II American sector <a href="/wiki/Displaced_persons_camp" title="Displaced persons camp">displaced persons camp</a>.</p>

<p>Until 2013, the City of Passau was subdivided into eight statistical districts, which in general coincide with formerly separate municipalities. Since 2013, the city is divided in 16 so-called areas of open council ("Bürgerversammlungsgebiete").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Passau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tourism in Passau focuses mainly on the three rivers, the <a href="/wiki/St._Stephen%27s_Cathedral,_Passau" title="St. Stephen's Cathedral, Passau">St. Stephen's Cathedral</a> (<i>Der Passauer Stephansdom</i>) and the "Old City" (<i>Die Altstadt</i>). With 17,774 pipes and 233 registers,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the organ at St. Stephen's was long held to be the largest church pipe organ in the world and is today second in size only to the organ at First Congregational Church, Los Angeles, which was expanded in 1994. Organ concerts are held daily between May and September. St.Stephen's is a true masterpiece of Italian Baroque, built by Italian architect <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Lurago" title="Carlo Lurago">Carlo Lurago</a> and decorated in part by <a href="/wiki/Carpoforo_Tencalla" title="Carpoforo Tencalla">Carpoforo Tencalla</a>. Many river cruises down the Danube start at Passau and there is a cycling path all the way down to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. It is also notable for its <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">gothic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">baroque architecture</a>. The town is dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Veste_Oberhaus" title="Veste Oberhaus">Veste Oberhaus</a> and the former fortress of the Bishop, on the mountain crest between the Danube and the Ilz rivers. Right beside the town hall is the <a href="/wiki/Scharfrichterhaus" title="Scharfrichterhaus">Scharfrichterhaus</a>, an important jazz and cabaret stage on which political cabaret is performed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migrant_entry_point">Migrant entry point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Passau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migrant entry point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its location on the German-Austrian border, and in the south-east of the country, Passau has become a major migrant entry point into Germany. <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">Refugees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">economic migrants</a> from the Middle East, Asia and Africa who have reached Europe, often entering either overland via Greece or across the sea via the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Operation_Triton" title="Operation Triton">Operation Triton</a>), then head north and sometimes enter Germany. In 2015 the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> reported that traffickers drive migrants and refugees through Austria and leave them on the side of the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">autobahn</a>. The migrants and refugees then often walk unaccompanied into Passau, the first German town northwards. This situation has caused the government of Passau to divert funds from flood prevention to housing and feeding the refugees and migrants, around 10% of whom are unaccompanied children.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

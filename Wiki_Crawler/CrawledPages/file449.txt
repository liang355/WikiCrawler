<p><b>Ansbach</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈansbax]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Ansbach.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/Ansbach.ogg" class="internal" title="Ansbach.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. It is the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">administrative region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mittelfranken" class="mw-redirect" title="Mittelfranken">Middle Franconia</a>. Ansbach is 25 miles (40&#160;km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> and 90 miles (140&#160;km) north of <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A4nkische_Rezat" class="mw-redirect" title="Fränkische Rezat">Fränkische Rezat</a> (Rezat River), a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> river. In 2004, its population was 40,723.</p>
<p>Developed in the 8th century as a <a href="/wiki/Benedictine_monastery" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine monastery">Benedictine monastery</a>, it became the seat of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a> family in 1331. In 1460, the Margraves of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Margraviate_of_Brandenburg%E2%80%93Ansbach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg–Ansbach (page does not exist)">Brandenburg-Ansbach</a> lived here. The city has a castle known as <a href="/wiki/Markgrafenschlo%C3%9F" class="mw-redirect" title="Markgrafenschloß">Margrafen–Schloss</a>, built between 1704–1738. It was not badly damaged during the <a href="/wiki/World_Wars" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wars">World Wars</a> and hence retains its original historical baroque sheen. Ansbach is now home to a US military base and to the <a href="/wiki/Ansbach_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Ansbach University of Applied Sciences">Ansbach University of Applied Sciences</a>.</p>
<p>The city has connections via autobahn <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_6" title="Bundesautobahn 6">A6</a> and highways <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_13&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bundesstraße 13 (page does not exist)">B13</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_14" title="Bundesstraße 14">B14</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ansbach_station" title="Ansbach station">Ansbach station</a> is on the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Crailsheim_railway" title="Nuremberg–Crailsheim railway">Nürnberg–Crailsheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treuchtlingen%E2%80%93W%C3%BCrzburg_railway" title="Treuchtlingen–Würzburg railway">Treuchtlingen–Würzburg</a> railways and is the terminus of line S4 of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_S-Bahn" title="Nuremberg S-Bahn">Nuremberg S-Bahn</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_origin">Name origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Ansbach</b> was originally called <b>Onoltesbach</b> (about 790 AD), a term composed of three parts.</p>
<p>The individual word elements are "Onold" (the city founder's name), the <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">Suffix</a> "-es" (a possessive ending, like "-'s" in English) and the <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> expression "pah" or "bach" (for <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">brook</a>). The name of the city has slightly changed throughout the centuries into <b>Onoltespah</b> (837 AD), <b>Onoldesbach</b> (1141 AD), <b>Onoldsbach</b> (1230 AD), <b>Onelspach</b> (1338 AD), <b>Onsbach</b> (1508 AD) and finally <b>Ansbach</b> (1732 AD).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was also formerly known as <b>Anspach</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to folklore, towards the end of the 7th century a group of Franconian peasants and their families went up into the wilderness to found a new settlement. Their leader Onold led them to an area called the "Rezattal" (Rezat valley). This is where they founded the "Urhöfe" (meaning the first farms: Knollenhof, Voggenhof and Rabenhof). Gradually more settlers, such as the "Winden-Tribe" came, and the farms grew into a small village. Many villages around Ansbach were founded by the "Winden" during that period (even today their settlements can easily identified by their names, like "Meinhardsz<b>winden</b>", "Dauten<b>winden</b>" or "Brods<b>winden</b>" for example). A <a href="/wiki/Benedictine_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine Order">Benedictine monastery</a> was established there around 748 by the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> noble <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gumbertus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gumbertus (page does not exist)">St&#160;Gumbertus</a>. The adjoining village of Onoltesbach is first noticed as a proper town in 1221.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The counts of <a href="/wiki/County_of_%C3%96ttingen" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Öttingen">Öttingen</a> ruled over Ansbach until the <a href="/wiki/Hohenzollern" class="mw-redirect" title="Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a> burgrave of <a href="/wiki/Burgraviate_of_N%C3%BCrnberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Burgraviate of Nürnberg">Nürnberg</a> took over in 1331. The Hohenzollerns made Ansbach the seat of their dynasty until their acquisition of the <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Margraviate of Brandenburg</a> in 1415. After the 1440 death of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Elector_of_Brandenburg" title="Frederick I, Elector of Brandenburg">Frederick&#160;I</a>, a cadet branch of the family established itself as the <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Ansbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Ansbach">margraves of Ansbach</a>. <a href="/wiki/George,_Margrave_of_Brandenburg-Ansbach" title="George, Margrave of Brandenburg-Ansbach">George the Pious</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> to Ansbach in 1528, leading to the secularization of <a href="/wiki/St._Gumbertus,_Ansbach" title="St. Gumbertus, Ansbach">Gumbertus Abbey</a> in 1563.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Markgrafenschlo%C3%9F" class="mw-redirect" title="Markgrafenschloß">Markgrafenschloß</a> was built between 1704–1738.<sup id="cite_ref-Spaltro2005_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spaltro2005-6">[6]</a></sup> Its gardens continued to be a notable attraction into the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1791, the last margrave sold his realm to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1796, the Duke of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Zweibr%C3%BCcken" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Zweibrücken">Zweibrücken</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I_Joseph_of_Bavaria" title="Maximilian I Joseph of Bavaria">Maximilian Joseph</a> — the future Bavarian king Max I Joseph — was exiled to Ansbach after Zweibrücken had been taken by the French. In Ansbach, <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_von_Montgelas" title="Maximilian von Montgelas">Maximilian von Montgelas</a> wrote an elaborate concept for the future political organization of Bavaria, which is known as the Ansbacher Mémoire.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Napoleon forced Prussia to cede Ansbach and its principality to <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavaria</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-7">[7]</a></sup> in the Franco-Prussian treaty of alliance signed at <a href="/wiki/Sch%C3%B6nbrunn_Palace" title="Schönbrunn Palace">Schönbrunn Palace</a> on 15 December 1805 at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Third_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Coalition">Third Coalition</a>. The act was confirmed by the 1815 <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-7">[7]</a></sup> Prussia was compensated with the Bavarian <a href="/wiki/Berg_(German_region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Berg (German region)">duchy of Berg</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ansbach became the capital of the circle of Middle Franconia following the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a>; at the time, it had a population of 12,635.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jewish families were resident in Ansbach from at least the end of the 18th century. They set up a Jewish Cemetery in the Ruglaender Strasse, which was vandalised and razed under the Nazi regime in the <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>. It was repaired in 1946, but it was damaged several times more. A plaque on the wall of the cemetery commemorates these events. The Jewish Congregation built its synagogue at No 3 Rosenbadstrasse, but it too was damaged by the SA, though it was not burnt down for fear of damaging the neighbouring buildings. It serves today as a "Symbolic House of God". A plaque in the entrance serves as a memorial to the synagogue and to Jewish residents who were murdered during the Holocaust.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="German Wikipedia is not a reliable source (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1940, at least 500 patients were deported from the Heil- und Pflegeanstalt Ansbach [<i>Ansbach Medical and Nursing Clinic</i>] to the extermination facilities Sonnenstein and Hartheim which were disguised as psychiatric institutions, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Action_T4" class="mw-redirect" title="Action T4">Action T4</a> euthanasia action. They were gassed there. At the clinic in Ansbach itself, around 50 intellectually disabled children were injected with the drug Luminal and killed that way. A plaque was erected in their memory in 1988 in the local hospital at No. 38 Feuchtwangerstrasse.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="German Wikipedia is not a reliable source (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, a subcamp of <a href="/wiki/Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp" title="Flossenbürg concentration camp">Flossenbürg concentration camp</a> was located here.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Also during the Second World War the Luftwaffe and Wehrmacht had bases here. The nearby airbase was the home station for the Stab &amp; I/KG53 (Staff &amp; 1st Group of Kampfgeschwader 53) operating 38 <a href="/wiki/Heinkel_He_111" title="Heinkel He 111">Heinkel He 111</a> bombers. On 1 September 1939 this unit was one of the many that participated in the <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland_(1939)" class="mw-redirect" title="Invasion of Poland (1939)">attack on Poland</a> that started the war. All of its bridges were destroyed during the course of the war. During the <a href="/wiki/Western_Allied_invasion_of_Germany" title="Western Allied invasion of Germany">Western Allied invasion of Germany</a> in April 1945, the airfield was seized by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Third_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Third Army">United States Third Army</a>, and used by the <a href="/wiki/USAAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAAF">USAAF</a> <a href="/wiki/354th_Fighter_Wing" title="354th Fighter Wing">354th Fighter Group</a> which flew <a href="/wiki/P-47_Thunderbolts" class="mw-redirect" title="P-47 Thunderbolts">P-47 Thunderbolts</a> from the aerodrome (designated <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Landing_Ground" title="Advanced Landing Ground">ALG R-82</a>) from late April until the German capitulation on 7 May 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> At the end of the war, 19-year-old student Robert Limpert tried to get the town to surrender to the US Forces without a fight. He was betrayed by <a href="/wiki/Hitler_Youth" title="Hitler Youth">Hitler Youth</a> and was hung from the portal of the City Hall by the city's military commander, Col. (<i>Oberst</i>) Ernst Meyer. Several memorials to his heroic deed have been erected over the years, despite opposition from some residents — in the Ludwigskirche, in the Gymnasium Carolinum and at No 6 Kronenstrasse.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="German Wikipedia is not a reliable source (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After the Second World War, Ansbach belonged to the American Zone. The American Military authorities established a displaced persons (DP) camp in what used to be a sanatorium in what is today the Strüth quarter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="German Wikipedia is not a reliable source (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bachwoche_Ansbach" title="Bachwoche Ansbach">Bachwoche Ansbach</a> has been held in Ansbach since 1947. Since 1970, Ansbach has enlarged its municipal area by incorporating adjacent communities. Ansbach hosts several units of the U.S. armed forces, associated with German units under <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>. There are five separate U.S. installations: Shipton Kaserne, home to <a href="/wiki/412th_Aviation_Support_Battalion" title="412th Aviation Support Battalion">412th Aviation Support Battalion</a>, Katterbach Kaserne, formerly the home of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Infantry Division (United States)">1st Infantry Division's</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=4th_Combat_Aviation_Brigade&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="4th Combat Aviation Brigade (page does not exist)">4th Combat Aviation Brigade</a>, which has been replaced by the <a href="/wiki/12th_Combat_Aviation_Brigade_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="12th Combat Aviation Brigade (United States)">12th Combat Aviation Brigade</a> as of 2006, as part of the 1st Infantry Division's return to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Riley" title="Fort Riley">Fort Riley</a>, Kansas; Bismarck Kaserne, which functions as a satellite post to Katterbach, hosting their Post Theater, barracks, Von Steuben Community Center, Military Police, and other support agencies, Barton Barracks, home to the USAG Ansbach and Bleidorn Barracks, which has a library and housing, and Urlas, which hosts the <a href="/wiki/Post_Exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Post Exchange">Post Exchange</a> as well as a housing area opened in 2010. Ansbach was also home to the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Armored Division (United States)">1st Armored Division (United States)</a> from 1972 to the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 July 2016 <a href="/wiki/2016_Ansbach_bombing" title="2016 Ansbach bombing">a bomb was detonated</a> in a restaurant in the city, killing at least one person and injuring multiple people. The perpetrator was reported to be a Syrian refugee whose asylum application had been rejected but who had been given exceptional leave to remain until the security situation in Syria returned to a safe condition. Witnesses reported he had tried to enter a nearby music festival but had been turned away, before detonating his device outside a nearby wine bar.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Around the time of the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a> in 1871, the chief manufactures of Ansbach were <a href="/wiki/Woolens" class="mw-redirect" title="Woolens">woollen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Textiles">cotton</a>, and half-<a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> goods; <a href="/wiki/Earthenware" title="Earthenware">earthenware</a>; <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>; <a href="/wiki/Cutlery" title="Cutlery">cutlery</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Playing_cards" class="mw-redirect" title="Playing cards">playing cards</a>. A considerable trade in grain, <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a> was also supported.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1878_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1878-7">[7]</a></sup> By the onset of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, it also produced <a href="/wiki/Machinery" class="mw-redirect" title="Machinery">machinery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toy" title="Toy">toys</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Embroidery" title="Embroidery">embroidery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEB1911_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEB1911-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today there is a large density of <a href="/wiki/Plastics_industry" title="Plastics industry">plastics industry</a> in the City and rural districts around Ansbach.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Margrave_of_Brandenburg-Ansbach" title="Frederick I, Margrave of Brandenburg-Ansbach">Frederick I, Margrave of Brandenburg-Ansbach</a> (1460–1536).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Albert_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Albert of Prussia">Albert of Prussia</a> (1490–1568), Grand Master of the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Order" title="Teutonic Order">Teutonic Order</a> and the first duke of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Prussia" title="Duchy of Prussia">Prussia</a>.</li>
<li>In the late 16th century, the physician to margrave Georg Friedrich was the famous botanist, <a href="/wiki/Leonhart_Fuchs" title="Leonhart Fuchs">Leonhart Fuchs</a> (1501–1566).</li>
<li>Ansbach was home of the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Simon_Marius" title="Simon Marius">Simon Marius</a> (1573–1625), who observed <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>'s moons from the castle's tower. Later he claimed to be the discoverer of the moons, which led to a dispute with the true discoverer, <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Johann_Peter_Uz" class="mw-redirect" title="Johann Peter Uz">Johann Peter Uz</a> (1720–1796), poet.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/August_von_Platen-Hallerm%C3%BCnde" title="August von Platen-Hallermünde">August von Platen-Hallermünde</a>, poet.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Georges_Oberhaeuser" title="Georges Oberhaeuser">Georges Oberhaeuser</a> (1798–1868), optician.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kaspar_Hauser" title="Kaspar Hauser">Kaspar Hauser</a> (1812–1833), lived in Ansbach from 1830 to 1833. He was stabbed (possibly self-inflicted) in the palace gardens.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pinchas_Kohn" title="Pinchas Kohn">Pinchas Kohn</a> (1867–1941), was the last rabbi of Ansbach. He was the rabbinical advisor to the German occupying forces of Poland in the First World War and was also one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/World_Agudath_Israel" title="World Agudath Israel">World Agudath Israel</a> movement.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Max_Westenh%C3%B6fer" title="Max Westenhöfer">Max Westenhöfer</a> (1871–1957), pathologist, professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Berlin" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chile" title="University of Chile">University of Chile</a>. Proposed the <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ape_hypothesis" title="Aquatic ape hypothesis">Aquatic ape hypothesis</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Herbert_Blendinger" title="Herbert Blendinger">Herbert Blendinger</a>, Violinist and composer was born in Ansbach in 1936.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matthias_Buchinger" title="Matthias Buchinger">Matthias Buchinger</a> (1674–1740), a German artist, magician and illustrator, born without hands or legs.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the novel <i>The Schirmer Inheritance</i> (1953) by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Ambler" title="Eric Ambler">Eric Ambler</a> (1909–1998), Sergeant Franz Schirmer of the Ansbach Dragoons is wounded in the battle of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Eylau" title="Battle of Eylau">Preussisch-Eylau</a> in 1807. He returns to Ansbach to settle but changes his name as he has been posted as a deserter. The bulk of the novel concerns efforts by an American law firm to trace his descendants to claim an inheritance.</p>

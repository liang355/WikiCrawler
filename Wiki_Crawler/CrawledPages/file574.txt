<p><b>Harvard Business School</b> (<b>HBS</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate</a> <a href="/wiki/Business_school" title="Business school">business school</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, United States. The school offers a large full-time <a href="/wiki/MBA" class="mw-redirect" title="MBA">MBA</a> program, doctoral programs, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Business X">HBX</a> and many executive education programs. It owns <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_Publishing" title="Harvard Business Publishing">Harvard Business Publishing</a></i>, which publishes business books, leadership articles, online management tools for corporate learning, <a href="/wiki/Case_method" title="Case method">case studies</a> and the monthly <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review" title="Harvard Business Review">Harvard Business Review</a></i>. It is home to the <a href="/wiki/Baker_Library/Bloomberg_Center" title="Baker Library/Bloomberg Center">Baker Library/Bloomberg Center</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Business_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The school started in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-businessinsiderfeloni25mostsuccessful_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessinsiderfeloni25mostsuccessful-2">[2]</a></sup> Initially established by the humanities faculty, it received independent status in 1910, and became a separate administrative unit in 1913. The first dean was historian <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Francis_Gay" title="Edwin Francis Gay">Edwin Francis Gay</a> (1867–1946).<sup id="cite_ref-obiteconomichistoryreview_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obiteconomichistoryreview-3">[3]</a></sup> Yogev (2001) explains the original concept:</p>
<dl>
<dd>This school of business and public administration was originally conceived as a school for diplomacy and government service on the model of the French <i>Ecole des Sciences Politiques.</i> The goal was an institution of higher learning that would offer a master of arts degree in the humanities field, with a major in business. In discussions about the curriculum, the suggestion was made to concentrate on specific business topics such as banking, railroads, and so on... Professor Lowell said the school would train qualified public administrators whom the government would have no choice but to employ, thereby building a better public administration... Harvard was blazing a new trail by educating young people for a career in business, just as its medical school trained doctors and its law faculty trained lawyers.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>From the start the school enjoyed a close relationship with the corporate world. Within a few years of its founding many business leaders were its alumni and were hiring other alumni for starting positions in their firms.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At its founding, the school accepted only male students. The <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Radcliffe_Program_in_Business_Administration" title="Harvard-Radcliffe Program in Business Administration">Training Course in Personnel Administration</a>, founded at <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_College" title="Radcliffe College">Radcliffe College</a> in 1937, was the beginning of business training for women at Harvard. HBS took over administration of that program from Radcliffe in 1954. In 1959, alumnae of the one-year program (by then known as the Harvard-Radcliffe Program in Business Administration) were permitted to apply to join the HBS MBA program as second-years. In December 1962, the faculty voted to allow women to enter the MBA program directly. The first women to apply directly to the MBA program matriculated in September 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-BuildingFoundation_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BuildingFoundation-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012-2013, HBS deans tried to improve the experience of female students and recruit more female professors.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimeskantorhbscasestudygender_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimeskantorhbscasestudygender-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Research_Centers">International Research Centers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Business_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International Research Centers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>HBS established nine global research centers and four regional offices<sup id="cite_ref-HBS_GIRC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBS_GIRC-10">[10]</a></sup> and functions through offices in Asia Pacific (Hong Kong, Shanghai, Singapore), California, Europe (Paris), South Asia (India),<sup id="cite_ref-HBS_India_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBS_India-11">[11]</a></sup> Middle East and North Africa (Dubai, Istanbul, Tel Aviv), Japan and Latin America (Buenos Aires, Mexico City, São Paulo).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Business_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Students can join one or more of the more than 80 clubs on campus. The Student Association (SA) is the main interface between the MBA student body and the faculty/administration.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="SVMP">SVMP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Business_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: SVMP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Summer Venture in Management Program (SVMP) is a one-week management training program for rising college seniors designed to increase diversity and opportunity in business education. Participants must be employed in a summer internship and be nominated by and have sponsorship from their company or organization to attend.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="HBX">HBX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Business_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: HBX">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>HBX, is an online learning initiative announced by the Harvard Business School in March 2014 to host online university-level courses. Initial programs are the Credential of Readiness (CORe) and Disruptive Strategy with <a href="/wiki/Clayton_M._Christensen" title="Clayton M. Christensen">Clayton Christensen</a>. Leading with Finance was added to the catalog in August 2016. HBX also created HBX Live, a virtual classroom based at WGBH in Boston. Duration of HBX Core course takes from 12 to 18 weeks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Precise_language" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated.">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_units">Academic units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Business_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Academic units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The school's faculty are divided into ten academic units: Accounting and Management; Business, Government and the International Economy; Entrepreneurial Management; Finance; General Management; Marketing; Negotiation, Organizations &amp; Markets; Organizational Behavior; Strategy; and Technology and Operations Management, business, etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Donor_programs">Donor programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Business_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Donor programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the fall of 2010, HBS <a href="/wiki/Tata_Group" title="Tata Group">Tata related companies</a> and charities donated $50 million for the construction of an executive center.<sup id="cite_ref-HBS_TH_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBS_TH-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The executive center have been named as Tata Hall, after <a href="/wiki/Ratan_Tata" title="Ratan Tata">Ratan Tata</a> (AMP in 1975), the chairman of <a href="/wiki/Tata_Sons" title="Tata Sons">Tata Sons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tata_Hall_HBS_Named_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tata_Hall_HBS_Named-31">[31]</a></sup> The total construction costs have been estimated at $100 million.<sup id="cite_ref-HBS_100M_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBS_100M-32">[32]</a></sup> The Tata Hall is located in the northeast corner of the HBS campus, the facility is devoted to the Harvard Business School's mid-career Executive Education program. It is seven stories tall with about 150,000 gross square feet. It houses approximately 180 bedrooms in addition to academic and multi-purpose spaces.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kresge Way now is located by the base of the former Kresge Hall, named for <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_S._Kresge" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebastian S. Kresge">Sebastian S. Kresge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In 2014, Kresge Hall was replaced by a new hall funded by a US$30 million donation by the family of the late <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Mulan_Chu_Chao" title="Ruth Mulan Chu Chao">Ruth Mulan Chu Chao</a>, whose four daughters all attended Harvard Business School.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The Executive Education quad currently includes McArthur, Baker, and Mellon Halls (residence), McCollum and Hawes (classroom), Chao Center, and Glass (administration).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

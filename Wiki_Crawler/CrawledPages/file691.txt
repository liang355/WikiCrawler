			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">2012 Harvard cheating scandal</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>2012 Harvard cheating scandal</b> involved approximately 125 <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> students who were investigated for <a href="/wiki/Cheating" title="Cheating">cheating</a> on the take-home <a href="/wiki/Final_examination" title="Final examination">final examination</a> of the spring 2012 edition of Government 1310: "Introduction to <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a>". Harvard announced the investigation publicly on August 30, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-gazette_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gazette-1">[1]</a></sup> Dean of Undergraduate Education Jay M. Harris described the case as "unprecedented in its scope and magnitude".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Harvard_Crimson" title="The Harvard Crimson">The Harvard Crimson</a></i> ranked the scandal as the news story most important to Harvard in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Teaching_fellow" title="Teaching fellow">teaching fellow</a> noticed similarities between a small number of exams during grading in May 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-gazette_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gazette-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boston_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-6">[6]</a></sup> The course's professor brought the case to the Harvard College Administrative Board, which reviewed all final exams, leading to individual cases against nearly half of the 279 students enrolled in the class, almost two percent of the <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a> student body.<sup id="cite_ref-gazette_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gazette-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boston_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-6">[6]</a></sup> The administrative board completed its investigation in December 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-complete_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-complete-7">[7]</a></sup> On February 1, 2013 Harvard revealed that "somewhat more than half" of the investigated students, estimated at 70%, were forced to withdraw.<sup id="cite_ref-boston-half_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-half-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nytimes-return_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-return-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span id="Government_1310:_&quot;Introduction_to_Congress&quot;"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_1310:_.22Introduction_to_Congress.22">Government 1310: "Introduction to Congress"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Government 1310: &quot;Introduction to Congress&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Government 1310: "Introduction to Congress" was led by assistant professor Matthew B. Platt in Spring 2010, 2011, and 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-harder_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-harder-10">[10]</a></sup> The course was offered to students of Harvard College and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Extension_School" title="Harvard Extension School">Harvard Extension School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It developed a reputation as an easy course, receiving a high proportion of "easy" or "very easy" ratings in the <i>Q Guide</i>, Harvard's collection of <a href="/wiki/Course_evaluation" title="Course evaluation">course evaluations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-harder_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-harder-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> According to some Spring 2012 students, Platt immediately confirmed this reputation by promising 120 <a href="/wiki/A_(grade)" class="mw-redirect" title="A (grade)">A</a>'s and stating that attendance was optional.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-salon_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-businessweek_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-globe-concerns_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-concerns-17">[17]</a></sup> Students who attended could share their notes.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-concerns_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-concerns-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Grades were determined by four take-home exams.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-harder_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-harder-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2010 and 2011, the take-home exams were essays, but in 2012 they were changed to a short answer format.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-harder_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-harder-10">[10]</a></sup> The change corresponded with a spike in difficulty and a drop in overall score, according to the <i>Q Guide</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-harder_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-harder-10">[10]</a></sup> Students said the short answer format facilitated collaboration.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-harder_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-harder-10">[10]</a></sup> Some guessed that the changes were forced from above.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes-return_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-return-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spring_2012_final_exam">Spring 2012 final exam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spring 2012 final exam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The spring 2012 final exam was assigned April 26 and due May 3 at 5:00&#160;p.m.<sup id="cite_ref-finalexam_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finalexam-18">[18]</a></sup> Its first page contained the instructions: "The exam is completely open book, open note, open internet, etc. However, in all other regards, this should fall under similar guidelines that apply to in-class exams. More specifically, students may not discuss the exam with others—this includes resident tutors, writing centers, etc."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-finalexam_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finalexam-18">[18]</a></sup> The use of <i><a href="/wiki/Etc." class="mw-redirect" title="Etc.">etc.</a></i> has been questioned.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Students complained about confusing questions on the final exam.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-harder_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-harder-10">[10]</a></sup> Due to "some good questions" from students, Platt clarified three exam questions by email on April 30.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-harder_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-harder-10">[10]</a></sup> Platt cancelled his <a href="/wiki/Office_hours" class="mw-redirect" title="Office hours">office hours</a> on the May 3 due date on short notice.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup> Many students received assistance from the teaching fellows.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nytimes-return_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-return-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-globe-concerns_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-concerns-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Spring_2013_–_present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Spring_2013_.E2.80.93_present">Spring 2013 – present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spring 2013 – present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Government 1310 had its spring 2013 Harvard College course listing removed as of October 7, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Platt taught the course through the Harvard Extension School only for spring 2013 and spring 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Grading was based on two essay exams, ten quizzes and the final.<sup id="cite_ref-2013-syllabus_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013-syllabus-24">[24]</a></sup> The collaboration policy forbade any collaboration.<sup id="cite_ref-2013-syllabus_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013-syllabus-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigation">Investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A teaching fellow noticed similarities between a small number of exams during grading in May 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-gazette_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gazette-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boston_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-6">[6]</a></sup> Platt reported the suspected <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">plagiarism</a> in a letter to administrative board secretary John "Jay" L. Ellison on May 14.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-platt-letter_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-platt-letter-25">[25]</a></sup> The similarities were first noticed in answers for the bonus short answer question "Describe two developments in the history of Congress that ostensibly gave individual MCs [members of Congress] in the House greater freedom and/or control but ultimately centralized power in the hands of party leadership."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-platt-letter_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-platt-letter-25">[25]</a></sup> Some students picked the "somewhat obscure" pair of the <a href="/wiki/Cannon_Revolt_of_1910" class="mw-redirect" title="Cannon Revolt of 1910">Cannon Revolt of 1910</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> and "all the answers use the same (incorrect) reading of the course material in arguments that are identically structured."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-platt-letter_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-platt-letter-25">[25]</a></sup> Additional comparisons revealed possible collaboration on the other questions.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-platt-letter_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-platt-letter-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The administrative board reviewed all exams over the summer and flagged roughly 125 for suspected collaboration.<sup id="cite_ref-gazette_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gazette-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup> The suspects constitute nearly half of the 279 students enrolled in the class and almost two percent of Harvard College's <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a> student body.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boston_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-6">[6]</a></sup> Harris said the similarities include "answers that look quite alike to answers that appear to have been lifted in their entirety."<sup id="cite_ref-harvardmagazine_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvardmagazine-26">[26]</a></sup> Harvard enlisted "supplemental fact finders" to deal with the load.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-Administration_Gov1310_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-Administration_Gov1310-27">[27]</a></sup> Peter F. Lake, a <a href="/wiki/Stetson_University_College_of_Law" title="Stetson University College of Law">Stetson University College of Law</a> professor approximated fifty hours per student totaling "essentially one administrator’s entire year of energy."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-Administration_Gov1310_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-Administration_Gov1310-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harvard announced the investigation publicly on August 30, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-gazette_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gazette-1">[1]</a></sup> Dean of undergraduate education <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jay_M._Harris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jay M. Harris (page does not exist)">Jay M. Harris</a> justified the announcement as a springboard to raise awareness and a <a href="/wiki/Teachable_moment" title="Teachable moment">teachable moment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-accused_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-accused-28">[28]</a></sup> A senior under investigation dismissed this explanation, writing, "Harvard chose to go public with this story to first and foremost save their own asses."<sup id="cite_ref-salon_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the same day as the announcement, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Faculty_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences">Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences</a> dean <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Smith_(computer_scientist)" title="Michael D. Smith (computer scientist)">Michael D. Smith</a> sent an email to all faculty members informing them of the investigation and suggesting that they clarify their collaboration policies.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvardmagazine_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvardmagazine-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-shopping_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-shopping-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wsj_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each student was given copies of their exams and similar ones, then had to submit a written explanation.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-forced_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-forced-31">[31]</a></sup> The student met with an administrative board subcommittee and was shown the other students' statements.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-forced_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-forced-31">[31]</a></sup> Some students named the classmates they collaborated with.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-forced_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-forced-31">[31]</a></sup> The subcommittee recommended an action to the full board.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-forced_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-forced-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cases were finalized in September and December 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-forced_31-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-forced-31">[31]</a></sup> Students forced to withdraw vanished from campus as verdicts were issued every Tuesday.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-concerns_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-concerns-17">[17]</a></sup> The administrative board completed its investigation in December 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-complete_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-complete-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discipline">Discipline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discipline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Potential discipline for <a href="/wiki/Academic_dishonesty" title="Academic dishonesty">academic dishonesty</a> includes a year's forced withdrawal.<sup id="cite_ref-gazette_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gazette-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-reforms_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-reforms-32">[32]</a></sup> In May 2010 the administrative board gained the ability to "exclude" students and fail them.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-reforms_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-reforms-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/IvyGate" title="IvyGate">IvyGate</a> published rumors that the administrative board developed a "tiered punishment scheme" based on general classes of collaboration but Faculty of Arts and Sciences spokesperson Jeff Neal stated that each case would be evaluated individually.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Students could withdraw voluntarily before their cases are closed, according to an email sent by Ellison to resident deans.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-accused_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-accused-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> If convicted and sentenced to a required withdrawal, the leave of absence would be credited as <a href="/wiki/Time_served" title="Time served">time served</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-accused_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-accused-28">[28]</a></sup> A forced withdrawal includes a permanent notation on the student's transcript.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-ad-board-scrutiny_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-ad-board-scrutiny-36">[36]</a></sup> Harvard biology professor <a href="/wiki/Richard_Losick" title="Richard Losick">Richard Losick</a> calls the note "a severe punishment" and one suspected student describes it as "almost the kiss of death in the academic realm."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-ad-board-scrutiny_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-ad-board-scrutiny-36">[36]</a></sup> During their absence, students must "hold a full-time, paid, non-academic job in a non-family situation, for at least six consecutive months" before becoming eligible for readmittance.<sup id="cite_ref-herald-fair_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-fair-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 1, 2013, Smith revealed that "somewhat more than half" of the students were forced to withdraw "for a period of time" and "roughly half" of the remainder were put on <a href="/wiki/Disciplinary_probation" title="Disciplinary probation">disciplinary probation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-boston-half_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-half-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Smith's e-mail covers all administrative board cases for the past term without mentioning Government 1310 or the scandal, but a Harvard official said they were from one course.<sup id="cite_ref-boston-half_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-half-8">[8]</a></sup> For the majority of cases, the "period of time" is two semesters according to students.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-details_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-details-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletes">Athletes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Athletes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

to Harvard."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-athletes_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-athletes-41">[41]</a></sup> According to estimates by students, over half the class and up to half of <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Students">Students</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Students">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Students claimed that collaboration like note-sharing and consulting <a href="/wiki/Teaching_fellow" title="Teaching fellow">teaching fellows</a> was widespread.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-businessweek_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-16">[16]</a></sup> One student produced shared lecture notes and argued successfully that they were the source.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes-forced_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-forced-58">[58]</a></sup> Students under investigation complained about uncertainty stemming from the length of the investigation and the Administrative Board's hiatus over the summer.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-accused_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-accused-28">[28]</a></sup> One 2012 graduate now working on <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> told <i><a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_Businessweek" title="Bloomberg Businessweek">Bloomberg Businessweek</a></i> "Dragging us into this investigation now, when we have financial obligations and jobs, seems very unfair."<sup id="cite_ref-businessweek_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-16">[16]</a></sup> Current students expressed concerns about <a href="/wiki/Pro-rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pro-rate">pro-rated</a> tuition costs where students forced to withdraw later would pay more.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Some students did not receive their verdicts until shortly before finals.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-concerns_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-concerns-17">[17]</a></sup> In the interest of "financial equity," Harvard calculated tuition refunds for all required withdrawals based on September 30, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-boston-half_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston-half-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Extension_School" title="Harvard Extension School">Harvard Extension School</a> students wrote to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/GovLoop" title="GovLoop">GovLoop</a> to debunk claims that Platt encouraged collaborating on exams.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of students responded to Harvard's announcement by going to the media themselves and "trying to present the other side" of the story.<sup id="cite_ref-businessweek_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-16">[16]</a></sup> A senior reached out to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Observer" class="mw-redirect" title="The New York Observer">The New York Observer</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Salon_(website)" title="Salon (website)">Salon</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-salon_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> He told <i>Salon</i> "we’re being scapegoated" and that many students are ready to file <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuits</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-salon_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-15">[15]</a></sup> One student who had contacted a lawyer told <i>The Harvard Crimson</i> "Harvard has created this war between the students and the fricking school, and this is a war that I am willing and very eager to fight."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-lawsuits_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-lawsuits-63">[63]</a></sup> Experts said that Harvard can be sued for procedural errors, lost employment opportunities or <a href="/wiki/Intentional_infliction_of_emotional_distress" title="Intentional infliction of emotional distress">intentional</a> or <a href="/wiki/Negligent_infliction_of_emotional_distress" title="Negligent infliction of emotional distress">negligent infliction of emotional distress</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-lawsuits_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-lawsuits-63">[63]</a></sup> After the final announcement, lawyers said that lawsuits would be difficult and unlikely.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-lawsuits-possible_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-lawsuits-possible-64">[64]</a></sup> Harvey A. Silverglate said "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Schaer_vs._Brandeis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schaer vs. Brandeis (page does not exist)">Schaer vs. Brandeis</a> makes Harvard very close to invulnerable."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-lawsuits-possible_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-lawsuits-possible-64">[64]</a></sup> One lawyer said students may be waiting to graduate and avoid retribution.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-lawsuits-possible_64-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-lawsuits-possible-64">[64]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_limitations" title="Statute of limitations">statute of limitations</a> for <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">breach of contract</a> is six years in Massachusetts.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-lawsuits-possible_64-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-lawsuits-possible-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Implicated students and parents were disappointed by Smith's e-mail and blamed Platt.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-details_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-details-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Other students thought the punishment was fair.<sup id="cite_ref-herald-fair_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-fair-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-globe-details_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-details-40">[40]</a></sup> Robert Peabody, an attorney for two students said the process was too slow and calling it "<a href="/wiki/Slow_slicing" class="mw-redirect" title="Slow slicing">death by a thousand nicks</a>", "living <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a>", "basically hell" and "twisting in the wind."<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-forced_31-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-forced-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> He said his clients "emotionally deteriorated over the course of the semester.”<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes-forced_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-forced-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Harvard Crimson'</i>s survey of graduating seniors of the class of 2013 estimated 32.0% cheating on "papers or take-home tests" but self-reported 7.0%.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvard">Harvard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Harvard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Honor_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Honor code">honor code</a> was drafted by Harvard’s Committee on Academic Integrity and adopted on May 6, 2014 by the Faculty of Arts and Sciences by "overwhelmingly" positive vote.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Colin_Diver" title="Colin Diver">Colin Diver</a>, former president of <a href="/wiki/Reed_College" title="Reed College">Reed College</a> writes that an "Honor Principle" must be the basis of a culture of <a href="/wiki/Academic_integrity" title="Academic integrity">academic integrity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Some professors defended the take-home exam format.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-takehome_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-takehome-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Erika Christakis and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_A._Christakis" title="Nicholas A. Christakis">Nicholas A. Christakis</a> write that there is a "national crisis of academic dishonesty."<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harry_R._Lewis" title="Harry R. Lewis">Harry R. Lewis</a> entreats Harvard to "<a href="/wiki/Know_thyself" title="Know thyself">Know Thyself</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Howard_Gardner" title="Howard Gardner">Howard Gardner</a> writes that the case exposes "ethical rot" at Harvard.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Gardner contemplates the scandal "as a <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">play</a> in four <a href="/wiki/Act_(drama)" title="Act (drama)">acts</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>Faust, Smith and Harris made statements regarding the investigation at the first Faculty of Arts and Sciences meeting of Fall 2012 on October 2.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Faust also granted an interview with <i>The Harvard Crimson</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-faust_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-faust-76">[76]</a></sup> She refused comment on <a href="/wiki/Student_athlete" title="Student athlete">student athletes</a> saying the investigation "includes a wide spectrum of students."<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-faust_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-faust-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>The instructors of Harvard's Expository Writing Program are using the scandal to accentuate the anti-plagiarism principles they have already been teaching.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>

to <i><a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_News" title="Bloomberg News">Bloomberg News</a></i> "Those students who cut and pasted exam answers deserved to get kicked out. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commentary">Commentary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Commentary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Editorial" title="Editorial">Editorials</a> by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Harvard_Crimson" title="The Harvard Crimson">The Harvard Crimson</a></i> call out widespread confusion over the acceptability of collaboration as the scandal's root cause<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> and focus on <a href="/wiki/Extracurricular_activity" title="The Cavalier Daily">The Cavalier Daily</a></i> blames the "poorly worded exam instructions" and the phrase "open Internet".<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> According to an editorial by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i>, the cheating exposes a lack of quality in Government 1310.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> An editorial by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Christian_Science_Monitor" title="The Christian Science class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Farhad_Manjoo" title="Farhad Manjoo">Farhad Manjoo</a>, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Slate_(magazine)" title="Slate <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yale">Yale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Yale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Yale" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale">Yale</a> Freshman Class Council designed a shirt for the <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Yale_football_games_(The_Game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Yale football games (The Game)">Harvard-Yale football game</a> replacing "<a href="/wiki/Veritas" title="Veritas">Veritas</a>" in the Harvard logo with "Cheaters" or "Cheatas" but the Yale Licensing Office rejected the design.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-yale-shirt_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yale-shirt-101">[101]</a></sup> The approved shirt has "Try cheating your way out of this one" on its back.<sup id="cite_ref-yale-shirt_101-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yale-shirt-101">[101]</a></sup> Harvard won the game.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_Board">Administrative Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Administrative Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 23–26, 2012 <i>The Harvard Crimson</i> published a four-part series about the Administrative Board and the changes made in 2009–2010.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-resident-deans_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-resident-deans-106">[106]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> <i>The Harvard Crimson</i> followed up with a critical editorial.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-adboard-penal_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-adboard-penal-109">[109]</a></sup> One criticism is resident deans are normally trusted advisors but communications with them are not protected by <a href="/wiki/Privilege_(evidence)" title="Privilege (evidence)">privilege</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-concerns_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-concerns-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-resident-deans_106-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-resident-deans-106">[106]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-adboard-penal_109-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-adboard-penal-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collaboration_policies">Collaboration policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Collaboration policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On August 30, 2012 the same day as the announcement, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Faculty_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences">Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences</a> Dean <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Smith_(computer_scientist)" title="Michael D. Smith (computer scientist)">Michael D. Smith</a> sent an email to all faculty members informing them of the investigation and suggesting that they clarify their collaboration policies.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson1_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvardmagazine_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvardmagazine-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crimson-shopping_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-shopping-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wsj_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harris reminded instructors that course syllabi must contain explicit collaboration policies before the Spring 2013 semester.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-collab_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-collab-110">[110]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Some classes also went over their policies in their first lecture.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-collab_110-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-collab-110">[110]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Search_of_deans'_email_accounts"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_of_deans.27_email_accounts">Search of deans' email accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Harvard_cheating_scandal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Search of deans' email accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On March 9, 2013 <i>The Boston Globe</i> reported that Harvard searched 16 resident deans' email accounts seeking the source of a leaked email shortly after the scandal broke.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak-112">[112]</a></sup> On March 11 Smith and <a href="/wiki/Evelynn_Hammonds" class="mw-redirect" title="Evelynn Hammonds">Evelynn Hammonds</a> confirmed the search in an official statement.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak2_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak2-113">[113]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leak-statement_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leak-statement-114">[114]</a></sup> One dean had forwarded the email to two students in their role as an advisor and it was redistributed, reaching <i>The Harvard Crimson</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak_112-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak-112">[112]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak2_113-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak2-113">[113]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leak-statement_114-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leak-statement-114">[114]</a></sup> In light of a second leak of an Administrative Board meeting and with no explanations forthcoming, administrators approved the email search.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak2_113-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak2-113">[113]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leak-statement_114-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leak-statement-114">[114]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">Information technology</a> staff searched the <a href="/wiki/Subject_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject line">subject lines</a> of the deans' staff email accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak_112-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak-112">[112]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leak-statement_114-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leak-statement-114">[114]</a></sup> Only the dean responsible was notified about the search.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak_112-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak-112">[112]</a></sup> The statement said Senior Resident Dean <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sharon_Howell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sharon Howell (page does not exist)">Sharon Howell</a> was also informed but she and an anonymous Harvard official said she was not.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak2_113-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak2-113">[113]</a></sup> The Faculty of Arts and Sciences <a href="/wiki/Privacy_policy" title="Privacy policy">privacy policy</a> requires members to be "notified at the earliest possible opportunity."<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak_112-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak-112">[112]</a></sup> The policy was partially due to suspected snooping by Harvard President <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">Lawrence Summers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak_112-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak-112">[112]</a></sup> It was not clear if resident deans are faculty or merely staff.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> The search breaks the faculty policy but not the staff policy.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Faculty members reacted negatively to the search.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup></p>
<p>Resident deans have separate administrative and personal email accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak_112-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak-112">[112]</a></sup> The first search examined only the administrative account.<sup id="cite_ref-globe-leak_112-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-leak-112">[112]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leak-statement_114-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leak-statement-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 2, 2013 Hammonds disclosed that additional searches were run on the deans' two email accounts seeking communication with reporters for <i>The Harvard Crimson</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> In April, Hammonds announced that her earlier statement had not been complete as she had failed to recollect a second email search, this time of the accounts of <a href="/wiki/Allston_Burr_Resident_Dean" title="Allston Burr Resident Dean">Allston Burr Resident Deans</a>, academics who live in Harvard's undergraduate housing and advise students. Hammonds did not inform Smith of this second search, violating the Faculty of Arts and Sciences' email privacy policy.<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Harvard Crimson</i> called on Hammonds to resign, stating: "Since Hammonds provided misinformation regarding the highly sensitive issue of email searches, and since she violated clear policy regarding those searches, her presence at the helm of the College stands as a roadblock to rebuilding trust between students, faculty, and the administration."<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 28, Hammonds announced that she would resign to lead a new Harvard research program on race and gender in science. Hammonds said that her decision to resign was unrelated to the email search incident.<sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup></p>
<p>An outside investigation run by Michael B. Keating of <a href="/wiki/Foley_Hoag" title="Foley Hoag">Foley Hoag</a> found that the searches were done in good faith.<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup></p>

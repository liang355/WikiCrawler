<p><b>Sever Hall</b> is a notable building designed by famed American architect <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hobson_Richardson" title="Henry Hobson Richardson">H. H. Richardson</a> and built in the late 1870s. It is located on the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, within <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Yard" title="Harvard Yard">Harvard Yard</a>. It was designated a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1970, recognized as one of Richardson's mature masterpieces.<sup id="cite_ref-nhl_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhl-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sever_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sever Hall was built from 1878 to 1880 with a gift from Anne Sever in honor of her deceased husband, James Warren Sever. It was designed as an academic building with classrooms, lecture halls, rooms for professors, etc., in a style now known as <a href="/wiki/Richardsonian_Romanesque" title="Richardsonian Romanesque">Richardsonian Romanesque</a> though in red brick rather than stone.</p>
<p>The building is 176 feet and 4&#160;inches long, by 74 feet and 4&#160;inches wide, with a height to <a href="/wiki/Cornice" title="Cornice">cornice</a> of about 50 feet, above which the hipped roof rises a further 30 feet. It is three stories tall, with a fourth story set within the roof. The main facade (west side) features two round bays set symmetrically about an entrance within a deeply recessed semi-circular archway. The east facade is similar but with a simpler, rectangular entrance. North and south facades are relatively austere expanses punctuated with windows.</p>
<p>About 1.3 million <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">bricks</a> were used in its construction. Of these, some 100,000 form the exterior facades, which feature 60 different varieties of red molded brick, as well as elaborate brick carvings. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_mortar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blood mortar (page does not exist)">Blood mortar</a> was used as a joiner originally, though <a href="/w/index.php?title=Polybond&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Polybond (page does not exist)">polybond</a> compounds have been used in restoration efforts since 1967.</p>
<p>The archway admitting entrance into the west facade possesses an acoustical oddity. Whispering directly into the bricks of the archway, while standing very close to one side of the arch, can be heard clearly on the other side of the arch (approximately twelve feet away).</p>
<p>In recent years, there has been renewed interest in Sever Hall among architectural historians,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> due to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Venturi" title="Robert Venturi">Robert Venturi</a>'s comment that it is his "favorite building in America."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He told <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Globe" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Globe">Boston Globe</a></i> critic <a href="/wiki/Robert_Campbell_(journalist)" title="Robert Campbell (journalist)">Robert Campbell</a>: "I have come to understand the validity of architecture as generic shelter rather than abstract-expressive sculpture, and as flexible loft for accommodating evolving functions. ... And then I love Sever Hall also for its aesthetic tension deriving from its vital details. I could stand and look at it all day. Thank you, H.H. Richardson."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sever_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sever has small classrooms and larger lecture halls, so it is mostly used as a general-purpose classroom building for humanities courses especially small sections, beginning language courses, and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Extension_School" title="Harvard Extension School">Harvard Extension School</a> classes. <a href="/wiki/Grossman_Library" title="Grossman Library">Grossman Library</a>, a non-circulating library serving Extension School students, is located on the third floor. The fourth floor of Sever, unnoticed by many of its students as the central stairwell does not lead to it, contains offices for Harvard's <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College#Concentrations" title="Harvard College">Visual and Environmental Studies</a> department. In the evenings and on weekends student groups hold meetings or run annual events. One of Sever's notable annual events is <a href="/wiki/Vericon" title="Vericon">Vericon</a>, run during the break between semesters by the <a href="/wiki/HRSFA" class="mw-redirect" title="HRSFA">Harvard-Radcliffe Science-Fiction Association</a>.</p>

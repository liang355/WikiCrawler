			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Scottish common sense realism</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Scottish Common Sense Realism</b>, also known as the <b>Scottish School of Common Sense</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-Boas_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boas-1">[1]</a></sup> is a school of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> that originated in the ideas of Scottish philosophers <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Reid" title="Thomas Reid">Thomas Reid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Ferguson" title="Adam Ferguson">Adam Ferguson</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Beattie_(poet)" title="James Beattie (poet)">James Beattie</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dugald_Stewart" title="Dugald Stewart">Dugald Stewart</a> during the 18th century <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a>. Reid emphasized man's innate ability to perceive common ideas and that this process is inherent in and interdependent with judgement. Common sense therefore, is the foundation of philosophical inquiry. Though best remembered for its opposition to the pervasive philosophy of <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>, Scottish Common Sense philosophy is influential and evident in the works of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> and late 18th century American politics.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_common_sense_realism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg/220px-Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg" width="220" height="271" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg/330px-Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg/440px-Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg 2x" data-file-width="461" data-file-height="567" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Scottish School of Common Sense was an epistemological philosophy that flourished in Scotland in the late 18th and early 19th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Redekop_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redekop-4">[4]</a></sup> Its roots can be found in responses to the writings of such philosophers as <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Berkeley" title="George Berkeley">George Berkeley</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>, and its most prominent members were <a href="/wiki/Dugald_Stewart" title="Dugald Stewart">Dugald Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Reid" title="Thomas Reid">Thomas Reid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Hamilton,_9th_Baronet" title="Sir William Hamilton, 9th Baronet">William Hamilton</a>. Philosophically, Scottish Realism served as a rebuttal to scepticism while keeping with the influential teachings of <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Redekop_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redekop-4">[4]</a></sup> While largely understated for many years,<sup id="cite_ref-Redekop_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redekop-4">[4]</a></sup> the influence it had on philosophers elsewhere in Europe, not to mention in the United States, is of a considerable magnitude.</p>
<p>One central concern of the school was to defend "common sense" against philosophical <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">paradox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scepticism" class="mw-redirect" title="Scepticism">scepticism</a>. It argued that common-sense beliefs govern the lives and thoughts even of those who avow non-commonsensical beliefs and that matters of common sense are inherent to the acquisition of knowledge. The qualities of its works were not generally consistent; <a href="/wiki/Edward_S._Reed" title="Edward S. Reed">Edward S. Reed</a> writes, e.g., "[Whereas] Thomas Reid wished to use common sense to develop philosophical wisdom, much of this school simply wanted to use common sense to attack any form of intellectual change."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teachings">Teachings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_common_sense_realism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Teachings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Scottish School of Common Sense was founded by Reid in opposition to <a href="/wiki/Descartes" class="mw-redirect" title="Descartes">Descartes</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">Theory of Ideas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reid_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid-6">[6]</a></sup> But the epistemology of <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">sense experience</a> led <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> and David Hume to a skeptical philosophy that realists found absurd and contrary to common experience. Thus Hume and his sceptical argument would serve as the primary foil to the development of Reid's philosophy. Under the tutelage of <a href="/wiki/George_Turnbull_(theologian)" title="George Turnbull (theologian)">George Turnbull</a>, Reid embraced the tenets of Providential Naturalism and its four interconnected tenets; using these as the basis for his refutation of the theory of ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-Reid_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid-6">[6]</a></sup> Reid painstakingly developed his treatise <i>An Inquiry into the Human Mind on the Principles of Common Sense</i> over the course of 40 years, often seeking the input of his contemporary philosophers within the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a> including Hume.<sup id="cite_ref-Reid_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reid-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reid articulated the basic principle of Common Sense Realism:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"If there are certain principles, as I think there are, which the constitution of our nature leads us to believe, and which we are under a necessity to take for granted in the common concerns of life, without being able to give a reason for them — these are what we call the principles of common sense; and what is manifestly contrary to them, is what we call absurd."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Scottish Common Sense Realism is rooted in Aristotelian thought and advocates an empirical and scientific philosophy wherein trust of our senses is implicit and necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-Haldan_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haldan-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:148</sup> The principles of common sense are fundamental to our accumulation of knowledge of both <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysical</a> and physical constructs.<sup id="cite_ref-Redekop_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redekop-4">[4]</a></sup> However, observation alone cannot account for all knowledge and truth can be garnered by reflection.<sup id="cite_ref-Haldan_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haldan-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:149</sup> In Reid's own words:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"I can likewise conceive an individual object that really exists, such as <a href="/wiki/St._Paul%27s_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paul's Cathedral">St. Paul's Church</a> in London. I have an idea of it; I conceive it. The immediate object of this conception is 400 miles distant; and I have no reason to think it acts upon me or that I act on it."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The school taught that every person had ordinary experiences that provided intuitively certain assurance of a) the existence of the self, b) the existence of real objects that could be seen and felt; and c) certain "first principles" upon which sound morality and religious beliefs could be established. These principles laid the foundation for Reid's influential <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Reid" title="Thomas Reid">theory of perception</a>.</p>
<p>In practice, philosophers of the Scottish school offered scientific explanations to historical events and advocated an unprejudiced and inter-disciplinary approach to education, free from religious and patriotic biases.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_of_perception">Theories of perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_common_sense_realism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theories of perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thomas Reid and Dugald Stewart offered related theories of perception rooted in Scottish Common Sense Realism. According to <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Wolterstorff" title="Nicholas Wolterstorff">Nicholas Wolterstorff</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, Reid's philosophy can be non-contentiously reduced to four basic precepts:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"(1) The objects of acts of perception are external objects-That is, mind-independent spatially-located entities;</dd>
<dd>(2) The necessary and sufficient condition for perceiving an external object is that the object cause in one a conception thereof and an immediate (non-inferential) belief about it;</dd>
<dd>(3) We human beings are so made that, in perception, the external object causes a conception of, and an immediate belief about, itself, by way of causing a sensation which in turn causes ('suggests') the conception and immediate belief;</dd>
<dd>(4) The sensation may cause, and often does in fact cause, the conception and belief without one's being sufficiently attentive to the sensation for a belief about it to be formed in one."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Dugald Stewart's theory of perception acknowledges a great influence from Reid whose philosophy he termed "fundamental laws of belief.".<sup id="cite_ref-Dabney_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dabney-13">[13]</a></sup> However, Stewart proffered a more moderate approach to realism and his theory of perception emphasized the utility of the senses.<sup id="cite_ref-Dabney_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dabney-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_common_sense_realism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common Sense Realism not only dominated Scottish thought in the 19th century, it had a major influence as well in France, the United States, and other countries. <a href="/wiki/Victor_Cousin" title="Victor Cousin">Victor Cousin</a> (1792–1867) was the most important proponent in France. Reidian thought was the "orthodox philosophy of colleges and universities"<sup id="cite_ref-redekop_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-redekop-14">[14]</a></sup> in the early 18th century and provided an intellectual bedrock for the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Redekop_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redekop-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Common Sense Realism swept American intellectual circles in the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:181</sup> Reid's philosophy was pervasive during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> and served as a stabilizing philosophical influence.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:180</sup> Hailed by some as the "father of modern psychiatry,"<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:173</sup> <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Rush" title="Benjamin Rush">Benjamin Rush</a>'s tutelage at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> imbued him with strong realist tendencies which influenced much of his scientific and political work including his moral opposition to slavery.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:174</sup> Evidence of the influence of Scottish Common Sense realism can readily be found in the philosophy of both <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>. Adams compared the contributions of Dugald Stewart favorably to works of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:170</sup> Scotsman and signer of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Witherspoon" title="John Witherspoon">John Witherspoon</a> presided over <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>; students under his tutelage included 12 state governors, 55 delegates to the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Convention_(United_States)" title="Constitutional Convention (United States)">Constitutional Convention</a> and future president <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:171</sup> His education at the University of Edinburgh made him a strong proponent of the Scottish Enlightenment and Realism. <a href="/wiki/James_McCosh" title="James McCosh">James McCosh</a> (1811–94) continued the influence of Scottish Realism at Princeton when he became president of the university in 1868, reviving its influence as a major stronghold of the movement. <a href="/wiki/Noah_Porter" title="Noah Porter">Noah Porter</a> (1811–92) taught Common Sense realism to generations of students at Yale.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamentalism">Fundamentalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_common_sense_realism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fundamentalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Princeton_Theological_Seminary.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Princeton_Theological_Seminary.jpg/220px-Princeton_Theological_Seminary.jpg" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Princeton_Theological_Seminary.jpg/330px-Princeton_Theological_Seminary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Princeton_Theological_Seminary.jpg/440px-Princeton_Theological_Seminary.jpg 2x" data-file-width="763" data-file-height="537" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Princeton_Theological_Seminary.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Princeton Seminary in the 1800s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Scottish Realism greatly influenced conservative religious thought and was strongest at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Seminary" class="mw-redirect" title="Princeton Seminary">Princeton Seminary</a> until the Seminary moved in new directions after 1929. The Princeton theologians built their elaborate system on the basis of "common-sense" realism, biblicism and confessionalism.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> James McCosh was brought from Queen's College, Belfast, to Princeton College's Chair of Moral Philosophy and Presidency because of his book "The Method of Divine Government," a Christian philosophy that was precursory to Charles Darwin's "The Origin of Species" (1865). The <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Theologians" class="mw-redirect" title="Princeton Theologians">Princeton Theologians</a> followed McCosh to adopt a stance of theistic evolution). They tried to persuade <a href="/wiki/John_Gresham_Machen" title="John Gresham Machen">John Gresham Machen</a> (1881–1937), a leader of <a href="/wiki/Fundamentalist_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Fundamentalist Christianity">the Fundamentalists</a> in the 1920s, but he would not stand with McCosh, nor could he stand for the shift the school was making. Therefore, he and a number of other Princeton faculty members left and started <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Theological_Seminary" title="Westminster Theological Seminary">Westminster Theological Seminary</a> in Philadelphia. This, however, did not stop McCosh from moving forward. It was his goal to develop Princeton as a Christian university in North America, as well as a forefront intellectual seminary of the Presbyterian Church. The faculty of the College and Seminary included both evolutionary thinkers and non-evolutionary thinkers. Much evangelical theology of the 21st century is based on Princeton theology and thus reflects Common Sense Realism.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> New Testament scholar Grant Osborne concludes that Scottish Common Sense Realism influenced biblical hermeneutics, that the surface level understanding of Scripture became popular, and individualistic interpretations abounded.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

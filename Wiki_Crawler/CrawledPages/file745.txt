			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Forward pass</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>In several forms of <a href="/wiki/Football" title="Football">football</a> a <b>forward pass</b> is a throwing of the ball in the direction that the offensive team is trying to move, towards the defensive team's goal line. The forward pass is one of the main distinguishers between <a href="/wiki/Gridiron_football" title="Gridiron football">gridiron football</a> (<a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">Canadian football</a>) in which the play is legal and widespread, and <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby football</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">league</a>) from which the North American games evolved, in which the play is illegal.</p>
<p>In some football codes, such as <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> (soccer), the kicked forward pass is used so ubiquitously that it is not thought of as a distinct kind of play at all. In these sports, the concept of <a href="/wiki/Offside_(sport)" title="Offside (sport)">offside</a> is used to regulate who can be in front of the play or be nearest to the goal. However, this has not always been the case. Some earlier incarnations of football allowed unlimited forward passing, while others had strict offside rules similar to rugby.</p>
<p>The development of the forward pass in American football shows how the game has evolved from its rugby roots into the distinctive game it is today. Illegal and experimental forward passes had been attempted as early as 1876, but the first legal forward pass in <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> took place in 1906, after a change in rules. Another change in rules occurred on January 18, 1951, which established that no center, tackle, or guard could receive a forward pass. Today, the only linemen who can receive a forward pass are the ends. Current rules regulate who may throw and who may receive a forward pass, and under what circumstances, as well as how the defensive team may try to prevent a pass from being completed. The primary pass thrower is the <a href="/wiki/Quarterback" title="Quarterback">quarterback</a>, and statistical analysis is used to determine a quarterback's success rate at passing in various situations, as well as a team's overall success at the "passing game."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_and_Canadian_football">American and Canadian football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forward_pass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: American and Canadian football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Luckthrowingfotball.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Luckthrowingfotball.jpg/150px-Luckthrowingfotball.jpg" width="150" height="318" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Luckthrowingfotball.jpg/225px-Luckthrowingfotball.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/54/Luckthrowingfotball.jpg 2x" data-file-width="236" data-file-height="500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Luckthrowingfotball.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Andrew_Luck" title="Andrew Luck">Andrew Luck</a> tosses a pass.</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">Canadian football</a>, a <b>forward pass</b>, usually referred to simply as a <b>pass</b>, consists of a player throwing the football towards the opponent's goal line. This is permitted only once during a <a href="/wiki/Line_of_scrimmage" title="Line of scrimmage">scrimmage</a> <a href="/wiki/Down_(gridiron_football)" title="Down (gridiron football)">down</a> by the offensive team before team possession has changed, provided the pass is thrown from in or behind the <a href="/wiki/Neutral_zone_(gridiron_football)" title="Neutral zone (gridiron football)">neutral zone</a>. An illegal forward pass can incur a yardage penalty and the loss of a down, although it may be legally intercepted by the opponents and advanced.</p>
<p>If an <a href="/wiki/Eligible_receiver" title="Eligible receiver">eligible receiver</a> on the passing team legally catches the ball, the pass is <b>completed</b> and the receiver may attempt to advance the ball. If an opposing player legally catches the ball (all defensive players are eligible receivers) it is an <a href="/wiki/Interception" title="Interception">interception</a>. That player's team immediately gains possession of the ball and he may attempt to advance the ball toward his opponent's goal. If no player is able to legally catch the ball it is an <a href="/wiki/Incomplete_pass" title="Incomplete pass">incomplete pass</a> and the ball becomes <a href="/wiki/Dead_ball_(American_football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dead ball (American football)">dead</a> the moment it touches the ground. It will then be returned to the original line of scrimmage for the next <a href="/wiki/Down_(football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Down (football)">down</a>. If any player interferes with an eligible receiver's ability to catch the ball it is <a href="/wiki/Pass_interference" title="Pass interference">pass interference</a> which draws a penalty of varying degree (largely depending upon the particular league's rules).</p>
<p>The person passing the ball must be a member of the <a href="/wiki/Offensive_team" class="mw-redirect" title="Offensive team">offensive team</a>, and the recipient of the forward pass must be an <a href="/wiki/Eligible_receiver" title="Eligible receiver">eligible receiver</a> and must touch the passed ball before any ineligible player.</p>
<p>The moment that a forward pass begins is important to the game. The pass begins the moment the passer's arm begins to move forward. If the passer drops the ball before this moment it is a <a href="/wiki/Fumble" title="Fumble">fumble</a> and therefore a <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_American_football#L" title="Glossary of American football">loose ball</a>. In this case anybody can gain possession of the ball before or after it touches the ground. If the passer drops the ball while his arm is moving forward it is a forward pass, regardless of where the ball lands or is first touched.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Matt_Hasselbeck_(8)_drops_back_to_pass.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Matt_Hasselbeck_%288%29_drops_back_to_pass.jpg/280px-Matt_Hasselbeck_%288%29_drops_back_to_pass.jpg" width="280" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Matt_Hasselbeck_%288%29_drops_back_to_pass.jpg/420px-Matt_Hasselbeck_%288%29_drops_back_to_pass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Matt_Hasselbeck_%288%29_drops_back_to_pass.jpg/560px-Matt_Hasselbeck_%288%29_drops_back_to_pass.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="792" /></a>

<p>The quarterback generally either starts a few paces behind the line of scrimmage or drops back a few paces after the ball is snapped. This places him in an area called the "pocket", which is a protective region formed by the offensive blockers up front and between the tackles on each side. A quarterback who runs out of this pocket is said to be scrambling. Under NFL and NCAA rules, once the quarterback moves out of the pocket the ball may be legally thrown away to prevent a sack. NFHS (high school) rules do not allow for a passer to intentionally throw an incomplete forward pass to save loss of yardage or conserve time, except for a spike to conserve time after a hand-to-hand snap. If he throws the ball away while still in the pocket then a foul called "intentional grounding" is assessed. In <a href="/wiki/Canadian_football" title="Canadian football">Canadian football</a> the passer must simply throw the ball across the line of scrimmage—whether he is inside or outside of the "pocket"—to avoid the foul of "intentionally grounding".</p>
<p>If a forward pass is caught near a sideline or endline it is a complete pass (or an interception) only if a receiver catches the ball <i>in bounds</i>. For a pass to be ruled complete in-bounds, depending on the rules either one or two feet must touch the ground within the field boundaries, after the ball is first grasped. In the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a> the receiver must touch the ground with both feet, but in most other codes—<a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football_League" title="Canadian Football League">CFL</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> and high school—one foot in bounds is enough.</p>
<p>Common to all <a href="/wiki/Gridiron_football" title="Gridiron football">gridiron</a> codes is the notion of control—a receiver must demonstrate control of the ball in order to be ruled in <i>possession</i> of it, while still in bounds, as defined by his code. If the receiver handles the ball but the official determines that he was still "bobbling" it prior to the end of the play, then the pass will be ruled incomplete.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_illegal_and_experimental_passes">Early illegal and experimental passes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forward_pass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early illegal and experimental passes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The forward pass had been attempted at least 30 years before the play was actually made legal. Passes "had been carried out successfully but illegally several times, including the 1876 <a href="/wiki/Yale" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale">Yale</a>–<a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a> game in which Yale's <a href="/wiki/Walter_Camp" title="Walter Camp">Walter Camp</a> threw forward to teammate Oliver Thompson as he was being tackled. Princeton's protest, one account said, went for naught when the referee 'tossed a coin to make his decision and allowed the touchdown to stand'&#160;".<sup id="cite_ref-vahe1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vahe1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Tar_Heels_football" title="North Carolina Tar Heels football">University of North Carolina</a> used the forward pass in an 1895 game against the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Bulldogs_football" title="Georgia Bulldogs football">University of Georgia</a>. However, the play was still illegal at the time. Bob Quincy stakes Carolina's claim in his 1973 book <i>They Made the Bell Tower Chime</i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Heisman" title="John Heisman">John Heisman</a>, namesake of the <a href="/wiki/Heisman_Trophy" title="Heisman Trophy">Heisman Trophy</a>, wrote 30 years later that, indeed, the Tar Heels had given birth to the forward pass against the Bulldogs (UGA). It was conceived to break a scoreless deadlock and give UNC a 6–0 win. The Carolinians were in a punting situation and a Georgia rush seemed destined to block the ball. The punter, with an impromptu dash to his right, tossed the ball and it was caught by <a href="/wiki/George_Stephens_(American_football)" title="George Stephens (American football)">George Stephens</a>, who ran 70 yards for a touchdown.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/1905_Fairmount_vs._Washburn_football_game" class="mw-redirect" title="1905 Fairmount vs. Washburn football game">1905 experimental game</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita, Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washburn_University" title="Washburn University">Washburn University</a> and Fairmount College (what would become <a href="/wiki/Wichita_State_University" title="Wichita State University">Wichita State</a>) used the pass before new rules allowing the play were approved in early 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson-4">[4]</a></sup> Credit for the first pass goes to Fairmount's Bill Davis, who completed a pass to Art Solter.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1906_rule_change">1906 rule change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forward_pass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1906 rule change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>1905 had been a bloody year on the gridiron; the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> reported 18 players had been killed and 159 seriously injured that season.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> There were moves to outlaw the game, but United States President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> personally intervened and demanded that the rules of the game be reformed. In a meeting of more than 60 schools in late 1905, the commitment was made to make the game safer. This meeting was the first step toward the establishment of what would become the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a> and was followed by several sessions to work out "the new rules."<sup id="cite_ref-WashPost-1906-1-28_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost-1906-1-28-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The final meeting of the Rules Committee tasked with reshaping the game was held on April 6, 1906, at which time the forward pass officially became a legal play.<sup id="cite_ref-nelson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>The New York Times</i> reported in September 1906 on the rationale for the changes: "The main efforts of the football reformers have been to 'open up the game'—that is to provide for the natural elimination of the so-called mass plays and bring about a game in which speed and real skill shall supersede so far as possible mere brute strength and force of weight."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the <i>Times</i> also reflected widespread skepticism as to whether the forward pass could be effectively integrated into the game: "There has been no team that has proved that the forward pass is anything but a doubtful, dangerous play to be used only in the last extremity."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Heisman" title="John Heisman">John Heisman</a> was instrumental in the rules' acceptance. The forward pass was not allowed in Canadian football until 1929.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_legal_pass">First legal pass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forward_pass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First legal pass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cochems_175.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7a/Cochems_175.jpg" width="175" height="190" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="175" data-file-height="190" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cochems_175.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cochems" title="Eddie Cochems">Eddie Cochems</a>, "Father of the Forward Pass", <i>1907</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most sources credit <a href="/wiki/Saint_Louis_University" title="Saint Louis University">St. Louis University's</a> <a href="/wiki/Bradbury_Robinson" title="Bradbury Robinson">Bradbury Robinson</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bellevue,_Ohio" title="Bellevue, Ohio">Bellevue, Ohio</a> with throwing the first legal forward pass. On September 5, 1906, in a game against <a href="/wiki/Carroll_College_(Wisconsin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Carroll College (Wisconsin)">Carroll College</a>, Robinson's first attempt at a forward pass fell incomplete and resulted in a turnover under the 1906 rules.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the same game, Robinson later completed a 20-yard touchdown pass to <a href="/wiki/Jack_Schneider" title="Jack Schneider">Jack Schneider</a>. The 1906 St. Louis team, coached by <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cochems" title="Eddie Cochems">Eddie Cochems</a>, was undefeated at 11–0 and featured the most potent offense in the country, outscoring their opponents 407–11. Football authority and <a href="/wiki/College_Football_Hall_of_Fame" title="College Football Hall of Fame">College Football Hall of Fame</a> coach <a href="/wiki/David_M._Nelson" title="David M. Nelson">David M. Nelson</a> wrote that "E. B. Cochems is to forward passing what the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a> are to aviation and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> is to the electric light."<sup id="cite_ref-nelson_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While St. Louis University completed the first legal forward pass in the first half of September, this accomplishment was in part because most schools did not begin their football schedule until early October.</p>
<p>In 1952, football coaching legend <a href="/wiki/Amos_Alonzo_Stagg" title="Amos Alonzo Stagg">Amos Alonzo Stagg</a> discounted accounts crediting any particular coach with being the innovator of the forward pass. Stagg noted that he had <a href="/wiki/Walter_Eckersall" title="Walter Eckersall">Walter Eckersall</a> working on pass plays and saw Pomeroy Sinnock of Illinois throw many passes in 1906. Stagg summed up his view as follows: "I have seen statements giving credit to certain people originating the forward pass. The fact is that all coaches were working on it. The first season, 1906, I personally had sixty-four different forward pass patterns."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1954, Stagg disputed Cochems' claim to have invented the forward pass:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Eddie Cochems, who coached at St. Louis University in 1906, also claimed to have invented the pass as we know it today ... It isn't so, because after the forward pass was legalized in 1906, most of the schools commenced experimenting with it and nearly all used."<sup id="cite_ref-Stagg_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stagg-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Stagg asserted that, as far back as 1894, before the rules committee even considered the forward pass, one of his players used to throw the ball "like a baseball pitcher."<sup id="cite_ref-Stagg_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stagg-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/College_Football_Hall_of_Fame" title="College Football Hall of Fame">Hall of Fame</a> coach <a href="/wiki/Gus_Dorais" title="Gus Dorais">Gus Dorais</a> told the <a href="/wiki/United_Press" class="mw-redirect" title="United Press">United Press</a> that "Eddie Cochems of the St. Louis University team of 1906–07–08 deserves the full credit."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Writing in <i><a href="/wiki/Collier%27s" title="Collier's">Collier's</a></i> more than 20 years earlier, Dorais' Notre Dame teammate <a href="/wiki/Knute_Rockne" title="Knute Rockne">Knute Rockne</a> acknowledged Cochems as the early leader in the use of the pass, observing, "One would have thought that so effective a play would have been instantly copied and become the vogue. The East, however, had not learned much or cared much about Midwest and Western football. Indeed, the East scarcely realized that football existed beyond the Alleghanies ..."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his history of the game, historian David Nelson concluded that the first forward passes were thrown on Christmas Day 1905 in a match between two small colleges in Kansas: "Although Cochems was the premier passing coach during the first year of the rule, the first forward passes were thrown at the end of the 1905 season in a game between Fairmount and Washburn colleges in Kansas."<sup id="cite_ref-nelson_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson-4">[4]</a></sup> According to Nelson, Washburn completed three passes, and Fairmount completed two.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RobinsonThrowing2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RobinsonThrowing2.jpg/180px-RobinsonThrowing2.jpg" width="180" height="109" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RobinsonThrowing2.jpg/270px-RobinsonThrowing2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RobinsonThrowing2.jpg/360px-RobinsonThrowing2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="428" data-file-height="259" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RobinsonThrowing2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bradbury_Robinson" title="Bradbury Robinson">Brad Robinson</a>, who threw the first legal forward pass.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Once the 1906 season got underway, many programs began experimenting with the forward pass. On September 26, 1906, Villanova's game against the Carlisle Indians was billed as "the first real game of football under the new rules."<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-16">[16]</a></sup> In the first play from scrimmage after the opening kicks, Villanova completed a pass that "succeeded in gaining ten yards."<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-16">[16]</a></sup> Following the Villanova-Carlisle game, <i>The New York Times</i> described the new passing game this way:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The passing was more of the character of that familiar in basket ball than that which has hitherto characterized football. Apparently it is the intention of football coaches to try repeatedly these frequent long and risky passes. Well executed they are undoubtedly highly spectacular, but the risk of dropping the ball is so great as to make the practice extremely hazardous and its desirability doubtful."<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Another coach sometimes credited with popularizing the overhead <a href="/wiki/Spiral_(football)" title="Spiral (football)">spiral</a> pass in 1906 is former <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a> All-American <a href="/wiki/Howard_R._Reiter" title="Howard R. Reiter">"Bosey" Reiter</a>. Reiter claimed to have invented the overhead spiral pass while playing professional football as a player-coach for <a href="/wiki/Connie_Mack" title="Connie Mack">Connie Mack</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics_(NFL)" title="Philadelphia Athletics (NFL)">Philadelphia Athletics</a> of the original <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League_(1902)" title="National Football League (1902)">National Football League (1902)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pass_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pass-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> While playing for the Athletics, Reiter was a teammate of Hawley Pierce, a former star for the <a href="/wiki/Carlisle_Indian_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle Indian School">Carlisle Indian School</a>. Pierce, a <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a>, taught Reiter to throw an underhand spiral pass, but Reiter had short arms and was unable to throw for distance from an underhand delivery. Accordingly, Reiter began working on an overhand spiral pass.<sup id="cite_ref-Pass_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pass-17">[17]</a></sup> Reiter recalled trying to imitate the motion of a baseball catcher throwing to second base. After practice and experimentation, Reiter "discovered he could get greater distance and accuracy throwing that way." <sup id="cite_ref-Pass_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pass-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1906, Reiter was the head coach at <a href="/wiki/Wesleyan_University" title="Wesleyan University">Wesleyan University</a>. In the opening game of the 1906 season against <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a>, Reiter's quarterback Sammy Moore completed a forward pass to Irvin van Tassell for a thirty-yard gain. <i>The New York Times</i> called it "the prettiest play of the day", as Wesleyan's quarterback "deftly passed the ball past the whole Yale team to his mate Van Tassel."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Van Tassel later described the historic play to the <a href="/wiki/United_Press" class="mw-redirect" title="United Press">United Press</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"I was the right halfback, and on this formation played one yard back of our right tackle. The quarterback, Sam Moore, took the ball from center and faded eight or 10 yards back of our line. Our two ends angled down the field toward the sidelines as a decoy, and I slipped through the strong side of our line straight down the center and past the secondary defense. The pass worked perfectly. However, the quarterback coming up fast nailed me as I caught it. This brought the ball well into Yale territory, about the 20-yard line."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The football season opened for most schools during the first week of October, and the impact of the forward pass was immediate:</p>
<ul>
<li>On October 3, 1906, the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Des_Moines_Daily_News&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Des Moines Daily News (page does not exist)">Des Moines Daily News</a></i> reported "probably the first use" of the "long forward pass" in the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Tigers_football" title="Missouri Tigers football">University of Missouri's</a> 23–4 win over <a href="/wiki/Truman_State_University" title="Truman State University">Kirksville Normal School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>On October 4, 1906, <a href="/wiki/1906_Princeton_Tigers_football_team" title="1906 Princeton Tigers football team">Princeton</a> opened its season with a 22–0 win over <a href="/wiki/Stevens_Institute_of_Technology" title="Stevens Institute of Technology">Stevens</a>. Press accounts indicate that Princeton put the forward pass to good use, as "old-time football gave way to the new game."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>On October 4, 1906, the <a href="/wiki/1906_Carlisle_Indians_football_team" title="1906 Carlisle Indians football team">Carlisle Indians</a> beat <a href="/wiki/Susquehanna_University" title="Susquehanna University">Susquehanna University</a> 40–0, as "the forward pass was used for a number of good gains."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>On October 4, 1906, <a href="/wiki/1906_Harvard_Crimson_football_team" title="1906 Harvard Crimson football team">Harvard</a> defeated <a href="/wiki/Bowdoin_College" title="Bowdoin College">Bowdoin</a> 10–0 "in a hard-fought contest that was featured by a newfangled and daring forward pass that Crimson worked in the closing minutes of play."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>On October 4, 1906, <a href="/wiki/Williams_Ephs_football" title="Williams Ephs football">Williams College</a> defeated the <a href="/wiki/UMass_Minutemen_football" title="UMass Minutemen football">Massachusetts Agricultural College</a>, scoring the game's only touchdown on a forward pass by Waters.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Some publications credit <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a> All-American <a href="/wiki/Paul_Veeder" title="Paul Veeder">Paul Veeder</a> with the "first forward pass in a major game." Veeder threw a 20 to 30-yard completion in leading Yale past <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson" title="Harvard Crimson">Harvard</a> 6–0 before 32,000 fans in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven</a> on November 24, 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However, that Yale/Harvard game was played three weeks after St. Louis completed 45 and 48-yard passes against <a href="/wiki/Kansas_Jayhawks_football" title="Kansas Jayhawks football">Kansas</a> before a crowd of 7,000 at <a href="/wiki/Sportsman%27s_Park" title="Sportsman's Park">Sportsman's Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_style_of_play">New style of play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forward_pass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New style of play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:295px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HackettPostGame.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/HackettPostGame.jpg" width="293" height="564" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="293" data-file-height="564" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HackettPostGame.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Referee <a href="/wiki/Horatio_B._Hackett" title="Horatio B. Hackett">Hackett</a>'s analysis of St. Louis' passing game against Iowa, <i><a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Globe-Democrat" title="St. Louis Globe-Democrat">St. Louis Globe-Democrat</a></i>, written by Ed Wray, November 30, 1906</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The forward pass was a central feature of Cochems' offensive scheme in 1906 as his St. Louis University team compiled an undefeated 11–0 season in which they outscored opponents by a combined score 407 to 11. The highlight of the campaign was St. Louis' 39–0 win over <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">Iowa</a>. Cochems' team reportedly completed eight passes in ten attempts for four touchdowns. "The average flight distance of the passes was twenty yards." Nelson continues, "the last play demonstrated the dramatic effect that the forward pass was having on football. St. Louis was on Iowa's thirty-five-yard line with a few seconds to play. Timekeeper Walter McCormack walked onto the field to end the game when the ball was thrown twenty-five yards and caught on the dead run for a touchdown."<sup id="cite_ref-nelson_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1906 Iowa game was refereed by one of the top football officials in the country, <a href="/wiki/West_Point" class="mw-redirect" title="West Point">West Point</a>'s Lt. <a href="/wiki/Horatio_B._Hackett" title="Horatio B. Hackett">Horatio B. "Stuffy" Hackett</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He had officiated games involving the top Eastern powers that year. Hackett, who would become a member of the football rules committee in December 1907<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and officiated games into the 1930s,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> was quoted the next day in Ed Wray's<sup id="cite_ref-wray_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wray-35">[35]</a></sup> <i>Globe-Democrat</i> article: "It was the most perfect exhibition ... of the new rules ... that I have seen all season and much better than that of Yale and Harvard. St. Louis' style of pass differs entirely from that in use in the east. ... The St. Louis university players shoot the ball hard and accurately to the man who is to receive it ... The fast throw by St. Louis enables the receiving player to dodge the opposing players, and it struck me as being all but perfect."<sup id="cite_ref-WRAYHACK_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WRAYHACK-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hackett is the only known expert witness to the passing offenses of both Cochems' 1906 squads and that of Stagg, who dismissed any special role for the St. Louis coach in the development of the pass. Hackett was an official in games involving both teams. As Wray recalled almost 40 years later: "Hackett told this writer that in no other game that he handled had he seen the forward pass as used by St. Louis U. nor such bewildering variations of it."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Cochems said that the poor Iowa showing resulted from its use of the old style play and its failure to effectively use the forward pass", Nelson writes. "Iowa did attempt two basketball-style forward passes."<sup id="cite_ref-nelson1_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson1-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>"During the 1906 season [Robinson] threw a sixty-seven yard pass ... and ... Schneider tossed a sixty-five yarder. Considering the size, shape and weight of the ball, these were extraordinary passes."<sup id="cite_ref-nelson1_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nelson1-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1907, after the first season of the forward pass, one football writer noted that, "with the single exception of Cochems, football teachers were groping in the dark."<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because St. Louis was geographically isolated from both the dominating teams and the major sports media (newspapers) of the era ... all centered in and focused on the East ... Cochems' groundbreaking offensive strategy was not picked up by the major teams. Pass-oriented offenses would not be adopted by the Eastern football powers until the next decade.</p>
<p>But that does not mean that other teams in the Midwest did not pick it up. <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schabinger" title="Arthur Schabinger">Arthur Schabinger</a>, quarterback for the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Emporia_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="College of Emporia Football">College of Emporia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, was reported to have regularly used the forward pass in 1910. Coach <a href="/wiki/Homer_Woodson_Hargiss" title="Homer Woodson Hargiss">H. W. "Bill" Hargiss'</a> "Presbies" are said to have featured the play in a 17–0 victory over <a href="/wiki/Washburn_University" title="Washburn University">Washburn University</a><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and in a 107–0 destruction of <a href="/wiki/Pittsburg_State_University" title="Pittsburg State University">Pittsburg State University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Coach <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Scobey_Warner" class="mw-redirect" title="Glenn Scobey Warner">Pop Warner</a> at <a href="/wiki/1907_Carlisle_Indians_football_team" title="1907 Carlisle Indians football team">Carlisle</a> had quarterback <a href="/wiki/Frank_Mount_Pleasant" title="Frank Mount Pleasant">Frank Mount Pleasant</a>, one of the first regular <a href="/wiki/Spiral_(football)" title="Spiral (football)">spiral</a> pass quarterbacks in football.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rockne">Rockne</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forward_pass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rockne">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RocknescoringonArmy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/RocknescoringonArmy.jpg/260px-RocknescoringonArmy.jpg" width="260" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/RocknescoringonArmy.jpg/390px-RocknescoringonArmy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/RocknescoringonArmy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="206" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RocknescoringonArmy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rockne running away from Army after a pass from Dorais, 1913.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Knute_Rockne" title="Knute Rockne">Knute Rockne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gus_Dorais" title="Gus Dorais">Gus Dorais</a> worked on the pass while lifeguarding on a <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a> beach at <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Point" title="Cedar Point">Cedar Point</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sandusky,_Ohio" title="Sandusky, Ohio">Sandusky, Ohio</a>, during the summer of 1913.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> That year, <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Harper" title="Jesse Harper">Jesse Harper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_Fighting_Irish_football" title="Notre Dame Fighting Irish football">Notre Dame</a> head coach, also showed how the pass could be used by a smaller team to beat a bigger one, first utilizing it to defeat rival <a href="/wiki/Army_Black_Knights_football" title="Army Black Knights football">Army</a>. After it was used against a major school on a national stage in this game, the forward pass rapidly gained popularity.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1919_Notre_Dame_Fighting_Irish_football_team" title="1919 Notre Dame Fighting Irish football team">1919</a> and <a href="/wiki/1920_Notre_Dame_Fighting_Irish_football_team" title="1920 Notre Dame Fighting Irish football team">1920</a> Notre Dame teams had <a href="/wiki/George_Gipp" title="George Gipp">George Gipp</a>, an ideal handler of the forward pass,<sup id="cite_ref-shape_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shape-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> who threw for 1,789 yards.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Mohardt" title="John Mohardt">John Mohardt</a> led the <a href="/wiki/1921_Notre_Dame_Fighting_Irish_football_team" title="1921 Notre Dame Fighting Irish football team">1921 Notre Dame team</a> to a 10-1 record with 781 rushing yards, 995 passing yards, 12 rushing touchdowns, and nine passing touchdowns.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Grantland_Rice" title="Grantland Rice">Grantland Rice</a> wrote that "Mohardt could throw the ball to within a foot or two of any given space" and noted that the 1921 team was the first at Notre Dame "to build its attack around a forward passing game, rather than use a forward passing game as a mere aid to the running game."<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Mohardt had both <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Anderson_(American_football_coach)" title="Eddie Anderson (American football coach)">Eddie Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Kiley" class="mw-redirect" title="Roger Kiley">Roger Kiley</a> at <a href="/wiki/End_(gridiron_football)" title="End (gridiron football)">end</a> to receive his passes.</p>

<p>From 1915–1916, <a href="/wiki/Pudge_Wyman" title="Pudge Wyman">Pudge Wyman</a> and <a href="/wiki/End_(gridiron_football)" title="End (gridiron football)">end</a> <a href="/wiki/Bert_Baston" title="Bert Baston">Bert Baston</a> of Minnesota were "one of the greatest forward-passing combinations in the history of the gridiron."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/1921_Rose_Bowl" title="1921 Rose Bowl">1921 Rose Bowl</a>, <a href="/wiki/1920_California_Golden_Bears_football_team" title="1920 California Golden Bears football team">California</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Harold_Muller" title="Harold Muller">Brick Muller</a> completed a touchdown against <a href="/wiki/1921_Washington_%26_Jefferson_Presidents_football_team" title="1921 Washington &amp; Jefferson Presidents football team">Washington &amp; Jefferson</a> which went 53 yards in the air, a feat previously thought impossible.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 1925, 62–13 victory over <a href="/wiki/Cornell_Big_Red_football" title="Cornell Big Red football">Cornell</a>, Dartmouth's <a href="/wiki/Andy_Oberlander" title="Andy Oberlander">Andy Oberlander</a> had 477 yards in total offense, including six touchdown passes,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> a Dartmouth record which still stands. He was responsible for some 500 yards of total offense.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Cornell coach <a href="/wiki/Gil_Dobie" title="Gil Dobie">Gil Dobie</a> responded "We won the game 13–0, passing is not football."<sup id="cite_ref-letter_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-letter-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1925_Michigan_Wolverines_football_team" title="1925 Michigan Wolverines football team">1925 Michigan team</a> was coach <a href="/wiki/Fielding_H._Yost" title="Fielding H. Yost">Fielding H. Yost</a>'s favorite and featured the passing tandem of <a href="/wiki/Benny_Friedman" title="Benny Friedman">Benny Friedman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bennie_Oosterbaan" title="Bennie Oosterbaan">Bennie Oosterbaan</a>.</p>

<p>Yost disciple <a href="/wiki/Dan_McGugin" title="Dan McGugin">Dan McGugin</a> coached <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_Commodores_football" title="Vanderbilt Commodores football">Vanderbilt</a> and was one of the first emphasize the forward pass.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/1907_Vanderbilt_Commodores_football_team" title="1907 Vanderbilt Commodores football team">1907 team</a> beat <a href="/wiki/1907_Sewanee_Tigers_football_team" title="1907 Sewanee Tigers football team">Sewanee</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Double_pass" title="Double pass">double pass</a> play <a href="/wiki/Grantland_Rice" title="Grantland Rice">Grantland Rice</a> cited as his biggest thrill in his years of watching sports.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> McGugin's <a href="/wiki/1927_Vanderbilt_Commodores_football_team" title="1927 Vanderbilt Commodores football team">1927 team</a> was piloted by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Spears" title="Bill Spears">Bill Spears</a>, who threw for over a thousand yards. According to one writer, Vanderbilt produced "almost certainly the legit top Heisman candidate in Spears, if there had been a <a href="/wiki/Heisman_Trophy" title="Heisman Trophy">Heisman Trophy</a> to award in 1927."<sup id="cite_ref-excite_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-excite-58">[58]</a></sup> McGugin disciple and former quarterback <a href="/wiki/Ray_Morrison" title="Ray Morrison">Ray Morrison</a> brought the pass to the southwest when he coached <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Mann" title="Gerald Mann">Gerald Mann</a> at <a href="/wiki/SMU_Mustangs_football" title="SMU Mustangs football">Southern Methodist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-shape_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shape-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_pass_in_a_professional_game">First pass in a professional game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forward_pass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: First pass in a professional game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first forward pass in a professional football game may have been thrown in an <a href="/wiki/Ohio_League" title="Ohio League">Ohio League</a> game played on October 25, 1906. The Ohio League, which traced its history to the 1890s, was a direct predecessor of today's <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a>. According to Robert W. Peterson in his book <i>Pigskin The Early Years of Pro Football</i>, the "passer was <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Parratt" title="Peggy Parratt">George W. (Peggy) Parratt</a>, probably the best quarterback of the era", who played for the <a href="/wiki/Massillon_Tigers" title="Massillon Tigers">Massillon, Ohio Tigers</a>, one of pro football's first franchises.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Citing the <a href="/wiki/Professional_Football_Researchers_Association" title="Professional Football Researchers Association">Professional Football Researchers Association</a> as his source, Peterson writes that "Parratt completed a short pass to end Dan Riley (real name, <a href="/wiki/Dan_Policowski" title="Dan Policowski">Dan Policowski</a>)" in a game played at <a href="/wiki/Massillon,_Ohio" title="Massillon, Ohio">Massillon</a> against a team from West Virginia. Since the Tigers "ran up a 61 to 0 score on the hapless Mountain Staters, the pass played no important part in the result."<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to National Football League history,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> it legalized the forward pass from anywhere behind the line of scrimmage on February 25, 1933. Before that rule change, a forward pass had to be made from 5 or more yards behind the line of scrimmage.</p>
<p>Forward passes were first permitted in Canadian football in 1929,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> but the tactic remained a minor part of the game for several years. <a href="/wiki/Jack_Jacobs" title="Jack Jacobs">Jack Jacobs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_Blue_Bombers" title="Winnipeg Blue Bombers">Winnipeg Blue Bombers</a> is recognized, not for inventing the forward pass, but for popularizing it in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Football_League_West_Division" title="Canadian Football League West Division">Western Interprovincial Football Union</a>, thus changing the Canadian game from a more run-dominated game to the passing game as seen today.</p>

<p>Specification of the size of the <a href="/wiki/Ball_(gridiron_football)" title="Ball (gridiron football)">ball</a> for the American game came in 1912, but it was still essentially a <a href="/wiki/Football_(ball)#Rugby_football" title="Football (ball)">rugby ball</a>. Increased use of the forward pass encouraged adoption of a narrower ball, starting with changes in the 1920s which enhanced rifled throwing and also spiral punting.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> This had the consequence of all but eliminating the <a href="/wiki/Drop_kick" title="Drop kick">drop kick</a> from the American game.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby_football">Rugby football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forward_pass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rugby football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the two codes of rugby (<a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">league</a>), a <b>forward pass</b> is against the rules. Normally this results in a <a href="/wiki/Scrum_(rugby)" title="Scrum (rugby)">scrum</a> to the opposing team, but on rare occasions a penalty may be awarded if the referee is of the belief that the ball was deliberately thrown forward.<sup id="cite_ref-australianrugbyleague.com.au_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-australianrugbyleague.com.au-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>In both codes of rugby the direction of the pass is relative to the player making the pass and not to the actual path relative to the ground. A forward pass occurs when the player passes the ball forward in relation to himself.<sup id="cite_ref-australianrugbyleague.com.au_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-australianrugbyleague.com.au-64">[64]</a></sup> (This applies only to the movement of the player, not to the direction in which he or she is facing, i.e. if the player is facing backwards and passes in front of him, it is not forward; and conversely, if he passes behind him, it is forward as he is passing toward the opponent's goal area.) In rugby league, the <a href="/wiki/Video_referee" class="mw-redirect" title="Video referee">video referee</a> may not make judgements on whether a pass is forward.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>

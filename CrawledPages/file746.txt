<p><b>The Ray Lavietes Basketball Pavilion at the Briggs Athletic Center</b> is a 2,195-seat multi-purpose <a href="/wiki/Arena" title="Arena">arena</a> in the Allston neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>. Owned by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, it is the second-oldest college basketball arena still in use (<a href="/wiki/Fordham_University" title="Fordham University">Fordham University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Rose_Hill_Gym" class="mw-redirect" title="Rose Hill Gym">Rose Hill Gym</a> (1924) is older).</p>
<p>The facility was originally named the Briggs Athletic Center in honor of LeBaron Russell Briggs, who served as dean of Harvard College from 1891 to 1902 and as the school's athletic director for 17 years. Briggs also served as president of the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a>. It included an indoor track and batting cages, which were popular with local collegiate and professional baseball players, including <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a>. In 1981, the Gordon Indoor Track and Tennis Facility (located adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Stadium" title="Harvard Stadium">Harvard Stadium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bright_Hockey_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Bright Hockey Center">Bright Hockey Center</a>) opened, and the building was refurbished as the new home to the Harvard basketball program, replacing the <a href="/wiki/Malkin_Athletic_Center" title="Malkin Athletic Center">Malkin Athletic Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>. The women's first game in the building was on November 26, 1982 against <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">Chicago</a>, and the men's inaugural game took place a day later against neighbor and rival <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a>. In March 1996, the building was rededicated to Ray Lavietes '36, a two-time basketball letterman who made a $2.1 million contribution to a second refurbishment project in 1995 and 1996.</p>

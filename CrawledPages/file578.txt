<p><b>Harvard Medical School</b> (<b>HMS</b>) is the graduate <a href="/wiki/Medical_school" title="Medical school">medical school</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. It is located in the <a href="/wiki/Longwood_Medical_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Longwood Medical Area">Longwood Medical Area</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mission_Hill,_Boston" title="Mission Hill, Boston">Mission Hill</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>.</p>
<p>There are approximately 2,900 full- and part-time voting faculty members consisting of assistant, associate, and full professors, and over 5,000 full or part-time, non-voting instructors.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Medical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harvard is the third-oldest medical school in the United States (after <a href="/wiki/Perelman_School_of_Medicine_at_the_University_of_Pennsylvania" title="Perelman School of Medicine at the University of Pennsylvania">Perelman School of Medicine at the University of Pennsylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University_College_of_Physicians_and_Surgeons" title="Columbia University College of Physicians and Surgeons">Columbia University College of Physicians and Surgeons</a>) and was founded by <a href="/wiki/John_Warren_(surgeon)" title="John Warren (surgeon)">John Warren</a> on September 19, 1782, with <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Waterhouse" title="Benjamin Waterhouse">Benjamin Waterhouse</a>, and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://hms.harvard.edu/about-hms/history-hms">Aaron Dexter</a>, "professor of chemistry and materia medica (pharmacology)."<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lectures were first held in the basement of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Hall" title="Harvard Hall">Harvard Hall</a>, then later in <a href="/wiki/Holden_Chapel" title="Holden Chapel">Holden Chapel</a>. Students paid no tuition, but purchased tickets to five or six daily lectures.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup> The first two students graduated in 1788.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1810 the school moved to <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>&#160;– first to what is now downtown Washington Street; then Mason Street (1816–1846, during which time it was sometimes referred to as the Massachusetts Medical College); North Grove Street (1847); Copley Square (1883); and finally its current location (the <a href="/wiki/Longwood_Medical_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Longwood Medical Area">Longwood Medical Area</a>) in 1906. In the Longwood campus there are five original marble-faced buildings of the quadrangle, which are used for laboratories, amphitheaters, and research space.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Charles_William_Eliot" title="Charles William Eliot">Charles William Eliot</a> became Harvard's president in 1869, he found the medical school in the worst condition of any part of the university; his reforms laid the groundwork for its transformation into one of the leading medical schools in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Innovations">Innovations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Medical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Innovations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harvard Medical School faculty have been associated with a number of important medical and public-health innovations:</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">
<ul>
<li>Introduction of smallpox vaccination to America</li>
<li>First use of <a href="/wiki/Anesthesia" title="Anesthesia">anesthesia</a> for pain control during surgery</li>
<li>The introduction of insulin to the US to treat <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes</a></li>
<li>Comprehending of the role of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B12</a> in treating anemia</li>
<li>Identification of <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme_A" title="Coenzyme A">coenzyme A</a> and understanding of proteins</li>
<li>Developing tissue culture methods for the <a href="/wiki/Poliovirus" title="Poliovirus">polio virus</a>, which paved the way for vaccines against polio</li>
<li>Mapping the visual system of the brain</li>
<li>Development of the first successful <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a> for <a href="/wiki/Childhood_leukemia" title="Childhood leukemia">childhood leukemia</a></li>
<li>Development of the first implantable cardiac pacemaker</li>
<li>Discovering the inheritance of immunity to infection; development of artificial skin for burn victims</li>
<li>The first successful heart valve surgery</li>
<li>The first successful human kidney transplant</li>
<li>The first reattachment of a severed human limb</li>
<li>Discovery of the genes that cause <a href="/wiki/Duchenne_muscular_dystrophy" title="Duchenne muscular dystrophy">Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntington%27s_disease" title="Huntington's disease">Huntington’s Disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig%27s_Disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Lou Gehrig's Disease">Lou Gehrig’s Disease</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer’s Disease</a>, among many others</li>
<li>Establishing the importance of tumor vascular supply (angiogenesis) and seeding the field of vascular biology</li>
<li>Discovery of the cause of <a href="/wiki/Pre-eclampsia" title="Pre-eclampsia">preeclampsia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadening_admissions">Broadening admissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Medical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Broadening admissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Women">Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Medical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>In mid-1847, Professor <a href="/wiki/Walter_Channing_(physician)" title="Walter Channing (physician)">Walter Channing</a>'s proposal that women be admitted to lectures and examinations was rejected by the <a href="/wiki/President_and_Fellows_of_Harvard_College" title="President and Fellows of Harvard College">President and Fellows of Harvard College</a>. Nonetheless, <a href="/wiki/Harriot_Kezia_Hunt" title="Harriot Kezia Hunt">Harriot Kezia Hunt</a> was soon after giving permission to attend medical lectures, but in 1850 this permission was withdrawn.</p>
<p>In 1866 two women with extensive medical education elsewhere applied but were denied admission. In 1867 a single faculty member's vote blocked the admission of <a href="/wiki/Susan_Dimock" title="Susan Dimock">Susan Dimock</a>. In 1872 Harvard declined a gift of $10,000 conditioned on medical school admitting women medical students on the same term as men. A similar offer of $50,000, by group of ten women including <a href="/wiki/Marie_Elizabeth_Zakrzewska" title="Marie Elizabeth Zakrzewska">Marie Elizabeth Zakrzewska</a>, was declined in 1882, a committee of five was appointed to study the matter. After the medical school moved from North Grove Street to Boylston Street in 1883, professor <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ingersoll_Bowditch" title="Henry Ingersoll Bowditch">Henry Ingersoll Bowditch</a>'s proposal that the North Grove Street premises be used for medical education for women was rejected.</p>
<p>In 1943 a dean's committee recommended the admission of women, the proportion of men and women being dependent solely on the qualifications of the applicants.<sup id="cite_ref-beecher_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beecher-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1945, the first class of women was admitted; projected benefits included helping male students learn to view women as equals, increasing the number of physicians in lower-paid specialties typically shunned by men, and replacing the weakest third of all-male classes with better-qualified women.<sup id="cite_ref-First_class_of_women_admitted_to_Harvard_Medical_School.2C_1945_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-First_class_of_women_admitted_to_Harvard_Medical_School.2C_1945-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1972 about one fifth of Harvard medical students were women.<sup id="cite_ref-beecher_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beecher-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="African-Americans">African-Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Medical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: African-Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1850 two black men, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Laing,_Jr." title="Daniel Laing, Jr.">Daniel Laing, Jr.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_H._Snowden" title="Isaac H. Snowden">Isaac H. Snowden</a>, were admitted to the school, but they were later expelled under pressure from faculty, and other students, who objected.</p>
<p>In 1968, in response to a petition signed by hundreds of medical students, the faculty established a commission on relations with the black community in Boston; at the time less than one percent of Harvard medical students were black. By 1973 the number of black students admitted had tripled, and by the next year it had quadrupled.<sup id="cite_ref-beecher_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beecher-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Curriculum">Curriculum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Medical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Curriculum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the early 20th century, the "block system", under which "each subject was treated intensively for a period and then dropped entirely", was eliminated.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup> In addition to the objective to test knowledge over memory, time was reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, Harvard introduced a new "Pathways" curriculum, intended to "foster active learning and critical thinking; earlier clinical experience; and advanced clinical and student-tailored basic/population science experiences that will provide customized pathways for every student."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Harvard-MIT_Division_of_Health_Sciences_and_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-MIT Division of Health Sciences and Technology">Harvard-MIT Division of Health Sciences and Technology</a> (HST) offers an alternative MD program with a stronger emphasis on biomedical research. The HST MD program is significantly smaller than the Pathways program, accepting only 30 applicants every year.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partners_Harvard_Medical_International">Partners Harvard Medical International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Medical_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Partners Harvard Medical International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harvard Medical School (HMS) has a medical-consulting arm, Partners Harvard Medical International (PHMI). PHMI has long-standing collaborative relationships with medical faculties at <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Maximilian_University_of_Munich" title="Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich">Ludwig Maximilians University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, Germany, <a href="/wiki/Alfaisal_University" title="Alfaisal University">Alfaisal University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_American_University" title="Lebanese American University">Lebanese American University</a> (LAU) in <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>. Other long-standing relationships include PHMI's work with <a href="/wiki/Asan_Medical_Center" title="Asan Medical Center">Asan Medical Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, South Korea, and <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Medical_and_Dental_University" title="Tokyo Medical and Dental University">Tokyo Medical and Dental University</a> in Japan. In 2007 PHMI began a 10‑year collaboration with Lebanese American University; in October 2009 LAU opened a new medical school with assistance from PHMI.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

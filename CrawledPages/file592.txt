<p><b>Paul Revere</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; December 21, 1734 <a href="/wiki/Old_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Style">O.S.</a>&#160;– May 10, 1818<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[N 1]</a></sup>) was an American <a href="/wiki/Silversmith" title="Silversmith">silversmith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraver</a>, early industrialist, and <a href="/wiki/Patriot_(American_Revolution)" title="Patriot (American Revolution)">Patriot</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. He is best known for his midnight ride to alert the colonial militia in April 1775 to the approach of <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British forces</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">battles of Lexington and Concord</a>, as dramatized in <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wadsworth_Longfellow" title="Henry Wadsworth Longfellow">Henry Wadsworth Longfellow</a>'s poem, "<a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere%27s_Ride" title="Paul Revere's Ride">Paul Revere's Ride</a>" (1861).</p>
<p>At age 41, Revere was a prosperous, established and prominent <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> silversmith. He had helped organize an <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_in_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Intelligence in the American Revolutionary War">intelligence and alarm</a> system to keep watch on the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> military. Revere later served as a Massachusetts militia officer, though his service ended after the <a href="/wiki/Penobscot_Expedition" title="Penobscot Expedition">Penobscot Expedition</a>, one of the most disastrous campaigns of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, for which he was absolved of blame.</p>
<p>Following the war, Revere returned to his silversmith trade. He used the profits from his expanding business to finance his work in iron casting, bronze bell and cannon casting, and the forging of copper bolts and spikes. In 1800 he became the first American to successfully roll copper into sheets for use as sheathing on naval vessels.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span></h2>
<p>Revere was born in the <a href="/wiki/North_End,_Boston" title="North End, Boston">North End</a> of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> on December 21, 1734, according to the <a href="/wiki/Old_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Style">Old Style</a> calendar then in use, or January 1, 1735, in the modern calendar.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber19987_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber19987-4">[3]</a></sup> His father, a French <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" class="mw-redirect" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a> born Apollos Rivoire, came to Boston at the age of 13 and was apprenticed to the silversmith <a href="/wiki/John_Coney_(silversmith)" title="John Coney (silversmith)">John Coney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber19987.E2.80.939_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber19987.E2.80.939-5">[4]</a></sup> By the time he married Deborah Hitchborn, a member of a long-standing Boston family that owned a small shipping wharf, in 1729, Rivoire had anglicized his name to Paul Revere. Their son, Paul Revere, was the third of 12 children and eventually the eldest surviving son.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1994297_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1994297-2">[2]</a></sup> Revere grew up in the environment of the extended Hitchborn family, and never learned his father's native language.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer19949_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer19949-6">[5]</a></sup> At 13 he left school and became an apprentice to his father. The silversmith trade afforded him connections with a cross-section of Boston society, which would serve him well when he became active in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199814.E2.80.9316_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199814.E2.80.9316-7">[6]</a></sup> As for religion, although his father attended <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> services, Revere was drawn to the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller201021_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller201021-8">[7]</a></sup> Revere eventually began attending the services of the political and provocative <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Mayhew" title="Jonathan Mayhew">Jonathan Mayhew</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Old_West_Church,_Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Old West Church, Boston, Massachusetts">West Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller201021_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller201021-8">[7]</a></sup> His father did not approve, and as a result father and son came to blows on one occasion. Revere relented and returned to his father's church, although he did become friends with Mayhew, and returned to the West Church in the late 1760s.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller201025.2C_103_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller201025.2C_103-9">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Revere's father died in 1754, when Paul was legally too young to officially be the master of the family silver shop.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199821_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199821-10">[9]</a></sup> In February 1756, during the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a> (the North American theater of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>), he enlisted in the provincial army. Possibly he made this decision because of the weak economy, since army service promised consistent pay.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199821.E2.80.9322_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199821.E2.80.9322-11">[10]</a></sup> Commissioned a second lieutenant in a provincial artillery regiment, he spent the summer at <a href="/wiki/Fort_William_Henry" title="Fort William Henry">Fort William Henry</a> at the southern end of <a href="/wiki/Lake_George_(New_York)" title="Lake George (New York)">Lake George</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> as part of an abortive plan for the capture of <a href="/wiki/Fort_St._Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort St. Frédéric">Fort St. Frédéric</a>. He did not stay long in the army, but returned to Boston and assumed control of the silver shop in his own name. On August 4, 1757, he married Sarah Orne (1736–1773); their first child was born eight months later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller201033_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller201033-12">[11]</a></sup> He and Sarah had eight children, but two died young, and only one, Mary, survived her father.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199415.2C_297_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199415.2C_297-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="1765–1774:_the_gathering_storm_of_revolution"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1765.E2.80.931774:_the_gathering_storm_of_revolution">1765–1774: the gathering storm of revolution</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Liberty" title="Sons of Liberty">Sons of Liberty</a></div>
<p>Revere's business began to suffer when the British economy entered a recession in the years following the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>, and declined further when the <a href="/wiki/Stamp_Act_1765" title="Stamp Act 1765">Stamp Act of 1765</a> resulted in a further downturn in the Massachusetts economy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199838.E2.80.9343_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199838.E2.80.9343-14">[13]</a></sup> Business was so poor that an attempt was made to attach his property in late 1765.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199820_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199820-15">[14]</a></sup> To help make ends meet he even took up <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>, a skill set he was taught by a practicing surgeon who lodged at a friend's house.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010104_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010104-16">[15]</a></sup> One client was Doctor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Warren" title="Joseph Warren">Joseph Warren</a>, a local physician and political opposition leader with whom Revere formed a close friendship.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello201055_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello201055-17">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998117_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998117-18">[17]</a></sup> Revere and Warren, in addition to having common political views, were also both active in the same local <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Masonic lodges</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199867_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199867-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Revere was not one of the "<a href="/wiki/Loyal_Nine" title="Loyal Nine">Loyal Nine</a>"—organizers of the earliest protests against the Stamp Act—he was well connected with its members, who were laborers and artisans.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199843_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199843-20">[19]</a></sup> Revere did not participate in some of the more raucous protests, such as the attack on the home of Lieutenant Governor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hutchinson_(governor)" title="Thomas Hutchinson (governor)">Thomas Hutchinson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199846.E2.80.9347_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199846.E2.80.9347-21">[20]</a></sup> In 1765, a group of militants who would become known as the "<a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Liberty" title="Sons of Liberty">Sons of Liberty</a>" formed, of which Revere was a member.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoss18911:112.E2.80.93115_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoss18911:112.E2.80.93115-22">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199836.2C_42_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199836.2C_42-23">[22]</a></sup> From 1765 on, in support of the dissident cause, he produced engravings and other artifacts with political themes. Among these engravings are a depiction of the arrival of British troops in 1768 (which he termed "an insolent parade") and a famous depiction of the March 1770 <a href="/wiki/Boston_Massacre" title="Boston Massacre">Boston Massacre</a> (see illustration). Although the latter was engraved by Revere and he included the inscription, "Engraved, Printed, &amp; Sold by Paul Revere Boston", it was modeled on a drawing by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Pelham_(engraver)" title="Henry Pelham (engraver)">Henry Pelham</a>, and Revere's engraving of the drawing was colored by a third man and printed by a fourth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199422_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199422-24">[23]</a></sup> Revere also produced a bowl commemorating the Massachusetts assembly's refusal to retract the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Circular_Letter" title="Massachusetts Circular Letter">Massachusetts Circular Letter</a>. (This letter, adopted in response to the 1767 <a href="/wiki/Townshend_Acts" title="Townshend Acts">Townshend Acts</a>, called for united colonial action against the acts. <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="George III of Great Britain">King George III</a> had issued a demand for its retraction.)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199422_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199422-24">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1770 Revere purchased a house on <a href="/wiki/North_Square_(Boston,_Massachusetts)" title="North Square (Boston, Massachusetts)">North Square</a> in Boston's North End. Now <a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere_House" title="Paul Revere House">a museum</a>, the house provided space for his growing family while he continued to maintain his shop at nearby Clark's Wharf.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber199871.E2.80.9372_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber199871.E2.80.9372-26">[25]</a></sup> Sarah died in 1773, and on October 10 of that year Revere married Rachel Walker (1745–1813). They had eight children, three of whom died young.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes1999485.E2.80.93487_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes1999485.E2.80.93487-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1773 the merchant ship <i>Dartmouth</i> arrived in Boston harbor carrying the first shipment of tea made under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Act" title="Tea Act">Tea Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoss18911:119_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoss18911:119-28">[27]</a></sup> This act authorized the <a href="/wiki/British_East_India_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="British East India Company">British East India Company</a> to ship tea (of which it had huge surpluses due to colonial boycotts organized in response to the Townshend Acts) directly to the colonies, bypassing colonial merchants. Passage of the act prompted calls for renewed protests against the tea shipments, on which Townshend duties were still levied.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlexander120_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlexander120-29">[28]</a></sup> Revere and Warren, as members of the informal <a href="/wiki/North_End_Caucus" class="mw-redirect" title="North End Caucus">North End Caucus</a>, organized a watch over the <i>Dartmouth</i> to prevent the unloading of the tea. Revere took his turns on guard duty,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010163_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010163-30">[29]</a></sup> and was one of the ringleaders in the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party" title="Boston Tea Party">Boston Tea Party</a> of December 16, when colonists (some disguised as Indians) dumped tea from the <i>Dartmouth</i> and two other ships into the harbor.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010165_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010165-31">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>From December 1773 to November 1775, Revere served as a courier for the Boston <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Safety_(American_Revolution)" class="mw-redirect" title="Committee of Safety (American Revolution)">Committee of Public Safety</a>, traveling to New York and Philadelphia to report on the political unrest in Boston. Research has documented 18 such rides. Notice of some of them was published in Massachusetts newspapers, and British authorities received further intelligence of them from Loyalist Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199427.3B_54.3B_Appendix_C_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199427.3B_54.3B_Appendix_C-32">[31]</a></sup> In 1774, his cousin John on the island of <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a> wrote to Paul that John had seen reports of Paul's role as an "express" (courier) in London newspapers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998101_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998101-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1774, the military governor of Massachusetts, General <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gage" title="Thomas Gage">Thomas Gage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Government_Act" title="Massachusetts Government Act">dissolved the provincial assembly</a> on orders from Great Britain. Governor Gage also <a href="/wiki/Boston_Port_Act" title="Boston Port Act">closed the port</a> of Boston and all over the city forced private citizens to quarter (provide lodging for) soldiers in their homes.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[N 2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this time, Revere and a group of 30 "mechanics" began meeting in secret at his favorite haunt, the <i>Green Dragon</i>, to coordinate the gathering and dissemination of intelligence by "watching the Movements of British Soldiers".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010181_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010181-35">[33]</a></sup> Around this time Revere regularly contributed politically charged engravings to the recently founded Patriot monthly, <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_American_Magazine" title="Royal American Magazine">Royal American Magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoss18911:83.E2.80.93100_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoss18911:83.E2.80.93100-36">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>He rode to <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_New_Hampshire" title="Portsmouth, New Hampshire">Portsmouth, New Hampshire</a> in December 1774 upon rumors of an impending landing of British troops there, a journey known in history as the <a href="/wiki/Powder_Alarm#Portsmouth_Alarm" title="Powder Alarm">Portsmouth Alarm</a>. Although the rumors were false, his ride sparked a rebel success by provoking locals to <a href="/wiki/Fort_William_and_Mary#1774_Raid" title="Fort William and Mary">raid Fort William and Mary</a>, defended by just six soldiers, for its gunpowder supply.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199452.E2.80.9357_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199452.E2.80.9357-37">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="&quot;Midnight_Ride&quot;"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Midnight_Ride.22">"Midnight Ride"</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"The British are coming" redirects here. For the Weezer song, see <a href="/wiki/The_British_Are_Coming_(song)" title="The British Are Coming (song)">The British Are Coming (song)</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">Battles of Lexington and Concord</a></div>
<p>When British Army activity on April 7, 1775 suggested the possibility of troop movements, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Warren" title="Joseph Warren">Joseph Warren</a> sent Revere to warn the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Provincial_Congress" title="Massachusetts Provincial Congress">Massachusetts Provincial Congress</a>, then sitting in <a href="/wiki/Concord,_Massachusetts" title="Concord, Massachusetts">Concord</a>, the site of one of the larger caches of Patriot military supplies. After receiving the warning, Concord residents began moving the military supplies away from the town.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010186_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010186-38">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>One week later, on April 14, General Gage received instructions from <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_(United_Kingdom)" title="Secretary of State (United Kingdom)">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Legge,_2nd_Earl_of_Dartmouth" title="William Legge, 2nd Earl of Dartmouth">William Legge, Earl of Dartmouth</a> (dispatched on January 27), to disarm the rebels, who were known to have hidden weapons in Concord, among other locations, and to imprison the rebellion's leaders, especially <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Adams" title="Samuel Adams">Samuel Adams</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Hancock" title="John Hancock">John Hancock</a>. Dartmouth gave Gage considerable discretion in his commands.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199475.E2.80.9376_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199475.E2.80.9376-39">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooks199937.E2.80.9338_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooks199937.E2.80.9338-40">[38]</a></sup> Gage issued orders to Lieutenant Colonel <a href="/wiki/Francis_Smith_(British_officer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Francis Smith (British officer)">Francis Smith</a> to proceed from Boston "with utmost expedition and <a href="/wiki/Military_secret" class="mw-redirect" title="Military secret">secrecy</a> to Concord, where you will seize and destroy... all Military stores.... But you will take care that the soldiers do not plunder the inhabitants or hurt private property." Gage did not issue written orders for the arrest of rebel leaders, as he feared doing so might spark an uprising.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199485_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199485-41">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>Between 9 and 10 p.m. on the night of April 18, 1775, Joseph Warren told Revere and <a href="/wiki/William_Dawes" title="William Dawes">William Dawes</a> that the king's troops were about to embark in boats from Boston bound for Cambridge and the road to Lexington and Concord. Warren's intelligence suggested that the most likely objectives of the regulars' movements later that night would be the capture of Adams and Hancock. They did not worry about the possibility of regulars marching to Concord, since the supplies at Concord were safe, but they did think their leaders in Lexington were unaware of the potential danger that night. Revere and Dawes were sent out to warn them and to alert colonial militias in nearby towns.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooks199941.E2.80.9342_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooks199941.E2.80.9342-42">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoatner1975622_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoatner1975622-43">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the days before April 18, Revere had instructed <a href="/wiki/Robert_Newman_(sexton)" title="Robert Newman (sexton)">Robert Newman</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sexton_(office)" title="Sexton (office)">sexton</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Old_North_Church" title="Old North Church">North Church</a>, to send a signal by lantern to alert colonists in <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlestown, Massachusetts">Charlestown</a> as to the movements of the troops when the information became known. In what is well known today by the phrase "one if by land, two if by sea", one lantern in the steeple would signal the army's choice of the land route while two lanterns would signal the route "by water" across the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a> (the movements would ultimately take the water route, and therefore two lanterns were placed in the steeple).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer199499_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer199499-44">[42]</a></sup> Revere first gave instructions to send the signal to Charlestown. He then crossed the Charles River by rowboat, slipping past the British warship <a href="/wiki/HMS_Somerset_(1748)" title="HMS Somerset (1748)">HMS <i>Somerset</i></a> at anchor. Crossings were banned at that hour, but Revere safely landed in Charlestown and rode to Lexington, avoiding a British patrol and later warning almost every house along the route. The Charlestown colonists dispatched additional riders to the north.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoatner1975622_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoatner1975622-43">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooks199942.E2.80.9344_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooks199942.E2.80.9344-45">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riding through present-day <a href="/wiki/Somerville,_Massachusetts" title="Somerville, Massachusetts">Somerville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medford,_Massachusetts" title="Medford, Massachusetts">Medford</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Massachusetts" title="Arlington, Massachusetts">Arlington</a>, Revere warned patriots along his route, many of whom set out on horseback to deliver warnings of their own. By the end of the night there were probably as many as 40 riders throughout <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_County,_Massachusetts" title="Middlesex County, Massachusetts">Middlesex County</a> carrying the news of the army's advance. Revere did not shout the phrase later attributed to him ("The British are coming!"): his mission depended on secrecy, the countryside was filled with British army patrols, and most of the Massachusetts colonists (who were predominantly <a href="/wiki/English_American" class="mw-redirect" title="English American">English</a> in ethnic origin)<sup id="cite_ref-1790census_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1790census-46">[44]</a></sup> still considered themselves British.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1994110_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1994110-47">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcDonaldMcDonald1980180_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcDonaldMcDonald1980180-48">[46]</a></sup> Revere's warning, according to eyewitness accounts of the ride and Revere's own descriptions, was "The <a href="/wiki/Regular_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Regular Army">Regulars</a> are coming out."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERevere1961_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERevere1961-49">[47]</a></sup> Revere arrived in Lexington around midnight, with Dawes arriving about a half-hour later. They met with Samuel Adams and John Hancock, who were spending the night with Hancock's relatives (in what is now called the <a href="/wiki/Hancock%E2%80%93Clarke_House" title="Hancock–Clarke House">Hancock–Clarke House</a>), and they spent a great deal of time discussing plans of action upon receiving the news. They believed that the forces leaving the city were too large for the sole task of arresting two men and that Concord was the main target.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrooks199950_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooks199950-50">[48]</a></sup> The Lexington men dispatched riders to the surrounding towns, and Revere and Dawes continued along the road to Concord accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Prescott" title="Samuel Prescott">Samuel Prescott</a>, a doctor who happened to be in Lexington "returning from a lady friend's house at the awkward hour of 1 a.m."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoatner1975622_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoatner1975622-43">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurrin2002205_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurrin2002205-51">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Revere, Dawes, and Prescott were detained by a British Army patrol in <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Massachusetts" title="Lincoln, Massachusetts">Lincoln</a> at a roadblock on the way to Concord.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoatner1975622_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoatner1975622-43">[41]</a></sup> Prescott jumped his horse over a wall and escaped into the woods; he eventually reached Concord. Dawes also escaped, though he fell off his horse not long after and did not complete the ride.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1994131.E2.80.93132.2C_144_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1994131.E2.80.93132.2C_144-52">[50]</a></sup></p>

<p>Revere was captured and questioned by the British soldiers at gunpoint. He told them of the army's movement from Boston, and that British army troops would be in some danger if they approached Lexington, because of the large number of hostile militia gathered there. He and other captives taken by the patrol were still escorted east toward Lexington, until about a half mile from Lexington they heard a gunshot. The British major demanded Revere explain the gunfire, and Revere replied it was a signal to "alarm the country". As the group drew closer to Lexington, the town bell began to clang rapidly, upon which one of the captives proclaimed to the British soldiers "The bell's a'ringing! The town's alarmed, and you're all dead men!"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1994136_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1994136-53">[51]</a></sup> The British soldiers gathered and decided not to press further towards Lexington but instead to free the prisoners and head back to warn their commanders.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1994133.E2.80.93136.2C_142.E2.80.93148_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1994133.E2.80.93136.2C_142.E2.80.93148-54">[52]</a></sup> The British confiscated Revere's horse and rode off to warn the approaching army column. Revere walked to Rev. <a href="/wiki/Jonas_Clarke" title="Jonas Clarke">Jonas Clarke</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hancock%E2%80%93Clarke_House" title="Hancock–Clarke House">house</a>, where Hancock and Adams were staying. As the battle on <a href="/wiki/Lexington_Green" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexington Green">Lexington Green</a> unfolded, Revere assisted Hancock and his family in their escape from Lexington, helping to carry a trunk of Hancock's papers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010198.E2.80.93200_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010198.E2.80.93200-55">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ride of the three men triggered a flexible system of "alarm and muster" that had been carefully developed months before, in reaction to the colonists' impotent response to the <a href="/wiki/Powder_Alarm" title="Powder Alarm">Powder Alarm</a> of September 1774. This system was an improved version of an old network of widespread notification and fast deployment of local militia forces in times of emergency. The colonists had periodically used this system all the way back to the early years of <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_Wars" title="American Indian Wars">Indian wars</a> in the colony, before it fell into disuse in the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>. In addition to other express riders delivering messages, bells, drums, alarm guns, bonfires, and a trumpet were used for rapid communication from town to town, notifying the rebels in dozens of eastern Massachusetts villages that they should muster their militias because the regulars in numbers greater than 500 were leaving Boston with possible hostile intentions. This system was so effective that people in towns 25 miles (40&#160;km) from Boston were aware of the army's movements while they were still unloading boats in Cambridge.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1994138.E2.80.93145_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1994138.E2.80.93145-56">[54]</a></sup> Unlike in the Powder Alarm, the alarm raised by the three riders successfully allowed the militia to confront the British troops in Concord, and then harry them all the way back to Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998103.E2.80.93111_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998103.E2.80.93111-57">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_Wadsworth_Longfellow" title="Henry Wadsworth Longfellow">Henry Wadsworth Longfellow</a> popularized Paul Revere in <i>The Midnight Ride of Paul Revere</i>, a poem first published in 1863 as part of <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_of_a_Wayside_Inn" title="Tales of a Wayside Inn">Tales of a Wayside Inn</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_years">War years</span></h2>
<p>Because Boston <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">was besieged</a> after the battles of Lexington and Concord, Revere could not return to the city, which was now firmly in British hands. He boarded in <a href="/wiki/Watertown,_Massachusetts" title="Watertown, Massachusetts">Watertown</a>, where he was eventually joined by Rachel and most of his children (Paul Jr., then 15, remained in Boston to mind the family properties).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998115_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998115-59">[57]</a></sup> After he was denied a commission in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>, he tried to find other ways to be useful to the rebel cause. He was retained by the provincial congress as a courier, and he printed local currency which the congress used to pay the troops around Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010201.E2.80.93208_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010201.E2.80.93208-60">[58]</a></sup></p>

<p>Since there was a desperate shortage of gunpowder, the provincial congress decided in November 1775 to send him to <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> to study the working of the only <a href="/wiki/Powder_mill" title="Powder mill">powder mill</a> in the colonies, in the hopes that he might be able to build a second one in Massachusetts. Revere called on the mill's owner, <a href="/wiki/Oswald_Eve" title="Oswald Eve">Oswald Eve</a>, armed with a letter from Continental Congressmen <a href="/wiki/Robert_Morris_(financier)" title="Robert Morris (financier)">Robert Morris</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Dickinson_(delegate)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Dickinson (delegate)">John Dickinson</a> asking Eve to "Chearfully &amp; from Public Spirited Motives give Mr. Revere such information as will inable him to Conduct the business on his return home."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello201062_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello201062-61">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGettemy1905169_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGettemy1905169-62">[60]</a></sup> Eve showed Revere around the mill, but refused to give him detailed drawings unless he was first paid a substantial bribe. Despite this chilly reception, Revere was able to discern useful information from the visit. He also acquired, through the work of Samuel Adams, plans for another powder mill. This information enabled Revere to set up a powder mill at Stoughton (present-day <a href="/wiki/Canton,_Massachusetts" title="Canton, Massachusetts">Canton</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello201062_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello201062-61">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998148_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998148-63">[61]</a></sup> The mill produced tons of gunpowder for the Patriot cause.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010214_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010214-64">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Revere's friend and compatriot Joseph Warren was killed in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bunker_Hill" title="Battle of Bunker Hill">Battle of Bunker Hill</a> on June 17, 1775.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010208_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010208-65">[63]</a></sup> Because soldiers killed in battle were often buried in <a href="/wiki/Mass_grave" title="Mass grave">mass graves</a> without ceremony, Warren's grave was unmarked. On March 21, 1776, several days after the British army left Boston, Revere, Warren's brothers, and a few friends went to the battlefield and found a grave containing two bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKetchum1999195_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKetchum1999195-66">[64]</a></sup> After being buried for nine months, Warren's face was unrecognizable, but Revere was able to identify Warren's body because he had placed a false tooth in Warren's mouth, and recognized the wire he had used for fastening it. Warren was given a proper funeral and reburied in a marked grave.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010215_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010215-67">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Militia_service">Militia service</span></h3>
<p>Upon returning to Boston in 1776, Revere was commissioned a <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">major</a> of <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> in the Massachusetts militia in that April, and transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> a month later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello201080_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello201080-68">[66]</a></sup> In November he was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel" title="Lieutenant colonel">lieutenant colonel</a>, and was stationed at <a href="/wiki/Castle_William" class="mw-redirect" title="Castle William">Castle William</a>, defending Boston harbor. He was generally second or third in the chain of command, and on several occasions he was given command of the fort.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello201080_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello201080-68">[66]</a></sup> He applied his engineering skills to maintaining the fort's armaments, even designing and building a <a href="/wiki/Caliper" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliper">caliper</a> to accurately measure cannonballs and cannon bore holes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello201080_68-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello201080-68">[66]</a></sup> The service at Castle William was relatively isolated, and personality friction prompted some men to file complaints against Revere.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010217_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010217-69">[67]</a></sup> The boredom was alleviated in late August 1777 when Revere was sent with a troop of soldiers to escort prisoners taken in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bennington" title="Battle of Bennington">Battle of Bennington</a> to Boston, where they were confined on board prison ships,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGettemy1906148.E2.80.93151_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGettemy1906148.E2.80.93151-70">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDrake1899128_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDrake1899128-71">[69]</a></sup> and again in September when he was briefly deployed to <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGettemy1906152.E2.80.93153_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGettemy1906152.E2.80.93153-72">[70]</a></sup></p>

<p>In August 1778 Revere's regiment served in a combined Franco-American expedition whose objective was to capture the British base at <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes1999343_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes1999343-73">[71]</a></sup> His regiment was responsible for erecting and maintaining artillery batteries on <a href="/wiki/Aquidneck_Island" title="Aquidneck Island">Aquidneck Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998132_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998132-74">[72]</a></sup> The attempt was abandoned by the French when their fleet was scattered in a storm, and Revere's regiment returned to Boston before the British sortied from Newport to force the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rhode_Island" title="Battle of Rhode Island">Battle of Rhode Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998133_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998133-75">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Penobscot_disaster">Penobscot disaster</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Penobscot_Expedition" title="Penobscot Expedition">Penobscot Expedition</a></div>
<p>The British in June 1779 established a new base on <a href="/wiki/Penobscot_Bay" title="Penobscot Bay">Penobscot Bay</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> (which was then part of Massachusetts).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010220_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010220-76">[74]</a></sup> Massachusetts authorities called out the militia, pressed into service available shipping, and organized <a href="/wiki/Penobscot_Expedition" title="Penobscot Expedition">a major expedition</a> to dislodge the British.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998134.E2.80.93135_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998134.E2.80.93135-77">[75]</a></sup> The expedition was a complete fiasco: its land and naval commanders squabbled over control of the expedition, and could not agree on strategy or tactics. The arrival of British reinforcements led to the destruction of the entire Massachusetts fleet.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998135.E2.80.93136_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998135.E2.80.93136-78">[76]</a></sup> Revere commanded the artillery units for the expedition, and was responsible for organizing the artillery train.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010224.E2.80.93225_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010224.E2.80.93225-79">[77]</a></sup> He participated in the taking of Bank's Island, from which artillery batteries could reach the British ships anchored before <a href="/wiki/Fort_George_(Castine,_Maine)" title="Fort George (Castine, Maine)">Fort George</a>. He next oversaw the transport of the guns from Bank's Island to a new position on the heights of the Bagaduce Peninsula that commanded the fort.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010229_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010229-80">[78]</a></sup> Although Revere was in favor of storming the fort, Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Lovell" title="Solomon Lovell">Solomon Lovell</a> opted for a siege instead. After further disagreements on how to proceed between Lovell and fleet commander <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Saltonstall" title="Dudley Saltonstall">Dudley Saltonstall</a>, Lovell decided to return to the transports on August 12, a decision supported by Revere.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998136_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998136-81">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Late the next day British sails were spotted. A mad scramble ensued, and on the 14th the fleet was in retreat heading up the <a href="/wiki/Penobscot_River" title="Penobscot River">Penobscot River</a>. Revere and his men were put ashore with their stores, and their transports destroyed. At one point Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Peleg_Wadsworth" title="Peleg Wadsworth">Peleg Wadsworth</a> ordered Revere to send his barge in an attempt to recover a ship drifting toward the enemy position. Revere at first resisted, but eventually complied, and Wadsworth told him to expect formal charges over the affair.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010234.E2.80.93236_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010234.E2.80.93236-82">[80]</a></sup> The incident separated Revere from his men. Moving overland, he eventually managed to regroup most of his troops, and returned to Boston on August 26. A variety of charges were made against Revere, some of which were exaggerated assignments of blame<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998136.E2.80.93138_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998136.E2.80.93138-83">[81]</a></sup> made by enemies he had made in his command at Castle William. The initial hearings on the matter in September 1779 were inconclusive, but he was asked to resign his post.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998136.E2.80.93138_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998136.E2.80.93138-83">[81]</a></sup> He repeatedly sought a full <a href="/wiki/Court-martial" title="Court-martial">court-martial</a> to clear his name, but it was not until February 1782 that a court martial heard the issue, exonerating him.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010238.E2.80.93239_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010238.E2.80.93239-84">[82]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETriber1998139_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETriber1998139-85">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_and_social_connections">Business and social connections</span></h3>

<p>During the Revolutionary War, Revere continued his efforts to move upwards in society into the gentry. After his failed efforts to become a military officer he attempted to become a merchant, but was hindered by a number of factors: while he was a fairly well-off member of the artisan class, he did not have the resources to afford the goods he would have sold as a merchant, nor were lenders in England willing to lend him the required startup capital. Other American merchants of the time collaborated with colleagues in England. However, Revere's inexperience as a merchant meant that he had not yet established such relationships and was not able to communicate as effectively on unfamiliar matters. Another factor preventing Revere’s success as a merchant was the economic climate of the time period after the war known as the <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Critical_Period" title="America's Critical Period">Critical Period</a>; while the colonies had seen a time of economic growth before the war, the colonies experienced a severe post-war depression, constraining the overall success of his business.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello201094_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello201094-86">[84]</a></sup></p>

<p>While Revere struggled as a merchant, his success as a silversmith enabled him to pursue and leverage more advanced technological developments for the purposes of mass production. For example, <a href="/wiki/Rolling_(metalworking)" title="Rolling (metalworking)">rolling</a> mills greatly improved the productivity of his silver shop and enabled his business to move further away from manufacturing high-end customized products in order to focus instead on the production of a more standardized set of goods.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010107.E2.80.93110_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010107.E2.80.93110-87">[85]</a></sup> In the 18th century, the standard of living continuously improved in America, as genteel goods became increasingly available to the masses.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFederhen2010153_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFederhen2010153-88">[86]</a></sup> Revere responded particularly well to this trend because his business was not solely manufacturing custom, high end purchases. Smaller products like teaspoons and buckles accounted for the majority of his work, allowing him to build a broad customer base.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFederhen2010154_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFederhen2010154-89">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>Revere's increased efficiency left financial and human resources available for the exploration of other products, which was essential to overcoming the fluctuating post-war economic climate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFalino200177_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFalino200177-90">[88]</a></sup> In addition to increasing production, the flatting mill enabled Revere to move towards a more managerial position.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010111.E2.80.93114_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010111.E2.80.93114-91">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Later_years:_entrepreneurship,_manufacturing,_and_politics"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years:_entrepreneurship.2C_manufacturing.2C_and_politics">Later years: entrepreneurship, manufacturing, and politics</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PaulRevereByGilbertStuart.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/PaulRevereByGilbertStuart.jpeg/170px-PaulRevereByGilbertStuart.jpeg" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/PaulRevereByGilbertStuart.jpeg/255px-PaulRevereByGilbertStuart.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/PaulRevereByGilbertStuart.jpeg/340px-PaulRevereByGilbertStuart.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="800" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PaulRevereByGilbertStuart.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1813 portrait of Revere by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Stuart" title="Gilbert Stuart">Gilbert Stuart</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the war, Revere became interested in metal work beyond gold and silver. By 1788 he had invested some of the profits from his growing silverworking trade to construct a large <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a>, which would allow him to work with larger quantities of metals at higher temperatures. He soon opened an iron foundry in Boston's <a href="/wiki/North_End,_Boston" title="North End, Boston">North End</a> that produced utilitarian cast iron items such as stove backs, fireplace tools, and window weights, marketed to a broad segment of Boston's population. <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010151.E2.80.93155_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010151.E2.80.93155-92">[90]</a></sup> Many of Revere’s business practices changed when he expanded his practice into ironworking, because he transitioned from just being an artisan to also being an entrepreneur and a manager. In order to make this transition successfully, Revere had to invest substantial quantities of investment capital and time in his foundry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010137_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010137-93">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technological_practices">Technological practices</span></h3>
<p>The quasi-<a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">industrialization</a> of his practice set Revere apart from his competition. “Revere’s rapid foundry success resulted from fortuitous timing, innate technical aptitude, thorough research, and the casting experience he gained from silverworking.”<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010154_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010154-94">[92]</a></sup> This technical proficiency allowed Revere to optimize his work and adapt to a new technological and entrepreneurial model. Revere’s location also benefited his endeavors. Revere was entering the field of iron casting in a time when New England cities were becoming centers of industry. The nature of technological advancement was such that many skilled entrepreneurs in a number of fields worked together, in what is known by <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Rosenberg" title="Nathan Rosenberg">Nathan Rosenberg</a> as <a href="/wiki/Technological_convergence" title="Technological convergence">technological convergence</a>, by which a number of companies work together on challenges in order to spur advances.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010252_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010252-95">[93]</a></sup> By accessing the knowledge of other nearby metal workers, Revere was able to successfully explore and master new technologies throughout his career.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_practices">Labor practices</span></h3>
<p>One of the biggest changes for Revere in his new business was organization of labor. In his earlier days, Revere primarily utilized the <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprenticeship</a> model standard for artisan shops at this time, but as his business expanded he hired employees (wage laborers) to work for his foundry. Many manufacturers of the era found this transition from master to employer difficult because many employees at the onset of the Industrial Revolution identified themselves as <a href="/wiki/Skilled_workers" class="mw-redirect" title="Skilled workers">skilled workers</a>, and thus wanted to be treated with the respect and autonomy accorded to <a href="/wiki/Artisans" class="mw-redirect" title="Artisans">artisans</a>. An artisan himself, Revere managed to avoid many of these labor conflicts by adopting a system of employment that still held trappings of the craft system in the form of worker freedoms such as work hour flexibility, wages in line with skill levels, and liquor on the job.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010276.2C_146_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010276.2C_146-96">[94]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Manufacturing:_church_bells,_cannon,_and_copper_products"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing:_church_bells.2C_cannon.2C_and_copper_products">Manufacturing: church bells, cannon, and copper products</span></h3>
<p>After mastering the iron casting process and realizing substantial profits from this new product line, Revere identified a burgeoning market for church bells in the religious revival known as the <a href="/wiki/Second_Great_Awakening" title="Second Great Awakening">Second Great Awakening</a> that followed the war. Beginning in 1792 he became one of America's best-known <a href="/wiki/Revere_Bells" class="mw-redirect" title="Revere Bells">bell casters</a>, working with sons Paul Jr. and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Warren_Revere_(businessman)" title="Joseph Warren Revere (businessman)">Joseph Warren Revere</a> in the firm <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Revere_%26_Sons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Paul Revere &amp; Sons (page does not exist)">Paul Revere &amp; Sons</a>. This firm cast the first bell made in Boston and ultimately produced hundreds of bells, a number of which remain in operation to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010168.E2.80.93171_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010168.E2.80.93171-97">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1794, Revere decided to take the next step in the evolution of his business, expanding his bronze casting work by learning to cast cannon for the federal government, state governments, and private clients. Although the government often had trouble paying him on time, its large orders inspired him to deepen his contracting and seek additional product lines of interest to the military.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010179.E2.80.93184_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010179.E2.80.93184-98">[96]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1795, a growing percentage of his foundry's business came from a new product, copper bolts, spikes, and other fittings that he sold to merchants and the Boston naval yard for ship construction. In 1801, Revere became a pioneer in the production of <a href="/wiki/Copper_sheathing" title="Copper sheathing">rolled copper</a>, opening North America's first copper mill south of Boston in <a href="/wiki/Canton,_Massachusetts" title="Canton, Massachusetts">Canton</a>. Copper from the <a href="/wiki/Revere_Copper_Company" title="Revere Copper Company">Revere Copper Company</a> was used to cover the original wooden dome of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_State_House" title="Massachusetts State House">Massachusetts State House</a> in 1802. His copper and brass works eventually grew, through sale and corporate merger, into a large corporation, Revere Copper and Brass, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010331.E2.80.93332_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010331.E2.80.93332-99">[97]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steps_towards_standardized_production">Steps towards standardized production</span></h3>
<p>During his earlier days as an artisan, especially when working with silver products, Revere produced "bespoke" or customized goods. As he shifted to ironworking, he found the need to produce more standardized products, because this made production cheaper.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010155_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010155-100">[98]</a></sup> To achieve the beginnings of standardization, Revere used identical molds for casting, especially in the fabrication of mass-produced items such as stoves, ovens, frames, and chimney backs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010141_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010141-101">[99]</a></sup> However, Revere did not totally embrace uniform production. For example, his bells and cannons were all unique products: these large objects required extensive fine-tuning and customization, and the small number of bells and cannon minimized the potential benefits of standardizing them.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010301_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010301-102">[100]</a></sup> In addition, even the products that he made in large quantities could not be truly standardized due to technological and skill limitations. His products were rarely (if ever) identical, but his processes were well systematized. “He came to realize that the foundry oven melded the characteristics of tools and machines: it required skilled labor and could be used in a flexible manner to produce different products, but an expert could produce consistent output by following a standard set of production practices.”<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartello2010154_94-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartello2010154-94">[92]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freemasonry">Freemasonry</span></h3>
<p>Revere was the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Master_(Masonic)" title="Grand Master (Masonic)">Grand Master</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Freemason" class="mw-redirect" title="Freemason">Freemasons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Lodge_of_Massachusetts" title="Grand Lodge of Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> when a box containing an assemblage of commemorative items was deposited under the cornerstone of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_State_House" title="Massachusetts State House">Massachusetts State House</a> on 4 July 1795 by <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Massachusetts" title="Governor of Massachusetts">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Adams" title="Samuel Adams">Samuel Adams</a>, assisted by Grand Master Revere and Deputy Grand Master, Colonel <a href="/wiki/William_Scollay" title="William Scollay">William Scollay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[101]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_final_years">Politics and final years</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Revere_Memorial,_Granary_Burying_Ground,_Boston,_Massachusetts.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Paul_Revere_Memorial%2C_Granary_Burying_Ground%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.jpg/170px-Paul_Revere_Memorial%2C_Granary_Burying_Ground%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.jpg" width="170" height="279" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Paul_Revere_Memorial%2C_Granary_Burying_Ground%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.jpg/255px-Paul_Revere_Memorial%2C_Granary_Burying_Ground%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Paul_Revere_Memorial%2C_Granary_Burying_Ground%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.jpg/340px-Paul_Revere_Memorial%2C_Granary_Burying_Ground%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2233" data-file-height="3661" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Revere_Memorial,_Granary_Burying_Ground,_Boston,_Massachusetts.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Paul Revere's grave site in the <a href="/wiki/Granary_Burying_Ground" title="Granary Burying Ground">Granary Burying Ground</a></div>

<p>Revere remained politically active throughout his life. His business plans in the late 1780s were often stymied by a shortage of adequate money in circulation. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a>'s national policies regarding banks and industrialization exactly matched his dreams, and he became an ardent <a href="/wiki/Federalist_Party" title="Federalist Party">Federalist</a> committed to building a robust economy and a powerful nation. Of particular interest to Revere was the question of protective tariffs; he and his son sent a petition to Congress in 1808 asking for protection for his sheet copper business.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[102]</a></sup> He continued to participate in local discussions of political issues even after his retirement in 1811, and in 1814 circulated a petition offering the government the services of Boston's artisans in protecting Boston during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010257_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010257-105">[103]</a></sup> Revere died on May 10, 1818, at the age of 83, at his home on Charter Street in Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010258_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010258-106">[104]</a></sup> Originally buried at the <a href="/wiki/Old_North_Church" title="Old North Church">Christ Church Cemetery</a> in Boston,<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[105]</a></sup> he is now buried in the <a href="/wiki/Granary_Burying_Ground" title="Granary Burying Ground">Granary Burying Ground</a> on <a href="/wiki/Tremont_Street" title="Tremont Street">Tremont Street</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[106]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPRMA198833_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPRMA198833-109">[107]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>
<p>After Revere's death, the family business was taken over by his oldest surviving son, Joseph Warren Revere.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller2010255.2C_260_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller2010255.2C_260-110">[108]</a></sup> The copper works founded in 1801 continues today as the Revere Copper Company, with manufacturing divisions in <a href="/wiki/Rome,_New_York" title="Rome, New York">Rome, New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Bedford,_Massachusetts" title="New Bedford, Massachusetts">New Bedford, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[109]</a></sup></p>
<p>Revere's original silverware, engravings, and other works are highly regarded today, and can be found on display in museums including the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts,_Boston" title="Museum of Fine Arts, Boston">Museum of Fine Arts, Boston</a><sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[110]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[111]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Revere_Bell" title="Revere Bell">Revere Bell</a>, presented in 1843 to the <a href="/wiki/St_Andrew%27s_Cathedral,_Singapore" title="St Andrew's Cathedral, Singapore">Church of St. Andrew</a> in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> by his daughter, Mrs. Maria Revere Balestier, wife of American <a href="/wiki/Consul_(representative)#United_States_consuls_in_the_early_years" title="Consul (representative)">consul</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Balestier" title="Joseph Balestier">Joseph Balestier</a>, is now displayed in the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Singapore" title="National Museum of Singapore">National Museum of Singapore</a>. This is the only bell cast by the Revere foundry that is outside the United States. For a time, it was displayed behind velvet ropes in the foyer of the United States Embassy in Singapore.<sup id="cite_ref-herbold_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herbold-114">[112]</a></sup></p>
<p>The communities of <a href="/wiki/Revere,_Massachusetts" title="Revere, Massachusetts">Revere, Massachusetts</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchmidt20027_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchmidt20027-115">[113]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Revere,_Minnesota" title="Revere, Minnesota">Revere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUpham1920451_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUpham1920451-116">[114]</a></sup> bear his name, as do <a href="/wiki/Revere_Beach" title="Revere Beach">Revere Beach</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchmidt20027_115-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchmidt20027-115">[113]</a></sup> in Revere, Massachusetts, Revere Avenue in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">The Bronx</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcNamara1978_117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcNamara1978-117">[115]</a></sup> Paul Revere Road in <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Massachusetts" title="Arlington, Massachusetts">Arlington, Massachusetts</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-National_Lancers_118-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Lancers-118">[116]</a></sup> and Paul Revere Apartments<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">[117]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>.</p>
<p>A 25-cent 1958 U.S. <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamp</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Issue" title="Liberty Issue">Liberty Series</a> honors Paul Revere, featuring the portrait by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Stuart" title="Gilbert Stuart">Gilbert Stuart</a>. He also appears on the $5,000 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Treasury_security#Series_EE" title="United States Treasury security">Series EE U.S. Savings Bond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">[118]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rock group <a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere_and_the_Raiders" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Revere and the Raiders">Paul Revere and the Raiders</a> had considerable popularity from the middle 1960s through the early 1970s. The band's namesake and organist was born Paul Revere Dick, named after Revere.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">[119]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Longfellow's_poem"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Longfellow.27s_poem">Longfellow's poem</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Revere_1958_Issue-25c.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Paul_Revere_1958_Issue-25c.jpg/170px-Paul_Revere_1958_Issue-25c.jpg" width="170" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Paul_Revere_1958_Issue-25c.jpg/255px-Paul_Revere_1958_Issue-25c.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Paul_Revere_1958_Issue-25c.jpg/340px-Paul_Revere_1958_Issue-25c.jpg 2x" data-file-width="546" data-file-height="629" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Revere_1958_Issue-25c.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Revere on 1958 U.S. stamp.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere%27s_Ride" title="Paul Revere's Ride">Paul Revere's Ride</a></div>
<p>In 1861, over 40 years after Revere's death, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wadsworth_Longfellow" title="Henry Wadsworth Longfellow">Henry Wadsworth Longfellow</a> made the midnight ride the subject of his poem "Paul Revere's Ride" which opens:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Listen, my children, and you shall hear<br />
Of the midnight ride of Paul Revere,<br />
On the eighteenth of April, in Seventy-Five;<br />
Hardly a man is now alive<br />
Who remembers that famous day and year</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Longfellow's poem is not historically accurate, but the inaccuracies were deliberate. Longfellow had researched the historical event, using such works as <a href="/wiki/George_Bancroft" title="George Bancroft">George Bancroft</a>'s <i>History of the United States</i>, but he <a href="/wiki/Artistic_license" title="Artistic license">changed the facts for poetic effect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1994331_122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1994331-122">[120]</a></sup> The poem was one of a series in which he sought to create American legends; earlier examples include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Song_of_Hiawatha" title="The Song of Hiawatha">The Song of Hiawatha</a></i> (1855) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Courtship_of_Miles_Standish" title="The Courtship of Miles Standish">The Courtship of Miles Standish</a></i> (1858).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERulandBradbury1991109_123-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERulandBradbury1991109-123">[121]</a></sup> Longfellow was successful in creating a legend: Revere's stature rose significantly in the years following the poem's publication.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1994333_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1994333-124">[122]</a></sup></p>
<p>Parts of the ride route in Massachusetts are now posted with signs marked "Revere's Ride". The route follows Main Street in <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlestown, Massachusetts">Charlestown</a>, Broadway and Main Street in <a href="/wiki/Somerville,_Massachusetts" title="Somerville, Massachusetts">Somerville</a>, Main Street and High Street in <a href="/wiki/Medford,_Massachusetts" title="Medford, Massachusetts">Medford</a>, Medford Street to <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Massachusetts" title="Arlington, Massachusetts">Arlington</a> center, and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Avenue_(Boston)" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts Avenue (Boston)">Massachusetts Avenue</a> the rest of the way through Lexington and into Lincoln. Revere's ride is reenacted annually.<sup id="cite_ref-National_Lancers_118-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Lancers-118">[116]</a></sup></p>
<p>Contrary to popular belief, Revere and Dawes were not the only riders; however, they were the only two to be noted in the poem. Samuel Prescott and Israel Bissell were also tasked to undertake the mission, Israel Bissell being the person to ride the farthest distance of all.</p>

<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Revere's date of birth is confused by the conversion between the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendars</a>, which offsets the date by 11 days, and by the fact that only his baptism, not his actual birth was recorded. <li id="cite_note-34"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-34">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Forcing private citizens to quarter soldiers in their homes would be one of the grievances enumerated in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">United States Declaration of Independence</a>—[The King] "has combined with others to subject us to a Jurisdiction foreign to our Constitution, and unacknowledged by our Laws; giving his Assent to their Acts of pretended Legislation: For quartering large Bodies of Armed Troops among us:..."—and the framers of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a> restricted the practice in the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="3rd Amendment to the United States Constitution">Third Amendment</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bill_of_Rights" title="United States Bill of Rights">Bill of Rights</a>.</span></li>
</ol>

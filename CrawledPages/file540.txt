<p><b>Catharine Drew Gilpin Faust</b>, (born September 18, 1947)<sup id="cite_ref-rimer_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rimer-1">[1]</a></sup> an American <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Harvard_University" title="President of Harvard University">President of Harvard University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crimson-pres_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crimson-pres-2">[2]</a></sup> Faust is the first woman to serve as Harvard's <a href="/wiki/President_of_Harvard_University" title="President of Harvard University">president</a> and the university's 28th president overall. Faust is the fifth woman to serve as president of an <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> university and is the former dean of the <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Institute_for_Advanced_Study" title="Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study">Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study</a>. Faust is Harvard's first president since 1672 without an undergraduate or graduate degree from Harvard, and the first to have been brought up in the South.<sup id="cite_ref-first-pres_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-first-pres-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bostonglobe22507_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bostonglobe22507-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2014, she was ranked as the 33rd most powerful woman in the world by <i>Forbes</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes14_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes14-5">[5]</a></sup> Faust is set to step down as President of Harvard in June 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drew_Gilpin_Faust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was born <b>Catharine Drew Gilpin</b> in New York City and raised in <a href="/wiki/Clarke_County,_Virginia" title="Clarke County, Virginia">Clarke County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_Valley" title="Shenandoah Valley">Shenandoah Valley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rimer_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rimer-1">[1]</a></sup> She is the daughter of Catharine Ginna (née Mellick) and McGhee Tyson Gilpin; her father was a <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a> graduate and breeder of thoroughbred horses.<sup id="cite_ref-rimer_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rimer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hollick_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hollick-7">[7]</a></sup> Her paternal great-grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Tyson" title="Lawrence Tyson">Lawrence Tyson</a>, was a U.S. Senator from Tennessee during the 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Faust also has New England ancestry and is a descendant of the <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> divine Rev. <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Edwards_(theologian)" title="Jonathan Edwards (theologian)">Jonathan Edwards</a>, the third president of <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hollick_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hollick-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>She graduated from <a href="/wiki/Concord_Academy" title="Concord Academy">Concord Academy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concord,_Massachusetts" title="Concord, Massachusetts">Concord, Massachusetts</a>, in 1964. She earned a BA <i>magna cum laude</i> with honors in history from <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Mawr_College" title="Bryn Mawr College">Bryn Mawr College</a> in 1968. She earned an MA in <a href="/wiki/American_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="American civilization">American civilization</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> in 1971 and a <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> in 1975, with a dissertation entitled "A Sacred Circle: The Social Role of the Intellectual in the Old South, 1840–1860".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the same year, she joined the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> faculty as assistant professor of American civilization. A specialist in the history of the South in the <a href="/wiki/Antebellum_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Antebellum era">antebellum</a> period and <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, Faust rose to become <a href="/wiki/Walter_Annenberg" title="Walter Annenberg">Walter Annenberg</a> Professor of History.</p>

<p>She is the author of six books, including <i>Mothers of Invention: Women of the Slaveholding South in the American Civil War</i> (1996), for which she won both the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_American_Historians" title="Society of American Historians">Society of American Historians</a> <a href="/wiki/Francis_Parkman_Prize" title="Francis Parkman Prize">Francis Parkman Prize</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Avery_O._Craven" class="mw-redirect" title="Avery O. Craven">Avery O. Craven</a> Award from the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_Historians" title="Organization of American Historians">Organization of American Historians</a> in 1997. Her other works include <i>James Henry Hammond and Old South,</i> a biography of <a href="/wiki/James_Henry_Hammond" title="James Henry Hammond">James Henry Hammond</a>, Governor of South Carolina from 1842–1844. <i>This Republic of Suffering</i> (2008) was a critically acclaimed exploration of how the United States' understanding of death was shaped by the high losses during the Civil War. It was a finalist for the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_History" title="Pulitzer Prize for History">Pulitzer Prize</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award_for_Nonfiction" title="National Book Award for Nonfiction">National Book Award</a>.</p>
<p>In 2001, Faust was appointed the first dean of the <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Institute_for_Advanced_Study" title="Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study">Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study</a>, established after the merger of <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_College" title="Radcliffe College">Radcliffe College</a> with <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rimer_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rimer-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="President_of_Harvard_University">President of Harvard University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drew_Gilpin_Faust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: President of Harvard University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On February 8, 2007, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Harvard_Crimson" title="The Harvard Crimson">The Harvard Crimson</a></i> reported that Faust had been selected as the next president of the university.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Following formal approval by the university's governing boards, her appointment was made official three days later.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her appointment followed the departure of <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">Lawrence H. Summers</a>, who resigned on June 30, 2006, after a series of <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers#Controversies" title="Lawrence Summers">controversial</a> statements that led to mounting criticism from members of Harvard's Faculty of Arts and Sciences. <a href="/wiki/Derek_Bok" title="Derek Bok">Derek Bok</a>, who had served as President of Harvard from 1971–1991, returned to serve as an interim president during the 2006–2007 academic year.</p>

<p>During a press conference on campus Faust said, "I hope that my own appointment can be one symbol of an opening of opportunities that would have been inconceivable even a generation ago." She also added, "I'm not the woman president of Harvard, I'm the president of Harvard."<sup id="cite_ref-first-pres_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-first-pres-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 12, 2007, Faust delivered her installation address, saying,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A university is not about results in the next quarter; it is not even about who a student has become by graduation. It is about learning that molds a lifetime, learning that transmits the heritage of millennia; learning that shapes the future.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>One of Faust's first initiatives was significantly increasing financial aid offered to students at Harvard College. On December 10, 2007, Faust announced a transformative new policy for middle-class and upper-middle-class students, which limited parental contributions to 10 percent for families making between $100,000 and $180,000 annually, and replaced loans with grants. In announcing the policy, Faust said, “Education is the engine that makes American democracy work.... And it has to work and that means people have to have access.”<sup id="cite_ref-aid_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aid-13">[13]</a></sup> The new policy expanded on earlier programs that eliminated contributions for families earning less than $60,000 a year and greatly reduced costs for families earning less than $100,000. Similar policies were subsequently adopted by Stanford, Yale, and many other private U.S. universities and colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-glater_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glater-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to promoting accessibility to higher education, Faust has testified before the U.S. Congress to promote increased funding for scientific research and support of junior faculty researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> She has made it a priority to revitalize the arts at Harvard and integrate them into the daily life of students and staff.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Faust has worked to further internationalize the University. In addition, she has been a strong advocate for <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and has set an ambitious goal of reducing the University’s greenhouse gas emissions by 2016, including those associated with prospective growth, by 30 percent below Harvard’s 2006 baseline.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2008, <a href="/wiki/Christina_Romer" title="Christina Romer">Christina Romer</a>, an economics professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, was not offered tenure at Harvard despite support from the members of the Harvard Economics Department. Because of the confidential nature of the process, which at Harvard includes a panel consisting of outside experts and internal faculty members from outside the department, Faust has declined to discuss press reports related to Romer’s tenure case.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Romer was later nominated by President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> to chair the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Economic_Advisers" title="Council of Economic Advisers">Council of Economic Advisers</a>. Also in Faust's tenure, Harvard's economics department witnessed an exodus of prominent faculty to Stanford and MIT, including <a href="/wiki/Raj_Chetty" title="Raj Chetty">Raj Chetty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Athey" title="Susan Athey">Susan Athey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guido_Imbens" title="Guido Imbens">Guido Imbens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drew_Fudenberg" title="Drew Fudenberg">Drew Fudenberg</a>, and Nobel Laureate <a href="/wiki/Alvin_E._Roth" title="Alvin E. Roth">Al Roth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the wake of a series of layoffs in June 2009, Faust was criticized for refusing to accept a pay cut in an effort to save jobs. In the months preceding the layoffs, various campus groups called upon Faust and other administrators to reduce their salaries as a means of cutting costs campus-wide.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Reports on Faust's salary differ: The <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Globe" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Globe">Boston Globe</a></i> reports that Faust made $775,043 in the 2007–2008 school year,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> while the <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson" title="Harvard Crimson">Harvard Crimson</a></i> reported that Faust made $693,739 in salary and benefits for the 2008–2009 <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In early 2009, the Harvard Corporation approved salary freezes for the president, deans, senior officers, management staff, and faculty, and offered an early retirement program. The University also undertook an involuntary reduction in staff of 2.4 percent of its employees.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2010, Faust and President <a href="/wiki/John_L._Hennessy" title="John L. Hennessy">John L. Hennessy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> co-wrote an editorial in support of passage of the <a href="/wiki/DREAM_Act" title="DREAM Act">DREAM Act</a>. The legislation was not passed by the <a href="/wiki/111th_United_States_Congress" title="111th United States Congress">111th United States Congress</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, Faust signed an agreement with Navy Secretary <a href="/wiki/Ray_Mabus" title="Ray Mabus">Ray Mabus</a>, JD '76, to formally return the Naval Reserve Officers Training Corps (NROTC) program to campus after almost 40 years, following the repeal of the "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_ask,_don%27t_tell" title="Don't ask, don't tell">Don't Ask Don't Tell</a>" law in December 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drew_Gilpin_Faust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Faust is married to <a href="/wiki/Charles_E._Rosenberg" title="Charles E. Rosenberg">Charles E. Rosenberg</a>, a historian of medicine at Harvard. They have a daughter, Jessica Rosenberg, who is a Harvard graduate and works for the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Yorker_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="New Yorker Magazine">New Yorker</a></i>. Faust also has a step daughter, Leah Rosenberg.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>She was previously married to Stephen Faust.<sup id="cite_ref-faust_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faust-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her first cousin is the movie and television actor <a href="/wiki/Jack_Gilpin" title="Jack Gilpin">Jack Gilpin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-faust_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faust-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Faust was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a> in 1988 and treated that year. She has enjoyed good health since then. She has declined to speak with the media with more details about her diagnosis or treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-cancer_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cancer-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

Faust's lecture was entitled "Telling War Stories: Reflections of a Civil War Historian".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>

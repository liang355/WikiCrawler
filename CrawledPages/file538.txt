<p><a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>'s endowment (valued at $37.6 billion as of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_endowment&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>)<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_at_a_Glance_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_at_a_Glance-1">[1]</a></sup> is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_colleges_and_universities_in_the_United_States_by_endowment" title="List of colleges and universities in the United States by endowment">largest academic endowment</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Along with Harvard's pension assets, working capital, and non-cash gifts, it is managed by <b>Harvard Management Company, Inc.</b> (HMC), a Harvard-owned <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">investment management</a> company.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvard_Management_Company">Harvard Management Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_endowment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Harvard Management Company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>HMC employs financial professionals to manage the approximately 12,000 funds that constitute the endowment. The company directly manages about one third of the total endowment portfolio while working closely with the external companies that manage the rest.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> HMC is headed by a professional, who holds the titles of president and <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> of the company.</p>
<p>Jack Meyer managed HMC from 1990 to September 30, 2005, beginning with an endowment worth $4.8 billion and ending with a value of $25.9 billion (including new contributions). During the last decade of his tenure, the endowment earned an annualized return of 15.9%.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In part after compensation disagreements, a number of HMC managers including Meyer himself left to form their own investment management firms. <i>Bloomberg</i> in 2011 reviewed a group of the resultant firms—Adage Capital Management LP, <a href="/wiki/Charlesbank_Capital_Partners" title="Charlesbank Capital Partners">Charlesbank Capital Partners</a> LLC, Convexity Capital Management LP (Meyer's), <a href="/wiki/Highfields_Capital_Management" title="Highfields Capital Management">Highfields Capital Management</a> LP and Regiment Capital Advisors LLC—which at that time between them managed $43 billion in assets.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The university hired <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_A._El-Erian" title="Mohamed A. El-Erian">Mohamed El-Erian</a> to succeed Meyer as HMC's next president and CEO. He came from the bond trading company <a href="/wiki/PIMCO" title="PIMCO">PIMCO</a> and pledged to "rebuild and reinvent" the company. He announced his leaving September 12, 2007 to return to PIMCO after guiding the endowment to a one-year return of 23%.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jane_Mendillo" title="Jane Mendillo">Jane Mendillo</a> was named the new head of HMC, effective July 1, 2008. She had been <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_College" title="Wellesley College">Wellesley College</a>’s chief investment officer since 2002. Prior to that, she served as vice president for external management at HMC. A 1984 graduate of <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> followed by an MBA from <a href="/wiki/Yale_School_of_Management" title="Yale School of Management">Yale School of Management</a>, Mendillo first joined HMC as an equities analyst in 1987. Her tenure was largely shaped by the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932010" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2010">financial crisis of 2007–2010</a>, with a cash squeeze in University operation and endowment performance, a shrinkage of endowment asset value, and errant interest rate, <a href="/wiki/Financial_derivatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial derivatives">financial derivatives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leveraged" class="mw-redirect" title="Leveraged">leveraged</a> positions, according to a Feb. 2009 news report. Staff of HMC was trimmed 25%, or by about 50 people, in line with shrinkage of about $8 billion (about 22%). A source for the report said that some of the positions in the endowment which had to be liquidated were in <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge funds</a> run by Meyer's Convexity Capital and <a href="/wiki/Seth_Klarman" title="Seth Klarman">Seth Klarman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Baupost_Group" title="Baupost Group">Baupost Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stephen Blyth became the CEO of HMC on January 1, 2015. Blyth joined the firm in 2006, and previously held roles as Head of Internal Management and Head of Public Markets, responsible for investments in public equity, credit and fixed income markets. An alumnus of Cambridge University and Harvard University, Blyth has also served as a Professor of the Practice of Statistics at Harvard University.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marc Seidner joined HMC as vice president for domestic <a href="/wiki/Fixed_income" title="Fixed income">fixed income</a> in an effort to revamp the company's <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bond</a> division in 2006. Mr. Seidner was previously the director of active core strategies at Standish Mellon Asset Management.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> reported on June 23, 2009 that Seidner was departing from the organization. On August 14, 2009 PIMCO announced that it had hired Seidner as an executive vice president and portfolio manager to manage a range of fixed-income portfolios.</p>
<p>In January 2017, CEO Narvekar announced the company would be <a href="/wiki/Layoff" title="Layoff">laying off</a> about half of the 230-employee staff by outsourcing some positions and closing all its internally managed <a href="/wiki/Hedge_funds" class="mw-redirect" title="Hedge funds">hedge funds</a> by the end of the 2017 fiscal year.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harken_Energy">Harken Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_University_endowment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Harken Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Harvard Management Company was linked to <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>'s so-called <a href="/wiki/Harken_Energy_scandal" title="Harken Energy scandal">Harken Energy scandal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Specifically, <a href="/wiki/Michael_R._Eisenson" title="Michael R. Eisenson">Michael R. Eisenson</a>, who would later found <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> firm <a href="/wiki/Charlesbank_Capital_Partners" title="Charlesbank Capital Partners">Charlesbank Capital Partners</a> was the Harvard representative on the Harken Energy board when Harvard made a $30 million investment into the ill-fated oil company venture.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> At the time, employees were accused of improperly investing their own money into Harken but Harvard deemed those investments appropriate.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

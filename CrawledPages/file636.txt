<p>The <b>John W. Weeks Bridge</b>, usually called the Weeks Footbridge (or simply Weeks Bridge), is a pedestrian bridge over the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a>, connecting <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Allston,_Boston,_MA" class="mw-redirect" title="Allston, Boston, MA">Allston</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_W._Weeks" title="John W. Weeks">John W. Weeks</a> was a longtime <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">U.S. Representative</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a>, from Massachusetts, as well as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">Secretary of War</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Warren_Harding" class="mw-redirect" title="Warren Harding">Harding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">Coolidge</a> administrations. The Weeks Bridge opened in 1927 to carry pedestrian traffic between the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>'s newly built Allston campus and the Business School's former home, Harvard's traditional campus in Cambridge. Its concrete underbelly conceals tentacles of the University's steam, electrical, and communications networks.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bridge is a popular vantage point from which to enjoy the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_Charles_Regatta" title="Head of the Charles Regatta">Head of the Charles Regatta</a>. An abrupt bend in the river prompts most boats to crowd through the bridge's center span, and collisions have occurred when <a href="/wiki/Coxswain_(rowing)" title="Coxswain (rowing)">coxswains</a> could not make themselves heard above the cheering of the crowd.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renovation">Renovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_W._Weeks_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Renovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On September 21, 2015 the Massachusetts <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Conservation_and_Recreation" title="Department of Conservation and Recreation">Department of Conservation and Recreation</a> held a ribbon cutting to mark completion of a $3 million renovation that made the Weeks bridge <a href="/wiki/Accessibility" title="Accessibility">handicap accessible</a> in compliance with the <a href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Americans with Disabilities Act">Americans with Disabilities Act</a>. Stairs at either end were replaced with ramps and accessibility-compliant railings were installed on the bridge. Other improvements included new accessible approach paths, new lighting using replicated historic fixtures, restoring the eroded river bank adjacent to the bridge, and repairs to the bridge masonry and structure. Harvard University contributed $150,000 to the project.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Disinvestment from South Africa</h1>

The campaign gained prominence in the mid-1980s on university campuses in the US. The debate headlined the October 1985 issue (above) of <a href="/wiki/Vassar_College" title="Vassar College">Vassar College</a>'s student newspaper.<sub><sup id="cite_ref-Vassar_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vassar-1">[1]</a></sup></sub></div>
</div>

<p><b>Disinvestment</b> (or <b>divestment</b>) <b>from South Africa</b> was first advocated in the 1960s, in protest of <a href="/wiki/History_of_South_Africa_in_the_apartheid_era" class="mw-redirect" title="History of South Africa in the apartheid era">South Africa's system of apartheid</a>, but was not implemented on a significant scale until the mid-1980s. The <a href="/wiki/Disinvestment" title="Disinvestment">disinvestment</a> campaign, after being realized in federal legislation enacted in 1986 by the United States, is credited by some<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> as pressuring the <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> Government to embark on <a href="/wiki/Negotiations_to_end_apartheid_in_South_Africa" title="Negotiations to end apartheid in South Africa">negotiations</a> ultimately leading to the dismantling of the Apartheid system.</p>

<p>In November 1962, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> passed <a href="/wiki/UN_General_Assembly_Resolution_1761" class="mw-redirect" title="UN General Assembly Resolution 1761">Resolution 1761</a>, a non-binding resolution establishing the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Special_Committee_against_Apartheid" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Special Committee against Apartheid">United Nations Special Committee against Apartheid</a> and called for imposing economic and other sanctions on South Africa. All Western nations were unhappy with the call for sanctions and as a result boycotted the committee.<sup id="cite_ref-Lisson_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lisson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following this passage of this resolution the UK-based <a href="/wiki/Anti-Apartheid_Movement" title="Anti-Apartheid Movement">Anti-Apartheid Movement</a> spearheaded the arrangements for an international conference on sanctions to be held in London in April 1964. According to Lisson, "The aim of the Conference was to work out the practicability of economic sanctions and their implications on the economies of South Africa, the UK, the US and the Protectorates. Knowing that the strongest opposition to the application of sanctions came from the West (and within the West, Britain), the Committee made every effort to attract as wide and varied a number of speakers and participants as possible so that the Conference findings would be regarded as objective."<sup id="cite_ref-Lisson_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lisson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The conference was named the <i><a href="/wiki/International_Conference_for_Economic_Sanctions_Against_South_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="International Conference for Economic Sanctions Against South Africa">International Conference for Economic Sanctions Against South Africa</a></i>. This conference, Lisson writes,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>established the necessity, the legality and the practicability of internationally organised sanctions against South Africa, whose policies were seen to have become a direct threat to peace and security in Africa and the world. Its findings also pointed out that in order to be effective, a programme of sanctions would need the active participation of Britain and the US, who were also the main obstacle to the implementation of such a policy.<sup id="cite_ref-Lisson_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lisson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempts_to_persuade_British_policymakers">Attempts to persuade British policymakers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Attempts to persuade British policymakers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The conference was not successful in persuading Britain to take up economic sanctions against South Africa though. Rather, the British government "remained firm in its view that the imposition of sanctions would be unconstitutional 'because we do not accept that this situation in South Africa constitutes a threat to international peace and security and we do not in any case believe that sanctions would have the effect of persuading the South African Government to change its policies'".<sup id="cite_ref-Lisson_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lisson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The AAM tried to make sanctions an election issue in the 1964 General Election in Britain. Candidates were asked to state their position on economic sanctions and other punitive measures against the South African government. Most candidates who responded answered in the affirmative. After the Labour Party sweep to power though, commitment to the anti-apartheid cause dissipated. In short order, Labour Party leader Harold Wilson told the press that his Labour Party was "not in favour of trade sanctions partly because, even if fully effective, they would harm the people we are most concerned about - the Africans and those white South Africans who are having to maintain some standard of decency there".<sup id="cite_ref-Lisson_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lisson-3">[3]</a></sup> Even so, Lisson writes that the "AAM still hoped that the new Labour Government would be more sensitive to the demands of public opinion than the previous Government." But by the end of 1964, it was clear that the election of the Labour Party had made little difference in the governments overall unwillingness to imposing sanctions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steadfast_rejection_by_the_West">Steadfast rejection by the West</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Steadfast rejection by the West">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lisson summarizes the situation at the UN in 1964:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>At the UN, Britain consistently refused to accept that the situation in South Africa fell under <a href="/wiki/Chapter_VII_of_the_United_Nations_Charter" title="Chapter VII of the United Nations Charter">Chapter VII of the [United Nations] Charter</a>. Instead, in collaboration with the US, it worked for a carefully worded appeal on the Rivonia and other political trials to try to appease Afro-Asian countries and public opinion at home and abroad; by early 1965 the issue of sanctions had lost momentum.<sup id="cite_ref-Lisson_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lisson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to Lisson, Britain's rejection was premised on its economic interests in South Africa, which would be put at risk if any type of meaningful economic sanctions were put in place.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1977, the voluntary UN arms embargo became mandatory with the passing of <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_Resolution_418" title="United Nations Security Council Resolution 418">United Nations Security Council Resolution 418</a>.</p>
<p>An oil embargo was introduced on 20 November 1987 when the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> adopted a voluntary international <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> <a href="/wiki/Embargo" class="mw-redirect" title="Embargo">embargo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="United_States_campaign_(1977–1989)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_campaign_.281977.E2.80.931989.29">United States campaign (1977–1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States campaign (1977–1989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="The_Sullivan_Principles_(1977)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sullivan_Principles_.281977.29">The Sullivan Principles (1977)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Sullivan Principles (1977)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sullivan_Principles" class="mw-redirect" title="Sullivan Principles">Sullivan Principles</a></div>
<p>Knight<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup> writes that anti-apartheid movement in the U.S found that Washington was unwilling to get involved in economically isolating South Africa. The movement responded by organized lobbying of individual businesses and institutional investors to end their involvement with or investments in the apartheid state as a matter of <a href="/wiki/Corporate_social_responsibility" title="Corporate social responsibility">corporate social responsibility</a>. This campaign was coordinated by several faith-based institutional investors eventually leading to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Interfaith_Center_on_Corporate_Responsibility" title="Interfaith Center on Corporate Responsibility">Interfaith Center on Corporate Responsibility</a>. An array of celebrities, including singer <a href="/wiki/Paul_Simon" title="Paul Simon">Paul Simon</a>, also participated.</p>
<p>The key instrument of this campaign was the so-called <a href="/wiki/Sullivan_Principles" class="mw-redirect" title="Sullivan Principles">Sullivan Principles</a>, authored by and named after the Rev. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Leon_Sullivan" title="Leon Sullivan">Leon Sullivan</a>. Leon Sullivan was an <a href="/wiki/African-American" class="mw-redirect" title="African-American">African-American</a> preacher in Philadelphia who, in 1977, was also a board member of the corporate giant <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>. At that time, General Motors was the largest employer of blacks in South Africa. The principles required that the corporation ensure that all employees are treated equally and in an integrated environment, both in and outside the workplace, and regardless of race, as a condition of doing business. These principles directly conflicted with the mandated racial discrimination and segregation policies of apartheid-era South Africa, thus making it impossible for businesses adopting the Sullivan Principles to continue doing business there.</p>
<p>While the anti-Apartheid movement lobbied individual businesses to adopt and comply with the Sullivan Principles, the movement opened an additional front with the institutional investors. Besides advocating that institutional investors withdraw any direct investments in South African-based companies, anti-Apartheid activists also lobbied for the divestment from all U.S.-based companies having South African interests who had not yet themselves adopted the Sullivan Principles. The institutional investors such as public pension funds were the most susceptible to these types of lobbying efforts.</p>
<p>Public companies with South Africa interests were thus confronted on two levels: First, <a href="/wiki/Shareholder_resolutions" class="mw-redirect" title="Shareholder resolutions">shareholder resolutions</a> were submitted by concerned stockholders who, admitted, posed more of a threat to the often-cherished corporate reputations than to the stock price. Second, the companies were presented with the significant financial threat whereby one or more of their major institutional investors decides to withdraw their investments.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The disinvestment campaign in the United States, which had been in existence for quite some years, gained critical mass following the Black political resistance to the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Republic_of_South_Africa_Act_(110_of_1983)" class="mw-redirect" title="Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act (110 of 1983)">1983 South African constitution</a> which included a "complex set of segregated parliaments". Richard Knight writes:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In a total rejection of apartheid, black South Africans mobilized to make the townships ungovernable, black local officials resigned in droves, and the government declared a State of Emergency in 1985 and used thousands of troops to quell "unrest". Television audiences throughout the world were to watch almost nightly reports of massive resistance to apartheid, the growth of a democratic movement, and the savage police and military response.<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The result of the widely televised South African response was "a dramatic expansion of international actions to isolate apartheid, actions that combined with the internal situation to force dramatic changes in South Africa's international economic relations".<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_campuses">University campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: University campuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Students organized to demand that their universities "divest", meaning that the universities were to cease investing in companies that traded or had operations in South Africa. At many universities, many students and faculty protested in order to force action on the issue. The first organized Anti-Apartheid Organization on University Campuses in the United States was CUAA founded at the University of California Berkeley by Ramon Sevilla. Sevilla was a principal organizer that had support from Nelson Mandela, with whom Sevilla was in communication while Mandela was imprisoned on Robben Island in South Africa as well as with The African National Congress (the ANC). Some of the most effective actions in support of the divestment of investments of U.S. companies doing business in South Africa took place between the years of 1976 and 1985, as Sevilla travelled throughout the American Continent and Europe gathering support for the overthrow of the South African Apartheid Government, which also led to his arrest at U.C. Berkeley on several occasions in the successful effort to force the University of California to divest all of their investments in companies doing business in South Africa, which also became the driving force for divestment worldwide in all companies doing business in the country of Apartheid South Africa. For example, in April 1986, 61 students were arrested after building a shantytown in front of the chancellor's office at <a href="/w/index.php?title=UC_Berkeley,_Ramon_Sevilla_Chair_CUAA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="UC Berkeley, Ramon Sevilla Chair CUAA (page does not exist)">UC Berkeley, Ramon Sevilla Chair CUAA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a result of these organized "divestment campaigns", the boards of trustees of several prominent universities voted to divest completely from <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and companies with major South African interests.</p>
<p>The first of these was <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_College#In_the_media" title="Hampshire College">Hampshire College</a> in 1977.</p>
<p>These initial successes set a pattern which was later repeated and many more campuses across the country. Activism surged in 1984 on the wave of public interest created by the wide television coverage of the then recent resistance efforts of the black South Africans.</p>
<p>Overall, according to Knight's analysis,<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup> the numbers year over year for educational institutions fully or partially divesting from South Africa were:</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Michigan_State_University">Michigan State University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Michigan State University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The anti-Apartheid disinvestment campaign on campuses began on the West coast and Midwest in 1977 at Michigan State University and Stanford University.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It had some early successes in 1978 at Michigan State University, which voted total divestiture, <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> at Columbia University;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the University of Wisconsin–Madison. Following the Michigan State University divestiture in 1978, in 1982, the State of Michigan Legislature and Governor voted for divestiture by all of the more than 30 State of Michigan colleges and universities, an action later struck down as unconstitutional by the Michigan Court of Appeals in response to a suit against the Act by the University of Michigan. <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Columbia_University">Columbia University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Columbia University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The initial Columbia divestment, focused largely on bonds and financial institutions directly involved with the South African regime.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It followed a year long campaign first initiated by students who had worked together to block the appointment of former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger to an endowed chair at the University in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Broadly backed by a diverse array of student groups and many notable faculty members the Committee Against Investment in South Africa held numerous teach-ins and demonstrations through the year focused on the trustees ties to the corporations doing business with South Africa. Trustee meetings were picketed and interrupted by demonstrations culminating in May 1978 in the takeover of the Graduate School of Business.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Smith_College">Smith College</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Smith College">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Smith_College" title="Smith College">Smith College</a>, in Northampton, Mass., which is connected to <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_College" title="Hampshire College">Hampshire College</a> in Amherst, Mass., through the <a href="/wiki/Five_College_Consortium" title="Five College Consortium">Five College Consortium</a> engaged with divesting from South Africa several years later. In the spring semester of 1986 students at Smith College protested the Board of Trustee's decision not to fully divest<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the college's endowment from companies in South Africa. Student protests included a sit-in in College Hall, the main administrative office which included nearly 100 students sleeping in over night on 24 February 1986. The next day students staged a full blockade of the building, not allowing any staff into the building and anticipating arrest, though the President of the college at the time, Mary Maples Dunn, refused to have the students arrested.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Smith_College_Student_Protest_in_1986.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Smith_College_Student_Protest_in_1986.jpg/220px-Smith_College_Student_Protest_in_1986.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Smith_College_Student_Protest_in_1986.jpg/330px-Smith_College_Student_Protest_in_1986.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Smith_College_Student_Protest_in_1986.jpg/440px-Smith_College_Student_Protest_in_1986.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2175" data-file-height="1421" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Smith_College_Student_Protest_in_1986.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Students of Smith College blocking the entrance to College Hall in 1986 as an act of protest urging the Board of Trustees to divest the college's endowment from South Africa</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A comprehensive list of the demands they made throughout the demonstration was published on 28 February 1986,<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_College_Archives_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_College_Archives-18">[18]</a></sup> at 8:30 am. The "Women at College Hall" agreed to end the blockade if The Board of Trustees agreed to "issue a statement of intent to deliberate again, with a quorum, the issue of divestment" before Spring Break, and that the Investor Responsibility Committee would meet with representatives from the South African Task Force, the Ethical Investment Committee, and students from the Divestment Committee to look at "a restructuring of the investment policy". Students also demanded that the Board of Trustees “recognize the need for more dialogue with the Smith College community” and that they act on this with more meetings and transparency. In relation to the action, students demanded that a required teach-in be conducted to educate the college and the Board of Trustees on divestment, South African apartheid, and the College Hall Occupation, in addition, a booklet would be compiled by the demonstrators that would be distributed to the college to educate the community on the movement. They also demanded that the president grant amnesty to anyone who directly or indirectly participated in the occupation.</p>
<p>On 1 March 1986, the protest ended when negotiations with administrators led to an agreement that the trustees would re-evaluate their decision, a mandatory teach-in would be held, and amnesty would be granted to anyone involved in the demonstration.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_College_Archives_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_College_Archives-18">[18]</a></sup> After student pressures, Smith College voted to divest all $39 million in stocks that they held in companies working in South Africa by 31 October 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvard_University">Harvard University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Harvard University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> only undertook a partial "divestment" from South Africa and only after significant resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-HarvardDivest_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarvardDivest-20">[20]</a></sup> Adam Soften and Aln Wirzbicki give this description:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Throughout the 1980s, Harvard professors for the most part avoided involvement with South Africa in protest of apartheid, and then president Derek C. Bok was a vocal supporter of work by the U.S. to prompt reform in South Africa. But the University was slow to pull its own investments out of companies doing business in South Africa, insisting that through its proxy votes, it could more effectively fight apartheid than by purging stocks from its portfolio. But after a decade of protests, Harvard did adopt a policy of selective divestment, and by the end of the 1980s was almost completely out of South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-HarvardDivest_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarvardDivest-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_California">University of California</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: University of California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a>, in contrast to the limited action undertaken by Harvard, authorized the withdrawal of three billion dollars worth of investments from the apartheid state. <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a> stated his belief that the University of California's massive divestment was particularly significant in abolishing white-minority rule in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gettysburg_College">Gettysburg College</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gettysburg College">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1989, after a three-year review by the <a href="/wiki/Gettysburg_College" title="Gettysburg College">Gettysburg College</a> Board of Trustees and a five-month campaign by the Salaam Committee--a campus group made up of students and faculty--the college voted to divest $5.4 million from companies connected to South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="States_and_cities">States and cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: States and cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boycott_-_Contaminated_with_apartheid_-_South_African_goods.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Boycott_-_Contaminated_with_apartheid_-_South_African_goods.jpg/220px-Boycott_-_Contaminated_with_apartheid_-_South_African_goods.jpg" width="220" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Boycott_-_Contaminated_with_apartheid_-_South_African_goods.jpg/330px-Boycott_-_Contaminated_with_apartheid_-_South_African_goods.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Boycott_-_Contaminated_with_apartheid_-_South_African_goods.jpg/440px-Boycott_-_Contaminated_with_apartheid_-_South_African_goods.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2219" data-file-height="2177" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boycott_-_Contaminated_with_apartheid_-_South_African_goods.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Boycott - Contaminated with apartheid - South African goods - 12 inch sticker used by activists, from the collection of the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In addition to campuses, anti-apartheid activists found concerned and sympathetic legislators in cities and states. Several states and localities did pass legislation ordering the sale of such securities, most notably the City and County of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, which passed legislation on June 5, 1978 not to invest "in corporations and banks doing business in or with South Africa".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The result was that "by the end of 1989 26 states, 22 counties and over 90 cities had taken some form of binding economic action against companies doing business in South Africa".<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup> Many public pension funds connected to these local governments were legislated to disinvestment from South African companies. These local governments also exerted pressure via enacting selective purchasing policies, "whereby cities give preference in bidding on contracts for goods and services to those companies who do not do business in South Africa".<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_involvement">Federal involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Federal involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The activity at the state and city level set the stage for action at the federal level.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Comprehensive_Anti-Apartheid_Act">Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Anti-Apartheid_Act" title="Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act">Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act</a></div>
<p>This began when the Senate and House of Representatives presented Ronald Reagan with the Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act of 1986 which had been introduced by Congressman Ronald Dellums, supported by the members of the Congressional Black Caucus in the House, and piloted through the House by Congressman Howard Wolpe, chairman of the House Africa Subcommittee. Ronald Reagan responded by using his veto, but surprisingly and, in testament to the strength of the anti-Apartheid movement, the Republican controlled Senate overrode his veto. Knight gives this description the act:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The Act banned new U.S. investment in South Africa, sales to the police and military, and new bank loans, except for the purpose of trade. Specific measures against trade included the prohibition of the import of agricultural goods, textiles, shellfish, steel, iron, uranium and the products of state-owned corporations.<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The results of the act were mixed in economic terms according to Knight:<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup> Between 1985 and 1987, U.S. imports from South Africa declined 35%, although the trend reverses in 1988 when imports increased by 15%. Between 1985 and 1998, U.S. exports to South Africa increased by 40%.</p>
<p>Knight attributes some of the increase in imports in 1988 to lax enforcement of the 1986 Act citing a 1989 study by the <a href="/wiki/General_Accounting_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="General Accounting Office">General Accounting Office</a>. Knight writes that a "major weakness of the Act is that it does little to prohibit exports to South Africa, even in such areas as computers and other capital goods".<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Budget_Reconciliation_Act">Budget Reconciliation Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Budget Reconciliation Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A second federal measure introduced by Representative <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rangel" title="Charles Rangel">Charles Rangel</a> in 1987 as an amendment to the Budget Reconciliation Act halted the ability of U.S. corporations from attaining tax reimbursements for taxes paid in South Africa. The result was that U.S. corporations operating in South Africa were subject to double taxation. According to Knight:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The sums of money involved are large. According to the Internal Revenue Service, taxes involved in 1982 were $211,593,000 on taxable income of $440,780,000. The U.S. Chamber of Commerce in South Africa has estimated that the measure increases the tax bill for U.S. companies from 57.5% to 72% of profits in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_legislative_efforts">Further legislative efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Further legislative efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An additionally and much harsher sanctions bill was passed by the House of Representatives (Congress) in August 1988. This bill mandated "the withdrawal of all U.S. companies from South Africa, the sale by U.S. residents of all investments in South African companies and an end to most trade, except for the import of certain strategic minerals".<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup> In the end, the bill didn't become law as wasn't able to pass the Senate. (In the United States legislative system a bill must be passed by both the Senate and the House of Representatives before it can be signed into law by the President.) Even so, the fact that such a harsh bill made any progress at all through the legislature "alerted both the South African government and U.S. business that significant further sanctions were likely to be forthcoming" if the political situation in South Africa remained unchanged.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_South_Africa">Effects on South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Effects on South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_effects">Economic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Economic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_South_Africa#Sanctions" title="Economic history of South Africa">Economic history of South Africa §&#160;Sanctions</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SA_exchange_controls_80s.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/SA_exchange_controls_80s.jpg/220px-SA_exchange_controls_80s.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/SA_exchange_controls_80s.jpg/330px-SA_exchange_controls_80s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/SA_exchange_controls_80s.jpg/440px-SA_exchange_controls_80s.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2192" data-file-height="1620" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SA_exchange_controls_80s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Four exchange control stamps in a South African passport from the mid-1980s allowing the passport holder to take a particular amount of currency out of the country. Exchange controls such as these were imposed by the South African government to restrict the outflow of capital from the country.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While post-colonial African countries had already imposed sanctions on South Africa in solidarity with the <a href="/wiki/Defiance_Campaign" title="Defiance Campaign">Defiance Campaign</a>, these measures had little effect because of the relatively small economies of those involved. The disinvestment campaign only impacted South Africa after the major Western nations, including the United States, got involved beginning in mid-1984. From 1984 onwards, according to Knight,<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup> because of the disinvestment campaign and the repayment of foreign loans, South Africa experienced considerable <a href="/wiki/Capital_flight" title="Capital flight">capital flight</a>. The net capital movement out of South Africa was:</p>
<ul>
<li>R9.2 billion in 1985</li>
<li>R6.1 billion in 1986</li>
<li>R3.1 billion in 1987</li>
<li>R5.5 billion in 1988</li>
</ul>
<p>The capital flight triggered a dramatic decline in the international exchange rate of the South African currency, the rand. The currency decline made imports more expensive which in turn caused inflation in South Africa to rise at a very steep 12-15% per year.<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The South African government did attempt to restrict the damaging outflow of capital. Knight writes that "in September 1985 it imposed a system of exchange control and a debt repayments standstill. Under exchange control, South African residents are generally prohibited from removing capital from the country and foreign investors can only remove investments via the <a href="/wiki/Financial_rand" title="Financial rand">financial rand</a>, which is traded at a 20% to 40% discount compared to the commercial rand. This means companies that disinvest get significantly fewer dollars for the capital they withdraw."<sup id="cite_ref-Knight1990_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight1990-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-apartheid_opposition">Anti-apartheid opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Anti-apartheid opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While disinvestment, boycotts and sanctions aimed at the removal of the apartheid system, there was also considerable opposition from within the anti-apartheid movement within South Africa coming from both black and white leaders. <a href="/wiki/Mangosuthu_Buthelezi" title="Mangosuthu Buthelezi">Mangosuthu Buthelezi</a>, Chief Minister of KwaZulu and leading black figure slammed sanctions, stating that "They can only harm all the people of Southern Africa. They can only lead to more hardships, particularly for the blacks."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Well known anti-apartheid opposition MPs <a href="/wiki/Helen_Suzman" title="Helen Suzman">Helen Suzman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Schwarz" title="Harry Schwarz">Harry Schwarz</a> also strongly opposed moves to disinvest from South Africa. Both politicians of the Progressive Federal Party, they argued that disinvestment would cause further economic hardships for black people, which would ultimately worsen the political climate for negotiations. Suzman described them as "self defeating, wrecking the economy and do not assist anybody irrespective of race". Schwarz also argued that "Morality is cheap when someone else is paying."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_criticism">Outside criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disinvestment_from_South_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Outside criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many criticised disinvestment because of its economic impact on ordinary black South Africans, such as <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, who described sanctions and disinvestment as "the way of poverty, starvation and destroying the hopes of the very people — all of them—whom you wish to help."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a>, then her <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Foreign Secretary</a>, said disinvestment would "feed white consciences outside South Africa, not black bellies within it",<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> although in 2013, he said that the Conservative Government led by Margaret Thatcher was wrong to oppose tougher sanctions against South Africa during the apartheid era.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservatives</a> opposed the disinvestment campaign, accusing its advocates of hypocrisy for not also proposing that the same sanctions be leveled on either the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> or the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">Libertarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Murray_Rothbard" title="Murray Rothbard">Murray Rothbard</a> also opposed this policy, asserting that the most-direct adverse impact of the boycott would actually be felt by the black workers in that country, and the best way to remedy the problem of apartheid was by promoting trade and the growth of <a href="/wiki/Free_market_capitalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Free market capitalism">free market capitalism</a> in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>, who was the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> during the time the disinvestment movement was at its peak, also opposed it, instead favoring a policy of "<a href="/wiki/Constructive_engagement" title="Constructive engagement">constructive engagement</a>" with the <a href="/wiki/Pretoria" title="Pretoria">Pretoria</a> government.</p>

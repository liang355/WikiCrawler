<p><b>Mönchengladbach</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[mœnçn̩ˈɡlatbax]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-M%C3%B6nchengladbach.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4a/De-M%C3%B6nchengladbach.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Mönchengladbach.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>), formerly known as <b>München-Gladbach</b> is a city in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is located west of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, halfway between <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> border.</p>
<p>Mönchengladbach is home of the <a href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (soccer)">football</a> <a href="/wiki/German_Football_League_Teams" class="mw-redirect" title="German Football League Teams">club</a> <a href="/wiki/Borussia_M%C3%B6nchengladbach" title="Borussia Mönchengladbach">Borussia Mönchengladbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> race car drivers <a href="/wiki/Nick_Heidfeld" title="Nick Heidfeld">Nick Heidfeld</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinz-Harald_Frentzen" title="Heinz-Harald Frentzen">Heinz-Harald Frentzen</a>, author/cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Walter_Moers" title="Walter Moers">Walter Moers</a>, cabaret artist <a href="/wiki/Volker_Pispers" title="Volker Pispers">Volker Pispers</a>, philosopher <a href="/wiki/Hans_Jonas" title="Hans Jonas">Hans Jonas</a>, Goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Marc-Andr%C3%A9_ter_Stegen" title="Marc-André ter Stegen">Marc-André ter Stegen</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and the former football players and managers <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnther_Netzer" class="mw-redirect" title="Günther Netzer">Günther Netzer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jupp_Heynckes" title="Jupp Heynckes">Jupp Heynckes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pilates" title="Joseph Pilates">Joseph Pilates</a>, the creator of the <a href="/wiki/Pilates" title="Pilates">Pilates</a> method of physical fitness, was born here in 1883.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6nchengladbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_and_origins">Name and origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6nchengladbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Name and origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original name of the city was <b>Gladbach</b>, which is even today often applied to the town. To distinguish the town from another town of the same name (the present <a href="/wiki/Bergisch_Gladbach" title="Bergisch Gladbach">Bergisch Gladbach</a>), it took the name <b>München-Gladbach</b> in 1888. This spelling could mislead people to think that Gladbach was a borough of <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> (<i>München</i> in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>), and consequently the name was changed to Mönchen-Gladbach in 1950 and Mönchengladbach in 1960.</p>
<p>The origin of the town was an <a href="/wiki/Gladbach_Abbey" title="Gladbach Abbey">abbey</a> founded in 974. It was named after the Gladbach, a narrow brook, that mostly runs subterraneously today. The abbey and adjoining villages became a town in the 14th century. The town of <a href="/wiki/Rheydt" title="Rheydt">Rheydt</a> is located nearby and is incorporated into Mönchengladbach today.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6nchengladbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first settlements in the area of Mönchengladbach are approximately 300,000–400,000 years old and show remains of <a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a>. There are numerous <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>.</p>
<p>The history of Mönchengladbach began with the construction of the Gladbach Minster and the founding of an abbey in the year 974 by <a href="/wiki/Gero,_Archbishop_of_Cologne" class="mw-redirect" title="Gero, Archbishop of Cologne">Gero, Archbishop of Cologne</a>, and his companion, the monk Sandrad of <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Trier" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Trier">Trier</a>.</p>
<p>To advance the settlement, the monks created a market north of the church in the 12th century. Craftsmen settled near the market. Gladbach received its <a href="/wiki/German_town_law" title="German town law">town charter</a> in 1364–1366. The "town" got a town wall made of stone, which had to be maintained by the citizens. Remains of that wall can be found at the Geroweiher, as can remains of the "Thick Tower", an old fortified tower at the Waldhausener hill. Until the end of the 18th century the city belonged to the department of <a href="/wiki/Grevenbroich" title="Grevenbroich">Grevenbroich</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_J%C3%BClich" title="Duchy of Jülich">duchy of Jülich</a>.</p>
<p>On 4 October 1794, the armed forces of the <a href="/wiki/French_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="French revolution">French revolution</a> marched into the town, one day before the fortress Jülich had been handed over. When the Holy Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Francis_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Francis II, Holy Roman Emperor">Francis II</a> ceded the left bank of the river <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> to France with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lun%C3%A9ville" title="Treaty of Lunéville">Treaty of Lunéville</a> in 1801, Gladbach fell under French laws suppressing religion. This was the end for the abbey, and the monastery was closed. On 31 October 1802, the last 31 monks left the monastery. The contents of the tremendous abbey library, well-known outside of Germany, were scattered or destroyed.</p>
<p>From 1798 until 1814, the Mairie Gladbach was part of Kanton Odenkirchen, Arrondissement Krefeld, of the French <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9partement_de_la_Roer" class="mw-redirect" title="Département de la Roer">Département de la Roer</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6nchengladbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1815, Gladbach became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> and seat of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gladbach_(district)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gladbach (district) (page does not exist)">Landkreis Gladbach</a>, which was dissolved in 1929. In 1815 Gladbach became seat of the Bürgermeisterei (Office of mayor), which was split in 1859 into two parts: the City of Gladbach and Office of Mayor Obergeburth. The latter was renamed to München-Gladbach-Land in 1907.</p>
<p>From 1933 through 1975, the neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Rheydt" title="Rheydt">Rheydt</a> was an independent city; the split from München-Gladbach was arranged by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a>, who was born locally. After reuniting with Mönchengladbach, the central station (Rheydt Hauptbahnhof) kept its original name, making Mönchengladbach the only city in Germany to have two rail stations each called Hauptbahnhof.</p>
<p>In response to the 10 May 1940 <a href="/wiki/German_invasion_of_Belgium" title="German invasion of Belgium">German invasion of Belgium</a>, Mönchengladbach was bombed by British <a href="/wiki/Bomber_Command" title="Bomber Command">Bomber Command</a> on the evening of 11 May. The bomber crews were attempting to interdict German troop movements on roads, intersections and rail lines in the area, especially the city's railyards. About half of the approximately 36 twin-engine <a href="/wiki/RAF" class="mw-redirect" title="RAF">RAF</a> bombers reportedly hit their targets, and three were shot down.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Diefendorf1993_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diefendorf1993-6">[6]</a></sup> Four people were killed on the ground, including a British woman living in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Second World War, in compensation of the occupation of the Netherlands by Germany, several German cities were proposed to be ceded to the Kingdom of the Netherlands. Though never approved, the project would have renamed Mönchengladbach to Monniken-Glabbeek.</p>
<p>Eventually, the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Province" title="Rhine Province">Rhine Province</a> was dissolved after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and the city became part of the new state of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> which was formed in 1946.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6nchengladbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city has two <a href="/wiki/Hauptbahnhof" class="mw-redirect" title="Hauptbahnhof">main railway stations</a>: <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6nchengladbach_Hauptbahnhof" title="Mönchengladbach Hauptbahnhof">Mönchengladbach Hauptbahnhof</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rheydt_Hauptbahnhof" title="Rheydt Hauptbahnhof">Rheydt Hauptbahnhof</a>, the result of the merger of the two cities, in which the deprecated name for Rheydt Hbf was never removed. <a href="/wiki/S8_(Rhine-Ruhr_S-Bahn)" class="mw-redirect" title="S8 (Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn)">Line 8</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr_S-Bahn" title="Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn">Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn</a> connects the city to <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hagen" title="Hagen">Hagen</a>; an extension further westwards is being discussed. A number of <a href="/wiki/RegionalExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="RegionalExpress">regional trains</a> serve Mönchengladbach. By the end of 2009 it was the largest city in Germany without regular <a href="/wiki/InterCity" title="InterCity">long-distance</a> services. With the new schedule for 2010, Mönchengladbach got an <a href="/wiki/InterCity" title="InterCity">InterCity</a>/<a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">Intercity-Express</a> connection twice a week.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city also has a commercial airport called <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf-M%C3%B6nchengladbach_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Düsseldorf-Mönchengladbach Airport">Düsseldorf Mönchengladbach</a>.</p>
<p>Local bus and rail transport is carried out by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=NEW-AG&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="NEW-AG (page does not exist)">NEW-AG</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Verkehrsverbund_Rhein-Ruhr" title="Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Ruhr">VRR</a> transport association regulations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6nchengladbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Borussia_Park_M%C3%B6nchengladbach.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Borussia_Park_M%C3%B6nchengladbach.jpg/210px-Borussia_Park_M%C3%B6nchengladbach.jpg" width="210" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Borussia_Park_M%C3%B6nchengladbach.jpg/315px-Borussia_Park_M%C3%B6nchengladbach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Borussia_Park_M%C3%B6nchengladbach.jpg/420px-Borussia_Park_M%C3%B6nchengladbach.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Borussia_Park_M%C3%B6nchengladbach.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Borussia-Park" title="Borussia-Park">Borussia-Park</a> is the stadium of <a href="/wiki/Fu%C3%9Fball-Bundesliga" class="mw-redirect" title="Fußball-Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a> club <a href="/wiki/Borussia_M%C3%B6nchengladbach" title="Borussia Mönchengladbach">Borussia Mönchengladbach</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Mönchengladbach has a long <a href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (soccer)">football</a> tradition. Its home club <a href="/wiki/Borussia_M%C3%B6nchengladbach" title="Borussia Mönchengladbach">Borussia Mönchengladbach</a> is one of the country's most well-known, best-supported, and successful teams.</p>
<p><i>Die Fohlen</i> ("The Foals") with more than 50,000 members is rated actually as fourth largest <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fan-club&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fan-club (page does not exist)">fan-club</a> in Germany. Famous for the attacking style, Gladbach belonged to the strongest European football teams in the 1970s, winning the Bundesliga 5 times. The Club lost the <a href="/wiki/European_Cup_1976-77#Final" class="mw-redirect" title="European Cup 1976-77">1977</a> final of the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">European Cup</a> to <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a>, but also made four appearances in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Europa_League" title="UEFA Europa League">UEFA Cup</a> final with wins in <a href="/wiki/1974%E2%80%9375_UEFA_Cup#Final" title="1974–75 UEFA Cup">1975</a> and <a href="/wiki/1978%E2%80%9379_UEFA_Cup#Final" title="1978–79 UEFA Cup">1979</a> against losses in <a href="/wiki/1972%E2%80%9373_UEFA_Cup#Final" title="1972–73 UEFA Cup">1973</a> and <a href="/wiki/1979%E2%80%9380_UEFA_Cup#Final" title="1979–80 UEFA Cup">1980</a>. However, after a last place finish in the top flight for the 2006–2007 season, they were relegated to the <a href="/wiki/2._Fu%C3%9Fball-Bundesliga" class="mw-redirect" title="2. Fußball-Bundesliga">2. Bundesliga</a> (1. and 2. National Leagues) for the 2007–2008 season. They are playing in the <a href="/wiki/Fu%C3%9Fball-Bundesliga" class="mw-redirect" title="Fußball-Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a> again since 2008, surviving relegation in the 2010–2011 season and finishing 4th in the 2011-12 season.</p>
<p>On 30 July 2004 the opening of the new stadium "<a href="/wiki/Borussia-Park" title="Borussia-Park">Borussia-Park</a>" was celebrated. It has a capacity of 54,700 visitors (seated: 34,300, standing: 20,400 / capacity on International games: 45,600). The <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">stadium</a> can be reached by <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">car</a> (through dedicated exit on the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> "A 61"), <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">train</a>.</p>
<p>The city hosted the <a href="/wiki/2006_Men%27s_World_Hockey_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="2006 Men's World Hockey Cup">FIH Hockey World Cup 2006</a> during the period of September 6–17 and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_FIH_Champions_Trophy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="2010 FIH Champions Trophy (page does not exist)">2010 FIH Champions Trophy</a> in July/August that year.</p>
<p>Since 1892, Mönchengladbach owns a <a href="/wiki/Harness_racing" title="Harness racing">harness racing</a> track called "Trabrennbahn Mönchengladbach".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%B6nchengladbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rheindahlen_Military_Complex" class="mw-redirect" title="Rheindahlen Military Complex">Rheindahlen Military Complex</a></div>

<p><b>Trier</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[tʁiːɐ̯]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Trier.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6f/Trier.ogg" class="internal" title="Trier.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Luxembourgish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Luxembourgish language">Luxembourgish</a>: <span lang="lb" xml:lang="lb"><i>Tréier</i></span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Luxembourgish" title="Help:IPA/Luxembourgish">[ˈtʀɜɪ̯ɐ]</a></span>), formerly known in English as <b>Treves</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Trèves</i></span>, <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[tʁɛv]</a></span>) and <b>Triers</b> (see also <a href="/wiki/Names_of_Trier_in_different_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Names of Trier in different languages">names in other languages</a>), is a city in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Moselle_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Moselle (river)">Moselle</a>. Trier lies in a valley between low vine-covered hills of red <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> in the west of the state of <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>, near the border with <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> and within the important <a href="/wiki/Mosel_(wine_region)" title="Mosel (wine region)">Moselle wine region</a>.</p>
<p>Founded by the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> in the late-4th century&#160;BC as <i>Treuorum</i>, it was later conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Romans" class="mw-redirect" title="Romans">Romans</a> in the late-1st century BC and renamed Trevorum or <i>Augusta&#160;Treverorum</i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "The City of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> among the <a href="/wiki/Treveri" title="Treveri">Treveri</a>"). Trier may be the oldest city in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-Trier_website_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trier_website-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is also the oldest <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">seat</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the Archbishop-Elector of <a href="/wiki/Archbishop-Electorate_of_Trier" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop-Electorate of Trier">Trier</a> was an important <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_the_Church" title="Prince of the Church">prince of the church</a>, as the archbishop-electorate controlled land from the French border to the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>. The Archbishop-Elector also had great significance as one of the seven <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">electors</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>.</p>
<p>With an approximate population of 105,000, Trier is the fourth-largest city in its state, after <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen" title="Ludwigshafen">Ludwigshafen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The nearest major cities are <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Luxembourg (city)">Luxembourg</a> (50&#160;km or 31&#160;mi to the southwest), <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken" title="Saarbrücken">Saarbrücken</a> (80 kilometres or 50 miles southeast), and <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a> (100&#160;km or 62&#160;mi northeast).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Trier" title="University of Trier">University of Trier</a>, the administration of the <a href="/wiki/Trier-Saarburg" title="Trier-Saarburg">Trier-Saarburg</a> district and the seat of the ADD (<i>Aufsichts- und Dienstleistungsdirektion</i>), which until 1999 was the borough authority of Trier, and the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_European_Law" title="Academy of European Law">Academy of European Law</a> (ERA) are all based in Trier. It is one of the five "<a href="/wiki/Central_place_theory" title="Central place theory">central places</a>" of the state of Rhineland-Palatinate. Along with Luxembourg, <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> and Saarbrücken, fellow constituent members of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=QuattroPole&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="QuattroPole (page does not exist)">QuattroPole</a> union of cities, it is central to the greater region encompassing <a href="/wiki/Saar-Lor-Lux" class="mw-redirect" title="Saar-Lor-Lux">Saar-Lor-Lux</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_(r%C3%A9gion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorraine (région)">Lorraine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>), Rhineland-Palatinate, and <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Trier" title="History of Trier">History of Trier</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trier_Porta_Nigra_BW_1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Trier_Porta_Nigra_BW_1.JPG/250px-Trier_Porta_Nigra_BW_1.JPG" width="250" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Trier_Porta_Nigra_BW_1.JPG/375px-Trier_Porta_Nigra_BW_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Trier_Porta_Nigra_BW_1.JPG/500px-Trier_Porta_Nigra_BW_1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3728" data-file-height="2548" /></a>

<p>The first traces of human settlement in the area of the city show evidence of <a href="/wiki/Linear_Pottery_culture" title="Linear Pottery culture">linear pottery</a> settlements dating from the early <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period. Since the last <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">pre-Christian</a> centuries, members of the <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celtic</a> tribe of the <a href="/wiki/Treveri" title="Treveri">Treveri</a> settled in the area of today's Trier.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The city of <a class="mw-selflink selflink">Trier</a> derives its name from the later Latin locative <i>in Trēverīs</i> for earlier <i>Augusta Treverorum</i>.</p>
<p>The historical record describes the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> subduing the <a href="/wiki/Treveri" title="Treveri">Treveri</a> in the <span class="nowrap">1st century <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span></span> and establishing Augusta Treverorum in 16&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The name distinguished it from the empire's <a href="/wiki/Augusta_(disambiguation)#Places" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Augusta (disambiguation)">many other cities</a> honoring the <a href="/wiki/Roman_emperor" title="Roman emperor">first emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>. The city later became the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Belgica" title="Gallia Belgica">Belgic Gaul</a>; after the <a href="/wiki/Diocletian_Reforms" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocletian Reforms">Diocletian Reforms</a>, it became the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefecture" title="Praetorian prefecture">prefecture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Praetorian_Prefecture_of_Gaul" class="mw-redirect" title="Praetorian Prefecture of Gaul">the Gauls</a>, overseeing much of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>. In the 4th century, Trier was one of the largest cities in the Roman Empire with a population around 75,000 and perhaps as much as 100,000.<sup id="cite_ref-landesmuseum-trier_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-landesmuseum-trier-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-google_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-academia_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-academia-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Porta_Nigra" title="Porta Nigra">Porta Nigra</a> ("Black Gate") dates from this era. A residence of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Roman Emperor">Western Roman Emperor</a>, Roman Trier was the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ambrose" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Ambrose">Saint Ambrose</a>. Sometime between 395 and 418, probably in 407 the Roman administration moved the staff of the Praetorian Prefecture about 2000 from the city to <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">Arles</a>. The city continued to be inhabited but was not as prosperous as before. However, it remained the seat of a governor and had state factories for the production of <a href="/wiki/Ballistae" class="mw-redirect" title="Ballistae">ballistae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_armor" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman armor">armor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_military_uniform" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman military uniform">woolen uniforms</a> for <a href="/wiki/Roman_soldiers" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman soldiers">the troops</a>, clothing for the civil service, and high-quality garments for the Court. Northern Gaul was held by the Romans along a line from north of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> to the coast at <a href="/wiki/Boulogne" class="mw-redirect" title="Boulogne">Boulogne</a> through what is today southern Belgium until 460. South of this line, Roman control was firm, as evidenced by the continuing operation of the imperial arms factory at <a href="/wiki/Amiens" title="Amiens">Amiens</a>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> seized Trier from Roman administration in 459. In 870, it became part of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Francia" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Francia">Eastern Francia</a>, which developed into the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. Relics of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Matthias" title="Saint Matthias">Saint Matthias</a> brought to the city initiated widespread pilgrimages. The bishops of the city grew increasingly powerful and the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Trier" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Trier">Archbishopric of Trier</a> was recognized as an <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">electorate</a> of the empire, one of the most powerful states of Germany. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Trier" title="University of Trier">University of Trier</a> was founded in the city in 1473. In the 17th century, the Archbishops and Prince-Electors of Trier relocated their residences to <a href="/wiki/Philippsburg" title="Philippsburg">Philippsburg</a> Castle in <a href="/wiki/Festung_Ehrenbreitstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Festung Ehrenbreitstein">Ehrenbreitstein</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a>. A session of the <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichstag (Holy Roman Empire)">Reichstag</a> was held in Trier in 1512, during which the demarcation of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Circle" title="Imperial Circle">Imperial Circles</a> was definitively established.</p>

<p>In the 17th and 18th centuries, Trier was sought after by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, who invaded during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Grand_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="War of the Grand Alliance">War of the Grand Alliance</a>, the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Polish_Succession" title="War of the Polish Succession">War of the Polish Succession</a>. France succeeded in finally claiming Trier in 1794 during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a>, and the electoral archbishopric was dissolved. After the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> ended in 1815, Trier passed to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>. The German philosopher and one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> was born in the city in 1818.</p>
<p>As part of the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Rhineland" class="mw-redirect" title="Prussian Rhineland">Prussian Rhineland</a>, Trier developed economically during the 19th century. The city rose in revolt during the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848_in_the_German_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Revolutions of 1848 in the German states">revolutions of 1848 in the German states</a>, although the rebels were forced to concede. It became part of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871.</p>
<p>In June 1940 over 60,000 British prisoners of war, captured at Dunkirk and Northern France, were marched to Trier, which became a staging post for British soldiers headed for German <a href="/wiki/Prisoner-of-war_camp" title="Prisoner-of-war camp">prisoner-of-war camps</a>. Trier was heavily bombed and bombarded in 1944 during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The city became part of the new state of <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a> after the war. The university, dissolved in 1797, was restarted in the 1970s, while the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Trier" title="Cathedral of Trier">Cathedral of Trier</a> was reopened in 1974. Trier officially celebrated its 2,000th anniversary in 1984.</p>

<p>Trier sits in a hollow midway along the <a href="/wiki/Moselle_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Moselle (river)">Moselle</a> valley, with the most significant portion of the city on the east bank of the river. Wooded and <a href="/wiki/Vineyard" title="Vineyard">vineyard</a>-covered slopes stretch up to the <a href="/wiki/Hunsr%C3%BCck" title="Hunsrück">Hunsrück</a> plateau in the south and the <a href="/wiki/Eifel" title="Eifel">Eifel</a> in the north. The border with the <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Grand Duchy of Luxembourg</a> is some 15&#160;km (9&#160;mi) away.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization_of_city_districts">Organization of city districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organization of city districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Trier urban area is divided into <b>19 city districts</b>. For each district there is an <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortsbeirat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ortsbeirat (page does not exist)">Ortsbeirat</a></i> (local council) of between 9 and 15 members, as well as an <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ortsvorsteher&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ortsvorsteher (page does not exist)">Ortsvorsteher</a></i> (local representative). The local councils are charged with hearing the important issues that affect the district, although the final decision on any issue rests with the city council. The local councils nevertheless have the freedom to undertake limited measures within the bounds of their districts and their budgets.</p>

<p>Trier is known for its well-preserved Roman and medieval buildings, which include:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <i><a href="/wiki/Porta_Nigra" title="Porta Nigra">Porta Nigra</a></i>, the best-preserved Roman <a href="/wiki/City_gate" title="City gate">city gate</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>;</li>
<li>ruins of three <a href="/wiki/Roman_baths" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman baths">Roman baths</a>, among them the largest Roman baths north of the Alps; including the <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Baths" title="Barbara Baths">Barbara Baths</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Trier_Imperial_Baths" title="Trier Imperial Baths">Trier Imperial Baths</a>;</li>
<li>the huge <a href="/wiki/Aula_Palatina" title="Aula Palatina">Constantine Basilica</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> in the original Roman sense, was the 67&#160;m (219.82&#160;ft) long throne hall of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I_of_the_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I of the Roman Empire">Constantine</a>; it is today used as a <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trier_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="Trier Cathedral">Trier Cathedral</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Trierer Dom</span> or <i>Dom St. Peter</i>), a Roman Catholic church that dates back to Roman times and is home to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Tunic" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Tunic">Holy Tunic</a>, a garment with a recorded history back to the 12th century, in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholic</a> tradition said to be the robe <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> was wearing when he died. It is exhibited only every few decades, at irregular intervals.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Liebfrauenkirche,_Trier" title="Liebfrauenkirche, Trier">Liebfrauenkirche</a></i> (German for <i>Church of <a href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Our dear Lady</a></i>), which is one of the most important early <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> cathedrals in Germany and falls into the architectural tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture#France" title="Gothic architecture">French Gothic</a> cathedrals;</li>
<li>the Roman <a href="/wiki/Trier_Amphitheater" title="Trier Amphitheater">Trier Amphitheater</a>;</li>
<li>the 2nd century AD <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B6merbr%C3%BCcke_(Trier)" class="mw-redirect" title="Römerbrücke (Trier)">Roman bridge</a> (<i>Römerbrücke</i>) across the Moselle, the oldest bridge north of the Alps still crossed by traffic;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/St._Matthias%27_Abbey,_Trier" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Matthias' Abbey, Trier">St. Matthias' Abbey</a> (<i>Abtei St. Matthias</i>), a still-in-use monastery in whose medieval church the only <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve Apostles">apostle</a> north of the Alps is held to be buried</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/St._Gangolf%27s_church" title="St. Gangolf's church">St. Gangolf's church</a> was the city's market church that rivalled the Archbishop's Trier Cathedral.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/St._Paulinus%27_Church,_Trier" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paulinus' Church, Trier">Saint Paulinus' Church</a>, one of the most important <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque</a> churches in Rhineland-Palatinate and designed in part by the architect <a href="/wiki/Balthasar_Neumann" title="Balthasar Neumann">Balthasar Neumann</a></li>
<li>two old <a href="/wiki/Treadwheel" title="Treadwheel">treadwheel</a> <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">cranes</a>, one being the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> "Old Crane" (<i>Alte Krahnen</i>) or "Trier Moselle Crane" (<i>Trierer Moselkrahn</i>) from 1413, and the other the 1774 <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque</a> crane called the "(Old) Customs Crane" (<i>(Alter) Zollkran</i>) or "Younger Moselle Crane" (<i>Jüngerer Moselkran</i>) (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_historical_harbour_cranes" title="List of historical harbour cranes">List of historical harbour cranes</a>)</li>
</ul>

<li><i><a href="/wiki/Rheinisches_Landesmuseum_Trier" title="Rheinisches Landesmuseum Trier">Rheinisches Landesmuseum</a></i> (one of the two most important German archaeological museums for the Roman period, along with the <i>Römisch-Germanisches Museum</i> in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>)</li>

<p>Trier is home to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Trier" title="University of Trier">University of Trier</a>, founded in 1473, closed in 1796 and restarted in 1970. The city also has the <a href="/wiki/Trier_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Trier University of Applied Sciences">Trier University of Applied Sciences</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_European_Law" title="Academy of European Law">Academy of European Law</a> (ERA) was established in 1992 and provides training in European law to legal practitioners. In 2010 there were about 40 <i>Kindergärten</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-trier_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trier-10">[10]</a></sup> 25 primary schools and 23 secondary schools in Trier, such as the <i>Humboldt Gymnasium Trier</i>, <i>Max Planck Gymnasium</i>, <i>Auguste Viktoria Gymnasium</i> and the <i>Nelson-Mandela Realschule Plus</i>, <i>Kurfürst-Balduin Realschule Plus</i>, <i>Realschule Plus Ehrang</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-trier2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trier2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Annual_events">Annual events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Annual events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Until 2014, Trier was home to Germany's largest Roman festival, <a href="/wiki/Brot_und_Spiele" title="Brot und Spiele">Brot und Spiele</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> for <i>Bread and Games</i> - a translation of the famous Latin phrase <a href="/wiki/Bread_and_circuses" title="Bread and circuses"><i>panem et circenses</i></a> from the <a href="/wiki/Satires_of_Juvenal" class="mw-redirect" title="Satires of Juvenal">satires</a> of <a href="/wiki/Juvenal" title="Juvenal">Juvenal</a>)</li>
<li>Trier has been the base for the <a href="/wiki/Rallye_Deutschland" title="Rallye Deutschland">German round</a> of the <a href="/wiki/World_Rally_Championship" title="World Rally Championship">World Rally Championship</a> since 2000, with the rally's presentation held next to the Porta Nigra.</li>
<li>Trier holds a lavish Christmas street festival every year called the Trier Christmas Market on the <i>Hauptmarkt</i> (Main Market Square) and the <i>Domfreihof</i> in front of the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Trier" title="Cathedral of Trier">Cathedral of Trier</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Trier_Hauptbahnhof" title="Trier Hauptbahnhof">Trier station</a> has direct <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">railway</a> connections to many cities in the region. The nearest cities by <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">train</a> are Cologne, Saarbrücken and Luxembourg. Via the motorways <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_1" title="Bundesautobahn 1">A 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_48" title="Bundesautobahn 48">A 48</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_64" title="Bundesautobahn 64">A 64</a> Trier is linked with Koblenz, Saarbrücken and Luxembourg. The nearest commercial (international) airports are in <a href="/wiki/Findel_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Findel Airport">Luxembourg</a> (0:40 h by car), <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt-Hahn" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt-Hahn">Frankfurt-Hahn</a> (1:00 h), <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Airport" title="Saarbrücken Airport">Saarbrücken</a> (1:00 h), <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main Airport">Frankfurt</a> (2:00 h) and <a href="/wiki/Cologne/Bonn_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Cologne/Bonn Airport">Cologne/Bonn</a> (2:00 h). The <a href="/wiki/Moselle_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Moselle (river)">Moselle</a> is an important waterway and is also used for river cruises. A new passenger railway service on the western side of the Mosel is scheduled to open in December 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

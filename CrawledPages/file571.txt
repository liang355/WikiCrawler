<p>The <b>Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study at Harvard</b> shares transformative ideas across the arts, humanities, sciences, and social sciences. The Institute comprises three programs:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Radcliffe Institute Fellowship Program annually supports the work of 50 artists and scholars, with an acceptance rate of 5 percent each year.</li>
<li>The Academic Ventures program is for collaborative research projects and hosts lectures and conferences.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Arthur_and_Elizabeth_Schlesinger_Library_on_the_History_of_Women_in_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur and Elizabeth Schlesinger Library on the History of Women in America">Arthur and Elizabeth Schlesinger Library on the History of Women in America</a> documents the lives of <a href="/wiki/American_women" class="mw-redirect" title="American women">American women</a> of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_women_in_the_United_States" title="History of women in the United States">past</a> and present for the future.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Radcliffe Institute hosts public events, many of which can be watched online. It is one of the nine member institutions of the <a href="/wiki/Some_Institutes_for_Advanced_Study" title="Some Institutes for Advanced Study">Some Institutes for Advanced Study</a> consortium. <a href="/wiki/Lizabeth_Cohen" title="Lizabeth Cohen">Lizabeth Cohen</a>, the Howard Mumford Jones Professor of American Studies in the Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences, is the dean of the Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radcliffe_Institute_for_Advanced_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 1, 1999, <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_College" title="Radcliffe College">Radcliffe College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> officially merged, establishing the Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study at Harvard. On January 1, 2001, historian Drew Gilpin Faust became dean of the Radcliffe Institute (she then became the president of Harvard University in 2007). Radcliffe's history as an institute began much before then: the Radcliffe Institute for Independent Study was founded in 1961 by the President of Radcliffe College, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Ingraham_Bunting" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary Ingraham Bunting">Mary Ingraham Bunting</a>. Following Bunting's vision and her desire to stem the exodus of highly trained educated women from promising careers, the Institute provided stipends as well as access to all of the resources of Harvard University to take up their chosen creative intellectual studies,.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes1998_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1998-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-yaffe365_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yaffe365-2">[2]</a></sup> The initial funding for the institute came from the Carnegie and Rockefeller Foundations,.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes1998_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1998-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-yaffe365_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yaffe365-2">[2]</a></sup> The Institute was renamed the Bunting Institute in 1978 in honor of Dr. Bunting and also supported women wishing to pursue advanced degrees on a part-time basis. The Institute was later renamed the Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study.</p>
<p>The current Institute came into being by the agreement of October 1, 1999, under which Radcliffe College merged formally into Harvard University.<sup id="cite_ref-radcliffe1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radcliffe1-3">[3]</a></sup> However, long before this date the focus of Radcliffe had already begun to shift, as undergraduate women had for a half-century taken their classes at Harvard, and for a quarter-century lived integrated in dormitories with Harvard men.</p>
<p>In 2001 the Institute was endowed with the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carol_K._Pforzheimer_Professorship&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carol K. Pforzheimer Professorship (page does not exist)">Carol K. Pforzheimer Professorship</a> at Radcliffe, the Institute’s first professorship by the Pforzheimer family who also endowed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carl_and_Lily_Pforzheimer_Foundation_Directorship&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carl and Lily Pforzheimer Foundation Directorship (page does not exist)">Carl and Lily Pforzheimer Foundation Directorship</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carol_K._Pforzheimer_Student_Fellowship&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carol K. Pforzheimer Student Fellowship (page does not exist)">Carol K. Pforzheimer Student Fellowship</a> at the Institute's <a href="/wiki/Schlesinger_Library" title="Schlesinger Library">Schlesinger Library</a> on the History of Women in America, which, with the Radcliffe Institute Fellowship Program, both of which date back to Radcliffe College days, are among the Institute's best-known features.<sup id="cite_ref-radcliffe1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radcliffe1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the period of transition from College to Institute, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Maples_Dunn" title="Mary Maples Dunn">Mary Maples Dunn</a> served as interim dean. On January 1, 2001, <a href="/wiki/Drew_Gilpin_Faust" title="Drew Gilpin Faust">Drew Gilpin Faust</a> became the Institute's first permanent dean; she stepped down in July 2007 to become <a href="/wiki/President_of_Harvard_University" title="President of Harvard University">President of Harvard University</a>. Barbara J. Grosz, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Higgins_Professor_of_Natural_Sciences&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Higgins Professor of Natural Sciences (page does not exist)">Higgins Professor of Natural Sciences</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_School_of_Engineering_and_Applied_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard School of Engineering and Applied Sciences">Harvard School of Engineering and Applied Sciences</a>, served as interim dean starting in July 2007 and was named dean of the institute on April 28, 2008. She stepped down from that post in June 2011.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schlesinger_Library">Schlesinger Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radcliffe_Institute_for_Advanced_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Schlesinger Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Schlesinger_Library" title="Schlesinger Library">Schlesinger Library</a></div>
<p>The Arthur and Elizabeth Schlesinger Library on the History of Women in America exists to document women's lives and endeavors. Its wealth of resources reveals the wide range of women's activities at home in the United States and abroad from the early 19th century to the present day. The library’s holdings include manuscripts; books and periodicals; and photographic and audiovisual material.</p>
<p>There are more than 2,500 unique manuscript collections from individuals, families, and organizations. Women's rights movements past and present, feminism, health and sexuality, social reform, and the <a href="/wiki/Education_of_women_and_girls" class="mw-redirect" title="Education of women and girls">education of women and girls</a> are manuscript holdings. Ordinary lives of women and families and the struggles and triumphs of women of accomplishment are richly documented in diaries and other personal records. Many collections, such as the papers of <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Perkins_Gilman" title="Charlotte Perkins Gilman">Charlotte Perkins Gilman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pauli_Murray" title="Pauli Murray">Pauli Murray</a>, and the records of the <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Women" title="National Organization for Women">National Organization for Women</a>, feature political, organizational, and economic questions. In addition to these collections, the library also houses the works of <a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">Susan B. Anthony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Earhart" title="Amelia Earhart">Amelia Earhart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Betty_Friedan" title="Betty Friedan">Betty Friedan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adrienne_Rich" title="Adrienne Rich">Adrienne Rich</a>, and many others.</p>
<p>Books and Periodicals: More than 80,000 printed volumes include scholarly monographs as well as popular works. These cover topics including women’s rights; women and work; women’s health; women of color; comparative material about women in other cultures; works on women in the arts and in music; women and family; feminist and anti-feminist theory; and lesbian writings. Hundreds of periodical titles, including popular magazines such as <i><a href="/wiki/Ladies%27_Home_Journal" title="Ladies' Home Journal">Ladies' Home Journal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ebony_(magazine)" title="Ebony (magazine)">Ebony</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Seventeen_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Seventeen (magazine)">Seventeen</a></i>, highlight domestic concerns, leisure pursuits, etiquette, fashion, and food.</p>
<p>Photographic and Audiovisual Material: More than 90,000 photographs, ranging from casual snapshots to the works of professional photographers, create an unparalleled visual record of private and public life. Audiotapes, videotapes and oral history tapes, and transcripts add the soundtrack to the story of women’s lives.</p>
<p>The library has two distinguished special collections. A <a href="/wiki/Cookbook" title="Cookbook">culinary collection</a> of over 15,000 books—spanning five centuries and <a href="/wiki/Global_cuisines" class="mw-redirect" title="Global cuisines">global cuisines</a>—is one of the world's most significant. This collection also includes the papers of several famous chefs and foodwriters such as <a href="/wiki/M._F._K._Fisher" title="M. F. K. Fisher">M. F. K. Fisher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julia_Child" title="Julia Child">Julia Child</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_David" title="Elizabeth David">Elizabeth David</a>. The archives of Radcliffe College, 1879-1999—including papers of college officers, students, and alumnae—richly record the history of women in higher education.</p>
<p>While its focus for collecting is <a href="/wiki/American_women" class="mw-redirect" title="American women">American women</a>, the library has an abundance of print and manuscript materials bearing on issues around the globe as a result of American women's extensive travel and foreign residence. Some examples are letters of early missionaries in China, activists' accounts of the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Encampment_for_a_Future_of_Peace_and_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="Women's Encampment for a Future of Peace and Justice">Women's Encampment for a Future of Peace and Justice</a>, and the speeches and writings of <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Graham_Du_Bois" title="Shirley Graham Du Bois">Shirley Graham Du Bois</a>.</p>
<p>Detailed records for the library’s manuscript collections as well as books and periodicals can be found in HOLLIS. The catalog record gives a description of the item or collection and provides other important information such as offsite location or access restrictions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radcliffe_Institute_Fellowship_Program">Radcliffe Institute Fellowship Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radcliffe_Institute_for_Advanced_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radcliffe Institute Fellowship Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Radcliffe Institute fellowships are designed to support scholars, scientists, artists, and writers of exceptional promise and demonstrated accomplishments who wish to pursue work in academic and professional fields and in the creative arts.</p>
<p>The Radcliffe Institute Fellowship Program was founded at Radcliffe College in 1960 as the Radcliffe Institute for Independent Study. In 1978 the Institute was renamed the Mary Ingraham Bunting Institute to honor Radcliffe College President <a href="/wiki/Mary_Bunting" title="Mary Bunting">Mary Bunting</a> whose initiative it was to create a postgraduate study center for female scholars and artists. Concerned about the prevailing "climate of unexpectation" for women at that time, Bunting deliberately sought to reverse that negative attitude by establishing the essential gifts of an Institute fellowship: time, financial support, a room of one's own, membership in a vital community of women, and access to all Radcliffe and Harvard resources.</p>
<p>Once Bunting’s idea was made public and the announcement appeared on the front page of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> in the fall of 1960, more than 2,000 women inquired about the "experiment." The outpouring of interest confirmed President Bunting's hunch—that a growing number of educated women were ready to resume intellectual or artistic work after raising families.</p>
<p>Since 1960, more than 1,300 scholars, scientists, artists, writers, and musicians have been named fellows. The <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Globe_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Globe Magazine">Boston Globe Magazine</a></i> called the Bunting Institute "America's Think Tank for Women," and the <i>Chronicle of Higher Education</i> described the Institute as a place where "lives get turned around, books get written, and discoveries are made, all the result of time spent among intellectual peers."</p>
<p>In addition, research clusters at the Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study use the Radcliffe Fellowship Program to draw together scholars to focus on particular themes. Previous cluster topics include unconscious prejudice and the law, immigration, randomness and computation, and cosmology and theoretical astrophysics.</p>
<p>The program is currently run out of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Byerly_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Byerly Hall (page does not exist)">Byerly Hall</a> one of the historic buildings of Radcliffe Yard.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_Ventures">Academic Ventures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radcliffe_Institute_for_Advanced_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Academic Ventures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to the Schlesinger Library and the Fellowship Program, Radcliffe's newest program, Academic Ventures (formerly known as Academic Engagement Programs) was created in 2009. It serves as an internal link for Harvard faculty, graduate and undergraduate students, and fellows, creating more opportunities for intellectual sharing, exploring, and collaboration. In addition, the general public is invited to attend select events.</p>

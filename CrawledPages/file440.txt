<p><b>Salzgitter</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[zalt͡sˈɡɪtɐ]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Salzgitter.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/De-Salzgitter.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Salzgitter.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/Independent_city#Germany" title="Independent city">independent city</a> in southeast <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, located between <a href="/wiki/Hildesheim" title="Hildesheim">Hildesheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Braunschweig" title="Braunschweig">Braunschweig</a>. Together with <a href="/wiki/Wolfsburg" title="Wolfsburg">Wolfsburg</a> and Braunschweig, Salzgitter is one of the seven <i>Oberzentren</i> of Lower Saxony (roughly equivalent to a <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a>). With 109,142 inhabitants and 223.94 square kilometres (86.46&#160;sq&#160;mi) (as of 30 January 2004), its <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">area</a> is the largest in Lower Saxony and one of the largest in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Salzgitter originated as a conglomeration of several small towns and villages, and is today made up of 31 boroughs, which are relatively compact conurbations with wide stretches of open country between them. The main shopping street of the young city is in the borough of Lebenstedt, and the <a href="/wiki/Central_business_district" title="Central business district">central business district</a> is in the borough of Salzgitter-Bad. The city is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Mittellandkanal" class="mw-redirect" title="Mittellandkanal">Mittellandkanal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Elbe-Seitenkanal" class="mw-redirect" title="Elbe-Seitenkanal">Elbe-Seitenkanal</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Distributary" title="Distributary">distributary</a>. The nearest <a href="/wiki/Metropolis" title="Metropolis">metropolises</a> are Braunschweig, about 23 kilometres (14 miles) to the northeast, and <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, about 51&#160;km (32 miles) to the northwest. The <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of the City of Salzgitter has exceeded 100,000 inhabitants since its foundation in 1942 (which made it a city (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Großstadt</i></span>) in contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a> (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Stadt</i></span>) by the German definition), when it was still called <i>Watenstedt-Salzgitter</i>. Beside Wolfsburg, <a href="/wiki/Leverkusen" title="Leverkusen">Leverkusen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eisenh%C3%BCttenstadt" title="Eisenhüttenstadt">Eisenhüttenstadt</a>, Salzgitter is therefore one of the few cities in Germany founded during the 20th century.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_name">History of the name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until 31 March 1942, "Salzgitter" was the name of a town where the borough <a href="/wiki/Salzgitter-Bad" title="Salzgitter-Bad">Salzgitter-Bad</a> now is. From then until 1951, "Salzgitter" was the name of a borough of the city Watenstedt-Salzgitter that existed at the time. In 1951, the borough Salzgitter was renamed Salzgitter-Bad; the name Salzgitter, having thus been freed up, became the new and more succinct name of the city that had been called "Watenstedt-Salzgitter" until then. (Nowadays, "Salzgitter-Watenstedt" is the name of a small borough with a few hundred inhabitants.)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Salzgitter is located in a bride dell coated with <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a>, between the Oderwald Forest and the Salzgitter-Höhenzug ("Salzgitter Hills"). The city stretches up to 24&#160;km (15&#160;mi) from north to south and up to 19&#160;km (12&#160;mi) from east to west. The highest point is the hill Hamberg (275&#160;m or 902&#160;ft), located northwest of Salzgitter-Bad.</p>

<p>The area of the City of Salzgitter consists of 31 boroughs (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Stadtteile</span>; often called <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">villages</a>): Bad, Barum, Beddingen, Beinum, Bleckenstedt, Bruchmachtersen, Calbecht, Drütte, Engelnstedt, Engerode, Flachstöckheim, Gebhardshagen, Gitter, Groß Mahner, Hallendorf, Heerte, Hohenrode, Immendorf, Lebenstedt, Lesse, Lichtenberg, Lobmachtersen, Ohlendorf, Osterlinde, Reppner, <a href="/wiki/Salzgitter-Ringelheim" title="Salzgitter-Ringelheim">Ringelheim</a>, Salder, Sauingen, Thiede, Üfingen and Watenstedt.</p>
<p>These 31 boroughs are combined to 7 towns (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Ortschaften</span>). Each town has an elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Town_council" title="Town council">town council</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Salzgitter originated in the beginning of the 14th century around salt springs near the village Verpstedt (later Vöppstedt). The name was derived from the neighbouring village Gitter (nowadays a city borough) as "up dem solte to Gytere", which means "salt near Gitter"; the first mention was in 1347. After 200 years of salt production at various springs, the <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasants</a> in the area which is nowadays Salzgitter were chartered around 1350, but lost municipal law again when being transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Duchy of Brunswick-Lüneburg">Duchy of Brunswick-Lüneburg</a> in the beginning of the 16th century. Later, Salzgitter belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Hildesheim" title="Bishopric of Hildesheim">diocese of Hildesheim</a>. When the diocese was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> in 1803, the municipal law was reconfirmed, but taken away once more in 1815, when Salzgitter became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Kingdom of Hanover</a>. In 1830, a brine bath was established in Salzgitter.</p>
<p>After the Kingdom of Hanover was transferred to Prussia in 1866, Salzgitter became a Prussian municipality, which was chartered again in 1929. Prior to that, the towns Vorsalz and Liebenhall had been incorporated (in 1926 and 1928, respectively). Salzgitter now belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Landkreis" class="mw-redirect" title="Landkreis">Landkreis</a> (district) of Goslar and included, apart from Salzgitter itself, also some small settlements like Gittertor, which is nowadays part of Salzgitter-Bad. In 1936, Kniestedt was incorporated; it is also part of Salzgitter-Bad now.</p>

<p>Due to the large <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> body in Salzgitter, which had been mentioned first in 1310, the <a href="/wiki/National_Socialist_German_Workers_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="National Socialist German Workers Party">National Socialists</a> founded the "<a href="/wiki/Reichswerke_Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Reichswerke Hermann Göring">Reichswerke Hermann Göring</a>" for ore mining and <a href="/wiki/Ironworks" title="Ironworks">iron production</a> in 1937. In order to facilitate an unobstructed development of the smelting works, a unique <a href="/wiki/Administration_(government)" title="Administration (government)">administration</a> structure in the whole area was conceived. Therefore, it was decreed in the <i>Order about the area settlement around the Hermann-Göring-Werke Salzgitter</i>, effective from 1 April 1942, to form a unified city district (<a href="/wiki/Independent_city" title="Independent city">independent city</a>). Towards this aim, the town of Salzgitter and the municipalities Beinum, Flachstöckheim, Groß-Mahner, Hohenrode, Ohlendorf and Ringelheim (7 in total, all belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Goslar_(district)" title="Goslar (district)">Landkreis Goslar</a>) and Barum, Beddingen, Bleckenstedt, Bruchmachtersen, Calbecht, Drütte, Engelnstedt, Engerode, Gebhardshagen, Hallendorf, Heerte, Immendorf, Lebenstedt, Lesse, Lichtenberg, Lobmachtersen, Osterlinde, Reppner, Salder, Thiede-Steterburg (nowadays simply Thiede) and Watenstedt (21 in total, all belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Wolfenb%C3%BCttel_(district)" title="Wolfenbüttel (district)">Landkreis Wolfenbüttel</a>) were merged to form the <i>Stadtkreis Watenstedt-Salzgitter</i>. As the neighbouring municipality Gitter had already been incorporated in 1938, the young city initially comprised 29 boroughs in 1942. Together with the remainder of the district of Goslar, the new independent municipality was integrated into the <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Brunswick" title="Free State of Brunswick">Free State of Brunswick</a>. In return, Braunschweig transferred the <a href="/wiki/Holzminden_(district)" title="Holzminden (district)">Landkreis Holzminden</a> to the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hanover" title="Province of Hanover">Province of Hanover</a>. In October, 1942, the <a href="/wiki/SS" class="mw-redirect" title="SS">SS</a> established the Drütte concentration camp, a subcamp of the <a href="/wiki/Neuengamme_concentration_camp" title="Neuengamme concentration camp">Neuengamme concentration camp</a>, to provide <a href="/wiki/Slave_labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave labour">slave labour</a> for the Hermann Göring Works.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This large subcamp held 2,800 inmates. There were three concentration camps located in Salzgitter. During the war, Salzgitter was severely damaged by several <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Bombing" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombing">bombings</a>. After the war, the State of Braunschweig became part of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">Land</a> <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a>, and Watenstedt-Salzgitter became an Independent City in the "Administrative District of Braunschweig" (later <a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">Regierungsbezirk</a> <a href="/wiki/Braunschweig_(region)" title="Braunschweig (region)">Braunschweig</a>).</p>

<p>In 1951, the city was renamed to "Stadt Salzgitter" (City of Salzgitter), while the borough Salzgitter was renamed to "Salzgitter-Bad", referring to the brine bath there. In the course of the local administrative reform of Lower-Saxony effective from 1 March 1974, the municipalities Üfingen and Sauingen (formerly Landkreis Wolfenbüttel) were incorporated, increasing the number of boroughs to 31. Iron ore continued to be mined in Salzgitter until 1982; in the former <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mine</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Konrad_mine" title="Konrad mine">Schacht Konrad</a></i> (Konrad mine), an <a href="/wiki/Deep_geological_repository" title="Deep geological repository">ultimate disposal place</a> for <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a> has been planned since 1975.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religions">Religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The area of the contemporary city Salzgitter originally belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Hildesheim" title="Bishopric of Hildesheim">diocese of Hildesheim</a>. In 1568, the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> was established in Salzgitter. Two <a href="/wiki/Superintendent_(ecclesiastical)" title="Superintendent (ecclesiastical)">superintendencies</a> came into existence. The southern part of the area where the city is nowadays (Superintendency Salzgitter) belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hanover" title="Province of Hanover">Province of Hanover</a> and thus ecclesiastically to the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_State_Church_of_Hanover" class="mw-redirect" title="Evangelical Lutheran State Church of Hanover">Evangelical Lutheran State Church of Hanover</a> (Hildesheim <a href="/wiki/Consistory_(Protestantism)" title="Consistory (Protestantism)">Consistory</a>). The northern part (superintendency Lebenstedt), however, belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Brunswick" title="Free State of Brunswick">Free State of Brunswick</a> and therefore to the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_Brunswick" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church in Brunswick">Evangelical Lutheran State Church in Brunswick</a>.</p>
<p>When the city Watenstedt-Salzgitter originated (1942), the whole city area was attached to the state of Brunswick, both politically and <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastic">ecclesiastically</a>. Thus all parishes of Salzgitter belong to the Church of Brunswick nowadays. The two superintendencies are called <i>Propstei</i> (<a href="/wiki/Provost_(religion)" title="Provost (religion)">provostry</a>) today. However, the <i>Propsteien</i> Salzgitter-Bad and Salzgitter-Lebenstedt also comprise <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parishes</a> that do not belong to the city of Salzgitter.</p>
<p>The Catholics, who moved into the city again afterwards, belonged&#160;– like before the Reformation&#160;– to the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Hildesheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Hildesheim">diocese of Hildesheim</a>, which established a separate <a href="/wiki/Deanery" title="Deanery">deanery</a> in Salzgitter, to which all parishes of the city belong nowadays.</p>
<p>Beside the two big churches, there are parishes, too, that belong to <a href="/wiki/Free_church" title="Free church">free churches</a>, for example a <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> parish, the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_God_(Anderson,_Indiana)" title="Church of God (Anderson, Indiana)">Church of God</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Brethren" title="Plymouth Brethren">Plymouth Brethren</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist Church</a>. In addition, there are several <a href="/wiki/New_Apostolic_Church" title="New Apostolic Church">New Apostolic Churches</a> in Salzgitter. Due to the <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">immigration</a> of foreign workers during the 1970s, there are some <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a>. According to calculations based on census data Salzgitter had the highest share of muslim migrants of all major cities in Lower Saxony in 2011. <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the creation of Salzgitter a state commissar was set in place as provisional Mayer of the city of Watenstedt-Salzgitter. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the military government of the <a href="/wiki/British_Occupation_zone_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="British Occupation zone of Germany">British zone of occupation</a> installed the <a href="/wiki/Communal" class="mw-redirect" title="Communal">communal</a> <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> of <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">Britain</a>. Furthermore, there is an elected Council in place. The Council elects one of its members to <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">Mayor</a> (German: <i>Oberbürgermeister</i>) as leader and representative of the city. Besides, since 1946 on there was the <i>Oberstadtdirektor</i> as the Chief Executive of the City Council. Since 2001, the office of the leader of the Council and the Chief Executive are merged into one, simply called Mayor. Being elected by the people, he represents the city and leads the Council.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Coat of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_arms_of_Salzgitter.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Coat_of_arms_of_Salzgitter.svg/110px-Coat_of_arms_of_Salzgitter.svg.png" width="110" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Coat_of_arms_of_Salzgitter.svg/165px-Coat_of_arms_of_Salzgitter.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Coat_of_arms_of_Salzgitter.svg/220px-Coat_of_arms_of_Salzgitter.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="224" data-file-height="265" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_arms_of_Salzgitter.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Coat of arms of the city of Salzgitter</div>

<p>Salzgitter's <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Coat of Arms">Coat of Arms</a> consists of a silver <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a> visible behind a silver <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle" title="Pinnacle">pinnacle</a> wall on which there is a <a href="/wiki/Buckler" title="Buckler">buckler</a> whose upper ground is green and adorned with two saltern instruments and whose lower ground is gold and adorned with a black sledge and black iron. On the red ground behind the furnace, there are two <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheaten</a> ears.</p>
<p>The Coat of Arms stands for the <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, which is important for many villages of Salzgitter, on the one hand, and for the <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>, which led to Salzgitter's foundation, on the other hand.</p>
<p>This Coat of Arms is from 1951. Before, Watenstedt-Salzgitter had got a different one. Also the former town Salzgitter had got various coats of arms from 1854 on.</p>
<p>Like many German cities, Salzgitter has used the city's logo for some years. It is a green field with a white snaking way that narrows towards the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic">Traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Road">Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the north of Salzgitter, there is an <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> (A 39) from Braunschweig to the <a href="/wiki/Interchange_(road)" title="Interchange (road)">interchange</a> Salzgitter (where you can change to Autobahn 7 (Kassel-Hanover). Salzgitter has got five <a href="/wiki/Grade_separation" title="Grade separation">grade-separated</a> interchanges to this Autobahn. East from Salzgitter, there is the Autobahn 395 (Braunschweig-<a href="/wiki/Goslar" title="Goslar">Goslar</a>), which can be reached from Salzgitter by four interchanges.<br />
Moreover, two <i>Bundesstraßen</i> (German <a href="/wiki/Highways" class="mw-redirect" title="Highways">highways</a>) go through Salzgitter.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Railway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Salzgitter has six <a href="/wiki/Railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway station">railway stations</a>. The most important one is in the quarter <a href="/wiki/Salzgitter-Ringelheim" title="Salzgitter-Ringelheim">Salzgitter-Ringelheim</a>, the most central one in Salzgitter-Lebenstedt. There is no <i>Hauptbahnhof</i> (main railway station) in Salzgitter. Salzgitter-Ringelheim's station is located on the <a href="/wiki/Halle_(Saale)" title="Halle (Saale)">Halle (Saale)</a>-Goslar-Salzgitter-Hildesheim-Hanover line. Another line leads into the <a href="/wiki/Harz" title="Harz">Harz</a> Mountains and to Braunschweig, passing <a href="/wiki/Salzgitter-Bad" title="Salzgitter-Bad">Salzgitter-Bad</a>. Salzgitter-Lebenstedt is the end of a local line coming from Braunschweig and passing the other train stops of Salzgitter.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transport">Public transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Public transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are three bus companies in Salzgitter. The bus network is quite important considering Salzgitter consists of many spread-out villages.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_sights">Culture and sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Culture and sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libraries">Libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three public libraries in Salzgitter. The main-library is located in Salzgitter-Lebenstedt (155 000 media) with branch-libraries in Salzgitter-Bad (42 000 media) and Salzgitter-Fredenberg (25 000 media).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is no theatre in Salzgitter nor any building used as one. Yet there are several representations at various places. For example, in Salzgitter-Bad there is a society rooting in the students' theater of the local grammar-school that supports the amateur play. They act on various stages, with an auditory between 100 and 600 people. Furthermore, there are irregular performances of <a href="/wiki/Musical_theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Musical theater">musicals</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In the quarter Salzgitter-Lebenstedt:
<ul>
<li>City <a href="/wiki/Monument" title="Monument">monument</a> (<i>Turm der Arbeit</i> - "Tower of work", the city's <a href="/wiki/Emblem" title="Emblem">emblem</a>, constructed in 1995. The monument tells about the suffering of the forced workers and <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi concentration camp">Nazi concentration camp</a> prisoners while building up industry during the <a href="/wiki/National_socialism" class="mw-redirect" title="National socialism">national socialism</a>, about the flight from home beyond the rivers <a href="/wiki/Oder_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Oder River">Oder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lusatian_Neisse" title="Lusatian Neisse">Neisse</a>, about the fight against the removal of the <a href="/wiki/Iron_works" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron works">iron works</a> and about Salzgitter's people's will to live and to rebuild.)</li>
<li>Town hall (built 1959-1963)</li>
<li>Ice sports hall (in far-east style)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>In the quarter Salzgitter-Bad:
<ul>
<li>Old Town</li>
<li>Thermalsolbad ("hot-springs brine bath")</li>
<li>Protestant church St. Mariae Jacobi; military defence church built in 1481</li>
<li>Catholic church St. Marien</li>
<li>Former Nicolai church (nowadays event room)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Salzgitter_Bismarck_Tower" title="Salzgitter Bismarck Tower">Salzgitter Bismarck Tower</a></li>
<li>Former town hall at the market place</li>
<li>Tilly house</li>
<li>farm house in Kniestedt (now care for old people and music school)</li>
<li>"Beamtensiedlung" (from 1930, dwellings of the employees of the smelting works)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a> church in Salzgitter-Engerode, chapel built in 1236, one of Lower-Saxony's oldest pilgrimage churches with <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescos</a> laid open</li>
<li>Wasserburg (castle), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzgitter-Gebhardshagen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Salzgitter-Gebhardshagen (page does not exist)">Salzgitter-Gebhardshagen</a>, nearly 1000 years old</li>
<li>Franzosenbrücke ("French bridge", stone arch <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridge</a> over the river Innerste near Salzgitter-Hohenrode</li>
<li>Salzgitter-Lichtenberg: Castle ruins, once built by <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Lion" title="Henry the Lion">Henry the Lion</a>, destroyed in 1552 and laid open again in the 1950s. Look-out and restaurant.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Salzgitter-Ringelheim" title="Salzgitter-Ringelheim">Salzgitter-Ringelheim</a>: Ringelheim Castle, former <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a>, founded in the 10th century, <a href="/wiki/Secularisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secularisation">secularised</a> in 1803. <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> church built in 1694, including a precious <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organ</a>; <a href="/wiki/Crucifix" title="Crucifix">crucifix</a> from the workshop of <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">Bishop</a> Bernward of Hildesheim (around 1000); large castle park (<i>Schlosspark</i>)</li>
<li>Salzgitter-Salder: Salder Castle with Municipal Museum, former emblem of the city; the castle built in the style of the "<a href="/wiki/Weser_renaissance" class="mw-redirect" title="Weser renaissance">Weser renaissance</a>" around 1600 was domicile of the noble family von Salder in the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Braunschweig" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Braunschweig">Duchy of Braunschweig</a>, later domain of the <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">duke</a>; nowadays museum of local history; castle church Maria-Magdalena with a circular floor plan.</li>
<li>Salzgitter-Thiede: <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">Convent</a> Steterburg, ladies' convent founded in 1003; there are still buildings from the 11th century. The house of the <a href="/wiki/Abbess" title="Abbess">abbess</a> was built in 1691. The church is from 1752. In 1938, the area was reconstructed to tenements.</li>
</ul>

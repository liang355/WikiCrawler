<p><b>Plzeň</b> (<small>Czech pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Czech" title="Help:IPA/Czech">[ˈpl̩.zɛɲ]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Cs-Plzen.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/14/Cs-Plzen.ogg" class="internal" title="Cs-Plzen.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>), also called <b>Pilsen</b> in English and German, is a city in western <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> About 90 kilometres (56 miles) west of <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, it is the fourth most populous city in the Czech Republic.</p>
<p>The city is known worldwide for <a href="/wiki/Pilsner" title="Pilsner">Pilsner</a> beer, created by Bavarian brewer <a href="/wiki/Josef_Groll" title="Josef Groll">Josef Groll</a> there in 1842.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Synagogue_in_Pilsen-2010.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Great_Synagogue_in_Pilsen-2010.jpg/170px-Great_Synagogue_in_Pilsen-2010.jpg" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Great_Synagogue_in_Pilsen-2010.jpg/255px-Great_Synagogue_in_Pilsen-2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Great_Synagogue_in_Pilsen-2010.jpg/340px-Great_Synagogue_in_Pilsen-2010.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3888" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Synagogue_in_Pilsen-2010.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Great_Synagogue_(Plze%C5%88)" title="Great Synagogue (Plzeň)">Great Synagogue</a> in Pilsen</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Plzeň was first mentioned as a castle in 976, as the scene of a battle between Duke <a href="/wiki/Boleslaus_II_the_Pious" class="mw-redirect" title="Boleslaus II the Pious">Boleslaus II the Pious</a> of Bohemia and <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Otto_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto II, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto II</a>. It became a town in 1295 when King <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_II_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus II of Bohemia">Wenceslaus II</a> granted Plzeň its <a href="/wiki/Civic_charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Civic charter">civic charter</a> as a "Royal City" and established a new town site, some 10&#160;km (6&#160;mi) away from the original settlement, which is the current town of <a href="/wiki/Star%C3%BD_Plzenec" title="Starý Plzenec">Starý Plzenec</a>. It quickly became an important town on trade routes leading to <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a>; in the 14th century, it was the third-largest town in Bohemia after Prague and <a href="/wiki/Kutn%C3%A1_Hora" title="Kutná Hora">Kutná Hora</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Hussite_Wars" title="Hussite Wars">Hussite Wars</a>, it was the centre of Catholic resistance to the <a href="/wiki/Hussites" title="Hussites">Hussites</a>: <a href="/wiki/Prokop_the_Great" title="Prokop the Great">Prokop the Great</a> unsuccessfully <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Pilsen_(1433-1434)" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege of Pilsen (1433-1434)">besieged</a> it three times, and it joined the league of Catholic nobles against King <a href="/wiki/George_of_Podebrady" class="mw-redirect" title="George of Podebrady">George of Podebrady</a>. In 1468, the town acquired a <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>; the Trojan Chronicle (<i>Kronika trojánská</i> in Czech), the first book published in Bohemia, was printed on it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_century">17th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 17th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Emperor <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor">Rudolf II</a> made Plzeň his seat from 1599–1600. During the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> the town was taken by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ernst,_Count_of_Mansfeld" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Ernst, Count of Mansfeld">Mansfeld</a> in 1618 after the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Plze%C5%88" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege of Plzeň">Siege of Plzeň</a> and it was not recaptured by Imperial troops until 1621. <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_von_Wallenstein" title="Albrecht von Wallenstein">Wallenstein</a> made it his winter quarters in 1633. The town was unsuccessfully besieged by the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Swedes</a> in 1637 and 1648. The town and region have been staunchly Catholic despite the Hussite Wars.</p>
<p>From the end of the 17th century, the architecture of Plzeň has been influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque</a> style. The city centre has been under <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage_preservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural heritage preservation">cultural heritage preservation</a> since 1989.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the second half of the 19th century Plzeň, already an important trade centre for <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, near the Bavarian/German border, began to industrialise rapidly. In 1869 <a href="/wiki/Emil_%C5%A0koda" title="Emil Škoda">Emil Škoda</a> started up the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a>, which became the most important and influential engineering company in the country and a crucial supplier of arms to the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Army" title="Austro-Hungarian Army">Austro-Hungarian Army</a>. By 1917 the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a> employed over 30,000 workers. After 1898 the second largest employer was the National Railways train workshop, with about 2,000 employees: this was the largest rail repair shop in all <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>. Between 1861 and 1877, the Plzeň railway junction was completed and in 1899 the first tram line started in the city. This burst of industry had two important effects: the growth of the local <a href="/wiki/Czechs" title="Czechs">Czech</a> (<a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavic</a>) population and of the urban poor. After 1868 first Czech mayor of the city was elected.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak" class="mw-redirect" title="Czechoslovak">Czechoslovak</a> independence from <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> in 1918 the German-speaking minority in the countryside bordering the city of Plzeň hoped to be united with Austria and were unhappy at being included in Czechoslovakia. Many allied themselves to the <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> cause after 1933, in the hope that <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> might be able to unite them with their German-speaking neighbours.</p>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Munich_Agreement" title="Munich Agreement">Munich Agreement</a> in 1938, Plzeň became literally a frontier town, after the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Sudetenland" title="Sudetenland">Sudetenland</a> moved the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> borders to the city's outer limits. During the <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> occupation from 1939 to 1945 the <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda Works</a> in Pilsen was forced to provide armaments for the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a>, and Czech contributions, particularly in the field of tanks, were noted.</p>
<p>Between 17 and 26 January 1942, over 2,000 Jewish inhabitants, most of Plzeň's Jewish population, were deported by the Nazis to the <a href="/wiki/Theresienstadt_concentration_camp" title="Theresienstadt concentration camp">Theresienstadt concentration camp</a>.</p>
<p>The German-speaking population was forcibly expelled from the city and indeed <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Germans_from_Czechoslovakia" title="Expulsion of Germans from Czechoslovakia">all of Czechoslovakia</a> after the end of the war in 1945, according to the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsdam agreement">Potsdam agreement</a>. All of their property was confiscated.</p>
<p>On 6 May 1945, near the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Plzeň was liberated from <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> by the <a href="/wiki/16th_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="16th Armored Division (United States)">16th Armored Division</a> of General <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">Patton's</a> <a href="/wiki/U.S._Third_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Third Army">3rd Army</a>. Also participating in the liberation of the city were elements of the 97th and 2nd Infantry Divisions supported by Polish, controversial, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Cross_Mountains_Brigade" title="Holy Cross Mountains Brigade">Holy Cross Mountains Brigade</a>. Other Third Army units liberated major portions of Western Bohemia. The rest of <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> was liberated from German control by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>. Elements of the 3rd Army, as well as units from the 1st Army, remained in Plzeň until late November 1945, assisting the Czechs with rebuilding.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communist_era">Communist era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Communist era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_of_1948" class="mw-redirect" title="Czechoslovak coup d'état of 1948">Communist Party of Czechoslovakia coup of February 1948</a>, the Czechoslovak government launched a currency reform in 1953. This reform caused a wave of discontent, including the <a href="/wiki/Uprising_in_Pilsen_(1953)" class="mw-redirect" title="Uprising in Pilsen (1953)">Plzeň uprising</a>. On 1 June 1953 over 20,000 people, mainly workers at the Škoda Works, began protesting against the government. Protesters forced their way into the town hall and threw communist symbols, furniture and other objects out of the windows. The protest caused a retaliation from the government; as part of their retaliation, they destroyed the statue of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Garrigue_Masaryk" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Garrigue Masaryk">Thomas Garrigue Masaryk</a>. The statue has since been re-erected.</p>
<p>The next year, a West German homing pigeon was lost near the Czechoslovak border. It returned two days later, bearing a strong anti-communist message, signed "Unbowed Pilsen." The bird, named <a href="/wiki/Leaping_Lena" title="Leaping Lena">Leaping Lena</a>, was taken to the United States where it was celebrated as a Cold War hero.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pilsner_beer">Pilsner beer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pilsner beer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pilsner_Urquell_Brewery.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Pilsner_Urquell_Brewery.jpg/220px-Pilsner_Urquell_Brewery.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Pilsner_Urquell_Brewery.jpg/330px-Pilsner_Urquell_Brewery.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Pilsner_Urquell_Brewery.jpg/440px-Pilsner_Urquell_Brewery.jpg 2x" data-file-width="950" data-file-height="634" /></a>

Traditional fermenting building (center) and modern fermenting building (left)</div>

</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pilsner.Urquell.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Pilsner.Urquell.JPG/170px-Pilsner.Urquell.JPG" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Pilsner.Urquell.JPG/255px-Pilsner.Urquell.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Pilsner.Urquell.JPG/340px-Pilsner.Urquell.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3029" data-file-height="4041" /></a>

Pilsner Urquell</div>

<p>In 1375 Bohemian king <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV</a> endowed the Dobrow Monastery near Plzen with the beer right, and it became the oldest brewery to survive to modern times.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The officials of Plzeň founded a city-owned brewery in 1839, <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Bürger Brauerei</i></span> (Citizens' Brewery – now <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88sk%C3%BD_Prazdroj" class="mw-redirect" title="Plzeňský Prazdroj">Plzeňský Prazdroj</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and recruited Bavarian brewer <a href="/wiki/Josef_Groll" title="Josef Groll">Josef Groll</a> (1813–1887) who produced the first batch of modern <a href="/wiki/Pilsner" title="Pilsner">Pilsner</a> beer on 5 October 1842. The combination of pale colour from the new malts, Pilsen's remarkably soft water, <a href="/wiki/Saaz_hops" title="Saaz hops">Saaz</a> <a href="/wiki/Noble_hops" class="mw-redirect" title="Noble hops">noble hops</a> from nearby <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDatec" title="Žatec">Žatec</a> (<i>Saaz</i> in German) and Bavarian-style lagering produced a clear, golden beer which was regarded as a sensation. Improving transport meant that this new beer was soon available throughout Central Europe and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Pilsner Brauart</i></span>-style brewing was widely imitated. In 1859, "Pilsner Bier" was registered as a brand name at the Chamber of Commerce and Trade in Plzeň. In 1898, the <a href="/wiki/Pilsner_Urquell" title="Pilsner Urquell">Pilsner Urquell</a> trade mark was created to put emphasis on it being the original brewery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_economy">Education and economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Education and economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plzeň is a centre of academic, business, and cultural life for the western part of the Czech Republic. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_West_Bohemia" title="University of West Bohemia">University of West Bohemia</a> in Plzeň is well known for its Faculty of Law, Faculty of Mechanical Engineering and Faculty of Applied Science in particular.</p>
<p>Since the late 1990s the city has experienced high growth in foreign investment. In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> mall developer Plaza Centers opened the <a href="/wiki/Pilsen_Plaza" title="Pilsen Plaza">Pilsen Plaza</a>, a 20,000-square-metre (220,000-square-foot) shopping mall and entertainment centre featuring a multiplex cinema from <a href="/wiki/Cinema_City_Czech_Republic" title="Cinema City Czech Republic">Cinema City Czech Republic</a>.</p>
<p>Plzeň produces about two-thirds of the Plzeň Region GDP, even though it contains only 29.8% of its population.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Based on these figures, the city of Plzeň has a total GDP of approximately $7.2&#160;billion, and a per capita GDP of $44,000. While part of this is explained by commuters to the city, it is one of the most prosperous cities in the Czech Republic.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda</a> company, established in Plzeň in 1859, has been an important element of Austro-Hungarian, Czechoslovak and Czech engineering, and one of the biggest European arms factories. During the Communist era (1948–1989) the company's production had been directed to the needs of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern Bloc</a>. Disarray in the era after the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Revolution" title="Velvet Revolution">Velvet Revolution</a>, and unsuccessful efforts to gain new Western markets, resulted in sales problems and debts. After a huge restructuring process the company now has just two principal subsidiaries: Škoda Transportation (locomotives, underground trains and trams, which have been sold to <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>; <a href="/wiki/Tacoma,_Washington" title="Tacoma, Washington">Tacoma</a>; <a href="/wiki/Seattle,_Washington" class="mw-redirect" title="Seattle, Washington">Seattle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>) and Škoda Power (turbines) now owned by the UK company.</p>
<p>Many foreign companies now have manufacturing bases in Plzeň, including <a href="/wiki/Daikin" title="Daikin">Daikin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panasonic" title="Panasonic">Panasonic</a>. There has been much discussion of redeveloping those large areas of the Škoda plant which the company no longer uses.</p>
<p>Plzeň also has the biggest distillery (<a href="/wiki/Fernet_Stock" title="Fernet Stock">Stock</a>) in the Czech Republic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Local hockey club <a href="/wiki/HC_Plze%C5%88_1929" class="mw-redirect" title="HC Plzeň 1929">HC Škoda Plzeň</a> and the football club <a href="/wiki/FC_Viktoria_Plze%C5%88" title="FC Viktoria Plzeň">FC Viktoria Plzeň</a> are among the most successful in the Czech Republic. Viktoria Plzeň has played in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> and <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Europa_League" title="UEFA Europa League">UEFA Europa League</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dioceses">Dioceses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dioceses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 31 May 1993 Plzeň has been the seat of the Catholic Diocese of Plzeň. The first and incumbent Bishop is František Radkovský. The diocese covers almost the entire territory of Plzeň region with a total of 818,700 inhabitants. The Diocese office is in St. Bartholomew's Cathedral on Republic Square in Plzeň. The diocese is divided into 10 vicariates with a total of 72 parishes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plze%C5%88_12.3.2012.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Plze%C5%88_12.3.2012.jpg/220px-Plze%C5%88_12.3.2012.jpg" width="220" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Plze%C5%88_12.3.2012.jpg/330px-Plze%C5%88_12.3.2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Plze%C5%88_12.3.2012.jpg/440px-Plze%C5%88_12.3.2012.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1797" data-file-height="1907" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plze%C5%88_12.3.2012.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_St._Bartholomew_(Plze%C5%88)" title="Cathedral of St. Bartholomew (Plzeň)">St. Bartholomew's Cathedral</a></div>

<p>The most prominent sights of Plzeň are the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_St._Bartholomew_(Plze%C5%88)" title="Cathedral of St. Bartholomew (Plzeň)">St. Bartholomew's Cathedral</a>, founded in the late 13th century, whose tower, at 102 metres (335 feet), is the highest in the Czech Republic, the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> Town Hall, and the <a href="/wiki/Moorish_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Moorish Revival">Moorish Revival</a> <a href="/wiki/Great_Synagogue_(Plze%C5%88)" title="Great Synagogue (Plzeň)">Great Synagogue</a>, the second largest synagogue in Europe, after the <a href="/wiki/Doh%C3%A1ny_Street_Synagogue" title="Dohány Street Synagogue">Dohány Street Synagogue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>. There is also a 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi) historic underground tunnel/cellar network, among the longest in Central Europe. Part of this network is open to the public for tours of about 750 metres (2,500 feet) in length and down to a depth of 12&#160;m (39&#160;ft).</p>
<p>Built in 1532, the former water tower was integrated into the city's fortification system at Prague Gate. Another storey was added in 1822 in French Imperial style. The Gothic portal (dating from the 1500s) was added in 1912, coming from another house that had been demolished. Above the portal there is a commemorative plaque dedicated to Dr <a href="/wiki/Josef_Skoda" class="mw-redirect" title="Josef Skoda">Josef Skoda</a> (a professor at the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Vienna University">Vienna University</a>), who was born next door on 10 December 1805.</p>
<p>Plzeň is also well known for the <a href="/wiki/Pilsner_Urquell" title="Pilsner Urquell">Pilsner Urquell</a> (since 1842) and <a href="/wiki/Gambrinus_(beer)" title="Gambrinus (beer)">Gambrinus</a> (since 1869) <a href="/wiki/Brewery" title="Brewery">breweries</a>, currently owned by Asahi Group Holdings. A popular tourist attraction is the Plzeňský Prazdroj brewery tour where visitors can discover the history of beer. The <a href="/wiki/Pilsener" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilsener">pilsener</a> style of beer was developed in Plzeň in the 19th century.</p>
<p>Plzeň was a <a href="/wiki/European_Capital_Of_Culture" class="mw-redirect" title="European Capital Of Culture">European Capital Of Culture</a> in 2015, along with <a href="/wiki/Mons,_Belgium" class="mw-redirect" title="Mons, Belgium">Mons, Belgium</a>.</p>

<p>The Plzeň metropolitan area is largely served by a network of <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">trams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trolleybus" title="Trolleybus">trolleybuses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a> operated by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.pmdp.cz/">PMDP</a>. Like other continental European cities, tickets bought from vending machines or small shops are valid for any transport run by the city of Plzeň. For residents of the city, a Plzeň Card can be purchased and through a system of "topping up" be used on any public transport with no limitations, as long as it is paid up and valid. Tickets can be purchased in vehicles with a contactless smart card.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most important transport link in the city is the <a href="/wiki/D5_motorway_(Czech_Republic)" title="D5 motorway (Czech Republic)">D5 highway</a> connecting <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. Plzeň is an important centre of Czech railway transport, with the crossing of five main railway lines:</p>
<ul>
<li>line Nr. 170: <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> – <a href="/wiki/Beroun" title="Beroun">Beroun</a> – <b>Plzeň</b> – <a href="/wiki/Cheb" title="Cheb">Cheb</a></li>
<li>line Nr. 180: <b>Plzeň</b> – <a href="/wiki/Doma%C5%BElice" title="Domažlice">Domažlice</a> – <a href="/wiki/Furth_im_Wald" title="Furth im Wald">Furth im Wald</a> (Germany)</li>
<li>line Nr. 183: <b>Plzeň</b> – <a href="/wiki/Klatovy" title="Klatovy">Klatovy</a> – <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDelezn%C3%A1_Ruda" title="Železná Ruda">Železná Ruda</a></li>
<li>line Nr. 160: <b>Plzeň</b> – <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDatec" title="Žatec">Žatec</a></li>
<li>line Nr. 190: <b>Plzeň</b> – <a href="/wiki/%C4%8Cesk%C3%A9_Bud%C4%9Bjovice" title="České Budějovice">České Budějovice</a></li>
</ul>
<p>A public domestic and private international <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a> is located 11&#160;km south-west from Plzeň, at the nearby village of <a href="/wiki/L%C3%ADn%C4%9B" title="Líně">Líně</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plze%C5%88&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plzeň has a cool and <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">Oceanic climate</a> (Cfb). Plzeň has low rainfall (604&#160;mm (24&#160;in) year average) evenly spread over the year. Precipitation occurs on average every second day, and the number of days with <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> is 19. It receives on average 1700 hours of sunshine though winters have longer periods without sunshine. Terrain features and a relatively low altitude (290 – 390 metres) give some shelter from strong winds. Winters are chilly but milder than some adjacent areas. Snow cover is erratic and lasts on average for 51 days. Though an average year has 113 days with minimum temperature below zero, the temperature falls below −15&#160;°C (5&#160;°F) on 5 days. The record low temperatures is around −20&#160;°C (−4&#160;°F). Winters are often murky with frequent long-standing haze. Spring is short, and in April to June there is blooming vegetation. Summer lasts from the end of May until the first third of September. During that period Plzeň has <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">changeable weather</a> which can be warm to hot. Temperatures are always above 5 degrees Celsius with nights between 8 to 18&#160;°C (46 to 64&#160;°F) and days between 16 to 35&#160;°C (61 to 95&#160;°F). Days are up to 16 hours long. Plzeň can be hot, especially during heat waves originating in the southern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. The number of hot days above 30&#160;°C (86&#160;°F) is steadily growing, with 5 months (late April – early September) of possible 30+ °C days. If hot weather does occur, it is often changes after a few weeks into cold and rainy weather with incoming Atlantic-based fronts. Nights can be unpleasantly cold even in summer, with high level of humidity. Winter frosts frequently occur from the second half of November to the end of March. February is the driest month with 32&#160;mm (1.3&#160;in) of precipitation, and July the wettest with 80&#160;mm (3.1&#160;in). The only natural hazards are occasional fast changes of weather with negative consequences, e.g. floodings.</p>
<p>Extreme values for years 2011 &amp; 2012: An extremely cold day of 2011 had −7.6&#160;°C (18.3&#160;°F) on average (23 February), −0.7 to −13.3&#160;°C (30.7 to 8.1&#160;°F) and extremely hot day 25.2&#160;°C (77.4&#160;°F) on average (24 August, 19.5 to 32.8&#160;°C (67.1 to 91.0&#160;°F). The year 2012 had the coldest day on 12 February with minimum plummeting to −22.1&#160;°C (−7.8&#160;°F) and maximum around −3.7&#160;°C (25.3&#160;°F) with average −14.9&#160;°C (5.2&#160;°F). The hottest day of 2012 occurred on 21 August with daily maximum temperature 33.9&#160;°C (93.0&#160;°F) and minimum staying on 19.4&#160;°C (66.9&#160;°F) with all day average on 25.5&#160;°C (77.9&#160;°F). Absolute minimum and maximum for both years were −22.1&#160;°C (−7.8&#160;°F) (February 2012) and 37.4&#160;°C (99.3&#160;°F) during August 2012.</p>
<p>Number of rainy/snowy days for 2011: 78; number of days with frost: 76; number of days with minimal temperatures below −10&#160;°C (14&#160;°F): 12; number of days with average temperature below zero: 35; number of days with daily average temperature higher than 10&#160;°C (50&#160;°F): 188; number of days with daily average higher than 20&#160;°C (68&#160;°F): 32. Total amount of precipitation for year 2011: 529.1&#160;mm; average year humidity value: 80.8%. Maximal temperature: 33.3&#160;°C (91.9&#160;°F); minimal temperature −14.8&#160;°C (5.4&#160;°F). Average 2011 temperature: 9.7&#160;°C (49&#160;°F); average speed of wind: 4.7&#160;km/h (2.9&#160;mph), mainly from SSE.</p>
<p>Number of days with frost was 96 during year 2012; 18 days had minima below −10&#160;°C (14&#160;°F) and 165 days with an average temperature on or above 10.0&#160;°C (50.0&#160;°F). Number of days with maxima on or above 20.0&#160;°C (68.0&#160;°F) was 42.</p>

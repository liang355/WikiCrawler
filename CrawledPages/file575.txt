<p><b>Harvard Stadium</b> is a U-shaped <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> stadium in the <a href="/wiki/Allston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Allston, Massachusetts">Allston</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, in the United States. Built in 1903, it was a pioneering execution of <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a> in the construction of large structures. Because of its early importance in these areas, and its influence on the design of later stadiums, it was designated a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-NRHP_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRHP-5">[5]</a></sup> The Stadium is owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and is home to the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson_football" title="Harvard Crimson football">Harvard Crimson football</a> program. It was also home for the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Patriots" title="New England Patriots">New England Patriots</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1970_Boston_Patriots_season" title="1970 Boston Patriots season">1970 season</a>, their first after the <a href="/wiki/AFL%E2%80%93NFL_merger" title="AFL–NFL merger">AFL–NFL merger</a>, before the completion of <a href="/wiki/Foxboro_Stadium" title="Foxboro Stadium">Foxboro Stadium</a>.</p>
<p>The stadium seats 30,323.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The stadium seated up to 57,166 in the past, as permanent steel stands (completing a straight-sided oval) were installed in the north end of the stadium in 1929. They were torn down after the 1951 season due to deterioration and reduced attendance. Afterwards, there were smaller temporary steel bleachers across the open end of the stadium until the building of the Murr Center (which is topped by the new scoreboard) in 1998.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harvard's stadium was constructed on 31 acres of land known as Soldiers Field, donated to <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lee_Higginson" title="Henry Lee Higginson">Henry Lee Higginson</a> in 1890 as a memorial to Harvard men who had died in the American Civil War.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The structure, similar in shape to the <a href="/wiki/Panathenaic_Stadium" title="Panathenaic Stadium">Panathenaic Stadium</a>, was completed in just 4½ months costing $310,000. Much of the funds raised came from a 25th Reunion gift by Harvard's Class of 1879. It is the home of the <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> team of Harvard. The stadium also hosted the Crimson <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a> teams until 1984 and was the home of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Patriots" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Patriots">Boston Patriots</a> during the 1970 season, until <a href="/wiki/Schaefer_Stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Schaefer Stadium">Schaefer Stadium</a> opened the following year.</p>
<p>Lewis Jerome Johnson, Prof. Civil Engineering, Harvard University, was a consultant to the design team for the Harvard Stadium. It is historically significant that this stadium represents the first vertical concrete structure to employ reinforced structural concrete. Prior to the erection of the stadium in 1902, reinforced structural concrete was used in horizontal, that is flooring, sidewalks, etc., design only. Prof. Johnson was the engineer of note responsible for incorporating the concept into the vertical structure of the stadium design. There is a plaque dedicating the stadium to his honor on the east end wall outside the stadium.</p>
<p>In 2006, Harvard installed both <a href="/wiki/FieldTurf" title="FieldTurf">FieldTurf</a> and lights.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_Stadium_Information_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_Stadium_Information-8">[8]</a></sup> On September 22, 2007, Harvard played its first night game at the stadium, against <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a>, winning <a href="/wiki/2007_Harvard_Crimson_football_team" title="2007 Harvard Crimson football team">24–17</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_American_Football">Impact on American Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact on American Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 20th century, <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> was an extremely violent sport. 18 players died and 159 were seriously injured in 1905 alone.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> There was a widespread movement to outlaw the game entirely but U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> intervened and demanded that the rules of the game be reformed. In 1906, Roosevelt met with representatives from 62 colleges and universities and formed the Intercollegiate Football Conference, the predecessor of the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The purpose of the committee was to develop a uniform set of rules and regulations to make the game safer. A leading proposal, at the time, was widening the field to allow more running room and decrease the chances of serious collisions. While it was very popular among committee members, Harvard objected. Their recently completed stadium could not accommodate a larger field. Because of the permanent nature of Harvard Stadium, the proposal was rejected and the <a href="/wiki/Forward_pass" title="Forward pass">forward pass</a> was legalized in April 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Harvard Stadium directly led to the creation of two of the most fundamental aspects of modern American football: standard field dimensions and the legal forward pass.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Events">Other Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is also the host of <a href="/wiki/Music_festival" title="Music festival">music festivals</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Amandla_Festival" title="Amandla Festival">Amandla Festival</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaican</a> reggae legend <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a> performed a historic concert in 1979. <a href="/wiki/Janis_Joplin" title="Janis Joplin">Janis Joplin</a> performed her last show at the stadium in 1970, shortly before her death. During the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a> held in Los Angeles, the stadium hosted several <a href="/wiki/Football_at_the_1984_Summer_Olympics" title="Football at the 1984 Summer Olympics">soccer</a> preliminaries.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Cannons" title="Boston Cannons">Boston Cannons</a>, a professional lacrosse team for <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Lacrosse" title="Major League Lacrosse">Major League Lacrosse</a>, moved their home site to the stadium. They previously played at <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Nickerson_Field" title="Nickerson Field">Nickerson Field</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Harvard installed both <a href="/wiki/FieldTurf" title="FieldTurf">FieldTurf</a> and lights<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_Stadium_Information_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_Stadium_Information-8">[8]</a></sup> allowing it to become the home stadium of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Cannons" title="Boston Cannons">Boston Cannons</a>. On September 22, 2007, Harvard played its first night game at the stadium, against <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a>, winning 24–17.</p>
<p>Beginning on April 11, 2009, Harvard Stadium became the home field of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Breakers_(WPS)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Breakers (WPS)">Boston Breakers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Professional_Soccer" title="Women's Professional Soccer">Women's Professional Soccer</a> (WPS) league when they beat <a href="/wiki/Saint_Louis_Athletica" title="Saint Louis Athletica">Saint Louis Athletica</a> 2–0.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Stadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harvard_stadium_2009h.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Harvard_stadium_2009h.JPG/220px-Harvard_stadium_2009h.JPG" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Harvard_stadium_2009h.JPG/330px-Harvard_stadium_2009h.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Harvard_stadium_2009h.JPG/440px-Harvard_stadium_2009h.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4752" data-file-height="2946" /></a>

The stadium in 2009</div>

<p>Although most of Harvard's campus is in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, the stadium and most other intercollegiate athletic facilities, along with <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>, lie to the South, across the Charles River, in the nearby Allston neighborhood of Boston. The stadium is the most iconic piece of the Soldiers Field athletic complex, which also includes the baseball stadium, outdoor track, an artificial turf field hockey/lacrosse field, two soccer stadiums, pools, Beren Tennis Center (outdoor), the Gordon Indoor Track, Dillon Fieldhouse, <a href="/wiki/Lavietes_Pavilion" title="Lavietes Pavilion">Lavietes Pavilion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bright_Hockey_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Bright Hockey Center">Bright Hockey Center</a>. Newell Boathouse, home of Harvard's men's crew, lies across Soldiers Field Road on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles</a>.</p>

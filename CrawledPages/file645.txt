			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Estabrook Woods</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Estabrook Woods</b> is a wild tract of more than 1,200 acres (4.9&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of woodland, hills, ledge, and swamp two miles (3&#160;km) north of the <a href="/wiki/Concord,_Massachusetts" title="Concord, Massachusetts">Town of Concord</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is the largest contiguous and undeveloped woodland within thirty miles of Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, the woods have a history of human disturbance dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquian</a> Native Americans who used controlled burning to clear tracts of land.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup> Later, colonists cleared much of Estabrook for agriculture and pastures, although vegetation has since rejuvenated.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-3">[3]</a></sup> The Woods are named for the Estabrook family, prominent in the area since colonial times.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The first Estabrook in the area, Capt. Joseph, purchased his farm, now part of Estabrook Woods, from the Pelham family, then of Rhode Island.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a> is intimately associated with this area, which he called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Easterbrooks_Country&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Easterbrooks Country (page does not exist)">Easterbrooks Country</a>. In his Oct. 20, 1857 journal entry, one of several on the woodland, he writes: “What a wild and rich domain that Easterbrooks Country! Not a cultivated, hardly a cultivatable field in it, and yet it delights all natural persons.”<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The woods are also home to the unimproved Estabrook Road, which <a href="/wiki/Minutemen" title="Minutemen">Minutemen</a> used at the start of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Today, stone markers mark the path taken by Minuteman traveling south toward Concord.</p>
<p>The Estabrook Woods are bordered by Lowell Road to the west, Monument Street to the east, and Bedford Road (Rt. 225) to the north.</p>
<p>Though accessible to the public, most of Estabrook is privately owned by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> (672 acres), <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_School" title="Middlesex School">Middlesex School</a> (180 acres), and a number of smaller landowners.</p>

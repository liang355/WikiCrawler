<p><b>Kassel</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈkasl̩]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Kassel.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c1/De-Kassel.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Kassel.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; spelled <b>Cassel</b> until 1928) is a city located on the <a href="/wiki/Fulda_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulda River">Fulda River</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>, Germany. It is the administrative seat of the <a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">Regierungsbezirk</a> <a href="/wiki/Kassel_(region)" title="Kassel (region)">Kassel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Germany" title="Districts of Germany">Kreis</a> <a href="/wiki/Kassel_(district)" title="Kassel (district)">of the same name</a> and had 200,507 inhabitants in December 2015. The former capital of the state of Hesse-Kassel has many palaces and parks, including the <a href="/wiki/Bergpark_Wilhelmsh%C3%B6he" title="Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe">Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. Kassel is also known for the <i><a href="/wiki/Documenta" title="Documenta">documenta</a></i> exhibitions of contemporary art.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Kassel was first mentioned in 913 AD, as the place where two <a href="/wiki/Deed" title="Deed">deeds</a> were signed by King <a href="/wiki/Conrad_of_Franconia" class="mw-redirect" title="Conrad of Franconia">Conrad I</a>. The place was called <i>Chasella</i> or <i>Chassalla</i> and was a fortification at a bridge crossing the <a href="/wiki/Fulda_(river)" title="Fulda (river)">Fulda river</a>. There are several - yet unproven - assumptions of the name's origin. It could be derived from the ancient <i>Castellum Cattorum</i>, a castle of the <a href="/wiki/Chatti" title="Chatti">Chatti</a>, a German tribe that had lived in the area since Roman times. Another assumption is a portmanteau from Frankonian "cas" - valley or recess and "sali" - hall or service building, which can be interpreted as (town)hall in a valley.</p>
<p>A deed from 1189 certifies that Cassel had city rights, but the date when they were granted is not known.</p>
<p>In 1567, the <a href="/wiki/Landgraviate_of_Hesse" title="Landgraviate of Hesse">Landgraviate of Hesse</a>, until then centered in <a href="/wiki/Marburg" title="Marburg">Marburg</a>, was divided among four sons, with <a href="/wiki/Landgraviate_of_Hesse-Kassel" title="Landgraviate of Hesse-Kassel">Hesse-Kassel</a> (or Hesse-Cassel) becoming one of its successor states. Kassel was its capital and became a centre of <a href="/wiki/Calvinist" class="mw-redirect" title="Calvinist">Calvinist</a> <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> in Germany. Strong fortifications were built to protect the Protestant stronghold against Catholic enemies. Secret societies, such as <a href="/wiki/Rosicrucianism" title="Rosicrucianism">Rosicrucianism</a> flourished, with Christian Rosenkreutz’s work <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fama_Fraternitis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fama Fraternitis (page does not exist)">Fama Fraternitis</a> first published in 1617. In 1685, Kassel became a refuge for 1,700 <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" class="mw-redirect" title="Huguenot">Huguenots</a> who found shelter in the newly established borough of Oberneustadt. <a href="/wiki/Charles_I,_Landgrave_of_Hesse-Kassel" title="Charles I, Landgrave of Hesse-Kassel">Landgrave Charles</a>, who was responsible for this humanitarian act, also ordered the construction of the <i>Oktagon</i> and of the <i>Orangerie</i>. In the late 18th Century, Hesse-Kassel became infamous for selling mercenaries (<a href="/wiki/Hessian_(soldiers)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hessian (soldiers)">Hessians</a>) to the British crown to help suppress the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> and to finance the construction of palaces and the Landgrave’s opulent lifestyle.</p>
<p>In the early 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a> lived in Kassel. They collected and wrote most of their <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tales" class="mw-redirect" title="Fairy tales">fairy tales</a> there. At that time, around 1803, the Landgraviate was elevated to a Principality and its ruler to <i><a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-elector</a></i>. Shortly after, it was annexed by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a> and in 1807 it became the capital of the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">Kingdom of Westphalia</a> under Napoleon's brother <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bonaparte" title="Jérôme Bonaparte">Jérôme</a>. The Electorate was restored in 1813.</p>
<p>Having sided with Austria in the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> to gain supremacy in Germany, the principality was annexed by <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> in 1866. The Prussian administration united <a href="/wiki/Nassau_(duchy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nassau (duchy)">Nassau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> and Hesse-Kassel into the new Prussian province of <a href="/wiki/Hesse-Nassau" title="Hesse-Nassau">Hesse-Nassau</a>. Kassel ceased to be a princely residence, but soon developed into a major industrial centre, as well as a major railway junction. <a href="/wiki/Henschel_%26_Son" title="Henschel &amp; Son">Henschel &amp; Son</a>, the largest railway <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">locomotive</a> manufacturer in Germany at the end of the nineteenth century, was based in Kassel.</p>
<p>In 1870, after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sedan" title="Battle of Sedan">Battle of Sedan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a> was sent as a prisoner to the <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Wilhelmsh%C3%B6he" title="Schloss Wilhelmshöhe">castle of Wilhelmshohe</a> above the city. During World War I the German military headquarters were located in the castle of Wilhelmshohe. In the late 1930s Nazis destroyed <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_H%C3%BCbsch" title="Heinrich Hübsch">Heinrich Hübsch</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kassel_Synagogue" title="Kassel Synagogue">Kassel Synagogue</a>.</p>
<p>During World War II, Kassel was the Headquarters for Germany's <a href="/wiki/Wehrkreis" class="mw-redirect" title="Wehrkreis">Wehrkreis</a> IX, and a local subcamp of <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">Dachau concentration camp</a> provided <a href="/wiki/Forced_labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced labour">forced labour</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Kassel_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Kassel in World War II">Henschel facilities</a>, which included tank production plants.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The most severe <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Kassel_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Kassel in World War II">bombing of Kassel in World War II</a> destroyed 90% of the downtown area, some 10,000 people were killed, and 150,000 were made homeless.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Most of the casualties were civilians or wounded soldiers recuperating in local hospitals, whereas <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Kassel_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Kassel in World War II">factories</a> survived the attack generally undamaged.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Karl_Gerland" title="Karl Gerland">Karl Gerland</a> replaced the regional <a href="/wiki/Gauleiter" title="Gauleiter">Gauleiter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Weinrich" title="Karl Weinrich">Karl Weinrich</a>, soon after the raid.</p>
<p>The Allied ground advance into Germany reached Kassel at the beginning of April 1945. The US <a href="/wiki/80th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="80th Infantry Division (United States)">80th Infantry Division</a> captured Kassel in bitter house-to-house fighting during 2–4 April 1945, which included numerous German panzer-grenadier counterattacks, and resulted in further widespread devastation to bombed and unbombed structures alike.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">Post-war</a>, most of the ancient buildings were not restored, and large parts of the city area were completely rebuilt in the style of the 1950s. A few historic buildings, however, such as the <a href="/wiki/Fridericianum" title="Fridericianum">Museum Fridericianum</a> (see below), were restored. In 1949, the interim parliament ("<a href="/wiki/Parlamentarischer_Rat" title="Parlamentarischer Rat">Parlamentarischer Rat</a>") eliminated Kassel in the first round as a city to become the provisional capital of the <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a> won). In 1964, the town hosted the fourth <i><a href="/wiki/Hessentag" title="Hessentag">Hessentag</a></i> state festival (again in 2013). In 1972 the Chancellor of West Germany <a href="/wiki/Willy_Brandt" title="Willy Brandt">Willy Brandt</a> and the Prime Minister of the German Democratic Republic <a href="/wiki/Willy_Stoph" class="mw-redirect" title="Willy Stoph">Willy Stoph</a> met in <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Wilhelmsh%C3%B6he" title="Schloss Wilhelmshöhe">Wilhelmshohe</a> castle for negotiations between the two German states. In 1991 the central rail station moved from "Hauptbahnhof" (English: main station) (today only used for regional trains) to "Kassel-Wilhelmshöhe".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Documenta_IX_Thomas_Sch%C3%BCtte_links.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Documenta_IX_Thomas_Sch%C3%BCtte_links.jpg/220px-Documenta_IX_Thomas_Sch%C3%BCtte_links.jpg" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Documenta_IX_Thomas_Sch%C3%BCtte_links.jpg/330px-Documenta_IX_Thomas_Sch%C3%BCtte_links.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Documenta_IX_Thomas_Sch%C3%BCtte_links.jpg/440px-Documenta_IX_Thomas_Sch%C3%BCtte_links.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3120" data-file-height="2192" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Documenta_IX_Thomas_Sch%C3%BCtte_links.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Installation by Thomas Schütte during Documenta IX, 1992</div>

<p>In 1558, the first German <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatory</a> was built in Kassel, followed in 1604 by the <i>Ottoneum</i>, the first permanent German theatre building. The old building is today the Natural History Museum, and the now-called <a href="/wiki/Staatstheater_Kassel" title="Staatstheater Kassel">Staatstheater Kassel</a> is located in a nearby building that was constructed in the 1950s. Since 1927, Kassel has been home to <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A4renreiter" title="Bärenreiter">Bärenreiter</a>, one of the world's most important music publishers.</p>
<p>Since 1955 the <a href="/wiki/Documenta" title="Documenta">Documenta</a>, an international <a href="/wiki/Art_exhibition" title="Art exhibition">exhibition</a> of <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_art" title="Contemporary art">contemporary art</a>, has been held regularly in Kassel. The Documenta now takes place every 5 years. The most recent exhibition, <a href="/wiki/Documenta_14" title="Documenta 14">documenta 14</a> is being held from June to September, 2017. As a result of the Documenta 6 (1977), Kassel became the first town in the world to be illuminated by <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> beams at night (Laserscape, by artist Horst H. Baumann). This laser installation is nowadays still visible at weekends. Other Documenta remnants (mainly sculptures) can be found in many places in Kassel; among those the "<a href="/wiki/7000_Oaks" title="7000 Oaks">7000 Oaks</a>", a work of <a href="/wiki/Land_art" title="Land art">land art</a> by the German artist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Beuys" title="Joseph Beuys">Joseph Beuys</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">Sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Kassel_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Kassel in World War II">bombing raids of 1943</a> destroyed 90% of the city centre. The city was almost completely rebuilt during the 1950s and is a combination of renovated or reconstructed old buildings and architecture of the 1950s. Outside the city centre, the suburbs are dominated by 19th-century architecture. The oldest monument is the Druselturm; the Brüderkirche and the <a href="/wiki/Martinskirche,_Kassel" title="Martinskirche, Kassel">Martinskirche</a> are also, in part, of medieval origin. The towers of the Martinskirche are from the 1950s.</p>
<h3><span id="Bergpark_Wilhelmshöhe"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bergpark_Wilhelmsh.C3.B6he">Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bergpark_Wilhelmsh%C3%B6he" title="Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe">Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe</a></div>

<p>The complex includes Wilhelmshöhe Palace (with the Antiquities Collection and Old Masters), the Hercules monument, and the Lions Castle. <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Wilhelmsh%C3%B6he" title="Schloss Wilhelmshöhe">Wilhelmshöhe Palace</a> above the city, was built in 1786 by landgrave Wilhelm IX of Hesse-Kassel. The palace is now a museum and houses an important collection of Graeco-Roman antiques and a fine gallery of paintings comprising the second largest collection of <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandts</a> in Germany. It is surrounded by the beautiful <a href="/wiki/Bergpark_Wilhelmsh%C3%B6he" title="Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe">Bergpark Wilhelmshöhe</a> with many appealing sights. The complex was named a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hercules_monument_(Kassel)" title="Hercules monument (Kassel)">Hercules monument</a> is a huge octagonal stone structure carrying a giant replica of <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">Hercules "Farnese"</a> (now at Museo Archeologico Nazionale in Naples, Italy). From its base down to Wilhelmshöhe Palace runs a long set of artificial cascades which delight visitors during the summer months. Every Sunday and Wednesday afternoon at 14:30 (from May until October) the famous <a href="/wiki/Hercules_monument_(Kassel)" title="Hercules monument (Kassel)">water features</a> take place. They start at the Oktagon and during a one-hour walk through the park visitors can follow the water's way until they reach the lake of the castle Wilhelmshöhe, where a fountain of about 50 metres (160&#160;ft) marks the end of the spectacle.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%B6wenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Löwenburg (page does not exist)">Löwenburg</a></i> ("Lions Castle") is a replica of a medieval castle, also built during the reign of Wilhelm IX. After the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> of 1870/71 <a href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoléon III">Napoléon III</a> was imprisoned in Wilhelmshöhe. In 1918 Wilhelmshöhe became the seat of the German Army High Command (OHL): it was there that the military commanders <a href="/wiki/Paul_von_Hindenburg" title="Paul von Hindenburg">Hindenburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erich_Ludendorff" title="Erich Ludendorff">Ludendorff</a> prepared the German capitulation.</p>
<h3><span id="Staatspark_Karlsaue_(Karlsaue_Park)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Staatspark_Karlsaue_.28Karlsaue_Park.29">Staatspark Karlsaue (Karlsaue Park)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Staatspark Karlsaue (Karlsaue Park)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another large park and also part of the <a href="/wiki/European_Garden_Heritage_Network" title="European Garden Heritage Network">European Garden Heritage Network</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Karlsaue" title="Karlsaue">Karlsaue</a> along the Fulda River. Established in the 16th century, it is famous for the <a href="/wiki/Orangerie_(Kassel)" title="Orangerie (Kassel)">Orangerie</a>, a palace built in 1710 as a summer residence for the landgraves. Today, the Orangerie contains the Museum of Astronomy and Technology, with a scale model of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> spanning the entire park and beyond. In addition, the Park Schönfeld contains a small, municipal <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_Kassel" title="Botanischer Garten Kassel">Botanischer Garten Kassel</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_museums">Art museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Art museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Europe's first public museum, the <i><a href="/wiki/Fridericianum" title="Fridericianum">Museum Fridericianum</a></i> was founded in 1779. By the end of the 19th century the museum held one of the largest collections in the world of watches and clocks. Other art museums in Kassel include:</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/KSV_Hessen_Kassel" title="KSV Hessen Kassel">Hessen Kassel</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> club in the city, who plays in the <a href="/wiki/Regionalliga_S%C3%BCdwest" title="Regionalliga Südwest">Regionalliga Südwest</a> in Germany. The city's own football stadium, the <a href="/wiki/Auestadion" title="Auestadion">Auestadion</a> was built in 1953 and is able to hold 18,737 people. It is located in the south of Kassel at the quarter Südstadt, next to the Karlsaue.</p>
<p>Kassel has a long ice hockey tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-DW1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DW1-10">[10]</a></sup> The team, the <a href="/wiki/Kassel_Huskies" title="Kassel Huskies">Kassel Huskies</a>, was active from 1977 to 2010. They were founding members of the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Eishockey_Liga" title="Deutsche Eishockey Liga">DEL</a> in 1994, belonging to the league from 1994-2006 and again from 2008-2010. In <a href="/wiki/1996%E2%80%9397_DEL_season" title="1996–97 DEL season">1997</a>, they were runners-up in the championship play-offs, losing to <a href="/wiki/Adler_Mannheim" title="Adler Mannheim">Adler Mannheim</a> and reached the semi-finals on three more occasions. The Huskies ran into financial difficulties and dissolved in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-DW1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DW1-10">[10]</a></sup> The "Young Huskies", which is a junior and youth hockey club, decided to enter a men's team in the Hessenliga.<sup id="cite_ref-DW1_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DW1-10">[10]</a></sup> This is the fifth division and the lowest men's competition in the state of <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DW1_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DW1-10">[10]</a></sup> The new club was expecting no more than 3,000 supporters for the first home game in the Hessenliga.<sup id="cite_ref-DW1_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DW1-10">[10]</a></sup> However, they had over 5,000 supporters come to watch.<sup id="cite_ref-DW1_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DW1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Kassel has <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Kassel" title="Trams in Kassel">seven tram lines</a> (1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8), with trams arriving usually every 15 minutes. The city also operates a <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> <a href="/wiki/Stadtbahn" title="Stadtbahn">Stadtbahn</a> network called <i>RegioTram</i> using <a href="/wiki/Alstom_Citadis" title="Alstom Citadis">Regio Citadis</a> low-floor trams which run on both tram and main line railway tracks with four lines (RT3, RT4, RT5, RT9). Moreover, a number of low-floor <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a> complete the Kassel public transport system. The introduction of low-floor buses led to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Kassel_kerb" title="Kassel kerb">Kassel kerb</a> which improves the <a href="/wiki/Accessibility" title="Accessibility">accessibility</a> at <a href="/wiki/Bus_stop" title="Bus stop">bus stops</a>.</p>
<p>The city is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">national rail network</a> at two stations, <a href="/wiki/Kassel_Hauptbahnhof" title="Kassel Hauptbahnhof">Kassel Central</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kassel-Wilhelmsh%C3%B6he_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Kassel-Wilhelmshöhe railway station">Kassel-Wilhelmshöhe</a>. The traditional central station (Hauptbahnhof) has been reduced to the status of a regional station since the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Hanover-W%C3%BCrzburg_high-speed_rail_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanover-Würzburg high-speed rail line">Hanover-Würzburg high-speed rail line</a> in 1991 and its station (Kassel-Wilhelmshöhe) on the high-speed line at which the <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a> (ICE) and <a href="/wiki/InterCity" title="InterCity">InterCity</a> services call.</p>
<p>Kassel is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">motorways</a> <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_7" title="Bundesautobahn 7">A 7</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_49" title="Bundesautobahn 49">A 49</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_44" title="Bundesautobahn 44">A 44</a>.</p>
<p>The city is served by <a href="/wiki/Kassel_Calden_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Kassel Calden Airport">Kassel Calden Airport</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_Kassel">University of Kassel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kassel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: University of Kassel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kassel" title="University of Kassel">University of Kassel</a> was founded in 1971, and is the newest university in the state of <a href="/wiki/Hessen" class="mw-redirect" title="Hessen">Hessen</a>. The University offers twelve international master's programs as well as two short-term international programs, the <a href="/wiki/International_Summer_University_(ISU)_Kassel" title="International Summer University (ISU) Kassel">Summer University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Winter_University_(IWU)_Kassel" title="International Winter University (IWU) Kassel">Winter University</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kunsthochschule_Kassel" title="Kunsthochschule Kassel">Kunsthochschule Kassel</a> is also part of the university.</p>

<li>Hessisches Finanzgericht</li>

</div>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Valerius_Cordus" title="Valerius Cordus">Valerius Cordus</a> (1515-1544) physician and botanist, authored <a href="/wiki/Pharmacopoeias" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmacopoeias">pharmacopoeias</a> and herbals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_IV,_Landgrave_of_Hesse-Kassel" title="William IV, Landgrave of Hesse-Kassel">William IV, Landgrave of Hesse-Kassel</a> (1532-1592)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Landgravine_Charlotte_of_Hesse-Kassel" class="mw-redirect" title="Landgravine Charlotte of Hesse-Kassel">Landgravine Charlotte of Hesse-Kassel</a> (1627-1686)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maria_Amalia_of_Courland" title="Maria Amalia of Courland">Maria Amalia of Courland</a> (1653-1711), noblewoman, participated in creation of park at <a href="/wiki/Karlsaue" title="Karlsaue">Karlsaue</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Simon_Louis_du_Ry" title="Simon Louis du Ry">Simon Louis du Ry</a> (1726-1799), architect.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Erich_Raspe" title="Rudolf Erich Raspe">Rudolf Erich Raspe</a> (1736 – 1794), a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kassel" title="University of Kassel">University of Kassel</a> librarian, who fled to England after embezzling significant funds from <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Landgrave_of_Hesse-Kassel" title="Frederick II, Landgrave of Hesse-Kassel">Frederick II, Landgrave of Hesse-Kassel</a> and wrote (or compiled) <i><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Surprising_Adventures_of_Baron_Munchausen" class="extiw" title="s:The Surprising Adventures of Baron Munchausen">The Surprising Adventures of Baron Munchhausen</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_Charles_of_Hesse-Kassel" title="Prince Charles of Hesse-Kassel">Prince Charles of Hesse-Kassel</a> (1744-1836).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_Frederick_of_Hesse-Kassel" title="Prince Frederick of Hesse-Kassel">Prince Frederick of Hesse-Kassel</a> (1747-1837).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gertrud_Elisabeth_Mara" title="Gertrud Elisabeth Mara">Gertrud Elisabeth Mara</a> (1749-1833), operatic soprano.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Georg_Friedrich_Sartorius" title="Georg Friedrich Sartorius">Georg Friedrich Sartorius</a> (1765-1828), research historian and economist.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louis_Spohr" title="Louis Spohr">Louis Spohr</a> (1784-1859), 19th-century composer and violinist, who is commemorated by a museum in the city.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bonaparte" title="Jérôme Bonaparte">Jérôme Bonaparte</a> (1784-1860), brother of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a>, while he was king of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">Westphalia</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a>, Jacob (1785–1863) and Wilhelm Grimm (1786–1859), academics, linguists, cultural researchers, and authors who collected folklore and published several collections of it as <a href="/wiki/Grimm%27s_Fairy_Tales" class="mw-redirect" title="Grimm's Fairy Tales">Grimm's Fairy Tales</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Princess_Augusta_of_Hesse-Kassel" title="Princess Augusta of Hesse-Kassel">HSH Princess Augusta of Hesse-Kassel</a> (1797-1889), Princess and Landgravine of Hesse-Kassel, consort to <a href="/wiki/Field_marshal_(United_Kingdom)" title="Field marshal (United Kingdom)">Field Marshal</a> HRH <a href="/wiki/Prince_Adolphus,_Duke_of_Cambridge" title="Prince Adolphus, Duke of Cambridge">The Prince Adolphus Frederick</a>, Duke of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Duchy_of_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Duchy of Cambridge (page does not exist)">Cambridge</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Armand_Strubberg" title="Friedrich Armand Strubberg">Friedrich Armand Strubberg</a> (1806–1889), merchant, physician, colonist in North America, direct descendant of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I_of_Sweden" title="Frederick I of Sweden">Frederick I of Sweden</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Rosengarten" title="Albrecht Rosengarten">Albrecht Rosengarten</a> (1809-1893), architect famous for synagogue buildings in central Europe.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Justus_Carl_Hasskarl" title="Justus Carl Hasskarl">Justus Carl Hasskarl</a> (1809-1894), botanist specialising in <a href="/wiki/Pteridophytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Pteridophytes">Pteridophytes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bryophytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Bryophytes">Bryophytes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spermatophytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Spermatophytes">Spermatophytes</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louise_of_Hesse-Kassel" title="Louise of Hesse-Kassel">Louise of Hesse-Kassel</a> (1811-1898), Princess and the queen consort to King <a href="/wiki/Christian_IX_of_Denmark" title="Christian IX of Denmark">Christian IX</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Denmark" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Denmark">Denmark</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kollars" title="Helmut Kollars">Helmut Kollars</a> (born 1968), writer and illustrator.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paul_Reuter" title="Paul Reuter">Paul Reuter</a> (1816–1899), founder of the <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> news agency.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Israel_Meyer_Japhet" title="Israel Meyer Japhet">Israel Meyer Japhet</a> (1818–1892) choir director in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Claus" title="Carl Friedrich Claus">Carl Friedrich Claus</a> (1827-1900), chemist.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adolf_Eugen_Fick" title="Adolf Eugen Fick">Adolf Eugen Fick</a> (1829–1901), physiologist.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hugo_Wilhelm_Arthur_Nahl" title="Hugo Wilhelm Arthur Nahl">Hugo Wilhelm Arthur Nahl</a> (1833-1899), artist who designed the <a href="/wiki/Seal_of_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Seal of California">Seal of California</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lucien_Scheler" title="Lucien Scheler">Lucien Scheler</a> (1902–1999), French poet, writer and publisher.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jakob_Stilling" title="Jakob Stilling">Jakob Stilling</a> (1842-1915), ophthalmologist, son of <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Stilling" title="Benedict Stilling">Benedict Stilling</a>, surgeon, and brother of <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Stilling" class="extiw" title="de:Heinrich Stilling">Heinrich Stilling</a>, pathologist.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Philipp_Scheidemann" title="Philipp Scheidemann">Philipp Scheidemann</a> (1865–1939), briefly Germany's Chancellor after World War I.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andreas_Dippel" title="Andreas Dippel">Andreas Dippel</a> (1866-1932), operatic tenor.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carl_Kaiserling" title="Carl Kaiserling">Carl Kaiserling</a> (1869-1942), pathologist.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franz_Rosenzweig" title="Franz Rosenzweig">Franz Rosenzweig</a> (1886–1929), philosopher.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/F._W._Murnau" title="F. W. Murnau">F. W. Murnau</a> (1888–1931), movie director in the <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">silent era</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helmut_Hasse" title="Helmut Hasse">Helmut Hasse</a> (1898–1979), who did fundamental work in algebra and number theory.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arnold_Bode" title="Arnold Bode">Arnold Bode</a> (1900-1977), architect, painter, designer and founder of the documenta.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leni_Junker" title="Leni Junker">Leni Junker</a> (1905-1997), athlete who competed mainly in the 100 metres.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Otto_Sander" title="Otto Sander">Otto Sander</a> (30 June 1941 – 12 September 2013), German film, theater, and voice actor.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yona_Melnik" title="Yona Melnik">Yona Melnik</a> (born 1949), Israeli Olympic judoka</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hubertus_Meyer-Burckhardt" title="Hubertus Meyer-Burckhardt">Hubertus Meyer-Burckhardt</a> (born 1956), German television journalist and talk show host.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barbara_Rudnik" title="Barbara Rudnik">Barbara Rudnik</a> (27 July 1958 - 23 May 2009), was a German actress.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Meryem_Sahra_Uzerli" class="mw-redirect" title="Meryem Sahra Uzerli">Meryem Sahra Uzerli</a> (born 1982), Turkish-German actress.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Annika_Mehlhorn" title="Annika Mehlhorn">Annika Mehlhorn</a> (born 1983), German butterfly and medley swimmer, who competed in the <a href="/wiki/2004_Summer_Olympics" title="2004 Summer Olympics">2004 Summer Olympics</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Milky_Chance" title="Milky Chance">Milky Chance</a> (present), German folk duo.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yunus_Mall%C4%B1" title="Yunus Mallı">Yunus Mallı</a> (born 1992), Turkish footballer.</li>
</ul>

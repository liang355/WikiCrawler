<p><b>Memorial Hall</b>, immediately north of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Yard" title="Harvard Yard">Harvard Yard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, is an imposing<sup id="cite_ref-google_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-google2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google2-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-google3_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google3-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/High_Victorian_Gothic" title="High Victorian Gothic">High Victorian Gothic</a> building honor­ing the sacrifices made by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a> men in defense of the Union during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>‍—‌"a symbol of Boston's commitment to the Unionist cause and the abolitionist movement in America."<sup id="cite_ref-google4_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google4-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Built on a former playing field known as the <b>Delta</b>, it was described by <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">Henry James</a> as consisting of</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>three main divisions: one of them a theater, for academic ceremonies; another a vast <a href="/wiki/Refectory" title="Refectory">refectory</a>, covered with a timbered roof, hung about with portraits and lighted by stained windows, like the halls of the colleges of <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a>; and the third, the most interesting, a chamber high, dim and severe, consecrated to the sons of the university who fell in the long Civil War.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>James' "three divisions" are known today as (respectively) <b>Sanders Theatre</b>; <b>Annenberg Hall</b> (formerly <b>Alumni Hall</b> or the <b>Great Hall</b>); and <b>Memorial Transept</b>. Beneath Annenberg Hall, <b>Loker Commons</b> offers a number of student facilities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception_and_construction">Conception and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Hall_(Harvard_University)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conception and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>Memorial Hall is, in the opinion of the <a href="/wiki/President_and_Fellows_of_Harvard_College" title="President and Fellows of Harvard College">President and Fellows</a>, the most valuable gift the Uni­ver­si­ty has ever received, with respect alike to cost, daily usefulness, and moral significance.
<div style="float:right;"><i>President's Report for 1877–78</i><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-PresReport_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PresReport-7">[7]</a></sup></div>
<p><br />
<br />
This happy commemorative creation of the Union&#160;... the great bristling brick Valhalla of the early "seventies," that house of honor and of hos­pi­tal­ity which [dispenses] laurels to the dead and dinners to the living.</p>
<div style="float:right;">Henry James (1905)<span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></div>
<p><br />
<br />
... a huge Victorian Gothic barn&#160;...</p>
<div style="float:right;"><i>Life</i> (1941)<span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Between 1865 and 1868 an alumni "Committee of Fifty" raised $370,000 (equal to one-twelfth of Harvard's entire endowment at the time) toward a new building in memory of Harvard men who had fought for the Union in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-fas_concept_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fas_concept-10">[10]</a></sup> particularly the 136 dead<sup id="cite_ref-bricks_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bricks-11">[11]</a></sup>‍—‌a "Hall of Alumni in which students and graduates might be inspired by the pictured and sculpted presence of her founders, benefactors, faculty, presidents, and most distinguished sons." When, about the same time,<sup id="cite_ref-fas_concept_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fas_concept-10">[10]</a></sup> a $40,000 bequest was received from Charles Sanders (class of 1802) for "a hall or theatre to be used on [any] public occasion connected with the College, whether literary or festive", a vision was formed of a single building containing a large theater as well as a large open hall, and thus meeting both goals.<sup id="cite_ref-fas_concept_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fas_concept-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A site was found on the "Delta", the triangle bounded by Cambridge, Kirkland, and Quincy Streets.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[A]</a></sup> The project was formally named Memorial Hall in September 1870, and on October 6 the cornerstone was laid,<sup id="cite_ref-DAR_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAR-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Wendell_Holmes,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr.">Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr.</a> composing a hymn for the occasion.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[B]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1878 the Committee of Fifty notified the <a href="/wiki/President_and_Fellows_of_Harvard_College" title="President and Fellows of Harvard College">President and Fellows</a> that the project was complete and the premises ready for formal transfer to the university. On July<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>8 the President and Fellows unanimously voted to "accept with profound gratitude this splendid and precious gift."<sup id="cite_ref-PresReport_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PresReport-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_and_facilities">Architecture and facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Hall_(Harvard_University)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture and facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The building's High Victorian Gothic design, by alumni <a href="/wiki/William_Robert_Ware" title="William Robert Ware">William Robert Ware</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Van_Brunt" title="Henry Van Brunt">Henry Van Brunt</a>, was selected in a blind competition. A 1907 publication gives dimensions of 305 by 113 feet, with a height of 190 feet at the tower;<sup id="cite_ref-DAR_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAR-12">[12]</a></sup> a 2012 source gave a height of 195 feet, making it the ninth-tallest building in Cambridge at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-emporis_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emporis-17">[15]</a></sup> Its 1970 <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> designation recognized it one of the nation's most dramatic examples of High Victorian Gothic architecture.<sup id="cite_ref-nhl_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhl-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>A general restoration was carried out between 1987 and 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1-20">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Annenberg_Hall">Annenberg Hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Hall_(Harvard_University)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Annenberg Hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>What was originally known as Alumni Hall<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>‍—‌nine thousand square feet shaped by massive wooden trusses, walnut paneling, and a blue, stenciled ceiling‍—‌was dedicated in 1874.</p>
<p>Originally intended for formal occasions such as alumni dinners, it was almost immediately converted to a dining commons, and was for fifty years the college's main dining hall (charging, in 1884, $3.97 for a month's meals).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup> In 1893 the <i>Harvard Graduates Magazine</i> described "the throngs of men who, at one o'clock, are to be seen racing across the yard from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Hall_(Harvard_University)" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Hall (Harvard University)">Harvard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boylston_Hall_(Harvard_University)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boylston Hall (Harvard University)">Boylston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sever_Hall" title="Sever Hall">Sever</a> [Halls], striving to reach [Memorial] Hall ahead of slower competitors for vacant seats at the overtaxed tables".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> But "as the center of University life moved south toward the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles</a>, [the dining commons] became less popular and closed in 1925"<span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1943_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1943-23">[21]</a></sup> (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College#house_system" title="Harvard College">Harvard College § House system</a></i>) after which Alumni Hall saw mostly light use, typically as a venue for dances, banquets, examinations, and the like. In 1934 <i>The New York Times</i> reported that Harvard officials had "at last found a use for Memorial Hall" by siting a rifle range in the basement.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>During World War II the <i>Crimson</i> reported<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1943_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1943-23">[21]</a></sup> that "the Great Hall" was being used "in winter-time for the 6 o'clock in the morning calisthenics of the [military] Chaplain's School" (though without explaining why <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Divinity_School" title="Harvard Divinity School">Harvard Divinity</a> students had been singled out for this treatment) and intimated that Stevens Laboratory, in the basement, "is doing secret work in acoustics."</p>
<p>After extensive renovations, in 1996 the space was renamed Annenberg Hall and supplanted, as the freshmen dining hall, the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Union" title="Harvard Union">Harvard Union</a>, which had performed that function during most of the intervening time.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1-20">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Memorial Transept [2,600 square feet (240&#160;m<sup>2</sup>)] consists of a 60-foot-high (18&#160;m) gothic vault above a marble floor, black walnut paneling and stenciled walls, a large stained-glass over each of two exterior doors, and‍—‌commemorating 136 Harvard men who died fighting for the Union‍—‌twenty-eight white tablets,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>tablets to one after another of the many who thus died‍—‌a thrilling list. One sees such old New England names as <a href="/wiki/Peabody_(surname)" title="Peabody (surname)">Peabody</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wadsworth_(surname)" title="Wadsworth (surname)">Wadsworth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bowditch_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Bowditch (disambiguation)">Bowditch</a>; one sees the name of <a href="/wiki/Fletcher_Webster" title="Fletcher Webster">Fletcher Webster</a>; one sees that an Edward Revere died at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Antietam" title="Battle of Antietam">Antietam</a> and a Paul Revere at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Gettysburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-shackleton_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shackleton-25">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>(<a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate</a> deaths are not represented.) Memorial Transept acts as a vestibule for Sanders Theatre.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sanders_Theatre">Sanders Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Hall_(Harvard_University)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sanders Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sanders Theatre (substantially completed in 1875, but first used for Harvard's 1876 commencement)<sup id="cite_ref-fas_concept_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fas_concept-10">[10]</a></sup> was inspired by Christopher Wren's <a href="/wiki/Sheldonian_Theatre" title="Sheldonian Theatre">Sheldonian Theatre</a>. Renowned for its acoustics, and with 1166 seats Harvard's largest meeting place,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="I thought it was the largest in /Cambridge/, and at the time of its building the largest in, um... some big area (November 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sanders is in great demand for lectures, concerts, ceremonies and conferences. <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King, Jr">Martin Luther King, Jr</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> have spoken there.</p>
<p>Sanders features <a href="/wiki/John_La_Farge" title="John La Farge">John La Farge</a>'s stained-glass window <i>Athena Tying a Mourning Fillet</i>; statues of <a href="/wiki/James_Otis,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="James Otis, Jr.">James Otis</a> (by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Crawford_(sculptor)" title="Thomas Crawford (sculptor)">Thomas Crawford</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Quincy_III" title="Josiah Quincy III">Josiah Quincy III</a> (by <a href="/wiki/William_Wetmore_Story" title="William Wetmore Story">William Wetmore Story</a>) flank the stage. The exterior gables display busts of great orators: <a href="/wiki/Demosthenes" title="Demosthenes">Demosthenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">John Chrysostom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques-B%C3%A9nigne_Bossuet" title="Jacques-Bénigne Bossuet">Jacques-Bénigne Bossuet</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Pitt,_1st_Earl_of_Chatham" title="William Pitt, 1st Earl of Chatham">William Pitt, 1st Earl of Chatham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Webster" title="Daniel Webster">Daniel Webster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sanders contributed in an unusual way to the early work of <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Clement_Sabine" title="Wallace Clement Sabine">Wallace Sabine</a>, considered the founder of architectural acoustics. In 1895, tasked with improving the dismal acoustical performance of the <a href="/wiki/Fogg_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Fogg Museum">Fogg Museum's</a> lecture hall, Sabine carried out a series of nocturnal experiments there, using hundreds of seat cushions borrowed from nearby Sanders as sound-absorbent material‍—‌each night's work being limited by the requirement that the cushions be back in Sanders in time for morning lectures there. The scientific unit of sound absorption, the <i><a href="/wiki/Sabin_(unit)" title="Sabin (unit)">sabin</a></i>, is very close to the absorption provided by one Sanders Theatre cushion.<sup id="cite_ref-google5_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google5-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loker_Commons">Loker Commons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Hall_(Harvard_University)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Loker Commons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beneath Annenberg Hall, Loker Commons offers a student pub, music practice spaces, and other facilities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fenestration">Fenestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Hall_(Harvard_University)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fenestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Twenty-two stained-glass windows, installed between 1879 and 1902, include several by <a href="/wiki/John_La_Farge" title="John La Farge">John La Farge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Comfort_Tiffany" title="Louis Comfort Tiffany">Louis Comfort Tiffany</a> Studios, <a href="/wiki/Donald_MacDonald_(Craftsman)" class="mw-redirect" title="Donald MacDonald (Craftsman)">Donald MacDonald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Wyman_Whitman" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarah Wyman Whitman">Sarah Wyman Whitman</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mills_(Dedham_artist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Mills (Dedham artist)">Charles Mills</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dedham_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dedham-29">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p><span class="nowrap">The central tower was nearly complete</span> by 1876, but criticism convinced Van Brunt and Ware to revise it in 1877. In 1897<sup id="cite_ref-jhj_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jhj-30">[28]</a></sup> was added what a 1905 guidebook described as "an enormous [four-faced clock which] detonates the hours in a manner which is by no means conducive to the sleep of the just and the rest of the weary",<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup> and which <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_John_Conant" title="Kenneth John Conant">Kenneth John Conant</a> termed "railroad Gothic".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="Clapper_Case"></span> In 1932 the clock's driving works, and the associated 155-pound (70<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>kg) bell-clapper, were somehow lowered 115 feet (35<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>m) to the ground without attracting attention; visiting <a href="/wiki/Yale" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale">Yale</a> students were suspected<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> but the clapper was never found. Three years later the disappearance of the replacement clapper, under similar circumstances, was rumored to be Yale's revenge for the theft of its mascot, <a href="/wiki/Handsome_Dan" title="Handsome Dan">Handsome Dan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1897 tower was destroyed by fire in 1956 and rebuilt, to its 1877–1897 appearance, in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-jhj_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jhj-30">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/John_Harvard_statue" class="mw-redirect" title="John Harvard statue">John Harvard statue</a> was originally (1884) installed before Memorial Hall's west façade, but in 1924 it was moved to its current location on the west side of <a href="/wiki/University_Hall_(Harvard_University)" title="University Hall (Harvard University)">University Hall</a>. Memorial Hall being still, at the time, the college dining hall, a Harvard Lampoon cartoon showed "John Harvard" dismounting his plinth, chair in tow, and holding his nose because he "couldn't stand the smell of 'Mem' any longer".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p><b>Lübeck</b> (<small>pronounced</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈlyːbɛk]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:GT_L%C3%BCbeck.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/48/GT_L%C3%BCbeck.ogg" class="internal" title="GT Lübeck.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Germany" title="Northern Germany">northern Germany</a>, and one of the major ports of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. On the river <a href="/wiki/Trave" title="Trave">Trave</a>, it was the leading city of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>, and because of its extensive <a href="/wiki/Brick_Gothic" title="Brick Gothic">Brick Gothic</a> architecture is listed by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> as a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. In 2015, it had a population of 218,523.</p>
<p>The old part of Lübeck is on an island enclosed by the Trave. The <a href="/wiki/Elbe%E2%80%93L%C3%BCbeck_Canal" title="Elbe–Lübeck Canal">Elbe–Lübeck Canal</a> connects the Trave with the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> River. Another important river near the town centre is the <a href="/wiki/Wakenitz" title="Wakenitz">Wakenitz</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_1" title="Bundesautobahn 1">Autobahn 1</a> connects Lübeck with <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. <a href="/wiki/Travem%C3%BCnde" title="Travemünde">Travemünde</a> is a sea resort and ferry port on the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>. <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck_Hauptbahnhof" title="Lübeck Hauptbahnhof">Lübeck Hauptbahnhof</a> links Lübeck to a number of railway lines, notably the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck%E2%80%93Hamburg_railway" title="Lübeck–Hamburg railway">line to Hamburg</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCbeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Liubice" title="Liubice">Liubice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Free_City_of_L%C3%BCbeck" title="Free City of Lübeck">Free City of Lübeck</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_L%C3%BCbeck" title="Timeline of Lübeck">Timeline of Lübeck</a></div>
<p>Humans settled in the area around what today is Lübeck after the <a href="/wiki/Weichselian_glaciation" title="Weichselian glaciation">last Ice Age</a> ended about 9700 BCE. Several <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dolmen" title="Dolmen">dolmens</a> can be found in the area.</p>
<p>Around AD 700, <a href="/wiki/Slavic_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic people">Slavic peoples</a> started moving into the eastern parts of <a href="/wiki/Holstein" title="Holstein">Holstein</a>, an area previously settled by <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic inhabitants</a>; the latter had moved on in the course of the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> (Holy Roman Emperor 800–814), whose efforts to <a href="/wiki/Christianisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Christianisation">Christianise</a> the area were opposed by the Germanic <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>, expelled many of the Saxons and brought in <a href="/wiki/Polabian_Slavs" title="Polabian Slavs">Polabian Slavs</a>, allied to Charlemagne, in their stead. <a href="/wiki/Liubice" title="Liubice">Liubice</a> (the place-name means "lovely") was founded on the banks of the river Trave about four kilometres (2.5 miles) north of the present-day city-centre of Lübeck. In the 10th century it became the most important settlement of the <a href="/wiki/Obotrites" title="Obotrites">Obotrite confederacy</a> and a castle was built. In 1128 the pagan <a href="/wiki/Rani_(Slavic_tribe)" title="Rani (Slavic tribe)">Rani</a> from <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCgen" title="Rügen">Rügen</a> razed Liubice.</p>
<p>In 1143 <a href="/wiki/Adolf_II_of_Holstein" title="Adolf II of Holstein">Adolf II</a>, Count of Schauenburg and Holstein, founded the modern town as a German settlement on the river island of <a href="/wiki/Bucu" title="Bucu">Bucu</a>. He built a new castle, first mentioned by the chronicler <a href="/wiki/Helmold" title="Helmold">Helmold</a> as existing in 1147. Adolf had to cede the castle to the Duke of Saxony, <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Lion" title="Henry the Lion">Henry the Lion</a>, in 1158. After Henry's fall from power in 1181 the town became an <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Free_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Free City">Imperial city</a> for eight years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Barbarossa</a> (reigned 1152–1190) ordained that the city should have a ruling council of twenty members. With the council dominated by merchants, pragmatic trade interests shaped Lübeck's politics for centuries. The council survived into the 19th century. The town and castle changed ownership for a period afterwards and formed part of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxony" title="Duchy of Saxony">Duchy of Saxony</a> until 1192, of the County of <a href="/wiki/Holstein" title="Holstein">Holstein</a> until 1217, and of the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bornh%C3%B6ved_(1227)" title="Battle of Bornhöved (1227)">Battle of Bornhöved</a> in 1227.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hanseatic_city">Hanseatic city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCbeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hanseatic city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Around 1200 the port became the main point of departure for colonists leaving for the Baltic territories conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Livonian_Order" title="Livonian Order">Livonian Order</a> and, later, by the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Order" title="Teutonic Order">Teutonic Order</a>. In 1226 Emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a> elevated the town to the status of an <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Free_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Free City">Imperial Free City</a>, by which it became the <a href="/wiki/Free_City_of_L%C3%BCbeck" title="Free City of Lübeck">Free City of Lübeck</a>.</p>

<p>In the 14th century Lübeck became the "Queen of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>", being by far the largest and most powerful member of that medieval trade organization. In 1375 Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV</a> named Lübeck one of the five "Glories of the Empire", a title shared with <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>.</p>

<p>Several conflicts about trading privileges resulted in fighting between Lübeck (with the Hanseatic League) and Denmark and Norway – with varying outcome. While Lübeck and the Hanseatic League prevailed in conflicts in 1435 and 1512, Lübeck lost when it became involved in the <a href="/wiki/Count%27s_Feud" title="Count's Feud">Count's Feud</a>, a civil war that raged in Denmark from 1534 to 1536. Lübeck also joined the pro-Lutheran <a href="/wiki/Schmalkaldic_League" title="Schmalkaldic League">Schmalkaldic League</a> of the mid-16th century.</p>

<p>After its defeat in the Count's Feud, Lübeck's power slowly declined. The city remained neutral in the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> of 1618–1648, but the combination of the devastation from the decades-long war and the new transatlantic orientation of European trade caused the Hanseatic League – and thus Lübeck with it – to decline in importance. However, even after the <i>de facto</i> disbanding of the Hanseatic League in 1669, Lübeck still remained an important trading town on the Baltic Sea.</p>

<h3><span id="Old_traditions,_new_challenges"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_traditions.2C_new_challenges">Old traditions, new challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCbeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Old traditions, new challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Franz Tunder was the organist in the Marienkirche. It was part of the tradition in this Lutheran congregation that the organist would pass on the duty in a dynastic marriage. In 1668 his daughter Anna Margarethe married the great Danish-German composer <a href="/wiki/Dieterich_Buxtehude" title="Dieterich Buxtehude">Dieterich Buxtehude</a>, who was the organist at the <a href="/wiki/Marienkirche,_L%C3%BCbeck" class="mw-redirect" title="Marienkirche, Lübeck">Marienkirche</a> in Lübeck until at least 1703. Some of the greatest composers of the day came to the church to hear his renowned playing.</p>
<p>In the course of the war of the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth Coalition">Fourth Coalition</a> against <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a>, troops under <a href="/wiki/Charles_XIV_John_of_Sweden" title="Charles XIV John of Sweden">Bernadotte</a> occupied the neutral Lübeck after a battle against <a href="/wiki/Gebhard_Leberecht_von_Bl%C3%BCcher" title="Gebhard Leberecht von Blücher">Blücher</a> on 6 November 1806. Under the <a href="/wiki/Continental_System" title="Continental System">Continental System</a>, the State bank went into bankruptcy. In 1811 the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">French Empire</a> formally annexed Lübeck as part of France; the anti-Napoleonic Allies liberated the area in 1813, and the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> of 1815 recognised Lübeck as an independent Free City.</p>

<p>In 1937 the <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazis</a> passed the so-called <a href="/wiki/Greater_Hamburg_Act" title="Greater Hamburg Act">Greater Hamburg Act</a>, which merged the city of Lübeck with Prussia.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> (1939–1945), Lübeck became the first German city to suffer substantial <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> (RAF) bombing. The attack of 28 March 1942 created a <a href="/wiki/Firestorm" title="Firestorm">firestorm</a> that caused severe damage to the historic centre. <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_L%C3%BCbeck_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Lübeck in World War II">This raid</a> destroyed three of the main churches and large parts of the built-up area; the bells of St Marienkircke plunged to the stone floor.<sup id="cite_ref-luebeck-tourism.de_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-luebeck-tourism.de-7">[7]</a></sup> Germany operated a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner-of-war_camp" title="Prisoner-of-war camp">POW camp</a> for officers, <a href="/wiki/Oflag_X-C" title="Oflag X-C">Oflag X-C</a>, near the city from 1940 until April 1945. The British <a href="/wiki/Second_Army_(United_Kingdom)" title="Second Army (United Kingdom)">Second Army</a> entered Lübeck on 2 May 1945 and occupied it without resistance.</p>
<p>On 3 May 1945 one of the biggest disasters in naval history occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_L%C3%BCbeck" title="Bay of Lübeck">Bay of Lübeck</a> when RAF bombers sank three ships: the <a href="/wiki/SS_Cap_Arcona" title="SS Cap Arcona">SS <i>Cap Arcona</i></a>, the <a href="/wiki/SS_Deutschland_(1923)" title="SS Deutschland (1923)">SS <i>Deutschland</i></a>, and the <a href="/wiki/SS_Thielbek_(1940)" title="SS Thielbek (1940)">SS <i>Thielbek</i></a> – which, unknown to them, were packed with concentration camp inmates. About 7,000 people died.</p>
<p>Lübeck's population grew considerably – from about 150,000 in 1939 to more than 220,000 after the war – owing to an influx of ethnic German refugees expelled from the so-called <a href="/wiki/Historical_Eastern_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Historical Eastern Germany">former Eastern provinces</a> of Germany in the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Bloc" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Bloc">Communist Bloc</a>. Lübeck remained part of Schleswig-Holstein after World War II (and consequently lay within <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>). It stood directly on what became the <a href="/wiki/Inner_German_border" title="Inner German border">inner German border</a> during the division of Germany into two states in the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> period. South of the city, the border followed the path of the river Wakenitz, which separated the Germanys by less than 10&#160;m (32.81&#160;ft) in many parts. The northernmost border-crossing was in Lübeck's district of Schlutup. Lübeck spent decades restoring its historic city centre. In 1987 <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> designated this area a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.</p>
<p>Lübeck became the scene of a notable art scandal in the 1950s. <a href="/wiki/Lothar_Malskat" title="Lothar Malskat">Lothar Malskat</a> was hired to restore the medieval <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a> of the cathedral of the Marienkirche, which were discovered after the cathedral had been badly damaged during World War II. Instead he painted new works which he passed off as restorations, fooling many experts. Malskat later revealed the deception himself. <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnter_Grass" title="Günter Grass">Günter Grass</a> featured this incident in his 1986 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rat_(novel)" title="The Rat (novel)">The Rat</a></i>.</p>

<p>On the night of 18 January 1996 a fire broke out in a home for foreign refugees, killing 10 people and severely injuring more than 30 others, mostly children. Most of the shelter's inhabitants thought it was a <a href="/wiki/Racist" class="mw-redirect" title="Racist">racist</a> attack, as they stated that they had encountered other overt hostility in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The police and the local court were criticized at the time for ruling out racism as a possible motive before even beginning preliminary investigations.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> But by 2002, the courts found all the Germans involved<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> not guilty; the perpetrators have not been caught.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2015, Lübeck hosted the G7 conference.<sup id="cite_ref-conference_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conference-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCbeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The largest ethnic minority groups are <a href="/wiki/Turkish_people" title="Turkish people">Turks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europeans</a> (<a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a>), <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europeans</a> (mostly <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italians</a>), <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europeans</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">Russians</a>), <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> and several smaller groups. As in numerous other German cities, there is also a growing <a href="/wiki/Afro-German" class="mw-redirect" title="Afro-German">Afro-German</a> community.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Population structure:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCbeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Much of the old town has kept a <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> appearance with old buildings and narrow streets. At one time the town could only be entered via any of four <a href="/wiki/City_gate" title="City gate">town gates</a>, of which today two remain, the well-known <a href="/wiki/Holstentor" title="Holstentor">Holstentor</a> (1478) and the <a href="/wiki/Burgtor" title="Burgtor">Burgtor</a> (1444).</p>
<p>The old town centre is dominated by seven church steeples. The oldest are the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck_Cathedral" title="Lübeck Cathedral">Lübecker Dom</a> (the city's cathedral) and the <a href="/wiki/Marienkirche,_L%C3%BCbeck" class="mw-redirect" title="Marienkirche, Lübeck">Marienkirche</a> (Saint Mary's), both from the 13th and 14th centuries.</p>
<p>Built in 1286, the Holy Spirit Hospital at Koberg is one of the oldest existing social institutions in the world and one of the most important buildings in the city. The Holy Spirit Hospital is in parts an old and nursing home. Historic parts can be visited.</p>

<li>the <a href="/wiki/Salzspeicher" title="Salzspeicher">Salzspeicher</a>, historic warehouses where salt delivered from <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCneburg" title="Lüneburg">Lüneburg</a> awaited shipment to Baltic ports.</li>
</ul>
<p>Like many other places in Germany, Lübeck has a long tradition of a Christmas market in December, which includes the famous handicrafts market inside the Heiligen-Geist-Hospital (Hospital of the Holy Spirit), located at the northern end of Königstrasse.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCbeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lübeck has many small museums, such as the <a href="/wiki/St._Anne%27s_Museum_Quarter,_L%C3%BCbeck" title="St. Anne's Museum Quarter, Lübeck">St. Anne's Museum Quarter, Lübeck</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Behnhaus" title="Behnhaus">Behnhaus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Hansemuseum" title="European Hansemuseum">European Hansemuseum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Holstentor" title="Holstentor">Holstentor</a>. <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck_Museum_of_Theatre_Puppets" title="Lübeck Museum of Theatre Puppets">Lübeck Museum of Theatre Puppets</a> is a privately run museum. Waterside attractions are a <a href="/wiki/Lightvessel" title="Lightvessel">lightvessel</a> that served <a href="/wiki/Fehmarnbelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Fehmarnbelt">Fehmarnbelt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lisa_von_L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lisa von Lübeck">Lisa von Lübeck</a>, a reconstruction of a Hanseatic 15th century <a href="/wiki/Caravel" title="Caravel">caravel</a>. The marzipan museum in the second floor of Café <a href="/wiki/Niederegger" title="Niederegger">Niederegger</a> in Breite Strasse explains the history of <a href="/wiki/Marzipan" title="Marzipan">marzipan</a>, shows historical wood molds for the production of marzipan blocks and a group of historical figures made of marzipan.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_drink">Food and drink</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCbeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Food and drink">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lübeck is famous for its <a href="/wiki/Marzipan" title="Marzipan">marzipan</a> industry. According to local legend, marzipan was first made in Lübeck, possibly in response either to a military siege of the city or a famine year. The story, perhaps apocryphal, is that the town ran out of all food except stored almonds and sugar, which were used to make loaves of marzipan "bread".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Others believe that marzipan was actually invented in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Persia</a> a few hundred years before Lübeck claims to have invented it. The best known producer is <a href="/wiki/Niederegger" title="Niederegger">Niederegger</a>, which tourists often visit while in Lübeck, especially at Christmas time.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Lübeck wine trade dates back to Hanseatic times. One Lübeck specialty is Rotspon (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:GeoTrinity_Rotspon.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9f/GeoTrinity_Rotspon.ogg" class="internal" title="GeoTrinity Rotspon.ogg">listen</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:GeoTrinity_Rotspon.ogg" title="File:GeoTrinity Rotspon.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>), wine made from grapes processed and fermented in France and transported in wooden barrels to Lübeck, where it is stored, aged and bottled.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCbeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lübeck has three universities, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_L%C3%BCbeck" title="University of Lübeck">University of Lübeck</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule_L%C3%BCbeck" class="mw-redirect" title="Fachhochschule Lübeck">Lübeck Academy of Applied Sciences</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Musikhochschule_L%C3%BCbeck" class="mw-redirect" title="Musikhochschule Lübeck">Lübeck Academy of Music</a>. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.gradschool.uni-luebeck.de">Graduate School for Computing in Medicine and Life Sciences</a> is a central faculty of the University and was founded by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.dfg.de/en/research_funding/coordinated_programmes/excellence_initiative/">German Excellence Initiative</a>. The <a href="/wiki/International_School_of_New_Media" title="International School of New Media">International School of New Media</a> is an affiliated institute of the University.</p>

<p>The industrial Lübeck-Herrenwyk area was until the beginning of the 1990s the location of a big metallurgical plant. The gas produced by this plant was used for making electricity in the Lübeck-Herrenwyk power station. In 1992, the Lübeck-Herrenwyk power station was demolished after the bankruptcy and demolition of the metallurgical plant and since 1994 its site houses the <a href="/wiki/Static_inverter_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Static inverter plant">static inverter plant</a> of the <a href="/wiki/High-voltage_direct_current" title="High-voltage direct current">HVDC</a> <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Cable" title="Baltic Cable">Baltic Cable</a>.</p>

<p><b>Mainz</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>German:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[maɪ̯nt͡s]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Mainz.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5d/De-Mainz.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Mainz.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Mogontiacum</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Mayence</i></span>) is the capital and largest city of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a> in Germany. It was the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Mainz" title="Electorate of Mainz">Electorate of Mainz</a> at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. In antiquity Mainz was a Roman fort city which commanded the west bank of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and formed part of the northernmost frontier of the Roman Empire; it was founded as a military post by the Romans in the late 1st century BC and became the provincial capital of <a href="/wiki/Germania_Superior" title="Germania Superior">Germania Superior</a>. The city is located on the river Rhine at its confluence with the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> opposite <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden" title="Wiesbaden">Wiesbaden</a>, in the western part of the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Rhine-Main" title="Frankfurt Rhine-Main">Frankfurt Rhine-Main</a> region; in the modern age, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> shares much of its regional importance.</p>
<p>The city is famous as the home of the invention of the movable-type <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>, as the first books printed using movable type were manufactured in Mainz by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Gutenberg</a> in the early 1450s. Until the twentieth century, Mainz was usually referred to in English by its French name: <b>Mayence</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mainz is located on the 50th latitude, on the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">west bank of the river Rhine</a>, opposite the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> with the Rhine. The population in the early 2012 was 200,957, an additional 18,619 people maintain a primary residence elsewhere but have a <a href="/wiki/Vacation_property" class="mw-redirect" title="Vacation property">second home</a> in Mainz. The city is part of the Rhein Metro area comprising 5.8 million people. Mainz can easily be reached from <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt International Airport</a> in 25 minutes by commuter railway (<a href="/wiki/S8_(Rhine-Main_S-Bahn)" title="S8 (Rhine-Main S-Bahn)">Line S8</a>).</p>

<p>Mainz is a <a href="/wiki/River_port" class="mw-redirect" title="River port">river port</a> city as the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> which connects with its main tributaries, such as the <a href="/wiki/Neckar" title="Neckar">Neckar</a>, the Main and, later, the <a href="/wiki/Moselle_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Moselle (river)">Moselle</a> and thereby continental Europe with the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Rotterdam" title="Port of Rotterdam">Port of Rotterdam</a> and thus the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>. Mainz's history and economy are closely tied to its proximity to the Rhine historically handling much of the region's waterborne cargo. Today's huge <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Mainz" title="Port of Mainz">container port hub</a> allowing <a href="/wiki/Multimodal_transport" title="Multimodal transport">trimodal transport</a> is located on the North Side of the town. The river also provides another positive effect, moderating Mainz's climate; making waterfront neighborhoods slightly warmer in winter and cooler in summer.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">last ice age</a>, sand dunes were deposited in the Rhine valley at what was to become the western edge of the city. The <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Sand_Dunes" title="Mainz Sand Dunes">Mainz Sand Dunes</a> area is now a nature reserve with a unique landscape and rare <i>steppe</i> vegetation for this area.</p>
<p>While the Mainz legion camp was founded in 13/12 BC on the Kästrich hill, the associated <a href="/wiki/Vicus" title="Vicus">vici</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canabae" class="mw-redirect" title="Canabae">canabae</a> were erected in direction to the Rhine. Historical sources and archaeological findings both prove the importance of the military and civilian Mogontiacum as a port city on the Rhine.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Mainz" title="Timeline of Mainz">Timeline of Mainz</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Mogontiacum">Roman Mogontiacum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman Mogontiacum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stadttor1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Stadttor1.jpg/220px-Stadttor1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Stadttor1.jpg/330px-Stadttor1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Stadttor1.jpg/440px-Stadttor1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stadttor1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Remains of a Roman town gate from the late 4th century</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Roman stronghold or <i><a href="/wiki/Castrum" class="mw-redirect" title="Castrum">castrum</a> <b>Mogontiacum</b></i>, the precursor to Mainz, was founded by the Roman general <a href="/wiki/Nero_Claudius_Drusus" title="Nero Claudius Drusus">Drusus</a> perhaps as early as 13/12 BC. As related by <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">Suetonius</a> the existence of <i>Mogontiacum</i> is well established by four years later (the account of the death and funeral of <a href="/wiki/Nero_Claudius_Drusus" title="Nero Claudius Drusus">Nero Claudius Drusus</a>), though several other theories suggest the site may have been established earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Although the city is situated opposite the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a>, the name of Mainz is not from Main, the similarity being perhaps due to <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">diachronic</a> analogy. Main is from Latin <i>Menus</i>, the name the Romans used for the river. <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">Linguistic</a> analysis of the many forms that the name "Mainz" has taken on make it clear that it is a simplification of <i>Mogontiacum</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The name appears to be <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> and ultimately it is. However, it had also become Roman and was selected by them with a special significance. The Roman soldiers defending <a href="/wiki/Gallia" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallia">Gallia</a> had adopted the Gallic god <a href="/wiki/Mogons" title="Mogons">Mogons</a> (Mogounus, Moguns, Mogonino), for the meaning of which etymology offers two basic options: "the great one", similar to Latin magnus, which was used in aggrandizing names such as <i>Alexander magnus</i>, "Alexander the Great" and <i>Pompeius magnus</i>, "Pompey the great", or the god of "might" personified as it appears in young servitors of any type whether of noble or ignoble birth.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zitadelle_Mainz_Drususstein.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Zitadelle_Mainz_Drususstein.jpg/170px-Zitadelle_Mainz_Drususstein.jpg" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Zitadelle_Mainz_Drususstein.jpg/255px-Zitadelle_Mainz_Drususstein.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Zitadelle_Mainz_Drususstein.jpg/340px-Zitadelle_Mainz_Drususstein.jpg 2x" data-file-width="667" data-file-height="1001" /></a>

<p>Mogontiacum was an important military town throughout Roman times, probably due to its strategic position at the confluence of the Main and the Rhine. The town of <i>Mogontiacum</i> grew up between the fort and the river. The castrum was the base of <a href="/wiki/Legio_XIIII_Gemina" class="mw-redirect" title="Legio XIIII Gemina">Legio XIIII <i>Gemina</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Legio_XVI_Gallica" title="Legio XVI Gallica">XVI <i>Gallica</i></a> (AD 9–43), <a href="/wiki/Legio_XXII_Primigenia" title="Legio XXII Primigenia">XXII <i>Primigenia</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Legio_IIII_Macedonica" class="mw-redirect" title="Legio IIII Macedonica">IIII <i>Macedonica</i></a> (43–70), <a href="/wiki/Legio_I_Adiutrix" title="Legio I Adiutrix">I <i>Adiutrix</i></a> (70–88), <a href="/wiki/Legio_XXI_Rapax" title="Legio XXI Rapax">XXI <i>Rapax</i></a> (70–89), and <a href="/wiki/Legio_XIIII_Gemina" class="mw-redirect" title="Legio XIIII Gemina">XIIII <i>Gemina</i></a> (70–92), among others. Mainz was also a base of a Roman river fleet, the <a href="/wiki/Classis_Germanica" title="Classis Germanica">Classis Germanica</a>. Remains of Roman troop ships (<a href="/wiki/Navis_lusoria" title="Navis lusoria">navis lusoria</a>) and a patrol boat from the late 4th century were discovered in 1982/86 and may now be viewed in the <i>Museum für Antike Schifffahrt</i>. A temple dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis Panthea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Magna Mater</a> was discovered in 2000<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and is open to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The city was the provincial capital of <a href="/wiki/Germania_Superior" title="Germania Superior">Germania Superior</a>, and had an important funeral monument dedicated to Drusus, to which people made pilgrimages for an annual festival from as far away as <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>. Among the famous buildings were the largest <a href="/wiki/Roman_theatre_(structure)" title="Roman theatre (structure)">theatre</a> north of the Alps and a bridge across the Rhine.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alemanni" title="Alemanni">Alemanni</a> forces under <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rando_(Alemanni_leader)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rando (Alemanni leader) (page does not exist)">Rando</a> sacked the city in 368. From the last day of 405<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> or 406, the Siling and Asding <a href="/wiki/Vandal" class="mw-redirect" title="Vandal">Vandals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a>, and other Germanic tribes <a href="/wiki/Crossing_of_the_Rhine" title="Crossing of the Rhine">crossed the Rhine</a>, possibly at Mainz. Christian chronicles relate that the bishop, Aureus, was put to death by the Alemannian Crocus. The way was open to the sack of <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a> and the invasion of Gaul.</p>
<p>Throughout the changes of time, the Roman castrum never seems to have been permanently abandoned as a military installation, which is a testimony to Roman military judgement. Different structures were built there at different times. The current citadel originated in 1660, but it replaced previous forts. It was used in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. One of the sights at the citadel is still the <a href="/wiki/Cenotaph" title="Cenotaph">cenotaph</a> raised by legionaries to commemorate their <a href="/wiki/Nero_Claudius_Drusus_Germanicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Nero Claudius Drusus Germanicus">Drusus</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frankish_Mainz">Frankish Mainz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Frankish Mainz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Through a series of incursions during the 4th century <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a> gradually lost its Belgic ethnic character of formerly Germanic tribes among Celts ruled by Romans and became predominantly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Alamanni" class="mw-redirect" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>. The Romans repeatedly reasserted control; however, the troops stationed at Mainz became chiefly non-Italic and the emperors had only one or two Italian ancestors in a pedigree that included chiefly peoples of the northern frontier.</p>
<p>The last emperor to station troops serving the western empire at Mainz was <a href="/wiki/Valentinian_III" title="Valentinian III">Valentinian III</a> (reigned 425–455), who relied heavily on his <i>Magister militum per Gallias</i>, <a href="/wiki/Flavius_A%C3%ABtius" class="mw-redirect" title="Flavius Aëtius">Flavius Aëtius</a>. By that time the army included large numbers of troops from the major Germanic confederacies along the Rhine, the Alamanni, the <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>. The Franks were an opponent that had risen to power and reputation among the Belgae of the lower Rhine during the 3rd century and repeatedly attempted to extend their influence upstream. In 358 the emperor <a href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" class="mw-redirect" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian</a> bought peace by giving them most of <a href="/wiki/Germania_Inferior" title="Germania Inferior">Germania Inferior</a>, which they possessed anyway, and imposing service in the Roman army in exchange.</p>
<p>European factions in the time of master Aëtius included Celts, Goths, Franks, Saxons, Alamanni, Huns, Italians, and Alans as well as numerous other minor peoples. Aëtius played them all off against one another in a masterly effort to keep the peace under Roman sovereignty. He used Hunnic troops a number of times. At last a day of reckoning arrived between Aëtius and <a href="/wiki/Attila" title="Attila">Attila</a>, both commanding polyglot, multi-ethnic troops. Attila went through Alsace in 451, devastating the country and destroying Mainz and Triers with their Roman garrisons. Shortly after he was thwarted by <a href="/wiki/Flavius_A%C3%ABtius" class="mw-redirect" title="Flavius Aëtius">Flavius Aëtius</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ch%C3%A2lons" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Châlons">Battle of Châlons</a>, the largest of the ancient world.</p>
<p>Aëtius was not to enjoy the victory long. He was assassinated in 454 by the hand of his employer, who in turn was stabbed to death by friends of Aëtius in 455. As far as the north was concerned this was the effective end of the Roman empire there. After some sanguinary but relatively brief contention a former subordinate of Aëtius, <a href="/wiki/Ricimer" title="Ricimer">Ricimer</a>, became emperor, taking the name Patrician. His father was a Suebian; his mother, a princess of the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>. Patrician did not rule the north directly but set up a client province there, which functioned independently. The capital was at <a href="/wiki/Soissons" title="Soissons">Soissons</a>. Even then its status was equivocal. Many insisted it was the <a href="/wiki/Domain_of_Soissons" class="mw-redirect" title="Domain of Soissons">Kingdom of Soissons</a>.</p>
<p>Previously the first of the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovingian">Merovingians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clodio" class="mw-redirect" title="Clodio">Clodio</a>, had been defeated by Aëtius at about 430. His son, <a href="/wiki/Merovaeus" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovaeus">Merovaeus</a>, fought on the Roman side against Attila, and his son, <a href="/wiki/Childeric_I" title="Childeric I">Childeric</a>, served in the domain of Soissons. Meanwhile, the Franks were gradually infiltrating and assuming power in this domain. They also moved up the Rhine and created a domain in the region of the former Germania Superior with capital at <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>. They became known as the <a href="/wiki/Ripuarian_Franks" title="Ripuarian Franks">Ripuarian Franks</a> as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Salian_Franks" title="Salian Franks">Salian Franks</a>. It is unlikely that much of a population transfer or displacement occurred. The former Belgae simply became Franks.</p>
<p>Events moved rapidly in the late 5th century. Clovis, son of Childeric, became king of the Salians in 481, ruling from <a href="/wiki/Tournai" title="Tournai">Tournai</a>. In 486 he defeated <a href="/wiki/Syagrius" title="Syagrius">Syagrius</a>, last governor of the Soissons domain, and took northern France. He extended his reign to <a href="/wiki/Cambrai" title="Cambrai">Cambrai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tongeren" title="Tongeren">Tongeren</a> in 490–491, and repelled the Alamanni in 496. Also in that year he converted to non-Arian Christianity.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">fall of the Western Roman Empire</a> in 476, the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> under the rule of <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis I</a> gained control over western Europe by the year 496. Clovis annexed the kingdom of Cologne in 508. Thereafter, Mainz, in its strategic position, became one of the bases of the Frankish kingdom. Mainz had sheltered a Christian community long before the conversion of Clovis. His successor <a href="/wiki/Dagobert_I" title="Dagobert I">Dagobert I</a> reinforced the walls of Mainz and made it one of his seats. A <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(coin)" title="Solidus (coin)">solidus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Theodebert_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Theodebert I">Theodebert I</a> (534–548) was minted at Mainz.</p>
<p>The Franks united the Celtic and Germanic tribes of Europe. The greatest Frank of all was <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> (768–814), who built a new empire in Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. Mainz from its central location became important to the empire and to Christianity. Meanwhile, language change was gradually working to divide the Franks. Mainz spoke a dialect termed <a href="/wiki/Ripuarian_language" title="Ripuarian language">Ripuarian</a>. On the death of Charlemagne, distinctions between France and Germany began to be made. Mainz was not central any longer but was on the border, creating a question of the nationality to which it belonged, which descended into modern times as the question of Alsace-Lorraine.</p>

<p>In the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle Age">Middle Ages</a>, Mainz was a centre for the <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianisation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic peoples">Slavic peoples</a>. The first Archbishop in Mainz, <a href="/wiki/Boniface" class="mw-redirect" title="Boniface">Boniface</a>, was killed in 754 while trying to convert the <a href="/wiki/Frisians" title="Frisians">Frisians</a> to Christianity and is buried in <a href="/wiki/Fulda" title="Fulda">Fulda</a>. <a href="/wiki/Harald_Klak" title="Harald Klak">Harald Klak</a>, king of Jutland, his family and followers, were baptized at Mainz in 826, in the <a href="/wiki/St._Alban%27s_Abbey,_Mainz" title="St. Alban's Abbey, Mainz">abbey of St. Alban's</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Other early archbishops of Mainz include <a href="/wiki/Rabanus_Maurus" title="Rabanus Maurus">Rabanus Maurus</a>, the scholar and author, and <a href="/wiki/Willigis" title="Willigis">Willigis</a> (975–1011), who began construction on the current building of the <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Cathedral" title="Mainz Cathedral">Mainz Cathedral</a> and founded the monastery of St. Stephan.</p>

<p>From the time of Willigis until the end of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> in 1806, the <a href="/wiki/Archbishops_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishops of Mainz">Archbishops of Mainz</a> were archchancellors of the Empire and the most important of the seven <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Electors</a> of the German emperor. Besides Rome, the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Mainz">diocese of Mainz</a> today is the only <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> in the world with an <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">episcopal see</a> that is called a <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> (<i>sancta sedes</i>). The Archbishops of Mainz traditionally were <i><a href="/wiki/Primas_germaniae" class="mw-redirect" title="Primas germaniae">primas germaniae</a></i>, the substitutes of the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>.</p>
<p>In 1244, Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_III_(archbishop_of_Mainz)" title="Siegfried III (archbishop of Mainz)">Siegfried III</a> granted Mainz a city charter, which included the right of the citizens to establish and elect a city council. The city saw a feud between two archbishops in 1461, namely <a href="/wiki/Diether_von_Isenburg" title="Diether von Isenburg">Diether von Isenburg</a>, who was elected Archbishop by the <a href="/wiki/Chapter_(religion)" title="Chapter (religion)">cathedral chapter</a> and supported by the citizens, and <a href="/wiki/Adolf_II_von_Nassau" class="mw-redirect" title="Adolf II von Nassau">Adolf II von Nassau</a>, who had been named Archbishop for Mainz by the pope. In 1462, the Archbishop Adolf II raided the city of Mainz, plundering and killing 400 inhabitants. At a tribunal, those who had survived lost all their property, which was then divided between those who promised to follow Adolf II. Those who would not promise to follow Adolf II (amongst them <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Johannes Gutenberg</a>) were driven out of the town or thrown into prison. The new Archbishop revoked the city charter of Mainz and put the city under his direct rule. Ironically, after the death of Adolf II his successor was again Diether von Isenburg, now legally elected by the chapter and named by the Pope.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Jewish_community">Early Jewish community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Early Jewish community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Jewish community of Mainz dates to the 10th century AD. It is noted for its religious education. Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Gershom_ben_Judah" title="Gershom ben Judah">Gershom ben Judah</a> (960–1040) taught there, among others. He concentrated on the study of the <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a>, creating a German Jewish tradition. Mainz is also the legendary home of the martyred Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Amnon_of_Mainz" title="Amnon of Mainz">Amnon of Mainz</a>, composer of the <a href="/wiki/Unetanneh_Tokef" title="Unetanneh Tokef">Unetanneh Tokef</a> prayer. The Jews of Mainz, <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a> created a supreme council to set standards in Jewish law and education in the 12th century.</p>
<p>The city of Mainz responded to the Jewish population in a variety of ways, behaving, in a sense, in a bipolar fashion towards them. Sometimes they were allowed freedom and were protected; at other times, they were persecuted. The Jews were expelled in 1012, 1462 (after which they were invited to return), and in 1474. Jews were attacked in <a href="/wiki/Rhineland_massacres" title="Rhineland massacres">1096</a> and by mobs in 1283. Outbreaks of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> were usually blamed on the Jews, at which times they were massacred, such as the burning of about 6,000 Jews alive in 1349.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuchman_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuchman-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nowadays the Jewish community is growing rapidly, and a <a href="/wiki/New_synagogue_Mainz" title="New synagogue Mainz">new synagogue</a> by the architect <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Herz" title="Manuel Herz">Manuel Herz</a> was constructed in 2010 on the site of the one destroyed under the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The community itself has 1,034 members, according to the Central Council of Jews in Germany, and at least twice as many Jews altogether since many are unaffiliated with Judaism.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Republic_of_Mainz">Republic of Mainz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Republic of Mainz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Mainz" title="Republic of Mainz">Republic of Mainz</a></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, the French Revolutionary army occupied Mainz in 1792; the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop of Mainz">Archbishop of Mainz</a>, Friedrich Karl Josef von Erthal, had already fled to <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a> by the time the French marched in. On 18 March 1793, the <a href="/wiki/Jacobin_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacobin Club">Jacobins</a> of Mainz, with other German democrats from about 130 towns in the <a href="/wiki/Palatinate_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Palatinate region">Rhenish Palatinate</a>, proclaimed the '<a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Mainz" title="Republic of Mainz">Republic of Mainz</a>'. Led by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Forster" title="Georg Forster">Georg Forster</a>, representatives of the Mainz Republic in Paris requested political affiliation of the Mainz Republic with France, but too late: <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> was not entirely happy with the idea of a democratic free state on German soil (although the French dominated Mainz was neither free nor democratic). Prussian troops had already occupied the area and besieged Mainz by the end of March, 1793. After a <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Mainz_(1793)" title="Siege of Mainz (1793)">siege</a> of 18 weeks, the French troops in Mainz surrendered on 23 July 1793; Prussians occupied the city and ended the Republic of Mainz. It came to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Mainz" title="Battle of Mainz">Battle of Mainz</a> in 1795 between Austria and France. Members of the Mainz <a href="/wiki/Jacobin_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacobin Club">Jacobin Club</a> were mistreated or imprisoned and punished for treason.</p>

<p>In 1797, the French returned. The army of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoléon Bonaparte</a> occupied the German territory to the west of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Campo_Formio" title="Treaty of Campo Formio">Treaty of Campo Formio</a> awarded France this entire area. On 17 February 1800, the French <i><a href="/wiki/Mont-Tonnerre" title="Mont-Tonnerre">Département du Mont-Tonnerre</a></i> was founded here, with Mainz as its capital, the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> being the new eastern frontier of la Grande Nation. Austria and <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> could not but approve this new border with France in 1801. However, after several defeats in Europe during the next years, the weakened Napoléon and his troops had to leave Mainz in May 1814.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhenish_Hesse">Rhenish Hesse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rhenish Hesse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1816, the part of the former French Département which is known today as <a href="/wiki/Rhenish_Hesse" title="Rhenish Hesse">Rhenish Hesse</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Rheinhessen</span>) was awarded to the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Hesse-Darmstadt</a>, Mainz being the capital of the new <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hessian</a> province of Rhenish Hesse. From 1816 to 1866, to the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a> Mainz was the most important fortress in the defence against France, and had a strong garrison of Austrian, <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> troops.</p>
<p>In the afternoon of 18 November 1857, a huge explosion rocked Mainz when the city's powder magazine, the <i>Pulverturm</i>, exploded. Approximately 150 people were killed and at least 500 injured; 57 buildings were destroyed and a similar number severely damaged in what was to be known as the <i>Powder Tower Explosion</i> or <i>Powder Explosion</i>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> in 1866, Mainz was declared a neutral zone. After the founding of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871, Mainz no longer was as important a stronghold, because in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">war of 1870/71</a> France had lost the territory of <a href="/wiki/Alsace-Lorraine" title="Alsace-Lorraine">Alsace-Lorraine</a> to Germany (which France had occupied piece by piece 1630/1795), and this defined the new border between the two countries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_expansion">Industrial expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Industrial expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mainz_BlickzumRhein_1890.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Mainz_BlickzumRhein_1890.jpg/220px-Mainz_BlickzumRhein_1890.jpg" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Mainz_BlickzumRhein_1890.jpg/330px-Mainz_BlickzumRhein_1890.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Mainz_BlickzumRhein_1890.jpg/440px-Mainz_BlickzumRhein_1890.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3545" data-file-height="2597" /></a>

<p>For centuries the inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Fortress_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Fortress Mainz">fortress of Mainz</a> had suffered from a severe shortage of space which led to disease and other inconveniences. In 1872 Mayor <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wallau" title="Carl Wallau">Carl Wallau</a> and the council of Mainz persuaded the military government to sign a contract to expand the city. Beginning in 1874, the city of Mainz assimilated the <i>Gartenfeld</i>, an idyllic area of meadows and fields along the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> to the north of the rampart. The city expansion more than doubled the urban area which allowed Mainz to participate in the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> which had previously avoided the city for decades.</p>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eduard_Krey%C3%9Fig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eduard Kreyßig (page does not exist)">Eduard Kreyßig</a> was the man who made this happen. Having been the master builder of the city of Mainz since 1865, Kreyßig had the vision for the new part of town, the <i>Neustadt</i>. He also planned the first sewer system for the old part of town since Roman times and persuaded the city government to relocate the railway line from the Rhine side to the west end of the town. <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Hauptbahnhof" title="Mainz Hauptbahnhof">The main station</a> was built from 1882 to 1884 according to the plans of Philipp Johann Berdellé.</p>

Mainz including expansion zone the Rhine (1898)</div>

<p>The Mainz master builder constructed a number of state-of-the-art public buildings, including the Mainz town hall — which was the largest of its kind in Germany at that time — as well a synagogue, the Rhine harbour and a number of public baths and school buildings. Kreyßig's last work was <a href="/wiki/Christuskirche,_Mainz" title="Christuskirche, Mainz">Christ Church</a> (<i>Christuskirche</i>), the largest Protestant church in the city and the first building constructed solely for the use of a Protestant congregation. In 1905 the demolition of the entire circumvallation and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rheingauwall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rheingauwall (page does not exist)">Rheingauwall</a> was taken in hand, according to imperial order of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_II,_German_Emperor" title="Wilhelm II, German Emperor">Wilhelm II</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> the <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Rhineland" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupation of the Rhineland">French occupied Mainz</a> between 1919 and 1930 according to the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> which went into effect 28 June 1919. The <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a> (in which Mainz is located) was to be a demilitarized zone until 1935 and the French garrison, representing the <i><a href="/wiki/Triple_Entente" title="Triple Entente">Triple Entente</a></i>, was to stay until reparations were paid.</p>
<p>In 1923 Mainz participated in the Rhineland separatist movement that proclaimed a republic in the Rhineland. It collapsed in 1924. The French withdrew on 30 June 1930. <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> became chancellor of Germany in January, 1933 and his political opponents, especially those of the Social Democratic Party, were either incarcerated or murdered. Some were able to move away from Mainz in time. One was the political organizer for the SPD, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Kellner" title="Friedrich Kellner">Friedrich Kellner</a>, who went to Laubach, where as the chief justice inspector of the district court he continued his opposition against the Nazis by recording their misdeeds in a 900-page <a href="/wiki/My_Opposition" title="My Opposition">diary</a>.</p>
<p>In March, 1933, a detachment from the <a href="/wiki/National_Socialist_German_Workers_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="National Socialist German Workers Party">National Socialist Party</a> in <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a> brought the party to Mainz. They hoisted the <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastika</a> on all public buildings and began to denounce the Jewish population in the newspapers. In 1936 the forces of the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> reentered the Rhineland with a great fanfare, the first move of the Third Reich's meteoric expansion. The former Triple Entente took no action.</p>
<p>During World War II the citadel at Mainz hosted the <a href="/wiki/Oflag_XII-B" title="Oflag XII-B">Oflag XII-B</a> prisoner of war camp.</p>
<p>The Bishop of Mainz, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Stohr" title="Albert Stohr">Albert Stohr</a>, formed an organization to help Jews escape from Germany.</p>
<p>During World War II, more than <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Mainz_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Mainz in World War II">30 air raids</a> destroyed about 80 percent of the city's center, including most of the historic buildings. Mainz was captured on 22 March 1945 against uneven German resistance (staunch in some sectors and weak in other parts of the city) by the <a href="/wiki/90th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="90th Infantry Division (United States)">90th Infantry Division</a> under <a href="/wiki/William_A._McNulty" title="William A. McNulty">William A. McNulty</a>, a formation of the XII Corps under Third Army commanded by General <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">George S. Patton</a>, Jr.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Patton used the ancient strategic gateway through <i>Germania Superior</i> to cross the Rhine south of Mainz, drive down the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> towards <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> and end the possibility of a Bavarian redoubt crossing the Alps in Austria when the war ended.</p>
<p>From 1945 to 1949, the city was part of the French zone of occupation. When the federal state of <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a> was founded on 30 August 1946 by the commander of the French army on the French occupation zone <a href="/wiki/Marie_Pierre_K%C5%93nig" class="mw-redirect" title="Marie Pierre Kœnig">Marie Pierre Kœnig</a>, Mainz became capital of the new state.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1962, the diarist, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Kellner" title="Friedrich Kellner">Friedrich Kellner</a>, returned to spend his last years in Mainz. His life in Mainz, and the impact of his <a href="/wiki/My_Opposition" title="My Opposition">writings</a>, is the subject of the Canadian documentary <i><a href="/wiki/My_Opposition:_The_Diaries_of_Friedrich_Kellner" title="My Opposition: The Diaries of Friedrich Kellner">My Opposition: The Diaries of Friedrich Kellner</a></i>.</p>
<p>Following the withdrawal of French forces from Mainz, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Europe" title="United States Army Europe">United States Army Europe</a> occupied the military bases in Mainz. Today USAREUR only occupies McCulley Barracks in Wackernheim and the <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Sand_Dunes" title="Mainz Sand Dunes">Mainz Sand Dunes</a> for training area. Mainz is home to the headquarters of the <i>Bundeswehr'</i>s <i><a href="/wiki/Military_district#Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Military district">Wehrbereichskommando</a></i> II and other units.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cityscape">Cityscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cityscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The destruction caused by the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">bombing of Mainz during World War II</a> led to the largest building boom in the history of the town. During the last war in Germany, more than 30 air raids destroyed about 80 percent of the city's center, including most of the historic buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The destructive attack on the afternoon of 27 February 1945 remains the most destructive of all 33 bombings that Mainz has suffered in World War II in the collective memory of most of the population living then. The air raid caused most of the dead and made an already hard-hit city largely leveled.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rathaus_Mainz_Jockel-Fuchs-Platz.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Rathaus_Mainz_Jockel-Fuchs-Platz.jpg/220px-Rathaus_Mainz_Jockel-Fuchs-Platz.jpg" width="220" height="71" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Rathaus_Mainz_Jockel-Fuchs-Platz.jpg/330px-Rathaus_Mainz_Jockel-Fuchs-Platz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Rathaus_Mainz_Jockel-Fuchs-Platz.jpg/440px-Rathaus_Mainz_Jockel-Fuchs-Platz.jpg 2x" data-file-width="9204" data-file-height="2960" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rathaus_Mainz_Jockel-Fuchs-Platz.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Town Hall by Jacobsen</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Nevertheless, the post-war reconstruction took place very slowly. While cities such as Frankfurt had been rebuilt fast by a central authority, only individual efforts were initially successful in rebuilding Mainz. The reason for this was that the French wanted Mainz to expand and to become a model city. Mainz lay within the <a href="/wiki/Allied-occupied_Germany" title="Allied-occupied Germany">French-controlled sector of Germany</a> and it was a French architect and town-planner, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marcel_Lods&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marcel Lods (page does not exist)">Marcel Lods</a>, who produced a Le Corbusier-style plan of an ideal architecture.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> But the very first interest of the inhabitants was the restoration of housing areas. Even after the failure of the model city plans it was the initiative of the French (founding of the Johannes Gutenberg <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Mainz">University of Mainz</a>, elevation of Mainz to the state capital of Rhineland-Palatinate, the early resumption of the <a href="/wiki/Mainz_carnival" title="Mainz carnival">Mainz carnival</a>) driving the city in a positive development after the war. The City Plan of 1958 by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_May" title="Ernst May">Ernst May</a> allowed a regulated reconstruction for the first time. In 1950, the seat of the government of Rhineland-Palatinate had been transferred to the new Mainz and in 1963 the seat of the new ZDF, notable architects were Adolf Bayer, Richard Jörg and Egon Hartmann. At the time of the two-thousand-years-anniversary in 1962 the city was largely reconstructed. During the 1950s and 1960s the Oberstadt had been extended, Münchfeld and Lerchenberg added as suburbs, the Altstadttangente (<a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">intersection of the old town</a>), new <a href="/wiki/Neighbourhood" title="Neighbourhood">neighbourhoods</a> as Westring and Südring contributed to the extension. By 1970 there remained only a few ruins. The new town hall of Mainz had been designed by <a href="/wiki/Arne_Jacobsen" title="Arne Jacobsen">Arne Jacobsen</a> and finished by <a href="/wiki/Dissing%2BWeitling" title="Dissing+Weitling">Dissing+Weitling</a>. The town used Jacobsens activity for the Danish <a href="/wiki/Novo_Nordisk" title="Novo Nordisk">Novo</a> erecting a new office and warehouse building to contact him. The <a href="/wiki/Urban_renewal" title="Urban renewal">urban renewal</a> of the old town changed the inner city. In the framework of the preparation of the cathedrals millennium, <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_zone" title="Pedestrian zone">pedestrian zones</a> were developed around the cathedral, in northern direction to the Neubrunnenplatz and in southern direction across the Leichhof to the Augustinerstraße and Kirschgarten. The 1980s brought the renewal of the façades on the Markt and a new inner-city neighbourhood on the Kästrich. During the 1990s the Kisselberg between Gonsenheim and Bretzenheim, the "Fort Malakoff Center" at the site of the old police barracks, the renewal of the Main Station and the demolition of the first post-war shopping center at the Markt followed by the erection of a new historicising building at the same place.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city of Mainz is divided into 15 local districts according to the main statute of the city of Mainz. Each local district has a district administration of 13 members and a directly elected mayor, who is the chairman of the district administration. This local council decides on important issues affecting the local area, however, the final decision on new policies is made by the Mainz's municipal council.</p>
<p>In accordance with section 29 paragraph 2 Local Government Act of <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>, which refers to municipalities of more than 150,000 inhabitants, the city council has 60 members.</p>

<p>Until 1945, the districts of <a href="/wiki/Bischofsheim_(Mainspitze)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bischofsheim (Mainspitze)">Bischofsheim</a> (now an independent town), <a href="/wiki/Ginsheim-Gustavsburg" title="Ginsheim-Gustavsburg">Ginsheim-Gustavsburg</a> (which together are an independent town) belonged to Mainz. The former suburbs Amöneburg, Kastel, and Kostheim — (in short, <i>AKK</i>) are now administrated by the city of <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden" title="Wiesbaden">Wiesbaden</a> (on the north bank of the river). The AKK was separated from Mainz when the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> was designated the boundary between the French occupation zone (the later state of <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>) and the U.S. occupation zone (<a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>) in 1945.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mainz is home to a <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a>, the <i>Mainzer Fassenacht</i> or <i>Fastnacht</i>, which has developed since the early 19th century. Carnival in Mainz has its roots in the criticism of social and political injustices under the shelter of cap and bells. Today, the uniforms of many traditional Carnival clubs still imitate and caricature the uniforms of the French and Prussian troops of the past. The height of the carnival season is on <a href="/wiki/Rosenmontag" title="Rosenmontag">Rosenmontag</a> ("rose Monday"), when there is a large parade in Mainz, with more than 500,000 people celebrating in the streets.</p>
<p>The first ever <a href="/wiki/Katholikentag" title="Katholikentag">Katholikentag</a>, a festival-like gathering of German Catholics, was held in Mainz in 1848.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Uni-Mainz-Forum-Januar_2006.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Uni-Mainz-Forum-Januar_2006.jpg/220px-Uni-Mainz-Forum-Januar_2006.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Uni-Mainz-Forum-Januar_2006.jpg/330px-Uni-Mainz-Forum-Januar_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Uni-Mainz-Forum-Januar_2006.jpg/440px-Uni-Mainz-Forum-Januar_2006.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2304" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Uni-Mainz-Forum-Januar_2006.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Johannes Gutenberg</a>, credited with the invention of the modern <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> with movable type, was born here and died here. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Mainz">Mainz University</a>, which was refounded in 1946, is named after <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Gutenberg</a>; the earlier University of Mainz that dated back to 1477 had been closed down by Napoleon's troops in 1798.</p>
<p>Mainz was one of three important centers of <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> theology and learning in Central Europe during the Middle Ages. Known collectively as <i>Shum</i>, the cities of <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a> and Mainz played a key role in the preservation and propagation of Talmudic scholarship.</p>
<p>The city is the seat of Zweites Deutsches Fernsehen (literally, "Second German Television", <a href="/wiki/ZDF" title="ZDF">ZDF</a>), one of two federal nationwide TV broadcasters. There are also a couple of radio stations based in Mainz.</p>
<p>Other cultural aspects of the city include:</p>
<ul>
<li>As city in the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Region" title="Greater Region">Greater Region</a>, Mainz participated in the program of the year of <a href="/wiki/European_Capital_of_Culture" title="European Capital of Culture">European Capital of Culture</a> 2007.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Walk_of_Fame_of_Cabaret" title="Walk of Fame of Cabaret">Walk of Fame of Cabaret</a> may be found nearby the Schillerplatz.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Music_publisher_(popular_music)" title="Music publisher (popular music)">music publisher</a> <a href="/wiki/Schott_Music" title="Schott Music">Schott Music</a> is located in Mainz.</li>
<li>One of the oldest brass instrument manufacturers in the world, <a href="/wiki/Gebr._Alexander" title="Gebr. Alexander">Gebr. Alexander</a> is located in Mainz.</li>
<li>Fans of Gospel music enjoy the yearly performances of <a href="/wiki/Colours_of_Gospel" title="Colours of Gospel">Colours of Gospel</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The local football club <a href="/wiki/1._FSV_Mainz_05" title="1. FSV Mainz 05">1. FSV Mainz 05</a> has a long history in the German football leagues. Since 2004 it has competed in the <a href="/wiki/Fu%C3%9Fball-Bundesliga" class="mw-redirect" title="Fußball-Bundesliga">Fußball-Bundesliga</a> (First German soccer league) except a break in second level in 2007–2008 season. Mainz is closely associated with renowned coach <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Klopp" title="Jürgen Klopp">Jürgen Klopp</a>, who spent the vast majority of his playing career at the club and was also the manager for seven years, leading the club to Bundesliga football for the first time. After leaving Mainz Klopp went on to win two Bundesliga titles and reaching a <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">Champions League</a> final with <a href="/wiki/Borussia_Dortmund" title="Borussia Dortmund">Borussia Dortmund</a>. In the summer 2011 the club opened its new stadium called <a href="/wiki/Coface_Arena" class="mw-redirect" title="Coface Arena">Coface Arena</a>. Further relevant football clubs are <a href="/wiki/TSV_Schott_Mainz" title="TSV Schott Mainz">TSV Schott Mainz</a>, <a href="/wiki/SV_Gonsenheim" title="SV Gonsenheim">SV Gonsenheim</a>, Fontana Finthen, FC Fortuna Mombach and FVgg Mombach 03.</p>
<p>The local wrestling club ASV Mainz 1888 is currently in the top division of team wrestling in Germany, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga_(wrestling)" title="Bundesliga (wrestling)">Bundesliga</a>. In 1973, 1977 and 2012 the ASV Mainz 1888 won the German championship.</p>
<p>In 2007 the <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Athletics" title="Mainz Athletics">Mainz Athletics</a> won the <a href="/wiki/German_champions_(baseball)#German_Men.27s_Champions" title="German champions (baseball)">German Men's Championship in baseball</a>.</p>
<p>As a result of the 2008 invasion of Georgia by Russian troops, Mainz acted as a neutral venue for the Georgian Vs Republic of Ireland football game.</p>
<p>The biggest basketball club in the city is the ASC Theresianum Mainz. Its men's team is playing in the Regionalliga and its women's team is playing in the 2.DBBL.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USC_Mainz">USC Mainz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: USC Mainz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Universitäts-Sportclub Mainz</b> (University Sports Club Mainz) is a German sports club based in Mainz. It was founded on 9 September 1959<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> by Berno Wischmann primarily for students of the University of Mainz. It is considered one of the most powerful Athletics Sports clubs in Germany. 50 athletes of USC have distinguished themselves in a half-century in club history at Olympic Games, World and European Championships. In particular in the decathlon dominated USC athletes for decades: Already at the European Championships in Budapest in 1966 Mainz won three (Werner von Moltke, Jörg Mattheis and Horst Beyer) all decathlon medals. In the all-time list of the USC, there are nine athletes who have achieved more than 8,000 points - at the head of Siegfried Wentz (8762 points in 1983) and Guido Kratschmer (1980 world record with 8667 points). Most successful athlete of the association is more fighter, sprinter and long jumper Ingrid Becker (Olympic champion in 1968 in the pentathlon and Olympic champion in 1972 in the 4 × 100 Metres Relay and European champion in 1971 in the long jump). Most famous athletes of the present are the sprinter Marion Wagner (world champion in 2001 in the 4 × 100 Metres Relay) and the pole vaulters Carolin Hingst (Eighth of the 2008 Olympics in Beijing) and Anna Battke.</p>
<p>Three world titles adorn the balance of USC Mainz. For the discus thrower Lars Riedel attended (1991 and 1993) and the already mentioned sprinter Marion Wagner (2001). Added to 5 titles at the European Championships, a total of 65 international medals and 260 victories at the German Athletics Championships.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The players of USC's basketball section played from the season 1968/69 to the season 1974/75 in the National Basketball League (BBL) of the German Basketball Federation (DBB). As a finalist to winning the DBB Cup in 1971 USC Mainz played in the <a href="/wiki/1971%E2%80%9372_FIBA_European_Cup_Winner%27s_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="1971–72 FIBA European Cup Winner's Cup">1971–72 FIBA European Cup Winner's Cup</a> against the Italian Cup winners of <a href="/wiki/Partenope_Napoli" class="mw-redirect" title="Partenope Napoli">Fides Napoli</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainz_Athletics">Mainz Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Mainz Athletics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Athletics" title="Mainz Athletics">Mainz Athletics</a></div>
<p>The <b>Baseball and Softball Club Mainz Athletics</b> is a German baseball and softball club located in the city of Mainz in <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>. The Athletics is one of the largest clubs in the <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga_(baseball)" title="Bundesliga (baseball)">Baseball-Bundesliga Süd</a> in terms of membership, claiming to have hundreds of active players. The club has played in the Baseball-Bundesliga for more than two decades, and has won the German Championship in 2007 and 2016.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wine_centre">Wine centre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Wine centre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mainz is one of the centers of the <a href="/wiki/German_wine" title="German wine">German wine</a> economy<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> as a center for wine trade and the seat of the state's wine minister. Due to the importance and history of the wine industry for the federal state, Rhineland-Palatinate is the only state to have such a department.</p>
<p>Since 2008, the city is also member of the Great Wine Capitals Global Network (GWC), an association of well-known wineculture-cities of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Many wine traders also work in the town. The <a href="/wiki/Sparkling_wine" title="Sparkling wine">sparkling wine</a> producer Kupferberg produced in Mainz-Hechtsheim and even <a href="/wiki/Henkell" class="mw-redirect" title="Henkell">Henkell</a> — now located on the other side of the river Rhine — had been founded once in Mainz. The famous <a href="/wiki/Blue_Nun" title="Blue Nun">Blue Nun</a>, one of the first branded wines, had been marketed by the family Sichel.</p>
<p>Mainz had been a wine growing region since Roman times and the image of the wine town Mainz is fostered by the tourist center. The <i>Haus des Deutschen Weines</i> (English: House of German Wine), is located in beside the theater. The Mainzer Weinmarkt (wine market) is one of the great wine fairs in Germany.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_industries">Other industries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Other industries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Schott_AG" title="Schott AG">Schott AG</a>, one of the world's largest glass manufactures, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Werner_%26_Mertz" title="Werner &amp; Mertz">Werner &amp; Mertz</a>, a large chemical factory, are based in Mainz. Other companies such as <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/QUINN_group#QUINN_Plastics" class="mw-redirect" title="QUINN group">QUINN Plastics</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Novo_Nordisk" title="Novo Nordisk">Novo Nordisk</a> have their German administration in Mainz as well.</p>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann-Joseph_Krug&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johann-Joseph Krug (page does not exist)">Johann-Joseph Krug</a>, founder of France's famous <a href="/wiki/Champagne_Krug" title="Champagne Krug">Krug</a> champagne house in 1843, was born in Mainz in 1800.</p>

<p>Mainz is a major transport hub in southern Germany. It is an important component in European distribution, as it has the fifth largest inter-modal port in Germany. The <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Mainz" title="Port of Mainz">Port of Mainz</a>, now handling mainly containers, is a sizable industrial area to the north of the city, along the banks of the Rhine. In order to open up space along the city's riverfront for residential development, it was shifted further northwards in 2010.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mainz_Central_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Mainz Central Station">Mainz Central Station</a> or <i>Mainz Hauptbahnhof</i>, is frequented by 80,000 travelers and visitors each day and is therefore one of the busiest 21 stations in Germany. It is a stop for the <a href="/wiki/S-Bahn_Rhein-Main" class="mw-redirect" title="S-Bahn Rhein-Main">S-Bahn</a> line <a href="/wiki/S8_(Rhine-Main_S-Bahn)" title="S8 (Rhine-Main S-Bahn)">S8</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Main-Verkehrsverbund" title="Rhein-Main-Verkehrsverbund">Rhein-Main-Verkehrsverbund</a>. It is served by 440 daily local and regional trains (<a href="/wiki/StadtExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="StadtExpress">StadtExpress</a>, <a href="/wiki/RegionalExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="RegionalExpress">RE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regionalbahn" title="Regionalbahn">RB</a>) and 78 long-distance trains (<a href="/wiki/InterCity" title="InterCity">IC</a>, <a href="/wiki/EuroCity" title="EuroCity">EC</a> and <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">ICE</a>). Intercity-Express lines connect Mainz with Frankfurt (Main), <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Hbf" class="mw-redirect" title="Karlsruhe Hbf">Karlsruhe Hbf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worms_Hauptbahnhof" title="Worms Hauptbahnhof">Worms Hauptbahnhof</a> and <a href="/wiki/Koblenz_Hauptbahnhof" title="Koblenz Hauptbahnhof">Koblenz Hauptbahnhof</a>. It is a terminus of the <a href="/wiki/West_Rhine_Railway" title="West Rhine Railway">West Rhine Railway</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mainz%E2%80%93Ludwigshafen_railway" title="Mainz–Ludwigshafen railway">Mainz–Ludwigshafen railway</a>, as well as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alzey%E2%80%93Mainz_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alzey–Mainz Railway (page does not exist)">Alzey–Mainz Railway</a> erected by the <a href="/wiki/Hessische_Ludwigsbahn" class="mw-redirect" title="Hessische Ludwigsbahn">Hessische Ludwigsbahn</a> in 1871. Access to the <a href="/wiki/East_Rhine_Railway" title="East Rhine Railway">East Rhine Railway</a> is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Kaiserbr%C3%BCcke_(Mainz)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaiserbrücke (Mainz)">Kaiserbrücke</a>, a railway bridge across the Rhine at the north end of Mainz.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transportation">Public transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Public transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The station is an interchange point for the <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Mainz" title="Trams in Mainz">Mainz tramway network</a>, and an important bus junction for the city and region (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhein-Nahe-Nahverkehrsverbund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rhein-Nahe-Nahverkehrsverbund (page does not exist)">RNN</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnibusverkehr_Rhein-Nahe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Omnibusverkehr Rhein-Nahe (page does not exist)">ORN</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainzer_Verkehrsgesellschaft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mainzer Verkehrsgesellschaft (page does not exist)">MVG</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycling">Cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mainz offers a wide array of bicycle transportation facilities and events, including several miles of on-street bike lanes. The Rheinradweg (Rhine Cycle Route) is an international cycle route, running from the source to the mouth of the Rhine, traversing four countries at a distance of 1,300&#160;km (810&#160;mi). Another cycling tour runs towards Bingen and further to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Rhine" title="Middle Rhine">Middle Rhine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="List of World Heritage Sites in Europe">UNESCO World Heritage Site (2002)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_transportation">Air transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Air transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mainz is served by <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Airport" title="Frankfurt Airport">Frankfurt Airport</a>, the busiest airport by passenger traffic in Germany by far, the third busiest in Europe and the ninth busiest worldwide in 2009. Located about 10 miles (16 kilometres) east of Mainz, it is connected to the city by an <a href="/wiki/S8_(Rhine-Main_S-Bahn)" title="S8 (Rhine-Main S-Bahn)">S-Bahn line</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-air_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-air-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The small <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Finthen_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Mainz Finthen Airport">Mainz Finthen Airport</a>, located just 3 miles (5&#160;km) southwest of Mainz, is used by <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> only. Another airport, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt-Hahn_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt-Hahn Airport">Frankfurt-Hahn Airport</a> located about 50 miles (80&#160;km) west of Mainz, is served by a few <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">low-cost carriers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-air_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-air-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_names">Alternative names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Alternative names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mainz has a number of <a href="/wiki/Names_of_European_cities_in_different_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Names of European cities in different languages">different names</a> in other languages and dialects. In <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> it is known as <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Mogontiacum</span></i> or <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Moguntiacum</span></i> and, in the local <a href="/wiki/West_Middle_German" class="mw-redirect" title="West Middle German">West Middle German</a> dialect, it is <i>Määnz</i> or <i>Meenz</i>. It is known as <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">Mayence</span></i> in French, <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it">Magonza</span></i> in Italian, <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">Maguncia</span></i> in Spanish, <i><span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">Mogúncia</span></i> in Portuguese, <i><span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">Moguncja</span></i> in Polish, <i>Magentza</i> (<span lang="yi" xml:lang="yi">מגנצא</span>) in Yiddish, and <i><span lang="cs" xml:lang="cs">Mohuč</span></i> in Czech and Slovakian. Its former English name <a href="/wiki/Mentz_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Mentz (disambiguation)">Mentz</a> is shared by two American cities named in its honor.</p>

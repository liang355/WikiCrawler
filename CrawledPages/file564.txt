			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Boston Brahmin</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Boston Brahmin</b> was a term understood to mean the (contraband) shipping moghuls who capitalised on abolition of slavery and abolition of opium trade in British Domains by moving trade outside of British jurisdiction. In the 20th and 21st Centuries, the term refers to members of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>'s traditional <a href="/wiki/Upper_class" title="Upper class">upper class</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PBS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-1">[1]</a></sup> They form an integral part of the historic core of the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Establishment" title="The Establishment">establishment</a>, along with other wealthy families of <a href="/wiki/Old_Philadelphians" title="Old Philadelphians">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, Virginia and Charleston.<sup id="cite_ref-Burt_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burt-2">[2]</a></sup> They are often associated with the distinctive <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_accent#Elite_use" title="Mid-Atlantic accent">Boston Brahmin accent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, and traditional Anglo-American customs and clothing. Descendants of early English colonists, such as those who came to America on the <i><a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a></i> or the <i><a href="/wiki/Arbella" title="Arbella">Arbella</a></i>, are often considered to be the most representative of the Boston Brahmins.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term was coined by the physician and writer <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Wendell_Holmes,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr.">Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr.</a>, in an 1860 article in the <i><a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Monthly" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Monthly">Atlantic Monthly</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The term <i><a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">Brahmin</a></i> refers to the highest ranking caste of people in the traditional <a href="/wiki/Caste_system_in_India" title="Caste system in India">caste system in India</a>. In the United States, it has been applied to the old, wealthy <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> families of British <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> origin which were influential in the development of American institutions and culture.</p>
<p>The term effectively underscores the strong conviction of the New England gentry that they were a people set apart by destiny to guide the American experiment as their ancestors had played a leading role in founding it. The term also illustrates the erudite and exclusive nature of the New England gentry as perceived by outsiders, and may also refer to their interest in Eastern religions, fostered perhaps by the impact in the 19th century of the <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Transcendentalist">transcendentalist</a> writings of New England literary icons such as <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">Walt Whitman</a>, and the enlightened appeal of <a href="/wiki/Universalist_Unitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Universalist Unitarian">Universalist Unitarian</a> movements of the same period.</p>

<p>The nature of the Brahmins is hinted at by the doggerel "Boston Toast" by <a href="/wiki/College_of_the_Holy_Cross" title="College of the Holy Cross">Holy Cross</a> alumnus <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Collins_Bossidy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Collins Bossidy (page does not exist)">John Collins Bossidy</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>And this is good old Boston,</dd>
<dd>The home of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_baked_beans" title="Boston baked beans">bean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Cod" title="Sacred Cod">cod</a>,</dd>
<dd>Where the <a href="/wiki/Lowell_family" title="Lowell family">Lowells</a> talk only to <a href="/wiki/Cabot_family" title="Cabot family">Cabots</a>,</dd>
<dd>And the Cabots talk only to God.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>While some 19th-century Brahmin families of large fortune were of bourgeois origin, still fewer were of a somewhat aristocratic origin. The new families were often the first to seek, in typically British fashion, suitable marriage alliances with those old aristocratic New England families that were descended from landowners in England to elevate and cement their social standing. The Winthrops, Dudleys, Saltonstalls, Winslows, and Lymans (descended from English magistrates, gentry, and aristocracy) were, by and large, happy with this arrangement. All of Boston's "Brahmin elite", therefore, maintained the received culture of the old English gentry, including cultivating the personal excellence that they imagined maintained the distinction between gentlemen and freemen, and between ladies and women. They saw it as their duty to maintain what they defined as high standards of excellence, duty, and restraint. Cultivated, urbane, and dignified, a Boston Brahmin was supposed to be the very essence of enlightened <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">aristocracy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The ideal Brahmin was not only wealthy, but displayed what was considered suitable personal virtues and character traits.</p>
<p>The Brahmin was expected to maintain the customary English reserve in his dress, manner, and deportment, cultivate the arts, support charities such as hospitals and colleges, and assume the role of community leader.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:14</sup> Although the ideal called on him to transcend commonplace business values, in practice many found the thrill of economic success quite attractive. The Brahmins warned each other against <a href="/wiki/Avarice" class="mw-redirect" title="Avarice">avarice</a> and insisted upon personal responsibility. Scandal and divorce were unacceptable. The total system was buttressed by the strong extended family ties present in Boston society. Young men attended the same prep schools, colleges, and private clubs,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and heirs married heiresses. Family not only served as an economic asset, but also as a means of moral restraint. Most belong to the <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopal</a> churches, although some were <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregationalists</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodists</a>. Politically they were successively <a href="/wiki/Federalist_Party" title="Federalist Party">Federalists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whig_Party_(United_States)" title="Whig Party (United States)">Whigs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republicans</a>. They were marked by their manners and once distinctive elocution, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Brahmin_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Brahmin accent">Boston Brahmin accent</a>, a version of the New England accent. Their distinctive Anglo-American manner of dress has been much imitated and is the foundation of the style now informally known as <a href="/wiki/Preppy" title="Preppy">preppy</a>. Many of the Brahmin families trace their ancestry back to the original 17th- and 18th-century colonial ruling class consisting of Massachusetts governors and magistrates, Harvard presidents, distinguished clergy and fellows of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Society of London">Royal Society of London</a> (a leading scientific body), while others entered New England aristocratic society during the 19th century with their profits from commerce and trade, often marrying into established Brahmin families.</p>

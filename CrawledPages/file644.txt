<p><b>Petersham</b> (pronounced "Peter Sam") is a town in <a href="/wiki/Worcester_County,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester County, Massachusetts">Worcester County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The population was 1,234 at the 2010 census. Petersham is home to a considerable amount of conservation land, including the <a href="/wiki/Quabbin_Reservoir" title="Quabbin Reservoir">Quabbin Reservation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Forest" title="Harvard Forest">Harvard Forest</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Swift_River_Reservation" title="Swift River Reservation">Swift River Reservation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Federated_Women%27s_Club_State_Forest" title="Federated Women's Club State Forest">Federated Women's Club State Forest</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petersham,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Petersham was first settled in 1733 and was officially incorporated in 1754. On February 4, 1787, it was the site of the second battle of <a href="/wiki/Shays%27_Rebellion" title="Shays' Rebellion">Shays' Rebellion</a>. The town is noted for its <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Petersham_Common_Historic_District" title="Petersham Common Historic District">Petersham Common Historic District</a>. About 45 buildings are listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>. The Country Store, an 1842 Greek Revival structure that has housed a general store on its main floor since its opening, sits just to the East of the common.</p>
<p>The town's lands were expanded greatly by the building of the <a href="/wiki/Quabbin_Reservoir" title="Quabbin Reservoir">Quabbin Reservoir</a> in 1938. When the towns of the <a href="/wiki/Quabbin_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Quabbin Valley">Swift River Valley</a> were disincorporated, Petersham and neighboring <a href="/wiki/New_Salem,_Massachusetts" title="New Salem, Massachusetts">New Salem</a> benefited the most, with Petersham receiving all of the former town of <a href="/wiki/Dana,_Massachusetts" title="Dana, Massachusetts">Dana</a>, much of the town of <a href="/wiki/Greenwich,_Massachusetts" title="Greenwich, Massachusetts">Greenwich</a>, and a small portion of the former town of <a href="/wiki/Prescott,_Massachusetts" title="Prescott, Massachusetts">Prescott</a> east of the Middle Branch of the Swift River. Its modern southwestern borders lie along the former East and Middle branches of the Swift River, and includes lands that were once part of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_Massachusetts" title="Hampshire County, Massachusetts">Hampshire County</a>.</p>
<p>A memorial was erected in the town in 1927 by the New England Society of Brooklyn, New York. The memorial commemorates General Benjamin Lincoln, who raised 3,000 troops and routed the rebellion on February 4, 1787. It ends with the line, "Obedience to the law is true liberty."<sup id="cite_ref-Peet_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peet-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_transportation">Geography and transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petersham,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography and transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the town has a total area of 68.3 square miles (177&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), of which 54.2 square miles (140&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 14.1 square miles (37&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) (20.60%) is water. By virtue of the lands it gained in the construction of the Quabbin Reservoir, Petersham is the largest town by land area in Worcester County, and the fifth-largest town of the 351 cities and towns in the Commonwealth. It is the largest town by area in Central and <a href="/wiki/Western_Massachusetts" title="Western Massachusetts">Western Massachusetts</a>, with the four largest towns being in southeastern Massachusetts (<a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Massachusetts" title="Plymouth, Massachusetts">Plymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middleborough,_Massachusetts" title="Middleborough, Massachusetts">Middleborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth,_Massachusetts" title="Dartmouth, Massachusetts">Dartmouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barnstable,_Massachusetts" title="Barnstable, Massachusetts">Barnstable</a>).</p>
<p>Much of the town's land is protected as part of the Quabbin Reservation, a protected area surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Quabbin_Reservoir" title="Quabbin Reservoir">Quabbin Reservoir</a>. The town's borders extend well into the eastern branch of the reservoir, and includes the lands around what were once Mount Pomeroy, Mount Zion (the largest such "island" in the reservoir), and several other former hills. The lands of the Reservation are only accessible on foot, and include the only disincorporated areas which are accessible to the public, at the former Dana Common, a 1.5 miles (2.4&#160;km) walk from Gate 40. Most of the town still drains into the Quabbin, along brooks that once met the Swift River. The town has several other hills that are part of the larger Worcester Hills region, including Whitney Hill and Camels Hump Hill, at 1,044 feet (318&#160;m) the highest point in town. Several other parts of the town are also protected, including the Federated Women's Club State Forest, the Petersham State Forest, Harvard state Forest, the Popple Camp Wildlife Management Area, and the Phillipston Wildlife Management Area, the latter three extending into neighboring towns. Most of the town is rural in nature, with the largest population located near the town common.</p>
<p>Petersham, by virtue of its territory in the Quabbin, is the westernmost town in Worcester County, bordering <a href="/wiki/Franklin_County,_Massachusetts" title="Franklin County, Massachusetts">Franklin County</a> to the west and a small portion of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_Massachusetts" title="Hampshire County, Massachusetts">Hampshire County</a> to the south. Petersham is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Athol,_Massachusetts" title="Athol, Massachusetts">Athol</a> to the northwest, <a href="/wiki/Phillipston,_Massachusetts" title="Phillipston, Massachusetts">Phillipston</a> to the northeast, <a href="/wiki/Barre,_Massachusetts" title="Barre, Massachusetts">Barre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hardwick,_Massachusetts" title="Hardwick, Massachusetts">Hardwick</a> to the southeast, <a href="/wiki/Ware,_Massachusetts" title="Ware, Massachusetts">Ware</a> to the south, and <a href="/wiki/New_Salem,_Massachusetts" title="New Salem, Massachusetts">New Salem</a> to the west. From the town common, Petersham is 29 miles (47&#160;km) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Worcester,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester, Massachusetts">Worcester</a>, 41 miles (66&#160;km) north-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Springfield,_Massachusetts" title="Springfield, Massachusetts">Springfield</a>, and 66 miles (106&#160;km) west-northwest of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>.</p>
<p>There are no interstates or other limited-access highways within town, with the nearest being <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_2" title="Massachusetts Route 2">Route 2</a>, the major east-west route across northern Massachusetts, which lies just north of the town. Near the center of town lies the junction of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_32" title="Massachusetts Route 32">Route 32</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_122" title="Massachusetts Route 122">Route 122</a>, with Route 32 entering from Athol and Route 122 entering from New Salem. The routes pass concurrently into the town of Barre before splitting again in the southern part of that town. The town center is also the northern terminus of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_32A" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts Route 32A">Route 32A</a>, which heads southward through Hardwick, providing a more direct route from where Route 32 turns eastward in the Hardwick village of Gilbertville to the Petersham town center. Just north of the town center, the southern terminus of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_101" title="Massachusetts Route 101">Route 101</a> lies along Route 32, heading into Phillipston along its route towards the New Hampshire border.</p>
<p>There are no means of mass transit in Petersham. The nearest general aviation airports are the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Municipal_Airport" title="Orange Municipal Airport">Orange Municipal Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gardner_Municipal_Airport_(Massachusetts)" title="Gardner Municipal Airport (Massachusetts)">Gardner Municipal Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tanner-Hiller_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Tanner-Hiller Airport">Tanner-Hiller Airport</a> in Barre. The nearest national air service can be reached at <a href="/wiki/Bradley_International_Airport" title="Bradley International Airport">Bradley International Airport</a> in Connecticut.</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup id="cite_ref-GR2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-12">[12]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 1,180 people, 438 households, and 299 families residing in the town. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 21.8 people per square mile (8.4/km²). There were 474 housing units at an average density of 8.7 per square mile (3.4/km²). The racial makeup of the town was 97.20% <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 0.68% <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.76% <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.25% <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.08% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 1.02% from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 1.10% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 438 households out of which 29.5% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 59.6% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 5.5% had a female householder with no husband present, and 31.7% were non-families. 27.2% of all households were made up of individuals and 11.4% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.48 and the average family size was 3.04.</p>
<p>In the town, the population was spread out with 22.4% under the age of 18, 4.3% from 18 to 24, 27.0% from 25 to 44, 28.7% from 45 to 64, and 17.5% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 43 years. For every 100 females there were 101.4 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 95.7 males.</p>
<p>The median income for a household in the town was $47,833, and the median income for a family was $58,125. Males had a median income of $39,265 versus $26,354 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the town was $24,222. About 2.0% of families and 5.8% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 4.2% of those under age 18 and 1.5% of those age 65 or over.</p>

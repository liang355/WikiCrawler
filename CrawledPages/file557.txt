<p><b>John Harvard</b> (1607–1638) was an English minister in America, "a godly gentleman and a lover of learning",<sup id="cite_ref-morison2_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morison2-1">[1]</a></sup> whose deathbed<sup id="cite_ref-HMagJanFeb2000_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HMagJanFeb2000-2">[2]</a></sup> bequest to the "schoale or Colledge" founded two years earlier by the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony" title="Massachusetts Bay Colony">Massachusetts Bay Colony</a> was so gratefully received that it was consequently ordered "that the Colledge agreed upon formerly to bee built at Cambridg shalbee called <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard Colledge</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-charter_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charter-3">[3]</a></sup> The institution considers him the most honored of its founders&#160;– those whose efforts and contributions in its early days "ensure[d] its permanence."</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/John_Harvard_(statue)" title="John Harvard (statue)">statue in his honor</a> is a prominent feature of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Yard" title="Harvard Yard">Harvard Yard</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(clergyman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(clergyman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harvard was born and raised in <a href="/wiki/Southwark" title="Southwark">Southwark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, England, (now part of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>), the fourth of nine children of Robert Harvard (1562–1625), a butcher and tavern owner, and his wife Katherine Rogers (1584–1635), a native of <a href="/wiki/Stratford-upon-Avon" title="Stratford-upon-Avon">Stratford-upon-Avon</a> whose father, Thomas Rogers (1540–1611), was an associate of <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare's</a> father (both served on the borough corporation's council). Harvard was baptised in the parish church of St Saviour's (now <a href="/wiki/Southwark_Cathedral" title="Southwark Cathedral">Southwark Cathedral</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-guide_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-4">[4]</a></sup> and attended <a href="/wiki/St_Saviour%27s_Grammar_School" title="St Saviour's Grammar School">St Saviour's Grammar School</a>, where his father was a member of the governing body and a warden of the Parish Church.</p>
<p>In 1625, <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a> reduced the immediate family to only John, his brother Thomas, and their mother. Katherine was soon remarried‍—‌firstly in 1626 to John Elletson (1580–1626), who died within a few months, then (1627) to <a href="/wiki/Richard_Yarward" title="Richard Yarward">Richard Yearwood</a> (1580–1632). She died in 1635, Thomas in 1637.</p>
<p>Left with some property, Harvard's mother was able to send him to <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_College,_Cambridge" title="Emmanuel College, Cambridge">Emmanuel College, Cambridge</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-acab_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acab-5">[5]</a></sup> where he earned his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> in 1632<sup id="cite_ref-acad_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acad-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">M.A.</a> in 1635 and was subsequently ordained a <a href="/wiki/Dissenter" title="Dissenter">dissenting minister</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-emma_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emma-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_career">Marriage and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(clergyman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1636, Harvard married Ann Sadler (1614–55) of <a href="/wiki/Ringmer" title="Ringmer">Ringmer</a>, sister of his college classmate <a href="/wiki/John_Sadler_(town_clerk)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Sadler (town clerk)">John Sadler</a>'s, at St Michael the Archangel Church, in the parish of South Malling, Lewes, East Sussex.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the spring or summer of 1637, the couple emigrated to <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, where Harvard became a <a href="/wiki/Freeman_(Colonial)" title="Freeman (Colonial)">freeman</a> of Massachusetts and,<sup id="cite_ref-acab_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acab-5">[5]</a></sup> settling in <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Boston" title="Charlestown, Boston">Charlestown</a>, a teaching elder of the First Church there<sup id="cite_ref-melnick_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-melnick-8">[8]</a></sup> and an assistant preacher.<sup id="cite_ref-emma_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emma-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1638, a tract of land was deeded<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to him there, and he was appointed that same year to a committee "to consider of some things tending toward a body of laws."<sup id="cite_ref-acab_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acab-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>He built his house on Country Road (later Market Street and now Main Street), next to Gravel Lane, a site that is now Harvard Mall. Harvard's orchard extended up the hill behind his house.<sup id="cite_ref-charlestown_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charlestown-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(clergyman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 14 September 1638, Harvard died of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> and was buried at Charlestown's <a href="/wiki/Phipps_Street_Burying_Ground" title="Phipps Street Burying Ground">Phipps Street Burying Ground</a>.</p>
<p>In 1828, Harvard University alumni erected a granite monument to his memory there,<sup id="cite_ref-acab_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acab-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Everett_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Everett-10">[10]</a></sup> his original stone having disappeared during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-melnick_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-melnick-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Two years before Harvard's death the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_General_Court" title="Massachusetts General Court">Great and General Court of the Massachu­setts Bay Colony</a>‍—‌desiring to "advance learning and perpetuate it to posterity: dreading to leave an illiterate ministry to the churches, when our present ministers shall lie in the dust"‍—‌appropriated <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>400 toward a "schoale or colledge"<sup id="cite_ref-charter_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charter-3">[3]</a></sup> at what was then called Newtowne.<sup id="cite_ref-fruits_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fruits-11">[11]</a></sup> In an oral will spoken to his wife<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1984_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1984-12">[12]</a></sup> the childless Harvard, who had inherited considerable sums from his father, mother, and brother,<sup id="cite_ref-grads_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grads-13">[13]</a></sup> bequeathed to the school <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>780‍—‌half of his monetary estate‍—‌with the remainder to his wife;<sup id="cite_ref-guide_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-4">[4]</a></sup> perhaps more importantly<sup id="cite_ref-potter1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-potter1-14">[14]</a></sup> he also gave his scholar's library comprising some 329 titles (totaling 400 volumes, some titles being multivolume works).<sup id="cite_ref-potter2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-potter2-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap">:192</sup> In gratitude, it was subsequently ordered "that the Colledge agreed upon formerly to bee built at Cambridg shalbee called <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard Colledge</a>."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-charter_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charter-3">[3]</a></sup> (Even before Harvard's death, Newtowne had been renamed<sup id="cite_ref-charter_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charter-3">[3]</a></sup> Cambridge, after the English <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">university</a> attended by many early colonists, including Harvard himself.)<sup id="cite_ref-degler_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-degler-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Founding_&quot;myth&quot;"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_.22myth.22">Founding "myth"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(clergyman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Founding &quot;myth&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Smartass" tourguides<sup id="cite_ref-ST_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ST-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-toes_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toes-18">[18]</a></sup> and the Harvard College undergraduate newspaper<sup id="cite_ref-crime1934_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crime1934-19">[19]</a></sup> commonly assert that John Harvard does not merit the honorific <i>founder</i>, because the Colony's vote had come two years prior to Harvard's bequest. But as detailed in a 1934 letter by the secretary of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Corporation">Harvard Corporation</a>, the founding of Harvard College was not the act of one but the work of many; John Harvard is therefore consid­ered not <i>the</i> founder, but rather <i>a</i><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>founder,<sup id="cite_ref-morison1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morison1-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mather_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mather-21">[21]</a></sup> of the school‍—‌though the timeliness and generosity of his contribu­tion have made him the most honored of these:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The quibble over the question whether John Harvard was entitled to be called the Founder of Harvard College seems to me one of the least profitable. The destruc­tion of myths is a legiti­mate sport, but its only justifica­tion is the establish­ment of truth in place of error.</p>
<p>If the founding of a universi­ty must be dated to a split second of time, then the founding of Harvard should perhaps be fixed by the fall of the presi­dent's gavel in announc­ing the passage of the vote of October 28, 1636. But if the founding is to be regarded as a process rather than as a single event [then John Harvard, by virtue of his bequest "at the very threshold of the College's existence and going further than any other contribu­tion made up to that time to ensure its permanence"] is clearly entitled to be consid­ered a founder. The General Court<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... acknowl­edged the fact by bestowing his name on the College. This was almost two years before the first President took office and four years before the first students were graduated.</p>
<p>These are all familiar facts and it is well that they should be understood by the sons of Harvard. There is no myth to be destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-greene_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greene-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials_and_tributes">Memorials and tributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(clergyman)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Memorials and tributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A statue in Harvard's honor—not, however, a <i>likeness</i> of him, there being nothing to indicate what he had looked like<sup id="cite_ref-emma_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emma-7">[7]</a></sup>—is a prominent feature of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Yard" title="Harvard Yard">Harvard Yard</a> (see <i><a href="/wiki/John_Harvard_(statue)" title="John Harvard (statue)">John Harvard statue</a></i>) and was featured on a 1986 stamp, part of the United States Postal Service's <a href="/wiki/Great_Americans_series" title="Great Americans series">Great Americans series</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stamp_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-23">[23]</a></sup> A figure representing him also appears in a stained-glass window in the chapel of <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_College,_Cambridge" title="Emmanuel College, Cambridge">Emmanuel College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-emma_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emma-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-acab_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acab-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/John_Harvard_Library" title="John Harvard Library">John Harvard Library</a> in Southwark, London, is named in Harvard's honor, as is the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Bridge" title="Harvard Bridge">Harvard Bridge</a> that connects Boston to Cambridge.<sup id="cite_ref-alger_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alger-24">[24]</a></sup> There is a memorial window in his honor in <a href="/wiki/Southwark_Cathedral" title="Southwark Cathedral">Southwark Cathedral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

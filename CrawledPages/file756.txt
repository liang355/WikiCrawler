<p><b>Squash</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Ball_game" title="Ball game">ball sport</a> played by two (singles) or four players (doubles) in a four-walled court with a small, hollow rubber ball. The players must alternate in striking the ball with their racket and hit the ball onto the playable surfaces of the four walls of the court.</p>
<p>The game was formerly called <b>squash rackets</b>, a reference to the "squashable" soft ball used in the game (compared with the harder ball used in its sister game <a href="/wiki/Rackets_(sport)" title="Rackets (sport)">rackets</a>).</p>
<p>The governing body of Squash, the <a href="/wiki/World_Squash_Federation" title="World Squash Federation">World Squash Federation</a> (WSF) is recognised by the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC), but the sport is not part of the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a>, despite a number of applications. Supporters continue to lobby for its incorporation in a future <a href="/wiki/Olympic_sports" title="Olympic sports">Olympic program</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of stringed rackets is shared with <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, which dates from the late sixteenth century, though is more directly descended from the game of <a href="/wiki/Rackets_(sport)" title="Rackets (sport)">rackets</a> from England. In "rackets", instead of hitting over a net as in sports such as tennis, players hit a squeezable ball against walls.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nanoldsquash2.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Nanoldsquash2.JPG/220px-Nanoldsquash2.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Nanoldsquash2.JPG/330px-Nanoldsquash2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Nanoldsquash2.JPG/440px-Nanoldsquash2.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2112" data-file-height="2816" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nanoldsquash2.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Old and new style squash rackets</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Squash was invented in <a href="/wiki/Harrow_School" title="Harrow School">Harrow School</a> out of the older game rackets around 1830 before the game spread to other schools, eventually becoming an international sport. The first courts built at this school were rather dangerous because they were near water pipes, buttresses, chimneys, and ledges. The school soon built four outside courts. <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">Natural rubber</a> was the material of choice for the ball. Students modified their rackets to have a smaller reach to play in these cramped conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-zug_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zug-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rackets have changed in a similar way to those used in tennis. Squash rackets used to be made out of laminated timber.<sup id="cite_ref-GRAYS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRAYS-2">[2]</a></sup> In the 1980s, construction shifted to lighter materials (such as aluminium and graphite) with small additions of components like Kevlar, boron and titanium. Natural "gut" strings were also replaced with synthetic strings.<sup id="cite_ref-GRAYS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRAYS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century the game increased in popularity with various schools, clubs and even private citizens building squash courts, but with no set dimensions. The first squash court in North America appeared at <a href="/wiki/St._Paul%27s_School_(Concord,_New_Hampshire)" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paul's School (Concord, New Hampshire)">St. Paul's School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Concord,_New_Hampshire" title="Concord, New Hampshire">Concord</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> in 1884. In 1904 in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, the earliest national association of squash in the world was formed as the United States Squash rackets Association, (USSRA), now known as <a href="/wiki/U.S._Squash" title="U.S. Squash">U.S. Squash</a>. In April 1907 the Tennis, rackets &amp; Fives Association set up a sub committee to set standards for squash. Then the sport soon formed, combining the three sports together called “Squash”. In 1912, the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a> had a squash court in <a href="/wiki/First_class_facilities_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="First class facilities of the RMS Titanic">first class</a>. The 1st-Class Squash Court was situated on G-Deck and the Spectators Viewing Gallery was on the deck above on F-Deck. To use the Court cost 50 cents in 1912. Passengers could use the court for 1 hour unless others were waiting. It was not until 1923 that the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Automobile_Club" title="Royal Automobile Club">Royal Automobile Club</a> hosted a meeting to further discuss the rules and regulations and another five years elapsed before the Squash rackets Association was formed to set standards for squash in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-zug_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zug-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_equipment">Playing equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Playing equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Standard <a href="/wiki/Racket_(sports_equipment)" title="Racket (sports equipment)">rackets</a> are governed by the rules of the game. Traditionally they were made of laminated wood (typically ash), with a small strung area using natural <a href="/wiki/Catgut" title="Catgut">gut</a> <a href="/wiki/Strings_(tennis)" title="Strings (tennis)">strings</a>. After a rule change in the mid-1980s, they are now almost always made of <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite materials</a> or metals (<a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a>, titanium, boron) with synthetic strings. Modern rackets have maximum dimensions of 686&#160;mm (27.0&#160;in) long and 215&#160;mm (8.5&#160;in) wide, with a maximum strung area of 500 square centimetres (77.5&#160;sq&#160;in). The permitted maximum weight is 255 grams (9.0&#160;oz), but most have a weight between 90 and 150&#160;grams (3–5.3&#160;oz.).</p>
<p>Squash balls are between 39.5 and 40.5&#160;mm in diameter, and have a weight of 23 to 25&#160;grams.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They are made with two pieces of rubber compound, glued together to form a hollow sphere and buffed to a matte finish. Different balls are provided for varying temperature and atmospheric conditions and standards of play: more experienced players use slow balls that have less bounce than those used by less experienced players (slower balls tend to "die" in court corners, rather than "standing up" to allow easier shots). Depending on its specific rubber composition, a squash ball has the property that it bounces more at higher temperatures. Squash balls must be hit dozens of times to warm them up at the beginning of a session; cold squash balls have very little bounce. <p>Some ball manufacturers such as Dunlop use a different method of grading balls based on experience. They still have the equivalent dot rating, but are named to help choose a ball that is appropriate for one's skill level. The four different ball types are Intro (Blue dot, 140% of Pro bounce), Progress (Red dot, 120% of Pro bounce), Competition (single yellow dot, 110% of Pro bounce) and Pro (double yellow dot).</p>
<p>The "double-yellow dot" ball, introduced in 2000, is the competition standard, replacing the earlier "yellow-dot" ball. There is also an "orange dot" ball for use at high altitudes.</p>
<p>Players wear comfortable sports clothing. In competition, men usually wear shorts and a T-shirt, tank top or a polo shirt. Women normally wear a skirt or <a href="/wiki/Skort" title="Skort">skort</a> and a T-shirt or a tank top, or a sports dress. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> recommends wearing goggles with polycarbonate lenses.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many squash venues mandate the use of eye protection and some association rules require that all juniors and doubles players must wear eye protection.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_court">The court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The squash court is a playing surface surrounded by four walls. The court surface contains a front line separating the front and back of the court and a half court line, separating the left and right hand sides of the back portion of the court, creating three 'boxes': the front half, the back left quarter and the back right quarter. Both the back two boxes contain smaller service boxes. The floor-markings on a squash court are only relevant during serves. The dimensions of this entire surface is</p>
<p><b>Squash Court Length:</b> 9750&#160;mm plus or minus 10&#160;mm <b>Squash Court Width:</b> 6400&#160;mm plus or minus 10&#160;mm <b>Squash Court Height:</b> 5640&#160;mm</p>
<p><b>Squash Court Diagonals:</b> 11665&#160;mm plus or minus 25&#160;mm.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are four walls to a squash court. The front wall, on which three parallel lines are marked, has the largest playing surface, whilst the back wall, which typically contains the entrance to the court, has the smallest. The out line runs along the top of the front wall, descending along the side walls to the back wall. There are no other markings on the side or back walls. Shots struck above or touching the out line, on any wall, are out. The bottom line of the front wall marks the top of the 'tin', a half metre-high metal area which if struck means that the ball is out. In this way the tin can be seen as analogous to the net in other racket sports such as <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>. The middle line of the front wall is the service line and is only relevant during serves.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_play">Game play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Game play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service">Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The players spin a racket to decide who serves first. This player starts the first rally by electing to serve from either the left or right service box. For a legal serve, one of the server's feet must be in the service box, not touching any part of the service box lines, as the player strikes the ball. After being struck by the racket, the ball must strike the front wall above the service line and below the out line and land in the opposite back quarter court. The receiving player can choose to <a href="/wiki/Squash_(sport)#Types_of_shots_played" title="Squash (sport)">volley</a> a serve after it has hit the front wall. If the server wins the point, the two players switch sides for the following point.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Play">Play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the serve, the players take turns hitting the ball against the front wall, above the tin and below the out line. The ball may strike the side or back walls at any time, as long as it hits below the out line. It must not hit the floor after hitting the racket and before hitting the front wall. A ball landing on either the out line or the line along the top of the tin is considered to be out. After the ball hits the front wall, it is allowed to bounce once on the floor (and any number of times against the side or back walls) before a player must return it. Players may move anywhere around the court but accidental or deliberate obstruction of the other player's movements is forbidden and could result in a let or a stroke. Players typically return to the centre of the court after making a shot.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scoring_systems">Scoring systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scoring systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Squash scoring systems have evolved over time. One unusual system consists of sets of 21 points. If ever both players are on 20-20, then the game continues until there is 2 points difference between them. Players can decide how many sets they want to do. This scoring system is called the "Florian's System". However, in recent times with the popularization of squash as an international sport, the Professional Squash Association (<a href="/wiki/Professional_Squash_Association" title="Professional Squash Association">PSA</a>) standard match consists of a best-of-5 sets with each set being decided by the first player to reach 11 points (keeping in mind the 2 point differential as cited above).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="English_scoring">English scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: English scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The original scoring system is known as English scoring, also called hand-out scoring. Under this system, if the server wins a rally, they receive a point, while if the returner wins rally, only the service changes (i.e., the ball goes "hand-out") and no point is given. The first player to reach 9 points wins the game. However, if the score reaches 8–8, the player who was first to reach 8 decides whether the game will be played to 9, as before (called "set one"), or to 10 (called "set two"). At one time this scoring system was preferred in Britain, and also among countries with traditional British ties, such as Australia, Canada, Pakistan, South Africa, India and Sri Lanka.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Point-a-rally_scoring">Point-a-rally scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Point-a-rally scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The current official scoring system for all levels of professional and amateur squash is called point-a-rally scoring (PARS). In PARS, the winner of a rally always receives a point, regardless of whether they were the server or returner. Games are played to 11, but in contrast to English scoring, players must win by two clear points. That is, if the score reaches 10–10, play continues until one player wins by two points. PARS to 11 is now used on the men's and women's professional tour, and the tin height has been lowered by two inches (to 17 inches) for all PSA events (men's and women's).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="American_scoring">American scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: American scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Another scoring system is American scoring. The rules of American scoring are identical to PARS, apart from games are played to 15. This system is not widely used because games were considered to last too long and the winner would usually be the fitter player, not necessarily the better player.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Competition matches are usually played to "best-of-five" (i.e. the first player to win three games)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_shots_played">Types of shots played</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Types of shots played">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In squash, there are many types of shots played that lead to interesting games and strategy.</p>
<ul>
<li>Straight drive: The ball is hit parallel and close to a side wall to travel deep to the back of the court (the 'basic' squash shot). Often referred to as a 'good length' shot.</li>
<li>Boast: The ball is played off a side wall at an angle, or the back wall, before hitting the front wall. A 'three wall boast' has the ball hit at a side wall such that the ball hits the front wall followed by the opposite side wall before finally hitting the floor. A 'back wall boast' has the ball struck against the back wall before reaching the front wall. It is typically not recommended to beginners due to the risk of hitting spectators above and behind players on solid backed courts. A 'trickle boast' is a boast performed at a shallow angle with the front wall at a low trajectory. A 'reverse boast' is a boast played towards the sidewall furtherst from the striker. It is played rarely at high levels due to the high risk of hitting the opponent and the typical ease of counterattack.</li>
<li>Volley: The ball is hit 'on the full' (before it touches the floor), usually directly to the front wall</li>
<li>Drop shot: The ball is hit gently against the front wall, to fall softly to the floor in the front corner.</li>
<li>Fake: The player swings at the ball and intentionally misses and then quickly swings again hitting the ball in the opposite direction of the original swing in order to deceive his or her opponent. The result is the player's opponent begins to move in the opposite direction and is unable to recover to the correct direction of the ball.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lob: The ball is hit softly and high on the front wall and with a high arc, so that it falls in a back corner of the court.</li>
<li>Cross Court: The ball is hit against the front wall at an angle such that the ball lands on the opposite side of the court to the striker.</li>
<li>Kill: The ball is hit hard and low on the front wall so that it travels no farther than half court.</li>
<li>Nick: The ball is hit such that after striking the front wall the ball connects with the junction between the side wall and the floor. The result is the ball either bouncing minimally or 'rolling' out of the nick, ending the rally.</li>
<li>Swayze: The ball is hit hard against the front wall to place the ball in the back corner of the court trapping the opposing player in the corner.</li>
<li>Taxi: which can also be referred to as a "huge" or "massive" taxi, is when a player is caught "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K8JCnn88_BM">completely wrongfooted</a>" by his opponent.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Philadelphia (or corkscrew): A shot played diagonally upwards into the front corner hitting the front wall first and then the side wall. The ball then lobs over the court with significant spin. Ideally it hits the opposite side wall at the back and travels parallel to the rear wall making a return very difficult. This shot is a favourite in exhibition squash but is susceptible to being volleyed.</li>
<li>Skid boast: A shot played from the back corners of the court where the ball is hit high along the sidewall with a small angle so that it hits the sidewall first, then hits high in the middle of the front wall continuing to cross the court while high in the air ideally hitting the opposite sidewall and landing close to the backwall to go past the opponent. As with the Philadelphia it is susceptible to being volleyed.</li>
<li>Mizuki: This shot is hit as a volley. Unlike a normal volley, the Mizuki shot is hit with the other side of the racket by turning the wrist which deceives the direction of the ball.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategy_and_tactics">Strategy and tactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Strategy and tactics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A key strategy in squash is known as "dominating the T" (the intersection of the red lines near the centre of the court, shaped like the letter "T", where the player is in the best position to retrieve the opponent's next shot). Skilled players will return a shot, and then move back toward the "T" before playing the next shot. From this position, the player can quickly access any part of the court to retrieve the opponent's next shot with a minimum of movement.</p>
<p>A common strategy is to hit the ball straight up the side walls to the back corners; this is the basic squash shot, referred to as a "rail," straight drive, wall, or "length." After hitting this shot, the player will then move to the centre of the court near the "T" to be well placed to retrieve the opponent's return. Attacking with soft or "short" shots to the front corners (referred to as "drop shots") causes the opponent to cover more of the court and may result in an outright winner. Boasts or angle shots are deliberately struck off one of the side walls before the ball reaches the front. They are used for deception and again to cause the opponent to cover more of the court.</p>
<p>Rallies between experienced players may involve 30 or more shots and therefore a very high premium is placed on fitness, both aerobic and anaerobic. As players become more skilled and, in particular, better able to retrieve shots, points often become a <a href="/wiki/Attrition_warfare" title="Attrition warfare">war of attrition</a>. At higher levels of the game, the fitter player has a major advantage.</p>
<p>Ability to change the direction of ball at the last instant is also a tactic used to unbalance the opponent. Expert players can anticipate the opponent's shot a few tenths of a second before the average player, giving them a chance to react sooner.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Depending on the style of play, it is common to refer to squash players<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> as</p>
<ul>
<li>Power players: powerful shots to take time away from their opponent. For example, <a href="/wiki/John_White_(squash_player)" title="John White (squash player)">John White</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omar_Mosaad" title="Omar Mosaad">Omar Mosaad</a>.</li>
<li>Shot makers: accurate shots to take time away from their opponent. For example, <a href="/wiki/Jonathon_Power" title="Jonathon Power">Jonathon Power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramy_Ashour" title="Ramy Ashour">Ramy Ashour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amr_Shabana" title="Amr Shabana">Amr Shabana</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Willstrop" title="James Willstrop">James Willstrop</a>.</li>
<li>Retrievers: excellent retrieval to counter power and accuracy and to return shots more quickly to take time away from their opponent. For example, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Nicol" title="Peter Nicol">Peter Nicol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A9gory_Gaultier" title="Grégory Gaultier">Grégory Gaultier</a>.</li>
<li>Attritional players: a consistently high-paced game both from shot speed and running speed to wear their opponent down over time. For example, <a href="/wiki/David_Palmer_(squash_player)" title="David Palmer (squash player)">David Palmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Matthew" title="Nick Matthew">Nick Matthew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jahangir_Khan" title="Jahangir Khan">Jahangir Khan</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interference_and_obstruction">Interference and obstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Interference and obstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Interference and obstruction are an inevitable aspect of this sport, since two players are confined within a shared space. Generally, the rules entitle players to a direct straight line access to the ball, room for a reasonable swing and an unobstructed shot to any part of the front wall. When interference occurs, a player may appeal for a "let" and the referee (or the players themselves if there is no official) then interprets the extent of the interference. The referee may elect to allow a let and the players then replay the point, or award a "stroke" to the appealing player (meaning that he is declared the winner of that point) depending on the degree of interference, whether the interfering player made an adequate effort to avoid interfering, and whether the player interfered with was likely to have hit a winning shot had the interference not occurred. An exception to all of this occurs when the interfering player is directly in the path of the other player's swing, effectively preventing the swing, in which case a stroke is always awarded.</p>
<p>When it is deemed that there has been little or no interference, or that it is impossible to say one way or the other, the rules provide that no let is to be allowed, in the interests of continuity of play and the discouraging of spurious appeals for lets. Because of the subjectivity in interpreting the nature and magnitude of interference, the awarding (or withholding) of lets and strokes is often controversial.</p>
<p>When a player's shot hits their opponent prior to hitting the front wall, interference has occurred. If the ball was travelling towards the side wall when it hit the opponent, or if it had already hit the side wall and was travelling directly to the front wall, it is usually a let. However, it is a stroke to the player who hit the ball if the ball was travelling straight to the front wall when the ball hit the opponent, without having first hit the side wall. Generally after a player has been hit by the ball, both players stand still; if the struck player is standing directly in front of the player who hit the ball he loses the stroke, if he is not straight in front, a let is played. If it is deemed that the player who is striking the ball is deliberately trying to hit his opponent, he will lose the stroke. An exception to all of this occurs when the player hitting the ball has "turned", i.e., let the ball pass him on one side, but then hit it on the other side as it came off the back wall. In these cases, the stroke goes to the player who was hit by the ball.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Referee">Referee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Referee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The referee is usually a certified position issued by the club or assigned squash league. The referee has dominant power over the squash players. Any conflict or interference is dealt with by the referee. The referee may also issue to take away points or games due to improper etiquette regarding conduct or rules. Refer to “Interference and Obstruction” for more detail. In addition the referee is usually responsible for the scoring of games. Nowadays, three referees are usually used in professional tournaments. The Central referee has responsibility to call the score and make decisions with the two side referees.</p>
<h2><span id="Cultural,_social,_and_health_aspects"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural.2C_social.2C_and_health_aspects">Cultural, social, and health aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cultural, social, and health aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several variations of squash played across the world. In the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Hardball_squash" title="Hardball squash">hardball</a> singles and doubles are played with a much harder ball and different size courts (as noted above). Hardball singles has lost much of its popularity in North America (in favour of the International version), but the hardball doubles game is still active. There is also a doubles version of squash played with the standard ball, sometimes on a wider court, and a more tennis-like variation known as <a href="/wiki/Squash_tennis" title="Squash tennis">squash tennis</a>.</p>
<p>The relatively small court and low-<a href="/wiki/Bouncing_ball" title="Bouncing ball">bouncing ball</a> makes scoring points harder and rallies usually longer than in its American cousin, <a href="/wiki/Racketball" class="mw-redirect" title="Racketball">racketball</a>, as the ball may be played to all four corners of the court. Since every ball must strike the front wall above the tin (unlike racketball), the ball cannot be easily "killed". Another difference between squash and racketball is the service game. Racketball allows for the entire back <a href="/wiki/Racketball#Equipment" class="mw-redirect" title="Racketball">court</a> (from 20-feet to 40-feet) to be used as a service return area; this makes returning serves much more challenging in racketball than squash. Racketball serves routinely exceed 140&#160;mph (225&#160;km/h) and are a crucial component of the game, similar to tennis.</p>
<p>Squash provides an excellent cardiovascular workout. In one hour of squash, a player may expend approximately 600 to 1000 food <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a> (3,000 to 4,000 <a href="/wiki/Kilojoule" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilojoule">kJ</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-santelmann2003_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-santelmann2003-15">[15]</a></sup> The sport also provides a good upper and lower body workout by exercising both the legs in running around the court and the arms and torso in swinging the racket. In 2003, <i>Forbes</i> rated squash as the number one healthiest sport to play.<sup id="cite_ref-santelmann2003_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-santelmann2003-15">[15]</a></sup> However, some studies have implicated squash as a cause of possible fatal <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrhythmia" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardiac arrhythmia">cardiac arrhythmia</a> and argued that squash is an inappropriate form of exercise for older men with <a href="/wiki/Coronary_artery_disease" title="Coronary artery disease">heart disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>According to the World Squash Federation, as of June 2009, there were 49908 squash courts in the world, with 188 countries and territories having at least one court. England had the greatest number at 8,500. The other countries with more than 1,000 courts, in descending order by number were Germany, Egypt, the United States of America, Australia, South Africa, Canada, Malaysia, France, the Netherlands, and Spain. Today, The United States has the fastest growing squash participation. There are an estimated 20 million squash players worldwide.</p>
<p>In June 2009, there were players from nineteen countries in the top fifty of the men's world rankings, with England and Egypt leading with eleven each.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The women's world rankings featured players from sixteen countries, led by England with eleven.</p>
<p>The men's and women's Professional Squash Association <a href="/wiki/PSA_World_Tour" class="mw-redirect" title="PSA World Tour">tour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Official_Men%27s_Squash_World_Ranking" title="Official Men's Squash World Ranking">men's rankings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Official_Women%27s_Squash_World_Ranking" title="Official Women's Squash World Ranking">women's rankings</a> are run by the <a href="/wiki/Professional_Squash_Association" title="Professional Squash Association">Professional Squash Association</a> (PSA).</p>
<p>The Professional Squash Tour is a tour based in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wider_acceptance">Wider acceptance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squash_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Wider acceptance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Squash has been featured regularly at the multi-sport events of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Games" title="Commonwealth Games">Commonwealth Games</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asian_Games" title="Asian Games">Asian Games</a> since 1998. Squash is also a regular sport at the <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_Games" title="Pan American Games">Pan American Games</a> since 1995. Squash players and associations have lobbied for many years for the sport to be accepted into the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a>, with no success to date. Squash narrowly missed being instated for the 2012 London Games and the 2016 Rio de Janeiro Games (missed out again as the IOC assembly decided to add golf and rugby sevens to the Olympic programme).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Squash also missed out as an event in the 2020 Olympic Games.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/125th_IOC_Session" title="125th IOC Session">125th IOC Session</a> in <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, the <a href="/wiki/IOC" class="mw-redirect" title="IOC">IOC</a> voted for <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">Wrestling</a> instead of Squash or <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">Baseball</a>/<a href="/wiki/Softball" title="Softball">Softball</a>. The usual reason cited for the failure of the sport to be adopted for Olympic competition is the difficulty of spectators to follow the action, especially via television.</p>
<p>A new bid is expected to be launched for the 2024 Olympics, when the time comes.</p>

<p>The (British) Squash Rackets Association (now known as <a href="/wiki/England_Squash" title="England Squash">England Squash</a>) conducted its first <a href="/wiki/British_Open_Squash_Championships" title="British Open Squash Championships">British Open</a> championship for men in December 1930, using a "challenge" system. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Read_(squash_player)" title="Charles Read (squash player)">Charles Read</a> was designated champion in 1930, but was beaten in home and away matches by <a href="/wiki/Don_Butcher" title="Don Butcher">Don Butcher</a>, who was then recorded as the champion for 1931. The championship continues to this day, but has been conducted with a "knockout" format since 1947.</p>

<a href="/wiki/Jahangir_Khan" title="Jahangir Khan">Jahangir Khan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> is widely considered to be the greatest player in the history of the game.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></div>

<p>The women's championship started in 1921, and has been dominated by relatively few players:<sup id="cite_ref-Championship_records_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Championship_records-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joyce_Cave" title="Joyce Cave">Joyce Cave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Cave" title="Nancy Cave">Nancy Cave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cecily_Fenwick" title="Cecily Fenwick">Cecily Fenwick</a> (England) in the 1920s; <a href="/wiki/Margot_Lumb" title="Margot Lumb">Margot Lumb</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Susan_Noel" title="Susan Noel">Susan Noel</a> (England) 1930s; <a href="/wiki/Janet_Morgan" title="Janet Morgan">Janet Morgan</a> (England) 1950s; <a href="/wiki/Heather_McKay" title="Heather McKay">Heather McKay</a> (Australia) 1960s and 1970s; <a href="/wiki/Vicki_Cardwell" title="Vicki Cardwell">Vicki Cardwell</a> (Australia) and <a href="/wiki/Susan_Devoy" title="Susan Devoy">Susan Devoy</a> (New Zealand) 1980s; <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Martin" title="Michelle Martin">Michelle Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Fitz-Gerald" title="Sarah Fitz-Gerald">Sarah Fitz-Gerald</a> (Australia) 1990s and <a href="/wiki/Nicol_David" title="Nicol David">Nicol David</a> (Malaysia) 2000s.</p>
<p>The Men's British Open has similarly been dominated by relatively few players:<sup id="cite_ref-Championship_records_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Championship_records-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/F.D._Amr_Bey" class="mw-redirect" title="F.D. Amr Bey">F.D. Amr Bey</a> (Egypt) in the 1930s; <a href="/wiki/Mahmoud_Karim" title="Mahmoud Karim">Mahmoud Karim</a> (Egypt) in the 1940s; brothers <a href="/wiki/Hashim_Khan" title="Hashim Khan">Hashim Khan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Azam_Khan_(squash_player)" title="Azam Khan (squash player)">Azam Khan</a> (Pakistan) in the 1950s and 1960s; <a href="/wiki/Jonah_Barrington_(squash_player)" title="Jonah Barrington (squash player)">Jonah Barrington</a> (Great Britain and Ireland) and <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Hunt" title="Geoff Hunt">Geoff Hunt</a> (Australia) in the 1960s and 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Jahangir_Khan" title="Jahangir Khan">Jahangir Khan</a> (Pakistan) 1980s&#160;; <a href="/wiki/Jansher_Khan" title="Jansher Khan">Jansher Khan</a> (Pakistan) in the 1990s and more recently, <a href="/wiki/David_Palmer_(squash_player)" title="David Palmer (squash player)">David Palmer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nick_Matthew" title="Nick Matthew">Nick Matthew</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Open_(squash)" class="mw-redirect" title="World Open (squash)">World Open (squash)</a> was inaugurated in 1976 and serves as the main competition today. <a href="/wiki/Jansher_Khan" title="Jansher Khan">Jansher Khan</a> holds the record of winning eight World titles followed by <a href="/wiki/Jahangir_Khan" title="Jahangir Khan">Jahangir Khan</a> with six, <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Hunt" title="Geoff Hunt">Geoff Hunt</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Amr_Shabana" title="Amr Shabana">Amr Shabana</a> four, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Matthew" title="Nick Matthew">Nick Matthew</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Ramy_Ashour" title="Ramy Ashour">Ramy Ashour</a> three. The women's record is held by <a href="/wiki/Nicol_David" title="Nicol David">Nicol David</a> with eight wins followed by <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Fitzgerald" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarah Fitzgerald">Sarah Fitzgerald</a> five, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Devoy" title="Susan Devoy">Susan Devoy</a> four, and <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Martin" title="Michelle Martin">Michelle Martin</a> three.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Heather_McKay" title="Heather McKay">Heather McKay</a> remained undefeated in competitive matches for 19 years (between 1962 and 1981) and won sixteen consecutive British Open titles between 1962 and 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Previous world number one <a href="/wiki/Peter_Nicol" title="Peter Nicol">Peter Nicol</a> stated that he believed squash had a "very realistic chance" of being added to the list of <a href="/wiki/Olympics" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympics">Olympic</a> sports for the <a href="/wiki/2016_Olympic_Games" class="mw-redirect" title="2016 Olympic Games">2016 Olympic Games</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> but it ultimately lost out to <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rugby_sevens" title="Rugby sevens">rugby sevens</a>.</p>

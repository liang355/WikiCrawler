<p><b>Bielefeld</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈbiːləfɛlt]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Bielefeld.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/De-Bielefeld.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Bielefeld.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in the <a href="/wiki/Ostwestfalen-Lippe" title="Ostwestfalen-Lippe">Ostwestfalen-Lippe</a> Region in the north-east of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. With a population of 336,352,<sup id="cite_ref-pop_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-2">[2]</a></sup> it is also the most populous city in the <a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">Regierungsbezirk</a> <a href="/wiki/Detmold_(region)" title="Detmold (region)">Detmold</a>.</p>

<p>The historical centre of the city is situated north of the <a href="/wiki/Teutoburg_Forest" title="Teutoburg Forest">Teutoburg Forest</a> line of hills, but modern Bielefeld also incorporates boroughs on the opposite side and on the hills.</p>
<p>Bielefeld is home to a significant number of internationally operating companies, including <a href="/wiki/Dr._Oetker" title="Dr. Oetker">Dr. Oetker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gildemeister_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Gildemeister AG">Gildemeister</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sch%C3%BCco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schüco (page does not exist)">Schüco</a>. It has a <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld_University" title="Bielefeld University">university</a> and several <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschulen</a>. Bielefeld is also famous for the <a href="/wiki/Bethel_Institution" title="Bethel Institution">Bethel Institution</a>, and for the <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld_Conspiracy" title="Bielefeld Conspiracy">Bielefeld Conspiracy</a>, which satirises <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">conspiracy theories</a> by claiming that Bielefeld does not exist. This concept has been used in the town's marketing and alluded to by <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:20em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 90%; background-color: #B0C4DE;">
<div class="" style="background-color: #B0C4DE; color: black; text-align: center; font-size: larger; font-weight: bold;">Historical Affiliations</div>
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div><a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> 1214-<br />
<p><a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> 14th century-<br />
<a href="/wiki/Berg_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Berg (state)">Berg (state)</a> 1346-<br />
<a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Margraviate of Brandenburg</a> 1614-<br />
<a href="/wiki/Minden-Ravensberg" title="Minden-Ravensberg">Minden-Ravensberg</a> 1719–1807<br />
<a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">Kingdom of Westphalia</a><br /></p>
<a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Province_of_Westphalia" title="Province of Westphalia">Province of Westphalia</a>) 1815-</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Founded in 1214 by Count Hermann IV of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Ravensberg" title="County of Ravensberg">Ravensberg</a> to guard a pass crossing the <a href="/wiki/Teutoburg_Forest" title="Teutoburg Forest">Teutoburg Forest</a>, Bielefeld was the "city of <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a>" as a minor member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>.</p>
<p>After the Cologne-Minden railway <a href="/wiki/Bad_Oeynhausen#History" title="Bad Oeynhausen">opened in 1849</a>, the Bozi brothers constructed the first large mechanised spinning mill in 1851. The Ravensberg Spinning Mill was built from 1854 to 1857, and metal works began to open in the 1860s.</p>
<p>Between 1904 and 1930, Bielefeld grew, opening a rebuilt railway station, a municipal theatre, and finally, the Rudolf-Oetker-Halle concert hall, renowned for its excellent acoustics.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/List_of_German_cars" class="mw-redirect" title="List of German cars">Dürkopp car was produced 1898-1927</a>. After printing emergency money (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Notgeld" title="Notgeld">Notgeld</a></span>) in 1923 during the <a href="/wiki/Inflation_in_the_Weimar_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Inflation in the Weimar Republic">inflation in the Weimar Republic</a>, Bielefeld was one of several towns that printed very attractive and highly collectable <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">banknotes</a> with designs on <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Velvet" title="Velvet">velvet</a>. These pieces were issued by the Bielefeld Stadtsparkasse (town's savings bank) and were sent all around the world in the early 1920s. These pieces are known as <i>Stoffgeld</i>&#160;– that is, money made from fabric.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Nazi Germany</dt>
</dl>
<p>The town's synagogue was <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">burned in 1938</a>. In 1944, <a href="/wiki/B-17_Flying_Fortress" class="mw-redirect" title="B-17 Flying Fortress">B-17 Flying Fortresses</a> bombed Bielefeld on September 20 (the gas works)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and October 7,<sup id="cite_ref-McKillop_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKillop-6">[6]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/RAF" class="mw-redirect" title="RAF">RAF</a> bombed on December 4/5.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1945, B-17s bombed the nearby Paderborn marshalling yard, the "Schildesche Railway Viaduct" was bombed on January 17, 1945,<sup id="cite_ref-McKillop_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKillop-6">[6]</a></sup> and on March 14 the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Slam_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Slam bomb">Grand Slam bomb</a> was used for the very first time against the viaduct. American troops entered the city in April 1945.</p>
<p>Founded in 1867 as a Bielefeld sewing machine repair company, AG Dürkoppwerke employed 1,665 people in 1892; it used <a href="/wiki/Waffenamt_codes" title="Waffenamt codes">Waffenamt code</a> "WaA547" from 1938 to 1939 as the Dürkopp-Werke, and merged with other Bielefeld companies to form Dürkopp Adler AG in 1990.</p>
<p>Due to the presence of a number of barracks built during the 1930s and its location next to the main East-West Autobahn in northern Germany, after World War II Bielefeld became a headquarters town for the fighting command of the <a href="/wiki/British_Army_of_the_Rhine" title="British Army of the Rhine">British Army of the Rhine</a> - BAOR (the administrative and strategic headquarters were at <a href="/wiki/Rheindahlen" title="Rheindahlen">Rheindahlen</a> near the Dutch border). Until the 1980s there was a large British presence in the barracks housing the headquarters of the British <a href="/wiki/I_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="I Corps (United Kingdom)">First Corps</a> and support units, as well as schools, <a href="/wiki/Navy,_Army_and_Air_Force_Institutes" title="Navy, Army and Air Force Institutes">NAAFI</a> shops, officers' and sergeants' messes and several estates of married quarters. The British presence was heavily scaled back after the reunification of Germany and most of the infrastructure has disappeared.</p>
<p>In 1973 the first villages on the south side of the Teutoburg Forest were incorporated.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_and_education">Industry and education</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bielefeld_um_1895.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Bielefeld_um_1895.jpg/220px-Bielefeld_um_1895.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Bielefeld_um_1895.jpg/330px-Bielefeld_um_1895.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Bielefeld_um_1895.jpg/440px-Bielefeld_um_1895.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3495" data-file-height="2493" /></a>

<p>Bielefeld was a linen-producing town, and in the early 1920s the Town's Savings Bank (Stadtsparkasse) issued money made of linen, silk and velvet. These items were known as 'stoffgeld'.</p>
<p>In addition to the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Home_appliances" class="mw-redirect" title="Home appliances">home appliances</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Heavy_industry" title="Heavy industry">heavy industries</a>, Bielefeld companies include <a href="/wiki/Dr._Oetker" title="Dr. Oetker">Dr. Oetker</a> (food manufacturing), Möller Group (leather products and plastics), Seidensticker (clothing and textiles) and <a href="/wiki/Bethel_Institution" title="Bethel Institution">Bethel Institution</a> with 17.000 employees.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bielefeld_University" title="Bielefeld University">Bielefeld University</a> was founded in 1969. Among its first professors was the notable contemporary German sociologist <a href="/wiki/Niklas_Luhmann" title="Niklas Luhmann">Niklas Luhmann</a>. Other institutions of <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> include the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130915104757/http://www.bethel.de/kiho">Theological Seminary Bethel (<i>Kirchliche Hochschule Bethel</i>)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Bielefeld University of Applied Sciences">Bielefeld University of Applied Sciences</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Fachhochschule Bielefeld</span>), which offers 21 courses in 8 different departments (agriculture and engineering are in <a href="/wiki/Minden" title="Minden">Minden</a>) and has been internationally recognized for its <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.raum-21.org">photography school</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span></h2>
<p>Two major <i><a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">autobahns</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_2" title="Bundesautobahn 2">A&#160;2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_33" title="Bundesautobahn 33">A&#160;33</a>, intersect in the south east of Bielefeld. The <i>Ostwestfalendamm</i> expressway connects the two parts of the city, naturally divided by the Teutoburg Forest. <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bielefeld Hauptbahnhof">Bielefeld Hauptbahnhof</a>, the main railway station of Bielefeld, is on the <a href="/wiki/Hamm%E2%80%93Minden_railway" title="Hamm–Minden railway">Hamm–Minden railway</a> and is part of the German <a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">ICE</a> high-speed railroad system. Bielefeld has a small airstrip, Flugplatz Bielefeld,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Senne" title="Senne">Senne</a> district but is mainly served by the three larger airports nearby, <a href="/wiki/Paderborn_Lippstadt_Airport" title="Paderborn Lippstadt Airport">Paderborn Lippstadt Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Osnabr%C3%BCck_International_Airport" title="Münster Osnabrück International Airport">Münster Osnabrück International Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hannover_Airport" title="Hannover Airport">Hannover Airport</a>.</p>
<p>Bielefeld boasts a well-developed public transport system, served mainly by the companies <i>moBiel</i><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (formerly <i>Stadtwerke Bielefeld - Verkehrsbetriebe</i>) and "BVO".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld_Stadtbahn" title="Bielefeld Stadtbahn">Bielefeld Stadtbahn</a> has four major lines and regional trains connect different parts of the city with nearby counties. Buses also run throughout the entire vicinity.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main sights</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sparrenberg_Castle" title="Sparrenberg Castle">Sparrenburg Castle</a> is Bielefeld's characteristic landmark. It was built between 1240 and 1250 by Count Ludwig von <a href="/wiki/County_of_Ravensberg" title="County of Ravensberg">Ravensberg</a>. The 37-metre-high (121&#160;ft) tower and the catacombs of the castle are open to the public.</p>
<p>The Old City Hall (<i>Altes Rathaus</i>) was built in 1904 and still serves the same function. Its façade reflects the so-called <a href="/wiki/Weserrenaissance" class="mw-redirect" title="Weserrenaissance">Weserrenaissance</a> and features elements of various <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architectural styles</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>. Though the mayor still holds office in the Old City Hall, most of the city's administration is housed in the adjacent <b>New City Hall</b> (<i>Neues Rathaus</i>).</p>

<p>The City Theatre (<i>Stadttheater</i>) is part of the same architectural ensemble as the Old City Hall, also built in 1904. It has a notable <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Jugendstil</a> façade, is Bielefeld's largest theatre and home of the <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld_Opera" title="Bielefeld Opera">Bielefeld Opera</a>. Another theatre (<i>Theater am Alten Markt</i>) resides in the former <a href="/wiki/City_and_town_halls" class="mw-redirect" title="City and town halls">town hall</a> building on the Old Market Square (<i>Alter Markt</i>), which also contains a row of restored 16th and 17th-century townhouses with noteworthy late Gothic and <a href="/wiki/Weser_Renaissance" title="Weser Renaissance">Weser Renaissance</a> style façades (<i>Bürgerhäuser am Alten Markt</i>).</p>
<p>The oldest city church is <i>Altstädter Nicolaikirche</i>. It is a Gothic <a href="/wiki/Hall_church" title="Hall church">hall church</a> with a height of 81.5&#160;m (267&#160;ft). It was founded in 1236 by the <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Paderborn" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric of Paderborn">Bishop of Paderborn</a>, and enlarged at the beginning of the 14th century. The church was damaged in World War II and later rebuilt. Three times a day, a <a href="/wiki/Carillon" title="Carillon">carillon</a> can be heard. The most valuable treasure of this church is a carved altar from <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>, decorated with 250 figures. A small museum housed within illustrates the history of the church up to World War II.</p>
<p>The largest church is the <i>Neustädter Marienkirche</i>, a Gothic hall church dating back to 1293, completed 1512. It stands 78&#160;m (256&#160;ft) tall and has a length of 52&#160;m (171&#160;ft). Historically speaking, this building is considered to be the most precious possession of the town. It was the starting point of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> in Bielefeld in 1553. A valuable wing-altar with 13 pictures, known as the <i>Marienaltar</i> is also kept inside. The <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> <a href="/wiki/Spire" title="Spire">spires</a> were destroyed in World War II and later replaced by two unusually-shaped "Gothic" clocktowers. The altarpiece of the Bielefeld church <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.marienkirche-bielefeld.de/"><i>Neustädter Marienkirche</i></a> from around 1400 is among the most prominent masterpieces of artwork of the German Middle Ages. Two of the altarpieces, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.metmuseum.org/Works_of_Art/recent_acquisitions/2000/co_rec_eur_2001.216.2.asp"><i>The Flagellation</i></a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.metmuseum.org/Works_of_Art/collection_database/european_paintings/the_crucifixion_master_of_the_berswordt_altar//objectview.aspx?OID=110001443&amp;collID=11&amp;dd1=11"><i>The Crucifixion</i></a> are now in the collection of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>.</p>
<p>Bielefeld is also the seat of the two largest <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_welfare_provision" class="mw-redirect" title="Social welfare provision">social welfare</a> establishments (<i>Diakonie</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bethel_Institution" title="Bethel Institution">Bethel Institution</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.johanneswerk.de/"><i>Evangelisches Johanneswerk</i></a>.</p>
<p>Other important cultural sights of the region are the <a href="/wiki/Kunsthalle_Bielefeld" title="Kunsthalle Bielefeld">art museum (Kunsthalle)</a>, the Rudolf Oetker concert hall (<i>Rudolf-Oetker-Halle</i>), and the city's municipal <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_Bielefeld" title="Botanischer Garten Bielefeld">Botanischer Garten Bielefeld</a>). Bielefeld is home to the widely known Bielefeld Children's Choir (<i>Bielefelder Kinderchor</i>), founded in 1932 and in the postwar era famed for its recordings of traditional German Christmas carols. Particularly notable is the choir's annual Christmas Concert in the Rudolf Oetker concert hall. Foreign tours have taken the choir to many European countries, and also the U.S.A. and Japan.</p>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCnenburg_Observation_Tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Hünenburg Observation Tower">Hünenburg</a> there is an <a href="/wiki/Observation_tower" title="Observation tower">observation tower</a>, next to a 164 meters (538 feet) high <a href="/wiki/Radio_masts_and_towers" title="Radio masts and towers">radio tower</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span></h2>
<p>Bielefeld is home to the professional <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> team <a href="/wiki/DSC_Arminia_Bielefeld" class="mw-redirect" title="DSC Arminia Bielefeld">DSC Arminia Bielefeld</a>. Currently member of <a href="/wiki/2._Bundesliga" title="2. Bundesliga">2. Bundesliga</a>, the club plays at the <a href="/wiki/Bielefelder_Alm" title="Bielefelder Alm">SchücoArena</a> stadium in the west of the town centre.</p>

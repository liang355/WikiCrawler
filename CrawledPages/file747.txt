			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Olympic-size swimming pool</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Long Course" redirects here. It is not to be confused with long course <a href="/wiki/Triathlon" title="Triathlon">triathlon</a>.</div>
<p>An <b>Olympic-size swimming pool</b> is the type of <a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool" title="Swimming pool">swimming pool</a> used in the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a>, where the race course is 50 metres (164.0&#160;ft) in length. This is typically referred to as "<b>long course</b>", distinguishing it from "<a href="/wiki/Short_course" title="Short course">short course</a>" which applies to competitions in pools that are 25 metres (82.0&#160;ft) in length. If touch panels are used in competition, then the distance between touch panels should be either 25 or 50 metres to qualify for <a href="/wiki/FINA" title="FINA">FINA</a> recognition. This means that Olympic pools are generally oversized, to accommodate touch panels used in competition.</p>
<p>An Olympic-size swimming pool is used as a colloquial unit of <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a>, to make approximate comparisons to similarly sized objects or volumes. It is not a specific definition, as there is no official limit on the depth of an Olympic pool. The value has an <a href="/wiki/Order_of_magnitude" title="Order of magnitude">order of magnitude</a> of 1 megaliter (ML).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>There must be two spaces 2.5&#160;m (8&#160;ft 2&#160;in) wide outside lanes 1 and 8 (in effect, two empty lanes).<sup id="cite_ref-FINA_Facilities_Rules_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FINA_Facilities_Rules-2">[2]</a></sup> The length of 50 metres (164&#160;ft) must be between the touch pads at the end of each lane, if they are used.<sup id="cite_ref-FINA_Facilities_Rules_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FINA_Facilities_Rules-2">[2]</a></sup> If starting blocks are used, then there must be a minimum depth of 1.35 metres (4.4&#160;ft) from between 1.0 metre (3&#160;ft) from the end of the pool to at least 6.0 metres (20&#160;ft) from the end of the pool. At all other points, the minimum depth is 1.0 metre (3&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-FINA_Facilities_Rules_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FINA_Facilities_Rules-2">[2]</a></sup> If the pool is used for Olympic Games or World Championships, then the minimum depth is increased to 2.0 metres (7&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-FINA_Facilities_Rules_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FINA_Facilities_Rules-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>At FINA's 2009 Congress, rules were approved for 10-lane courses for competition, as an alternative to the more traditional 8-lane course.</p>

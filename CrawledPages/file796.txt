<p><b>Stephen Jay Greenblatt</b> (born November 7, 1943) is an American Shakespearean, literary historian, and author. He is <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Professor" title="Harvard University Professor">John Cogan University Professor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">Humanities</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. He is the general editor of <i>The Norton Shakespeare</i> (2015) and the general editor and a contributor to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Norton_Anthology_of_English_Literature" title="The Norton Anthology of English Literature">The Norton Anthology of English Literature</a></i>.</p>
<p>Greenblatt is one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/New_Historicism" title="New Historicism">New Historicism</a>, a set of critical practices that he often refers to as "cultural poetics"; his works have been influential since the early 1980s when he introduced the term. Greenblatt has written and edited numerous books and articles relevant to New Historicism, the study of culture, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> studies and <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> studies and is considered to be an expert in these fields. He is also co-founder of the literary-cultural journal <i><a href="/wiki/Representations" title="Representations">Representations</a></i>, which often publishes articles by new historicists. His most popular work is <i>Will in the World</i>, a biography of Shakespeare that was on the <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">New York Times</a></i> <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="The New York Times Best Seller list">Best Seller List</a> for nine weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He won the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_General_Non-Fiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulitzer Prize for General Non-Fiction">Pulitzer Prize for General Non-Fiction</a> in 2012 and the <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award_for_Nonfiction" title="National Book Award for Nonfiction">National Book Award for Nonfiction</a> in 2011 for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Swerve:_How_the_World_Became_Modern" title="The Swerve: How the World Became Modern">The Swerve: How the World Became Modern</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Greenblatt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_career">Education and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Greenblatt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greenblatt was born in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> and raised in <a href="/wiki/Newton,_Massachusetts" title="Newton, Massachusetts">Newton, Massachusetts</a>. After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Newton_North_High_School" title="Newton North High School">Newton North High School</a>, he was educated at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">BA</a> 1964, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">PhD</a> 1969) and <a href="/wiki/Pembroke_College,_Cambridge" title="Pembroke College, Cambridge">Pembroke College, Cambridge</a> (<a href="/wiki/Master_of_Philosophy" title="Master of Philosophy">MPhil</a> 1966).<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_2005_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2005-4">[4]</a></sup> Greenblatt has since taught at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. He was Class of 1972 Professor at Berkeley (becoming a full professor in 1980) and taught there for 28 years before taking a position at Harvard University.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was named John Cogan University Professor of the Humanities in 2000. Greenblatt is considered "a key figure in the shift from literary to cultural poetics and from textual to contextual interpretation in U.S. English departments in the 1980s and 1990s."<sup id="cite_ref-Leitch_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leitch-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greenblatt was a long-term fellow of the Wissenschaftskolleg in Berlin.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As a visiting professor and lecturer, Greenblatt has taught at such institutions as the École des Hautes Études, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florence" title="University of Florence">University of Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_University" title="Kyoto University">Kyoto University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peking_University" title="Peking University">Peking University</a>. He was a resident fellow at the American Academy of Rome,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and is a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>, the American Philosophical Society, and the American Academy of Arts and Letters; he has been president of the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Language_Association" title="Modern Language Association">Modern Language Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Greenblatt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greenblatt self-identifies as an Eastern European Jew, an <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_Jews" title="Lithuanian Jews">Litvak</a>. His observant Jewish grandparents were born in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>; his paternal grandparents were from <a href="/wiki/Kovno" class="mw-redirect" title="Kovno">Kovno</a> and his maternal grandparents were from <a href="/wiki/Vilna" class="mw-redirect" title="Vilna">Vilna</a>. Greenblatt's grandparents immigrated to the United States during the early 1890s in order to escape a Czarist Russification plan to <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_Russia" title="Conscription in Russia">conscript</a> young Jewish men into the Russian army.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greenblatt was married to Ellen Schmidt from 1969 to 1996; they have two sons (Joshua, an attorney, and Aaron, a doctor).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1998, he married fellow academic Ramie Targoff, also a Renaissance expert and a professor at <a href="/wiki/Brandeis_University" title="Brandeis University">Brandeis University</a>, whom he has described as his soulmate; they have one son, Harry.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_2005_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2005-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Greenblatt has written extensively on <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, culture and <a href="/wiki/New_Historicism" title="New Historicism">New Historicism</a> (which he often refers to as "cultural poetics"). Much of his work has been "part of a collective project", such as his work as co-editor of the Berkeley-based literary-cultural journal <i>Representations</i> (which he co-founded in 1983), as editor of publications such as the <i>Norton Anthology of English Literature</i>, and as co-author of books such as <i>Practicing New Historicism</i> (2000), which he wrote with <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Gallagher" title="Catherine Gallagher">Catherine Gallagher</a>. Greenblatt has also written on such subjects as travelling in <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a> and China, <a href="/wiki/Story-telling" class="mw-redirect" title="Story-telling">story-telling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracles</a>.</p>
<p>Greenblatt's collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Charles_L._Mee" title="Charles L. Mee">Charles L. Mee</a>, <i>Cardenio</i>, premiered on May 8, 2008, at the <a href="/wiki/American_Repertory_Theater" title="American Repertory Theater">American Repertory Theater</a> in Cambridge, Massachusetts. While the critical response to <i>Cardenio</i> was mixed, audiences responded quite positively. The American Repertory Theater has posted audience responses on the organization's blog. <i>Cardenio</i> has been adapted for performance in ten countries, with additional international productions planned.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Historicism">New Historicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Greenblatt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New Historicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greenblatt first used the term "<a href="/wiki/New_Historicism" title="New Historicism">New Historicism</a>" in his 1982 introduction to <i>The Power of Forms in the English Renaissance</i> wherein he uses Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I's</a> "bitter reaction to the revival of Shakespeare's <i><a href="/wiki/Richard_II_(play)" title="Richard II (play)">Richard II</a></i> on the eve of the Essex rebellion" to illustrate the "mutual permeability of the literary and the historical".<sup id="cite_ref-Reader_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-11">[11]</a></sup> New Historicism is regarded by many to have influenced "every traditional period of English literary history".<sup id="cite_ref-Cadzow_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cadzow-12">[12]</a></sup> Some critics have charged that it is "antithetical to literary and <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a> value, that it reduces the historical to the literary or the literary to the historical, that it denies human agency and creativity, that it is somehow out to subvert the politics of cultural and critical theory [and] that it is anti-theoretical".<sup id="cite_ref-Reader_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-11">[11]</a></sup> Scholars have observed that New Historicism is, in fact, "neither new nor historical."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Others praise New Historicism as "a collection of practices" employed by critics to gain a more comprehensive understanding of literature by considering it in historical context while treating history itself as "historically contingent on the present in which [it is] constructed".<sup id="cite_ref-Reader_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As stated by Shakespeare scholar Jonathan Bate, the approach of New Historicism has been "the most influential strand of criticism over the last 25 years, with its view that literary creations are cultural formations shaped by 'the circulation of social energy'."<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_2005_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2005-4">[4]</a></sup> When told that several American job advertisements were requesting responses from experts in New Historicism, he remembered thinking: "'You've got to be kidding. You know it was just something we made up!' I began to see there were institutional consequences to what seemed like a not particularly deeply thought-out term."<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_2005_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2005-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has also said that "My deep, ongoing interest is in the relation between literature and history, the process through which certain remarkable works of art are at once embedded in a highly specific life-world and seem to pull free of that life-world. I am constantly struck by the strangeness of reading works that seem addressed, personally and intimately, to me, and yet were written by people who crumbled to dust long ago".<sup id="cite_ref-Named_Professor_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Named_Professor-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greenblatt's works on New Historicism and "cultural poetics" include <i>Practicing New Historicism</i> (2000) (with <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Gallagher" title="Catherine Gallagher">Catherine Gallagher</a>), in which Greenblatt discusses how "they anecdote ... appears as the 'touch of the real'" and <i>Towards a Poetics of Culture</i> (1987), in which Greenblatt asserts that the question of "how art and society are interrelated," as posed by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Lyotard" title="Jean-François Lyotard">Jean-François Lyotard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fredric_Jameson" title="Fredric Jameson">Fredric Jameson</a>, "cannot be answered by appealing to a single theoretical stance".<sup id="cite_ref-Cadzow_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cadzow-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>Renaissance Self-Fashioning</i> and the introduction to the <i><a href="/wiki/W._W._Norton" class="mw-redirect" title="W. W. Norton">Norton Shakespeare</a></i> are regarded as good examples of Greenblatt's application of new historicist practices.<sup id="cite_ref-Reader_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shakespeare_and_Renaissance_studies">Shakespeare and Renaissance studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Greenblatt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shakespeare and Renaissance studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stephen_Greenblatt_book_cover.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Will in the World</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>"I believe that nothing comes of nothing, even in Shakespeare. I wanted to know where he got the matter he was working with and what he did with that matter".<sup id="cite_ref-Purgatory_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purgatory-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Greenblatt states in "<i>King Lear</i> and Harsnett's 'Double-Fiction'" that "Shakespeare's self-consciousness is in significant ways bound up with the institutions and the symbology of power it anatomizes".<sup id="cite_ref-Richter_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richter-16">[16]</a></sup> His work on Shakespeare has addressed such topics as ghosts, purgatory, anxiety, exorcists and revenge. He is also a general editor of the <i>Norton Shakespeare</i>.</p>
<p>Greenblatt's New Historicism opposes the ways in which <a href="/wiki/New_Criticism" title="New Criticism">New Criticism</a> consigns texts "to an autonomous aesthetic realm that [dissociates] Renaissance writing from other forms of cultural production" and the historicist notion that Renaissance <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">texts</a> mirror "a coherent world-view that was held by a whole population," asserting instead "that critics who [wish] to understand sixteenth- and seventeenth-century writing must delineate the ways the texts they [study] were linked to the network of institutions, practices, and beliefs that constituted Renaissance culture in its entirety".<sup id="cite_ref-Cadzow_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cadzow-12">[12]</a></sup> Greenblatt's work in Renaissance studies includes <i>Renaissance Self-Fashioning</i> (1980), which "had a transformative impact on Renaissance studies".<sup id="cite_ref-Reader_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norton_Anthology_of_English_Literature"><i>Norton Anthology of English Literature</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Greenblatt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Norton Anthology of English Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greenblatt joined <a href="/wiki/M._H._Abrams" title="M. H. Abrams">M. H. Abrams</a> as general editor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Norton_Anthology_of_English_Literature" title="The Norton Anthology of English Literature">The Norton Anthology of English Literature</a></i> published by <a href="/wiki/W._W._Norton" class="mw-redirect" title="W. W. Norton">W. W. Norton</a> during the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> He is also the co-editor of the anthology's section on Renaissance literature<sup id="cite_ref-Gewertz_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gewertz-18">[18]</a></sup> and the general editor of the <i>Norton Shakespeare</i>, "currently his most influential piece of public pedagogy."<sup id="cite_ref-Reader_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reader-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

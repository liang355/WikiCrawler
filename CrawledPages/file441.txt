<p><b>Siegen</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈziːɡn̩]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-siegen.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6f/De-siegen.ogg" class="internal" title="De-siegen.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in Germany, in the south <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalian</a> part of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>.</p>
<p>It is located in the district of <a href="/wiki/Siegen-Wittgenstein" title="Siegen-Wittgenstein">Siegen-Wittgenstein</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arnsberg_(region)" title="Arnsberg (region)">Arnsberg</a> region. The <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> town (18.600 students in the 2013–2014 winter semester) is the district seat, and is ranked as a "higher centre" in the South Westphalian urban agglomeration.</p>
<p>In 1975, in the process of municipal reforms and amalgamations, Siegen's population exceeded the 100,000 mark.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city of Siegen lies in the basin of the upper reaches of the river <a href="/wiki/Sieg_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sieg (river)">Sieg</a>. From there, lateral valleys branch off in many directions. The heights of the surrounding mountains, wherever they are not actually settled, are covered in <a href="/wiki/Coppicing" title="Coppicing">coppice</a>. To the north lies the <a href="/wiki/Sauerland" title="Sauerland">Sauerland</a>, to the northwest the <a href="/wiki/Rothaargebirge" class="mw-redirect" title="Rothaargebirge">Rothaargebirge</a> and to the southwest the <a href="/wiki/Westerwald" title="Westerwald">Westerwald</a>.</p>
<p>The nearest cities to Siegen, taking into account average travelling distances, are <a href="/wiki/Hagen" title="Hagen">Hagen</a> to the north (83&#160;km), <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a> to the southeast (125&#160;km), <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a> to the southwest (105&#160;km) and <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> to the west (93&#160;km).</p>
<p>As the crow flies the distances to these places are, however, 65&#160;km (40&#160;mi) (Hagen), 95&#160;km (59&#160;mi) (Frankfurt), 65&#160;km (40&#160;mi) (Koblenz) and 75&#160;km (47&#160;mi) (Cologne).</p>
<p>The city lies on the German-<a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> holiday road called the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Route" title="Orange Route">Orange Route</a>, joining towns, cities and regions associated with the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Orange" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Orange">House of Orange</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_area">City area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: City area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stadtgliederung_Siegen.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Stadtgliederung_Siegen.png/250px-Stadtgliederung_Siegen.png" width="250" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Stadtgliederung_Siegen.png/375px-Stadtgliederung_Siegen.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Stadtgliederung_Siegen.png/500px-Stadtgliederung_Siegen.png 2x" data-file-width="627" data-file-height="656" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stadtgliederung_Siegen.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Divisions within the city.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The city's total land area is roughly 115&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (44&#160;sq&#160;mi). Its greatest east-west span is about 12&#160;km (7&#160;mi), and its greatest north-south span is about 12&#160;km (7&#160;mi). City limits are 48&#160;km (30&#160;mi) long. Siegen lies at a median elevation of 290&#160;m (951&#160;ft) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. The city's greatest elevation is the peak of the Pfannenberg at 499&#160;m (1,637&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a> at southern city limits. Siegen's lowest point is 215&#160;m (705&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a> at Niederschelden at southwestern city limits, which there also forms the state boundary with <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>. Roughly 60% of the city's land is wooded, making Siegen one of Germany's greenest cities.</p>
<p>The city area is divided into six zones, called <i>Bezirke</i> in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and comparable to boroughs in some cities, which themselves are further divided into various communities (<i>Ortsteile</i> and <i>Stadtteile</i>). Each "borough" has a borough board consisting of 15 voting and 15 non-voting members who are appointed by city council with regard to each party's share of the vote in the municipal elections in the borough in question. The borough boards decide on matters particular to their respective boroughs. These matters are laid down in Siegen's city charter.</p>
<h4><span id="Siegen's_six_boroughs_and_communities_belonging_to_each"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Siegen.27s_six_boroughs_and_communities_belonging_to_each">Siegen's six boroughs and communities belonging to each</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Siegen's six boroughs and communities belonging to each">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>District I (<a href="/wiki/Geisweid_(Siegen)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geisweid (Siegen)">Geisweid</a>): Birlenbach, Meiswinkel, Langenholdinghausen, Geisweid, Dillnhütten, Sohlbach, Buchen, Niedersetzen, Obersetzen</li>
<li>District II (Weidenau):</li>
<li>District III (Ost): Kaan-Marienborn, parts of Alt-Siegen (Giersberg), Bürbach, Volnsberg, Breitenbach, Feuersbach</li>
<li>District IV (Mitte): Alt-Siegen <i>(parts not belonging to borough III or V)</i></li>
<li>District V (West): Seelbach, Trupbach and parts of Alt-Siegen (Wellersberg, Fischbacherberg, Achenbach, Rothenberg)</li>
<li>District VI (Süd): Oberschelden, Gosenbach, Niederschelden, Eiserfeld, Eisern</li>
</ul>
<p>Along with the boroughs and communities into which the city is divided, as mandated by law, there are also further subdivisions within the communities, each with its own name, but none with distinctly clear borders. They are called <i>Quartiere</i>, which can be rendered as "quarters" or "neighbourhoods". Examples of these include the Unterstadt, the Oberstadt, Hammerhütte, Lindenberg, Charlottental, Haardter Berg (with the university) and the Alte Dreisbach. Some neighbourhoods even straddle community boundaries, like Sieghütte, parts of which can be found in both Siegen-Mitte and Weidenau. Moreover, some neighbourhoods even overlap each other. Unlike the boroughs (<i>Bezirke</i>) or communities (<i>Ortsteile</i> and <i>Stadtteile</i>), the <i>Quartiere</i> have no statistical or administrative importance. They do, however, serve some function as to their inhabitants' identity, but more practically than that, they are also useful for finding one's way with a city map and using in bus route names and on public notices and traffic signs. Many of the <i>Hüttentalstraße</i> city Autobahn's exits are also named after the <i>Quartiere</i> that they serve.</p>
<p>The communities of Weidenau, Geisweid, Birlenbach, Langenholdinghausen, Buchen, Sohlbach, Dillnhütten, Niedersetzen, Obersetzen and Meiswinkel formed from 1 July 1966 to 31 December 1974 the town of Hüttental. The communities of Eiserfeld, Eisern, Gosenbach, Niederschelden and Oberschelden formed the town of Eiserfeld between those same two dates.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighbouring_communities">Neighbouring communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neighbouring communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city of Siegen borders in the north on the town of <a href="/wiki/Kreuztal" title="Kreuztal">Kreuztal</a> and the community of <a href="/wiki/Wenden_(Sauerland)" title="Wenden (Sauerland)">Wenden</a>, in the east on the town of <a href="/wiki/Netphen" title="Netphen">Netphen</a>, in the southeast on the community of <a href="/wiki/Wilnsdorf" title="Wilnsdorf">Wilnsdorf</a>, in the south on the community of <a href="/wiki/Neunkirchen_(Siegerland)" title="Neunkirchen (Siegerland)">Neunkirchen</a>, in the west on the community of <a href="/wiki/Mudersbach" title="Mudersbach">Mudersbach</a> (<a href="/wiki/Altenkirchen_(district)" title="Altenkirchen (district)">Altenkirchen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>) and in the northwest on the town of <a href="/wiki/Freudenberg,_Westphalia" title="Freudenberg, Westphalia">Freudenberg</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name Siegen comes from the possibly <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> river name Sieg. It is, however, unclear whether there is any relation between this name and the Celtic-<a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> Sicambri (Ger. <i>Sugambrer</i>) people, who in pre-Christian times lived in parts of North Rhine-Westphalia. The first documentary mention of the place called <i>Sigena</i> dates from 1079. The city's history is markedly shaped by <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, which locally began as far back as <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène</a> times. Bearing witness to this longtime industry are the many mines that can be found within city limits.</p>
<p>In 1224, Siegen is mentioned as a newly built town whose ownership was shared by the Count of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Nassau" title="County of Nassau">Nassau</a>, Heinrich the Rich, and <a href="/wiki/Engelbert_II_of_Berg" title="Engelbert II of Berg">Engelbert II of Berg</a>, Archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Cologne" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Cologne">Cologne</a> after the latter transferred one half of the ownership to the former. Moreover, there is proof that the <i>Oberes Schloss</i> ("upper stately home") was already standing at this time. On 19 October 1303, the town was granted <i>Soester Stadtrecht</i>, or Soest town rights. The town remained under the two overlords' joint ownership until 1 February 1381, only then passing fully into Nassau hands.</p>
<p>In the 16th century, the town of Siegen bore a formidable defensive look. It was surrounded by mighty walls with 16 towers and three town gates, and was home to a great <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a>. The town was stricken several times by townwide fires. Documents record such fires in 1592, and from 10 to 20 April 1695.</p>
<p>In 1536, in the buildings that had once housed a <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">Monastery</a>, Heinrich the Rich built a "paedagogium", out of which later grew today's <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Gymnasium</a> at Siegen's <i>Löhrtor</i> (gate). Johann VII of Nassau-Siegen ("Johann the Intermediary") built in 1616 a knightly war school in the still standing old armoury on Burgstraße. He also built on the site of an old Franciscan Monastery the <i>Unteres Schloss</i> ("lower stately home"). His son Johann VIII ("The Younger") returned in 1612 to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, and also wanted to use force to make the towsfolk, too, convert back to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>. In 1632, Nassau-Siegen was conquered by the Swedes, after which his half-brother <a href="/wiki/John_Maurice,_Prince_of_Nassau-Siegen" title="John Maurice, Prince of Nassau-Siegen">John Maurice of Nassau-Siegen</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> commander in Brazil, re-introduced Protestantism. John VIII died in 1638 and was succeeded by his only son <a href="/wiki/Johan_Frans_Desideratus_of_Nassau-Siegen" class="mw-redirect" title="Johan Frans Desideratus of Nassau-Siegen">Johan Frans Desideratus</a>, who had to cede part of Nassau-Siegen (north of the Sieg river) to the Protestant branch of the family. John Maurice's leadership served in 1650–1651 to bring about a split in the Siegerland along denominational lines.</p>
<p>Under Wilhelm Hyacinth of Nassau-Siegen, violence broke out between the two denominational groups. When on 29 March 1707 townsman Friedrich Flender was killed, Wilhelm Hyacinth was himself unseated and furthermore driven out of the town. Wilhelm Hyacinth was the last in the line of Nassau-Siegen's Catholic rulers, dying in 1743. Already in 1734, though, the Reformed line had died out, too, with Friedrich Wilhelm's death, leading <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles VI, Holy Roman Emperor</a> to transfer power in the territory to the Prince of <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Orange" title="Principality of Orange">Orange</a> and the Prince of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Nassau-Diez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Principality of Nassau-Diez (page does not exist)">Nassau-Diez</a>. Under their leadership, mining, the main source of wealth, blossomed, along with <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">silviculture</a>. When Prince William of Orange refused to join the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine" title="Confederation of the Rhine">Confederation of the Rhine</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>, he found himself unseated by the French leader and the Siegerland passed to the Grand Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Berg_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Berg (state)">Berg</a>. After Napoleon's downfall in 1813, however, William I regained his former German inheritances, but in 1815 he ceded them to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> for the Grand Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>. Siegen was assigned to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen_(district)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Siegen (district) (page does not exist)">Siegen district</a>, first in the <a href="/wiki/Koblenz_(region)" title="Koblenz (region)">Koblenz region</a>, and as of 1817 in the <a href="/wiki/Arnsberg_(region)" title="Arnsberg (region)">Arnsberg region</a> within the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Westphalia" title="Province of Westphalia">Province of Westphalia</a>.</p>
<p>Under Prussian rule, Siegen developed into the South Westphalian centre that it is today. On 1 March 1923, Siegen was set apart from the district bearing its name, and became a <a href="/wiki/List_of_German_urban_districts" class="mw-redirect" title="List of German urban districts">district-free town</a>, while still keeping its function as seat of the district of which it was no longer part, and which was itself merged with Wittgenstein district under district reform in 1975. Siegen also lost its district-free status at this time, becoming part of the new Siegen-Wittgenstein district, the name that the district has borne since 1984.</p>
<p>During World War II, Siegen was repeatedly bombed by the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> owing to a crucial railroad that crossed through the town. On 1 April 1945, the US <a href="/wiki/8th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="8th Infantry Division (United States)">8th Infantry Division</a> began the Allied ground assault against Siegen and the dominating military-significant high ground north of the river. The battle against determined German forces at Siegen continued through 2 April 1945, until organized resistance was finally overwhelmed by the division on 3 April 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The town of Siegen belonged in the beginning to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Mainz">Archbishopric of Mainz</a>, or more precisely to its deaconry of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arfeld&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arfeld (page does not exist)">Arfeld</a>. There was a White Nun <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">convent</a> in town that folded in the 15th century. Furthermore, there was a <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> Monastery that was dissolved in 1533 after the Nassau overlords had introduced the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> in 1530. After that, the town was first <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a>, but in 1550, the Principality of Nassau converted to the Reformed Church. Subsequently, Siegen was a predominantly <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> town, but not so strongly that the <a href="/wiki/Counterreformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Counterreformation">Counterreformation</a> could not gain ground in 1623, with one fifth of the townsfolk and those living in the surrounding area becoming Catholic once again. As of 1626, there was once more a monastery in town, this time a <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> one. After passing to Prussia in 1815, the union between Lutheran and Reformed churches was introduced in Siegen, as it was throughout Prussia, but the town's parishes kept their Reformed emphasis. As part of the Westphalian Provincial Church (now the Church of Westphalia), Siegen became the seat of a Superintendency. A similar entity still exists in Siegen, known as the <i>Kirchenkreis</i>, or church district, to which all the city's parishes nowadays belong, unless they are Free Church parishes. This church district encompasses the whole of South Westphalia all the way to <a href="/wiki/Olpe_(district)" title="Olpe (district)">Olpe</a>.</p>
<p>The town's Catholics, even after the Reformation, still belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Mainz">Archbishopric of Mainz</a>. With the restructuring of the Catholic Church early in the 19th century, Siegen was assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Paderborn" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Paderborn">Archbishopric of Paderborn</a> and became the seat of a district <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a>, today a deaconry, to which all the district's Catholic parishes belong. Paderborn was raised to Archbishopric in 1929.</p>
<p>Besides the Roman Catholic Church, Siegen also has a <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek Orthodox">Greek Orthodox</a> parish and a <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanian Orthodox">Romanian Orthodox</a> parish.</p>
<p>Moreover, there are various <a href="/wiki/Free_church" title="Free church">free churches</a> established in Siegen, among them several Evangelical Free Church parishes (<a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">Baptists</a>), an Evangelical-Methodist Church, an <a href="/wiki/Independent_Evangelical-Lutheran_Church" title="Independent Evangelical-Lutheran Church">Independent Evangelical-Lutheran Church</a> (SELK), a <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist</a> parish, several Free Evangelical parishes (FeG), the Achenbach Christian Community, the Christian Assembly, <a href="/wiki/Calvary_Chapel" title="Calvary Chapel">Calvary Chapel</a> and the Siegen-Meiswinkel Mission Community.</p>
<p>Further religious communities in Siegen are <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_Apostolic_Church" title="New Apostolic Church">New Apostolic Church</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>, an Original Christianity community and <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í</a>. Moreover, owing to a great number of Turkish and Arab migrants in Siegen, the Muslim community is also very much in evidence there and there are several mosques in Siegen run by Turkish, Arab and Albanian communities. There are said to be more than 15,000 Muslims out of Siegen's total population.</p>

<p>In 1897, Siegen had 20,000 inhabitants. By 1939, this figure had doubled to 40,000. In the Second World War, the town lost roughly 30% of its inhabitants (12,000). The population had fallen to 28,000 by 1945 and only in 1952 did it once again reach prewar levels.</p>
<p>On 1 January 1975, Siegen's population surpassed 100,000 through the amalgamation of Hüttental (38,867 inhabitants in 1974) and Eiserfeld (22,354 inhabitants in 1974), making it a city. With 117,224 inhabitants it also at the same time reached its all-time highest population. At the end of June 2005, according to the North Rhine-Westphalia State Office for Data Processing and Statistics (<i>Landesamt für Datenverarbeitung und Statistik Nordrhein-Westfalen</i>), 105,328 people made Siegen their main abode. Since 1975, the population has fallen by roughly 10% (12,000).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayors">Mayors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mayors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the city's helm since the 13th century, there have been several mayors of whom evidence survives. In 1304 and 1305, a council ("consules") was mentioned for the first time. As early as 1224, however, documents mention <i>Burgmänner</i> ("Castle Men") as well as three <i>Bürgermeister</i> ("Mayors") who were changed yearly. As of 1500, only two mayors were chosen every year. In the 18th century, the <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a> were achieving ever greater influence in the town. Thereafter the "ruling" or "office-holding" old <a href="/wiki/Shoemaking" title="Shoemaking">shoemaking</a> master represented the "common townsfolk" on the council. The <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">mediaeval</a> town charter was kept right up until 1809, and parts of it even held until 1815, but that year there was a self-endorsing council with 12 members, over which presided the mayor. As of 1824, the suburbs got their own chief administrator who was subordinate to the mayor of Siegen. In 1836, the Prussian municipal system was introduced. After the town was separated from Siegen district in 1923, the mayor was given the title <i>Oberbürgermeister</i> (roughly equivalent to Lord Mayor). The mayor who came to office in 1919 kept his position right through the time of the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>, whereas some mayors in other German and Austrian towns, whose political views were at odds with the <a href="/wiki/Nazis" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazis">Nazis</a>', were removed forcibly.</p>
<p>After the Second World War, the military government of the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Occupation_Zones_in_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Allied Occupation Zones in Germany">British Zone of Occupation</a> installed a new mayor, and in 1946 it introduced a new town charter based on a British model, leading to a "Council of the town" elected by the people, whose members were called <i>Stadtverordnete</i> (town councillors). In the beginning, the council chose one from its midst as the mayor as the town's head and representative, which was an honorary function. Furthermore, as of 1946, the council also began choosing a full-time <i>Oberstadtdirektor</i>, or Higher Town Director, to lead the town's administration.</p>
<p>After Siegen was reunited with the district of the same name in 1975, these two officials bore the titles <i>Bürgermeister</i> and <i>Stadtdirektor</i> respectively. In 1999, this two-headed arrangement was forsaken in favour of a single city leader, with the title <i>Bürgermeister</i>, who serves as chairman or chairwoman of city council, leader of city administration and city representative. He or she is directly elected nowadays.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Coat of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Siegen's civic <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> might be described thus: Argent a town wall embattled gules with an open gateway argent, therein in an inescutcheon azure a lion rampant Or armed and langued gules, issuant from the wall a bishop in robe and mitre azure (trimmed in argent) holding in his hand dexter a crozier argent with crook Or sinister, in his hand sinister, upraised, an open book argent with pages edged gules.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> in the city's arms is the Bishop of <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Cologne" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric of Cologne">Cologne</a>. The wall symbolizes the city itself, and the <a href="/wiki/Inescutcheon" class="mw-redirect" title="Inescutcheon">inescutcheon</a> in the gateway shows the Lion of Nassau in blue and gold (or yellow), which are Nassau's colours. The arms are based on the oldest known town seal, from 1248. The inescutcheon once also had gold billets (upright rectangles) around the lion, but these do not appear in what became the town's (and later city's) coat of arms in 1875.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.ngw.nl/int/dld/s/siegen.htm">[1]</a></p>

<p>In the Apollo-Theater (a downtown former 1930s <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">cinema</a> that underwent remodelling and opened in mid-2007), Siegen has one of the current decade's most important newly built theatres. A controlling interest in this venture is held by TheaterSiegen intendant Magnus Reitschuster.</p>
<p>Since 1992, the media and cultural house Lÿz has been a venue for <a href="/wiki/Cabaret" title="Cabaret">cabaret</a>, music and theatre in Siegen. On the two stages, roughly 150 events appear every season.</p>
<p>Bigger events take place at the <i>Bühne der Stadt</i> ("City's Stage", with about 820 seats), the Siegerlandhalle (1&#160;800&#160;m², 2,300 seats) or the Bismarckhalle. As well, there are regular open-air concerts and productions in the inner yard at <i>Unteres Schloss</i>.</p>

<p>Worth a visit are the Museum for Modern Art, or <i><b>Museum für Gegenwartskunst</b></i> (Tuesday to Sunday 11:00–18:00, Thursday 11:00–20:00, closed Mondays) and the <i><b>Haus Seel</b></i> - City Gallery (permanent changing exhibitions Tuesday to Friday 14:00–18:00, Saturday and Sunday 10:00–13:00 and 14:00–20:00, closed Mondays)<br />
The <i><b>Haus Oranienstraße</b></i> , built at 1900 in the Italian country house style, is attached to the <i><b>Siegerlandmuseum</b></i> since 1993 as an exhibit forum. Here you will find a varied program of temporary exhibitions of all kinds, from archaeological excavations to contemporary avant-garde art. Poet and author readings are also part of the program, such as classic concerts.(Tuesday to Sunday 10:00–18:00, closed on Mondays)</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Beatles-Museum">Beatles-Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Beatles-Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Geisweid is found the Beatles-Museum, run by Harold Krämer. According to the 2000 <a href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Guinness Book of Records">Guinness Book of Records</a>, the 27&#160;m² museum is the smallest public museum in the world devoted to the four <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpudlian</a> musicians. The collection consists of more than 17,000 sound storage media, souvenirs, film posters, <a href="/wiki/Autograph" title="Autograph">autographs</a> and quite a few other things.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the town was about 80% destroyed in the Second World War, Siegen has kept a number of buildings worth seeing, such as the two stately homes, the <i>Oberes Schloss</i> and the <i>Unteres Schloss</i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oberes_Schloss">Oberes Schloss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Oberes Schloss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The castle on the Siegberg was first mentioned in a document in 1259 and was the family seat of the House of Nassau. Since 1905, the <i><b>Siegerlandmuseum</b></i> has been here. Alongside regional exhibits – among them an artificial demonstration mine – are many paintings. The centrepiece of this collection is made up of works by the man who is arguably the best known son of the city, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a>. There is also quite a comprehensive collection of portraits of members of the Houses of Nassau and Orange. The top floor is devoted to 19th-century home décor. A kitchen from the Siegerland, a bedroom and many pieces of furniture from the <a href="/wiki/Biedermeier" title="Biedermeier">Biedermeier</a> era give one an impression of life in the region in bygone days.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Unteres_Schloss">Unteres Schloss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Unteres Schloss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Late in the 17th century, the Lower Stately Home came into being in its current form, somewhat like an open rectangle. The <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Germany">Evangelical</a> line of the House of Nassau-Siegen resided here. Also belonging to the <i>Schloss</i> is the <i>"Dicker Turm"</i>, or "Fat Tower" with a <a href="/wiki/Carillon" title="Carillon">carillon</a>. In 1959, the then town of Siegen built a memorial for victims of war and tyranny. Inside the <i>Schloss</i> is also found the crypt of the Evangelical branch of the Nassau princely house.</p>
<p>Today, the <i>Schloss</i> serves as a state authority building in which the North Rhine-Westphalia Building and Property Establishment, the State Environment Office, the Office for Occupational Health and Safety and the Attendorn Correctional Facility (Siegen Branch Facility) are all housed. By the city's plans, a university is supposed to move into the <i>Unteres Schloss</i> within the next few years, but at this time, the plan is failing to find any financial backing.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Two churches in downtown Siegen are to be brought to the visitor's attention: The <i>Martinikirche</i> dating from the 11th century and the <i>Nikolaikirche</i> at the marketplace with its unusual eight-sided shape and its golden <i>Krönchen</i> ("coronet") – the city's landmark – on the church tower, which is a prominent feature of Siegen's skyline (Siegen is sometimes called <i>Krönchenstadt</i> for this unusual feature). Another church is the <i>Marienkirche</i>, built by the Jesuits between 1702 and 1729. Also worth seeing are Siegen's Old Town and several museums in the city core.</p>

<p>In the southwest of the city core, at the foot of the Ziegenberg, is a spherical gasholder, or <a href="/wiki/Gasometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Gasometer">gasometer</a>, which is protected by law as a monument. It is one of the oldest spherical gasholders still preserved. Another peculiarity is its riveted casing. Only three other such gasholders are known to exist worldwide (all in Germany, in <a href="/wiki/Schwerte" title="Schwerte">Schwerte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Offenburg" title="Offenburg">Offenburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld" title="Bielefeld">Bielefeld</a>). The holder had to be moved a few metres owing to construction on the municipal Autobahn, the <i>Hüttentalstraße</i>, and in the residential area of Ziegenberg, and it now forms the symbolic sun as part of a scale model of the planets above the gasholder.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Things_to_see_nearby">Things to see nearby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Things to see nearby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Worthy of recommendation for tourists are hiking tours and outings in Siegen's scenically charming environs. Also worth a visit are a few small towns around Siegen, such as <a href="/wiki/Hilchenbach" title="Hilchenbach">Hilchenbach</a> or <a href="/wiki/Freudenberg,_Westphalia" title="Freudenberg, Westphalia">Freudenberg</a>. Furthermore, there are a few well known <a href="/wiki/Brewery" title="Brewery">breweries</a> in the area that can be visited, for instance <a href="/wiki/Krombacher" class="mw-redirect" title="Krombacher">Krombacher</a> and Eichener Brauerei in Kreuztal, along with other smaller breweries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cemeteries">Cemeteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Cemeteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Within Siegen's city limits are 36 municipally run <a href="/wiki/Cemetery" title="Cemetery">cemeteries</a>. Ten of them are already closed and are available for burials only because of existing laws. The cemeteries have a total area of 730&#160;000&#160;m² with roughly 65,000 graves. Characteristic of regional cemeteries are hillside graves and a green, parklike layout that even affords wildlife a chance at reoccupying the land.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city sport league includes 160 sport clubs in which roughly 37,000 members are active. National importance was achieved in 2005 by the <a href="/wiki/Sportfreunde_Siegen" title="Sportfreunde Siegen">Sportfreunde Siegen</a> men's <a href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (soccer)">football</a> team at <a href="/wiki/Leimbachstadion" title="Leimbachstadion">Leimbachstadion</a> when they rose from the <i><a href="/wiki/Regionalliga_(football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Regionalliga (football)">Regionalliga Süd</a></i> up to the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Bundesliga_(football)" class="mw-redirect" title="2nd Bundesliga (football)">2nd Bundesliga</a>, although the next year they dropped back down again. As six-time German Champions, the TSV Siegen women's football team was very successful in the 1990s.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tales_and_legends_in_Siegen">Tales and legends in Siegen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Tales and legends in Siegen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Dilldappe</i> is an old mythical creature from the Siegerland. It lives mainly in the Siegerland <i>Hauberg</i> (coöperatively managed woodlots). Early in the 1980s, the writer and cartoonist Matthias Kringe first published a calendar about the creature, written in <i>Siegerländer Platt</i>, the local dialect of German.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_transport">Air transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Air transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the south of the district in the community of <a href="/wiki/Burbach,_North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="Burbach, North Rhine-Westphalia">Burbach</a> lies the <a href="/wiki/Siegerland_Airport" title="Siegerland Airport">Siegerland Airport</a> through which Siegen is connected to many other airports in Germany.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bicycle_transport">Bicycle transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Bicycle transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The city of Siegen lies on <a href="/wiki/European_long-distance_paths" title="European long-distance paths">European long-distance path E1</a> running from the middle of Sweden to <a href="/wiki/Umbria" title="Umbria">Umbria</a> in Italy. There is, however, no organized <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a> trail network in Siegen. Riding in bus lanes is sporadically allowed. Bicycle transport is generally poorly developed in Siegen. Owing to <a href="/wiki/Dual_carriageway" title="Dual carriageway">dual carriageways</a> within the city and nearby, as well as transport planning that rather favours cars, bicycle transport has been somewhat thwarted. Together with the partly hilly topography, there is therefore little in the way of bicycle transport in Siegen.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Siegen_station" title="Siegen station">Siegen station</a> lies at the junction of the following <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railway</a> lines:</p>
<ul>
<li>The two-track <a href="/wiki/Railway_electrification_system" title="Railway electrification system">electrified</a> <a href="/wiki/Ruhr%E2%80%93Sieg_railway" title="Ruhr–Sieg railway">Ruhr–Sieg line</a> <i>(Ruhr-Sieg-Strecke)</i> is used for regional services, usually hourly, of the <i>Ruhr-Sieg-Express</i> (RE 16) and the <i>Ruhr-Sieg-Bahn</i> (RB 91).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Rothaar_Railway" title="Rothaar Railway">Rothaar Railway</a> <i>(Rothaarbahn)</i> is served, usually hourly by <i>Rothaar-Bahn</i> (RB 93) services, connecting in <a href="/wiki/Erndtebr%C3%BCck" title="Erndtebrück">Erndtebrück</a> to <i>Obere Lahntal-Bahn</i> (RB 94/RMV Line 43) services, usually two-hourly, on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upper_Lahn_Valley_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Upper Lahn Valley Railway (page does not exist)">Upper Lahn Valley Railway</a> <i>(Obere Lahntal-Bahn)</i>.</li>
<li>The two-track electrified <a href="/wiki/Dill_Railway" title="Dill Railway">Dill Railway</a> <i>(Dill-Strecke)</i> is served, usually two-hourly, by the <i>Rhein-Sieg-Express</i> (RE 9) and the <i>Main-Sieg-Express</i> (RE 99) and hourly by the <i>Sieg-Dill-Bahn</i> (RB 95) (only to and from <a href="/wiki/Dillenburg" title="Dillenburg">Dillenburg</a>).</li>
<li>The mostly two-track electrified <a href="/wiki/Sieg_Railway" title="Sieg Railway">Sieg line</a> <i>(Siegstrecke)</i> is served, usually hourly, by the <i>Rhein-Sieg-Express</i> (RE 9) and <i>Sieg-Dill-Bahn</i> (RB 95) services (only to and from Au).</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus_transport">Bus transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Bus transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 18 March 1895, the <i>Netphener Omnibusgesellschaft</i> opened the world's first <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">busline</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Petrol" class="mw-redirect" title="Petrol">petrol</a>-powered omnibus, running a service from its base in <a href="/wiki/Netphen" title="Netphen">Netphen</a> to Siegen and Deuz. Today, local road transport offers many regional, express and local buses, along with a night bus service connecting Siegen with its outlying communities and neighbouring towns. They are run by the South Westphalia Transport Services (<i>Verkehrsbetriebe Westfalen-Süd</i>; VWS) whose headquarters are in Siegen.</p>
<p>For the whole of local public transport, the tariff of the South Westphalia Transport Community (<i>Verkehrsgemeinschaft Westfalen-Süd</i>; VGWS) applies, regardless of the North Rhine-Westphalia tariff.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With regard to long-distance roads, the city of Siegen is connected to <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahnen</a> <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_45" title="Bundesautobahn 45">A&#160;45</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dortmund" title="Dortmund">Dortmund</a> – <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_4" title="Bundesautobahn 4">A&#160;4</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> – <a href="/wiki/Olpe,_Germany" title="Olpe, Germany">Olpe</a>), and to Federal Highways (<i><a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe" title="Bundesstraße">Bundesstraßen</a></i>) 54, 54n, 62 and 62n.</p>
<p>Siegen is home to the tallest bridge of the A&#160;45 and one of the highest in Germany, the Siegtalbrücke. Built from 1964 to 1969, it is approximately 96&#160;m high and 1&#160;050&#160;m long.</p>

<p>The shape that the city takes has much to do with the <i>Hüttentalstraße</i> (HTS) (Federal Highways 54n and 62n) which serves as a municipal Autobahn – much of it elevated – that runs through the city area.</p>
<p>The A&#160;4 between <a href="/wiki/Wenden_(Sauerland)" title="Wenden (Sauerland)">Wenden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kreuztal" title="Kreuztal">Kreuztal</a> has been newly built. In the heights over Kreuztal's outlying community of Krombach, it now joins with the HTS. On this 12-kilometre stretch of highway are eight valley bridges as well as ten under- and overpasses. This new part was opened on 1 December 2007.</p>

<p>Siegen is South Westphalia's service and administrative centre. Much of its industry is based on <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">metalworking</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pedestrian_zone" title="Pedestrian zone">Pedestrian precincts</a> in Bahnhofsstraße ("Railway Station Street") and Kölner Tor ("Cologne Gate"), both downtown, have made <a href="/wiki/Shopping" title="Shopping">shopping</a> in Siegen very convenient, as have the shopping centres City-Galerie (opened in 1998) and <i>Sieg Carré</i> (opened in 2006, both downtown) and <i>Siegerlandzentrum</i> (Siegen-Weidenau), and the Marburger Straße shopping street (downtown). In 2005, an <a href="/wiki/IKEA" title="IKEA">IKEA</a> store and a Peek &amp; Cloppenburg location opened.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_institutions">Public institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Public institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Siegen is home to the Siegen-Wittgenstein district administration and location of a Chamber of Industry and Trade (<i>Industrie- und Handelskammer</i>, or IHK, one of 81 such regional institutions in Germany) for the districts of Siegen-Wittgenstein and <a href="/wiki/Olpe_(district)" title="Olpe (district)">Olpe</a>.</p>
<p>Home to the Siegen State Court, a local court and a labour court, the city is also an important court centre.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Siegen, the <i><a href="/wiki/Westdeutscher_Rundfunk" title="Westdeutscher Rundfunk">Westdeutscher Rundfunk</a></i> (WDR) maintains a studio where regional radio and television news for South Westphalia is produced. The daily broadcast goes out in the "South Westphalia" regional window following the programme <i>Aktuelle Stunde</i> (a newsmagazine show) on WDR's third channel. Moreover, WDR also runs a transmission facility for <a href="/wiki/VHF" class="mw-redirect" title="VHF">VHF</a> radio and television (once also <a href="/wiki/Medium_wave" title="Medium wave">medium wave</a>) at the Giersberg.</p>
<p>Daily newspapers in Siegen include not only the <i>Siegener Zeitung</i> but also local editions of the <i>Westfälische Rundschau</i> and the <i>Westfalenpost</i>. All three appear in the morning, although the first was until 2000 an afternoon paper. Furthermore, at the <i>"Obergraben"</i> is found the regional, Radio-<a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">NRW</a>-connected Radio Siegen's studio building.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_institutions">Educational institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Educational institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_education">Higher education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Higher education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Siegen is headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Siegen" title="University of Siegen">University of Siegen</a>, founded on 1 August 1972 as the <i>Gesamthochschule Siegen</i>.</p>
<p>As well, the <a href="/wiki/FOM_%E2%80%93_Hochschule_f%C3%BCr_Oekonomie_und_Management_%E2%80%93_University_of_Applied_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="FOM – Hochschule für Oekonomie und Management – University of Applied Sciences">Fachhochschule für Oekonomie &amp; Management</a> has a study centre, which is shared with the Academy of Administration and Economics.</p>
<p>Siegen also has various general-education and professional schools, and the Siegerlandkolleg.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Rubens_Prize_of_the_City_of_Siegen">The Rubens Prize of the City of Siegen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=54" title="Edit section: The Rubens Prize of the City of Siegen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Rubens Prize of the City of Siegen, founded in 1955, is awarded every five years to a <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painter</a> or <a href="/wiki/Graphic_artist" class="mw-redirect" title="Graphic artist">graphic artist</a> whose life's work has been groundbreaking on the European stage. The title recalls the painter-diplomat <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a>, who expressed in his life's work the thought of European unity, long before it could become a political reality. Peter Paul Rubens – who was born in Siegen, grew up in Cologne and <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>, trained in art in Italy, was esteemed in France and acted as a diplomat in Spain and England – as the main master of European Baroque painting set those artistic and European standards to which the awarding of the prize has been bound since 1957–1958.</p>
<p>Past prizewinners:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hans_Hartung" title="Hans Hartung">Hans Hartung</a> (1957/1958)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Morandi" title="Giorgio Morandi">Giorgio Morandi</a> (1962)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon_(painter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Francis Bacon (painter)">Francis Bacon</a> (1967)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antoni_T%C3%A0pies" title="Antoni Tàpies">Antoni Tàpies</a> (1972)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fritz_Winter" title="Fritz Winter">Fritz Winter</a> (1977)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emil_Schumacher" title="Emil Schumacher">Emil Schumacher</a> (1982)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cy_Twombly" title="Cy Twombly">Cy Twombly</a> (1987)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rupprecht_Geiger" title="Rupprecht Geiger">Rupprecht Geiger</a> (1992)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lucian_Freud" title="Lucian Freud">Lucian Freud</a> (1997)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maria_Lassnig" title="Maria Lassnig">Maria Lassnig</a> (2002)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Bridget_Riley" title="Bridget Riley">Bridget Riley</a> (2012)</li>
</ul>

			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Research university</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>research university</b> is a <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> that expects all its <a href="/wiki/Academic_tenure" title="Academic tenure">tenured</a> and tenure-track <a href="/wiki/Faculty_(academic_staff)" title="Faculty (academic staff)">faculty</a> to continuously engage in <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>, as opposed to merely requiring it as a condition of an initial appointment or tenure.<sup id="cite_ref-Pelikan_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pelikan-1">[1]</a></sup> Such universities can be recognized by their strong focus on innovative research and the prestige of their brand names.<sup id="cite_ref-CollegeSolution_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CollegeSolution-2">[2]</a></sup> On the one hand, research universities strive to recruit faculty who are the most brilliant minds in their <a href="/wiki/Discipline_(academia)" title="Discipline (academia)">disciplines</a> in the world, and their students enjoy the opportunity to learn from such experts.<sup id="cite_ref-Andreatta1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andreatta1-3">[3]</a></sup> On the other hand, new students are often disappointed to realize their undergraduate courses at research universities are overly academic and fail to provide <a href="/wiki/Vocational_education" title="Vocational education">vocational training</a> with immediate "real world" applications; but many employers value degrees from research universities because they know that such coursework develops fundamental skills like <a href="/wiki/Critical_thinking" title="Critical thinking">critical thinking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Andreatta2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andreatta2-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Higher education institutions which are not research universities (or do not aspire to that designation) instead place more emphasis on teaching or other aspects of <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_education" title="Tertiary education">tertiary education</a>, and their faculties are under less pressure to <a href="/wiki/Publish_or_perish" title="Publish or perish">publish or perish</a>.</p>
<p>The concept of the modern research university first arose in 19th century <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and developed into its most advanced and successful form in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, centered on three foundational principles: "integration of teaching and research, academic freedom," and a conception of the nature of research as open-ended and unending.<sup id="cite_ref-Menand_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Menand-5">[5]</a></sup> From the late 20th century to the present, U.S. research universities have dominated most international <a href="/wiki/College_and_university_rankings" title="College and university rankings">college and university rankings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cole_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Roger L. Geiger, a historian specializing in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_higher_education_in_the_United_States" title="History of higher education in the United States">history of higher education in the United States</a>, has argued that "the model for the American research university was established by five <a href="/wiki/Colonial_colleges" title="Colonial colleges">colonial colleges</a> chartered before the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> (<a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia</a>); five state universities (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">Michigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">Wisconsin</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana%E2%80%93Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign">Illinois</a>, and <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">California</a>); and five private institutions conceived from their inception as research universities (<a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford</a>, and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">Chicago</a>)."<sup id="cite_ref-Crow_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crow-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

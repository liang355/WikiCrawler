<p><b>Ulm</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈʔʊlm]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Ulm.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Ulm.ogg" class="internal" title="Ulm.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in the federal German state of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>, situated on the <a href="/wiki/River_Danube" class="mw-redirect" title="River Danube">River Danube</a>. The city, whose population is estimated at almost 120,000 (2015), forms an urban district of its own (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Stadtkreis</span>) and is the administrative seat of the <a href="/wiki/Alb-Donau_(district)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alb-Donau (district)">Alb-Donau district</a>. Founded around 850, Ulm is rich in history and traditions as a former <a href="/wiki/Free_Imperial_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Imperial City">Free Imperial City</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">freie Reichsstadt</span>). Today, it is an economic centre due to its varied industries, and it is the seat of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ulm" title="University of Ulm">University of Ulm</a>. Internationally, Ulm is primarily known for having the church with the tallest steeple in the world (161.53&#160;m or 529.95&#160;ft), the Gothic minster (<a href="/wiki/Ulm_Minster" title="Ulm Minster">Ulm Minster</a>, German: Ulmer Münster), and as the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ulm_Fu%C3%9Fg%C3%A4ngerzone.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ulm_Fu%C3%9Fg%C3%A4ngerzone.jpg/220px-Ulm_Fu%C3%9Fg%C3%A4ngerzone.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ulm_Fu%C3%9Fg%C3%A4ngerzone.jpg/330px-Ulm_Fu%C3%9Fg%C3%A4ngerzone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ulm_Fu%C3%9Fg%C3%A4ngerzone.jpg/440px-Ulm_Fu%C3%9Fg%C3%A4ngerzone.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2112" data-file-height="2816" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ulm_Fu%C3%9Fg%C3%A4ngerzone.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View from the <a href="/wiki/Ulm_Munster" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulm Munster">Münster</a> towards Hirschstraße.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ulm lies at the point where the rivers <a href="/wiki/Blau_(Danube)" title="Blau (Danube)">Blau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iller" title="Iller">Iller</a> join the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>, at an altitude of 479&#160;m (1,571.52&#160;<a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">ft</a>) <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a>. Most parts of the city, including the old town, are situated on the left bank of the Danube; only the districts of Wiblingen, Gögglingen, Donaustetten and Unterweiler lie on the right bank. Across from the old town, on the other side of the river, lies the <a href="/wiki/Twin_cities" title="Twin cities">twin city</a> of <a href="/wiki/Neu-Ulm" title="Neu-Ulm">Neu-Ulm</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, smaller than Ulm and, until 1810, a part of it (population c. 50,000).</p>
<p>Except for the Danube in the south, the city is surrounded by forests and hills which rise to altitudes of over 620 metres (2,034.12 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">feet</a>), some of them part of the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_Alb" class="mw-redirect" title="Swabian Alb">Swabian Alb</a>. South of the Danube, plains and hills finally end in the northern edge of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, which are approximately 100 kilometres (62 miles) from Ulm and are visible from the city on clear days.</p>
<p>The city of Ulm is situated in the northern part of the North Alpine <a href="/wiki/Foreland_basin" title="Foreland basin">Foreland basin</a>, where the basin reaches the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_Alb" class="mw-redirect" title="Swabian Alb">Swabian Alb</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Turritellenplatte" title="Turritellenplatte">Turritellenplatte</a> of Ermingen ("Erminger Turritellenplatte") is a famous palaeontological site of <a href="/wiki/Burdigalian" title="Burdigalian">Burdigalian</a> age.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighboring_communes">Neighboring communes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Neighboring communes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the right (south-eastern) side of Danube and Iller there is the Bavarian district town <a href="/wiki/Neu-Ulm" title="Neu-Ulm">Neu-Ulm</a>. On the left (north-western) side Ulm is almost completely surrounded by the Alb-Danube district. The neighboring communes of Baden-Württemberg are the following: <a href="/wiki/Illerkirchberg" title="Illerkirchberg">Illerkirchberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staig" title="Staig">Staig</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCttisheim" title="Hüttisheim">Hüttisheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erbach_an_der_Donau" title="Erbach an der Donau">Erbach (Donau)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blaubeuren" title="Blaubeuren">Blaubeuren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blaustein" title="Blaustein">Blaustein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dornstadt" title="Dornstadt">Dornstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beimerstetten" title="Beimerstetten">Beimerstetten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Langenau" title="Langenau">Langenau</a> as well as the eastern neighboring community <a href="/wiki/Elchingen" title="Elchingen">Elchingen</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Town_subdivisions">Town subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Town subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The&#160;city&#160;is&#160;divided into 18 districts (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadtteile" class="extiw" title="de:Stadtteile">Stadtteile</a></span>): Ulm-Mitte, Böfingen, Donaustetten, Donautal, Eggingen, Einsingen, Ermingen, Eselsberg, Gögglingen, Grimmelfingen, Jungingen, Lehr, Mähringen, Oststadt, Söflingen (with Harthausen), Unterweiler, Weststadt, and Wiblingen.</p>
<p>Nine districts that were integrated during the latest municipality reform in the 1970s (Eggingen, Einsingen, Ermingen, Gögglingen-Donaustetten, Jungingen, Lehr, Mähringen und Unterweiler). They have own local councils which acquire an important consulting position to the whole city council concerning issues that are related to the prevailing districts. But at the end, final decisions can only be made by the city council of the entire city of Ulm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Free_Imperial_City_of_Ulm" title="Free Imperial City of Ulm">Free Imperial City of Ulm</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Braun_Ulm_UBHD.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Braun_Ulm_UBHD.jpg/220px-Braun_Ulm_UBHD.jpg" width="220" height="54" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Braun_Ulm_UBHD.jpg/330px-Braun_Ulm_UBHD.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Braun_Ulm_UBHD.jpg/440px-Braun_Ulm_UBHD.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2052" data-file-height="500" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Braun_Ulm_UBHD.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ulm in 1572 by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Braun" title="Georg Braun">Frans Hogenberg</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The oldest traceable settlement of the Ulm area began in the early <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period, around 5000 BC. Settlements of this time have been identified at the villages of Eggingen and Lehr, today districts of the city. In the city area of Ulm proper, the oldest find dates from the late Neolithic period. The earliest written mention of Ulm is dated 22 July 854 AD, when King <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_German" title="Louis the German">Louis the German</a> signed a document in the King's palace of "Hulma" in the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Swabia" title="Duchy of Swabia">Duchy of Swabia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-500px_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-500px-2">[2]</a></sup> The city was declared an <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Free_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Free City">Imperial City</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichsstadt</span>) by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Friedrich Barbarossa</a> in 1181.</p>
<p>At&#160;first,&#160;Ulm's significance was due to the privilege of a <i>Königspfalz</i>, a place of accommodation for the medieval German kings and emperors on their frequent travels. Later, Ulm became a city of traders and craftsmen. One of the most important legal documents of the city, an agreement between the Ulm patricians and the trade guilds (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Großer Schwörbrief</span>), dates from 1397. This document, considered an early city constitution, and the beginning of the construction of an enormous church (<a href="/wiki/Ulm_Minster" title="Ulm Minster">Ulm Minster</a>, 1377), financed by the inhabitants of Ulm themselves rather than by the church, demonstrate the assertiveness of Ulm's mediaeval citizens. Ulm blossomed during the 15th and 16th centuries, mostly due to the export of high-quality <a href="/wiki/Textiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Textiles">textiles</a>. The city was situated at the crossroads of important trade routes extending to Italy. These centuries, during which many important buildings were erected, also represented the zenith of art in Ulm, especially for <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptors</a> like <a href="/wiki/Hans_Multscher" title="Hans Multscher">Hans Multscher</a> and <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6rg_Syrlin_the_Elder" title="Jörg Syrlin the Elder">Jörg Syrlin the Elder</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>, Ulm became Protestant (1530). With the establishment of new trade routes following the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> (16th century) and the outbreak and consequences of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> (1618–48), the city began to decline gradually. Around 1700, it was alternately invaded several times by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> soldiers.</p>

<p>In the wars following the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, the city was alternately occupied by French and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> forces, with the former ones destroying the city fortifications. In 1803, it lost the status of Imperial City and was absorbed into <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. During the campaign of 1805, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> managed to trap the invading Austrian army of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Freiherr_Mack_von_Leiberich" class="mw-redirect" title="Karl Freiherr Mack von Leiberich">General Mack</a> and forced it to surrender in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ulm" title="Battle of Ulm">Battle of Ulm</a>. In 1810, Ulm was incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Kingdom of Württemberg">Kingdom of Württemberg</a> and lost its districts on the other bank of the Danube, which came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Neu-Ulm" title="Neu-Ulm">Neu-Ulm</a> (New Ulm).</p>
<p>In the mid-19th century, the city was <a href="/wiki/Fortresses_of_the_German_Confederation" title="Fortresses of the German Confederation">designated a fortress</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a> with huge military construction works directed primarily against the threat of a French invasion. The city became an important centre of <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialisation</a> in southern Germany in the second half of the 19th century, its built-up area now being extended beyond the medieval walls. The construction of the huge minster, which had been interrupted in the 16th century for economic reasons, was resumed and eventually finished (1844–91) in a wave of German national enthusiasm for the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.</p>
<p>From 1933 to 1935, a <a href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Concentration camp">concentration camp</a> primarily for political opponents of the regime was established on the Kuhberg, one of the hills surrounding Ulm. The <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> of Ulm, around 500 people, were first discriminated against and later persecuted; their <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> was torn down after <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a> in November 1938. The sole RAF <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">strategic bombing during World War II</a> against Ulm occurred on December 17, 1944, against the two large <a href="/wiki/Transportation_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation Plan">lorry factories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Meillerwagen" title="Meillerwagen">Magirus</a>-Deutz and <a href="/wiki/Karl_K%C3%A4ssbohrer_Fahrzeugwerke" title="Karl Kässbohrer Fahrzeugwerke">Kässbohrer</a>, as well as other industries, barracks, and depots in Ulm. The Gallwitz Barracks and several military hospitals were among 14 Wehrmacht establishments destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The raid killed 707 Ulm inhabitants and left 25,000 homeless and after all the bombings, over 80% of the medieval city centre lay in ruins.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Most of the city was rebuilt in the plain and simple style of the 1950s and 1960s, but some of the historic landmark buildings have been restored. Due to its almost complete destruction in 1944, the Hirschstraße part of the city primarily consists of modern architecture. Ulm experienced substantial growth in the decades following World War II, with the establishment of large new housing projects and new industrial zones. In 1967, <a href="/wiki/Ulm_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulm University">Ulm University</a> was founded, which proved to be of great importance for the development of the city. Particularly since the 1980s, the transition from classical industry towards the high-tech sector has accelerated, with, for example, the establishment of research centres of companies like <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Siemens AG">Siemens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> and a number of small applied research institutes near the university campus. The city today is still growing, forming a <a href="/wiki/Twin_cities" title="Twin cities">twin city</a> of 170,000 inhabitants together with its neighbouring Bavarian city of <a href="/wiki/Neu-Ulm" title="Neu-Ulm">Neu-Ulm</a>, and seems to benefit from its central position between the cities of <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and thus between the cultural and economic hubs of southern Germany.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirche_St_Georg_Ulm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Kirche_St_Georg_Ulm.jpg/220px-Kirche_St_Georg_Ulm.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Kirche_St_Georg_Ulm.jpg/330px-Kirche_St_Georg_Ulm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Kirche_St_Georg_Ulm.jpg/440px-Kirche_St_Georg_Ulm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirche_St_Georg_Ulm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Saint_George" title="Saint George">Saint George</a>'s Catholic church, Ulm</div>

<p>The city has very old trading traditions dating from medieval times and a long history of industrialisation, beginning with the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway station">railway station</a> in 1850. The most important sector is still classical industry (machinery, especially motor vehicles; <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceuticals" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmaceuticals">pharmaceuticals</a>). The establishment of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ulm" title="University of Ulm">University of Ulm</a> in 1967, which focuses on <a href="/wiki/Biomedicine" title="Biomedicine">biomedicine</a>, the sciences, and engineering, helped support a transition to high-tech industry, especially after the crisis of classical industries in the 1980s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Companies with headquarters in Ulm include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Britax" title="Britax">Britax</a> (Child safety products)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007 the City of Ulm was awarded the European Energy Award for its remarkable local energy management and its efforts to combat climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Examples of these efforts are a <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> power plant operated by the <i>Fernwärme Ulm GmbH</i> (10 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a> electrical output), and the world's biggest <a href="/wiki/Passive_house" title="Passive house">passive house</a> office building, the so-called <i>Energon</i>, located in the "Science City" near the university campus. Moreover, the city of Ulm boasts the second largest <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> production in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> For all new buildings, a strict energy standard (German KFW40 standard) has been mandatory since April 2008. Ulm Minster has been fully powered by renewables since January 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Until the end of 2011 as a European pilot project a self-sustaining <a href="/wiki/Data-centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Data-centre">data-centre</a> will be constructed in the west-city of Ulm.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> There is a solar-powered ferry that crosses the Danube 7 days a week in summer.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The "Bündnis 100% Erneuerbare Energien" was founded in February 2010 with the aim of bringing together the people and organisations seeking to promote the transition to <a href="/wiki/100%25_renewable_energy" title="100% renewable energy">100% renewable energy</a> in Ulm and Neu-Ulm by 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Strassenbahn_ulm_stadion.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Strassenbahn_ulm_stadion.JPG/220px-Strassenbahn_ulm_stadion.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Strassenbahn_ulm_stadion.JPG/330px-Strassenbahn_ulm_stadion.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Strassenbahn_ulm_stadion.JPG/440px-Strassenbahn_ulm_stadion.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>

Tram in Ulm</div>

<p>Ulm is situated at the crossroads of the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_8" title="Bundesautobahn 8">A8 motorway</a> (connecting the principal cities of southern Germany, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_7" title="Bundesautobahn 7">A7 motorway</a> (one of the motorways running from northern to southern Europe).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ulm_Hauptbahnhof" title="Ulm Hauptbahnhof">The city's railway station</a> is served, among other lines, by one of the European train routes (<a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> – <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> – <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> – Ulm – <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> – <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> – <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>). Direct connections to <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> are also available.</p>
<p>Ulm's public transport system is based on several bus lines and a tram line. Construction of a second tram line started in 2015. Several streets in the old town are for the use of pedestrians and cyclists only. Ulm was the first area to be served by the <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Car2Go" title="Car2Go">Car2Go</a> <a href="/wiki/Carsharing" title="Carsharing">carsharing</a> service in 2008. However, the service in Ulm was discontinued at the end of 2014.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_culture">Education and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Education and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ulm_public_library_above.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Ulm_public_library_above.jpg/220px-Ulm_public_library_above.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Ulm_public_library_above.jpg/330px-Ulm_public_library_above.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Ulm_public_library_above.jpg/440px-Ulm_public_library_above.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ulm" title="University of Ulm">University of Ulm</a> was founded in 1967 and focuses on the sciences, medicine, engineering, and mathematics / economics. With about 7,200 students, it is one of the smaller universities in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ulm is also the seat of the city's <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">University of Applied Sciences</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Fachhochschule</span>), founded in 1960 as a public school of engineering. The school also houses numerous students from around the world as part of an international study abroad programme.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1953, <a href="/wiki/Inge_Scholl" title="Inge Scholl">Inge Aicher-Scholl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otl_Aicher" title="Otl Aicher">Otl Aicher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Bill" title="Max Bill">Max Bill</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Ulm_School_of_Design" title="Ulm School of Design">Ulm School of Design</a> (German: Hochschule für Gestaltung - HfG Ulm), a design school in the tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a>, which was, however, closed in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ulm's public library features over 480,000 print media. The city has a public theatre with drama, opera and ballet,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> several small theatres,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and a professional philharmonic orchestra.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic">Historic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Historic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ulm_Minster" title="Ulm Minster">Ulm Minster</a> (German: Ulmer Münster, built 1377–1891) with the world's highest church <a href="/wiki/Steeple_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Steeple (architecture)">steeple</a> (161.53&#160;m (529.95&#160;<a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">ft</a>) high and 768 steps). Choir stalls by Jörg Syrlin the Elder (1469–74), famous sculpture <i><a href="/wiki/Schmerzensmann" class="mw-redirect" title="Schmerzensmann">Schmerzensmann</a></i> (Man of Sorrows) by Hans Multscher (1429).</li>
<li>The old <i>Fischerviertel</i> (fishermen's quarter) on the <a href="/wiki/Blau_(Danube)" title="Blau (Danube)">River Blau</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Timber_framing" title="Timber framing">half-timbered houses</a>, cobblestone streets, and picturesque footbridges. Interesting sights here are the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schiefes_Haus_Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schiefes Haus Ulm (page does not exist)">Schiefes Haus Ulm</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schiefes_Haus_Ulm" class="extiw" title="de:Schiefes Haus Ulm">de</a>)</span></i> (crooked house), a 16th-century house today used as a hotel, and the <i>Alte Münz</i> (Old Mint), a mediaeval building extended in the 16th and 17th centuries in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> style.</li>
<li>The remaining section of the city walls, along the river, with the 14th-century <i>Metzgerturm</i> (butchers' tower) (36&#160;m (118.11&#160;<a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">ft</a>) high).</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Seat_of_local_government" title="Seat of local government">Rathaus</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Town_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Town Hall">Town Hall</a>), built in 1370, featuring some brilliantly coloured murals dating from the mid-16th century. On the gable is an astronomical clock dating from 1520. Restored after serious damage in 1944. Photos of the Rathaus can be seen at <a href="/wiki/Tripadvisor.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Tripadvisor.com">Tripadvisor.com</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i>Krone</i> inn, a medieval complex of several houses (15th / 16th century, extensions from the 19th century), where German kings and emperors were accommodated during their travels.</li>
<li>Several large buildings from the late Middle Ages / renaissance used for various purposes (especially storage of food and weapons), e.g. <i>Schwörhaus, Kornhaus, Salzstadel, Büchsenstadel, Zeughaus, Neuer Bau</i>.</li>
<li>Ulm Federal Fortifications are the largest preserved fortifications and were built from 1842 to 1859 to protect from attacks by France.</li>
<li>The historic district <i>Auf dem Kreuz</i>, a residential area with many buildings from before 1700.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Wiblingen_Abbey" title="Wiblingen Abbey">Wiblingen Abbey</a></i>, a former benedictine abbey in the suburb of Wiblingen in the south of Ulm. The church shows characteristics of late <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Classicism" title="Classicism">classicism</a>. Its library is a masterpiece of <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">rococo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary">Contemporary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Contemporary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Building of the <a href="/wiki/Ulm_School_of_Design" title="Ulm School of Design">Ulm School of Design</a>, (German: Hochschule für Gestaltung - HfG Ulm), an important school of design (1953–68) in the succession of the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a>.</li>
<li><i>Stadthaus</i>, a house for public events built by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Meier" title="Richard Meier">Richard Meier</a>, directly adjacent to the minster.</li>
<li><i>Stadtbibliothek</i>, the building of the <a href="/wiki/Public_library" title="Public library">public library</a> of Ulm was erected by <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_B%C3%B6hm" title="Gottfried Böhm">Gottfried Böhm</a> in the form of a glass pyramid and is situated directly adjacent to the town hall.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Albert Einstein Memorial - A small memorial at the site of the house where <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> was born in Bahnhofstraße, between the present-day newspaper offices and the bank. The house itself and the whole district were destroyed in the <a href="/wiki/Firebombing" title="Firebombing">firebombing</a> of 1944.</li>
<li>Memorial to <a href="/wiki/Hans_Scholl" title="Hans Scholl">Hans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Scholl" title="Sophie Scholl">Sophie Scholl</a> - A small memorial on the Münsterplatz in memory of these two members of the <a href="/wiki/Wei%C3%9Fe_Rose" class="mw-redirect" title="Weiße Rose">Weiße Rose</a> (White Rose, a resistance group opposed to the Nazi regime), who spent their youth in Ulm. Their family's house near the memorial was destroyed in the firebombing of 1944.</li>
<li>The Memorial to Deserters - Located near the University's botanical garden, it commemorates those who deserted from the Wehrmacht during World War II. It was originally erected on September 9, 1989, and was moved to its current location in July 2005. The Monument represents the idea: "Desertion is not reprehensible, war is".</li>
</ul>

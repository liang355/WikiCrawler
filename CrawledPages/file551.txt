<p>The <b>Harvard Crimson</b> are the athletic teams of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. The school's teams compete in <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_I" title="NCAA Division I">NCAA Division I</a>. As of 2013, there were 42 Division I <a href="/wiki/College_athletics" title="College athletics">intercollegiate</a> <a href="/wiki/Varsity_team" title="Varsity team">varsity</a> <a href="/wiki/Sports" class="mw-redirect" title="Sports">sports</a> teams for women and men at Harvard, more than at any other NCAA Division I college in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Like the other <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> universities, Harvard does not offer <a href="/wiki/Athletic_scholarships" class="mw-redirect" title="Athletic scholarships">athletic scholarships</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The school has won national championships in men's football (12), golf (6), men's ice hockey (1), women's lacrosse (1), men's soccer (4), coed sailing (1), women's sailing (1), fencing (1), women's rowing (1), and men's individual wrestling (3)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (September 2015)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> .</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harvard_crimsons_v_brown_2009.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Harvard_crimsons_v_brown_2009.JPG/220px-Harvard_crimsons_v_brown_2009.JPG" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Harvard_crimsons_v_brown_2009.JPG/330px-Harvard_crimsons_v_brown_2009.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Harvard_crimsons_v_brown_2009.JPG/440px-Harvard_crimsons_v_brown_2009.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>

<dd><i>See: <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson_football" title="Harvard Crimson football">Harvard Crimson football</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Stadium" title="Harvard Stadium">Harvard Stadium</a></i></dd>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">football</a> team has competed since 1873. They have won ten <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_I_FBS_National_Football_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="NCAA Division I FBS National Football Championship">national championships</a> when the school competed in what is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Football_Bowl_Subdivision" class="mw-redirect" title="Football Bowl Subdivision">FBS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They are perhaps best known for their <a href="/wiki/Harvard%E2%80%93Yale_football_rivalry" title="Harvard–Yale football rivalry">rivalry</a> with <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a>, known as "<a href="/wiki/Harvard%E2%80%93Yale_football_rivalry" title="Harvard–Yale football rivalry">The Game</a>". Sixteen former players have been inducted into the <a href="/wiki/College_Football_Hall_of_Fame" title="College Football Hall of Fame">College Football Hall of Fame</a>.</p>
<p>Harvard's athletic rivalry with <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a> is intense in every sport in which they meet, coming to a climax each fall in their annual <a href="/wiki/Football_(American)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (American)">football</a> meeting, which dates back to 1875. While Harvard's football team is no longer one of the country's best as it often was a century ago during football's early days (it won the <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bowl_Game" title="Rose Bowl Game">Rose Bowl</a> in 1920), both it and Yale have influenced the way the game is played. In 1903, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Stadium" title="Harvard Stadium">Harvard Stadium</a> introduced a new era into football with the first-ever permanent reinforced concrete stadium of its kind in the country. The stadium's structure actually played a role in the evolution of the college game. Seeking to reduce the alarming number of deaths and serious injuries in the sport, the Father of Football, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Camp" title="Walter Camp">Walter Camp</a> (former captain of the Yale football team), suggested widening the field to open up the game. But the state-of-the-art Harvard Stadium was too narrow to accommodate a wider playing surface. So, other steps had to be taken. Camp would instead support revolutionary new rules for the 1906 season. These included legalizing the <a href="/wiki/Forward_pass" title="Forward pass">forward pass</a>, perhaps the most significant rule change in the sport's history.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:174px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ralph_Horween_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Ralph_Horween_2.jpg" width="172" height="321" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="172" data-file-height="321" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ralph_Horween_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Ralph_Horween" title="Ralph Horween">Ralph Horween</a></div>

<p>In both 1919 and 1920, headed by <a href="/wiki/All-American" class="mw-redirect" title="All-American">All-American</a> brothers <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Horween" title="Arnold Horween">Arnold Horween</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Horween" title="Ralph Horween">Ralph Horween</a>, Harvard was undefeated (9–0–1, as they outscored their competition 229–19, and 8–0–1, respectively).<sup id="cite_ref-googleusercontent1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-googleusercontent1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The team won the 1920 <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bowl_(game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rose Bowl (game)">Rose Bowl</a> against the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oregon" title="University of Oregon">University of Oregon</a>, 7–6.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-google1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google1-17">[17]</a></sup> It was the only bowl appearance in Harvard history.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes1996_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1996-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span id="Men's_golf"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Men.27s_golf">Men's golf</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Men's golf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Harvard has won six <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_I_Men%27s_Golf_Championships" title="NCAA Division I Men's Golf Championships">national collegiate team championships</a>: 1898 (fall),<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> 1899, 1901, 1902 (fall), 1903, and 1904. They have crowned eight individual national champions: James Curtis (1898, fall), Halstead Lindsley (1901), <a href="/wiki/Chandler_Egan" title="Chandler Egan">Chandler Egan</a> (1902, fall), A. L. White (1904), H. H. Wilder (1908), F. C. Davison (1912), Edward Allis (1914), J. W. Hubbell (1916). They won the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> championship in 1975, their only league championship.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_hockey">Ice hockey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ice hockey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Men's_ice_hockey"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Men.27s_ice_hockey">Men's ice hockey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Men's ice hockey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Harvard Crimson men's ice hockey">Harvard Crimson men's ice hockey</a></div>
<p>The men's ice hockey team is one of the oldest <a href="/wiki/College_ice_hockey" title="College ice hockey">intercollegiate ice hockey</a> teams in the United States, having played their first game on January 19, 1898 in a 0–6 loss to <a href="/wiki/Brown_Bears_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Brown Bears men's ice hockey">Brown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Former head coach William H. Claflin and former captain George Owen are credited with the first use of <a href="/wiki/Line_(ice_hockey)" title="Line (ice hockey)">line change</a> in a game against <a href="/wiki/Yale_Bulldogs_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Yale Bulldogs men's ice hockey">Yale</a> on March 3, 1923 when the Crimson substituted entire forward lines instead of individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-22">[22]</a></sup> The men's ice hockey team won the <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Ice_Hockey_Championship#Division_I" title="NCAA Men's Ice Hockey Championship">NCAA Division I Championship</a> on April 1, 1989, defeating the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers men's ice hockey">Minnesota Golden Gophers</a> 4-3 in overtime.<sup id="cite_ref-89ncaa_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89ncaa-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/ECAC_Hockey#Men.27s_ECAC_championship_games" title="ECAC Hockey">Cleary Cup</a>, awarded to the ECAC regular-season champion, is named for former Harvard <a href="/wiki/All-America" title="All-America">All-American</a> hockey player, coach, and athletic director <a href="/wiki/Bill_Cleary_(hockey)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill Cleary (hockey)">Bill Cleary</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_men%27s_national_ice_hockey_team" title="United States men's national ice hockey team">U.S. hockey team</a> that won the <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey_at_the_1960_Winter_Olympics" title="Ice hockey at the 1960 Winter Olympics">1960 Winter Olympics</a> <a href="/wiki/Gold_medal" title="Gold medal">gold medal</a>. The team competes in <a href="/wiki/ECAC_Hockey" title="ECAC Hockey">ECAC Hockey</a> along with six other Ivy League schools and is coached by Harvard alumnus, <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey_at_the_1992_Winter_Olympics" title="Ice hockey at the 1992 Winter Olympics">Olympian</a>, and former <a href="/wiki/NHL" class="mw-redirect" title="NHL">NHL</a> forward, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Donato" title="Ted Donato">Ted Donato</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Donato_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donato-24">[24]</a></sup> Harvard competes in one of the most heated rivalries of college hockey at least twice each season against Harvard's archrival, the <a href="/wiki/Cornell_Big_Red_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Cornell Big Red men's ice hockey">Cornell Big Red</a>, in installments of the <a href="/wiki/Cornell-Harvard_hockey_rivalry" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornell-Harvard hockey rivalry">Cornell-Harvard hockey rivalry</a>. Cornell and Harvard are the most storied programs currently in the ECAC.</p>

<h4><span id="Women's_lacrosse"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Women.27s_lacrosse">Women's lacrosse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Women's lacrosse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/College_lacrosse" title="College lacrosse">College lacrosse</a></div>
<p>Harvard became the first Ivy League institution to win a NCAA championship title in a women's sport, when its women's lacrosse team won the <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Women%27s_Lacrosse_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="NCAA Women's Lacrosse Championship">NCAA championship</a> in 1990. The team was runner-up in 1989 and 1992.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rowing">Rowing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Rowing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Weld_Boathouse" title="Weld Boathouse">Weld Boathouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Association_of_Rowing_Colleges" title="Eastern Association of Rowing Colleges">Eastern Association of Rowing Colleges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Association_of_Women%27s_Rowing_Colleges" title="Eastern Association of Women's Rowing Colleges">Eastern Association of Women's Rowing Colleges</a></div>
<p>Older than <i>The Game</i> by 23 years, the <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Yale_Regatta" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Yale Regatta">Harvard-Yale Regatta</a> was the original source of the <a href="/wiki/College_rivalry" title="College rivalry">athletic rivalry</a> between the two schools. It is held annually in June on the Thames river in eastern Connecticut. Both the Harvard heavyweight and lightweight teams are typically considered to be among the top teams in the country in <a href="/wiki/College_rowing_(United_States)" title="College rowing (United States)">rowing</a>, having won numerous national championships in recent years.</p>
<p>For a time the Harvard lightweight men's team had one of the "oddest" streaks in collegiate sports, having won the national championships in every odd year from 1989 to 2003 (and in no corresponding even years).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The streak was broken when Harvard lost to Yale by almost 4 seconds in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The women's heavyweight rowing team were NCAA Champions in 2003.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby">Rugby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Rugby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Harvard_rugby" title="Harvard rugby">Harvard rugby</a></div>

<p>The Harvard Rugby Football Club is the <a href="/wiki/College_rugby" class="mw-redirect" title="College rugby">college rugby</a> team of Harvard. Founded in 1872, Harvard RFC is Harvard's oldest athletic team, and the oldest rugby club in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-thecrimson.com_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thecrimson.com-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Harvard competes in the <a href="/wiki/Ivy_Rugby_Conference" title="Ivy Rugby Conference">Ivy League</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-americanrugbynews.com_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-americanrugbynews.com-30">[30]</a></sup> and won the Ivy League Championship in 2007, 2003 and 1994, and were National Champions in 1984. In 2009, the men joined a newly established <a href="/wiki/Ivy_Rugby_Conference" title="Ivy Rugby Conference">Ivy Rugby Conference</a> that kicked off as a separate conference in Division 1.<sup id="cite_ref-americanrugbynews.com_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-americanrugbynews.com-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Harvard added women's rugby as a varsity sport in 2013, increasing the number of sports the school offers to 42.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The NCAA classifies women's rugby as an <a href="/wiki/College_rugby#Women.27s_Rugby:_An_Emerging_Varsity_Sport" class="mw-redirect" title="College rugby">emerging varsity sport</a>.</p>

<p>Before the NCAA began its tournament in 1959, the annual national champion was declared by the <a href="/wiki/Intercollegiate_Soccer_Football_Association" title="Intercollegiate Soccer Football Association">Intercollegiate Association Football League</a> (IAFL) — from 1911 to 1926 — and then the <a href="/wiki/Intercollegiate_Soccer_Football_Association" title="Intercollegiate Soccer Football Association">Intercollegiate Soccer Football Association</a> (ISFA), from 1927 to 1958. From 1911 to 1958, Harvard won four national championships.</p>

<p>Women's soccer was elevated from a club to a varsity sport at Harvard in 1977. Bob Scalise, Harvard's current athletic director, was the first head coach. The team has won thirteen Ivy League Championships: 1978, 1979, 1981, 1995, 1996, 1997, 1999, 2008, 2009, 2011, 2013, 2014, and 2016. Currently the coaches are Chris Hamblin, Mike Calise and Kerry Baldwin. <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://gocrimson.com/sports/wsoc/index">http://gocrimson.com/sports/wsoc/index</a></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wrestling">Wrestling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Wrestling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Intercollegiate_Wrestling_Association" title="Eastern Intercollegiate Wrestling Association">Eastern Intercollegiate Wrestling Association</a></div>
<p>Head coach <a href="/wiki/Jay_Weiss" title="Jay Weiss">Jay Weiss</a> and his coaching staff have put the Harvard Wrestling program on the map. First established in 1913, the Harvard wrestling team is celebrating its 100th anniversary in 2013-14, making the Crimson one of the oldest <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_wrestling" title="Collegiate wrestling">collegiate wrestling</a> programs in the nation. As part of that celebration GoCrimson.com has released the "Top Moments in Harvard Wrestling History" in collaboration with the Harvard Crimson Wrestling team. The team practices and competes in the <a href="/wiki/Malkin_Athletic_Center" title="Malkin Athletic Center">Malkin Athletic Center</a>. In 1938, The Harvard Wrestling team featured the program's first national champion, John Harkness. <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Jantzen" title="Jesse Jantzen">Jesse Jantzen</a> ’04 graduated in 2004 as the most accomplished wrestler in Harvard history. The Crimson’s all-time leader in wins (132), win percentage (.910) and pins (50), Jantzen’s accomplishments also include: 2004 NCAA Champion, 2004 NCAA Most Outstanding Wrestler, 3 Time NCAA All-American, 3 Time EIWA Champion, 4 Time NCAA Qualifier.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harvard has several athletic facilities, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lavietes_Pavilion" title="Lavietes Pavilion">Lavietes Pavilion</a>, a multi-purpose arena and home to the basketball teams. The Malkin Athletic Center, known as the "MAC," serves both as the university's primary recreation facility and as home to the varsity men's and women's volleyball, men's and women's fencing, and wrestling teams. The five-story building includes two cardio rooms, an <a href="/wiki/Olympic-size_swimming_pool" title="Olympic-size swimming pool">Olympic-size swimming pool</a>, a smaller pool for aquaerobics and other activities, a mezzanine, where all types of classes are held at all hours of the day, and an indoor cycling studio, three weight rooms, and a three-court gym floor to play basketball. The MAC also offers personal trainers and specialty classes. The MAC is also home to volleyball, fencing, and wrestling. The offices of several of the school's varsity coaches are also in the MAC.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Weld_Boathouse" title="Weld Boathouse">Weld Boathouse</a> and Newell Boathouse house the women's and men's rowing teams, respectively. The men's crew also uses the Red Top complex in Ledyard, CT, as their training camp for the annual <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Yale_Regatta" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Yale Regatta">Harvard-Yale Regatta</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bright_Hockey_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Bright Hockey Center">Bright Hockey Center</a> hosts the ice hockey teams, and the Murr Center serves both as a home for the squash and tennis teams as well as a strength and conditioning center for all athletic sports.</p>
<p>Other facilities include: <a href="/wiki/Joseph_J._O%27Donnell_Field" title="Joseph J. O'Donnell Field">Joseph J. O'Donnell Field</a><sup id="cite_ref-ODonnellField_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODonnellField-1">[1]</a></sup> (baseball), <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Stadium" title="Harvard Stadium">Harvard Stadium</a> (football), Cumnock Turf and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Stadium" title="Harvard Stadium">Harvard Stadium</a> (lacrosse), <a href="/wiki/Soldiers_Field_Soccer_Stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Soldiers Field Soccer Stadium">Soldiers Field Soccer Stadium</a><sup id="cite_ref-SoldiersFieldSoccer_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SoldiersFieldSoccer-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ohiri_Field" title="Ohiri Field">Ohiri Field</a> (soccer), and Blodgett Pool (swimming and diving, water polo).</p>

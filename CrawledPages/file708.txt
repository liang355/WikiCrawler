			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Leopold and Rudolf Blaschka</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Leopold Blaschka</b> (27 May 1822 – 3 July 1895) and his son <b>Rudolf Blaschka</b> (17 June 1857 – 1 May 1939) were <a href="/wiki/Dresden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Dresden, Germany">Dresden, Germany</a> glass artists native to the Bohemian (Czech)–German borderland, known for the production of biological models such as the <a href="/wiki/Glass_sea_creatures" title="Glass sea creatures">glass sea creatures</a> and their more famous successors the <a href="/wiki/Glass_Flowers" title="Glass Flowers">Glass Flowers</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Born_to_glass">Born to glass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_and_Rudolf_Blaschka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Born to glass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Blaschka family is old in honor and skill in world of glass artistry - originating from <a href="/wiki/Josef%C5%AFv_D%C5%AFl_(Jablonec_nad_Nisou_District)" title="Josefův Důl (Jablonec nad Nisou District)">Josefuv Dul</a> (Antoniwald) in the Iser or <a href="/wiki/Izera_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Izera Mountains">Izera Mountains</a>, a region known for processing glass, metals and gems<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup> - their business having spanned more than 300 years, working in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-designmuseum_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-designmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup> Bohemia (in an area now in the Czech Republic) and Germany by the time Leopold and Rudolf achieved fame.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harvell.2C_Drew_2016_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvell.2C_Drew_2016-4">[4]</a></sup> This, unquestionably, is the root of Leopold's famous quote - written in an 1889 letter to <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lee_Ware" title="Mary Lee Ware">Mary Lee Ware</a> - regarding the source of his skill: <i>"Many people think that we have some secret apparatus by which we can squeeze glass suddenly into these forms, but it is not so. We have tact. My son Rudolf has more than I have, because he is my son, and tact increases in every generation. The only way to become a glass modeler of skill, I have often said to people, is to get a good great-grandfather who loved glass; then he is to have a son with like tastes; he is to be your grandfather. He in turn will have a son who must, as your father, be passionately fond of glass. You, as his son, can then try your hand, and it is your own fault if you do not succeed. But, if you do not have such ancestors, it is not your fault. My grandfather was the most widely known glassworker in Bohemia."</i><sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vitreous_tract_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vitreous_tract-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/%C4%8Cesk%C3%BD_Dub" title="Český Dub">Český Dub</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, and one of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Blaschke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Joseph Blaschke (page does not exist)">Joseph Blaschke</a>'s three sons,<sup id="cite_ref-Harvell.2C_Drew_2016_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvell.2C_Drew_2016-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-heraldica_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica-7">[7]</a></sup> Leopold, alone of his brothers, displayed the necessary artistic skills as a child, and was apprenticed to a <a href="/wiki/Goldsmith" title="Goldsmith">goldsmith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gemcutter" title="Gemcutter">gemcutter</a> in <a href="/wiki/Turnov" title="Turnov">Turnov</a>, a town in the <a href="/wiki/Liberec_Region" title="Liberec Region">Liberec Region</a> of today's Czech Republic.<sup id="cite_ref-heraldica_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica-7">[7]</a></sup> He then joined the family business, which produced glass ornaments and <a href="/wiki/Glass_eye" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass eye">glass eyes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-designmuseum_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-designmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup> He developed a technique which he termed "glass-spinning", which permitted the construction of highly precise and detailed works in glass. He also <a href="/wiki/Latinised" class="mw-redirect" title="Latinised">Latinised</a> his family name to "Blaschka", and began to focus the business on the manufacture of glass eyes.<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Leopold's son Josef Augustin Blaschka - presumably named after Leopold's father - was born within four years of his marriage to Caroline Zimmermann in 1846.<sup id="cite_ref-heraldica_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica-7">[7]</a></sup> Tragically, however, in 1850 both Carolina and little Josef died, the latter to a cholera epidemic, and then, a year later in 1851, Leopold lost in father.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-heraldica_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_marine_invertebrates">Glass marine invertebrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_and_Rudolf_Blaschka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Glass marine invertebrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Glass_sea_creatures" title="Glass sea creatures">Glass sea creatures</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blaschka_4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Blaschka_4.jpg/220px-Blaschka_4.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Blaschka_4.jpg/330px-Blaschka_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Blaschka_4.jpg/440px-Blaschka_4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1504" data-file-height="954" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blaschka_4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Blaschka model of jellyfish</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1853, Leopold Blaschka - grief-stricken and in need of a vacation - traveled to the United States. En route, however, the ship was becalmed and lay still upon the sea for two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-cmog_invertebrates_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmog_invertebrates-9">[9]</a></sup> During this period of forced idleness, Leopold studied and sketched the local <a href="/wiki/Marine_invertebrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine invertebrate">marine invertebrate</a> population, intrigued by the transparency of their bodies which was next of kin to the glass his family had long worked.<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup> Indeed, Leopold felt a sense of quiet, inspirational, wonder at these luminescent ocean dwellers, a sense which he we recorded and has been since translated by a Henri Reiling: <i>"It is a beautiful night in May. Hopeful, we look out over the darkness of the sea, which is as smooth as a mirror; there emerges all around in various places a flashlike bundle of light beams, as if it is surrounded by thousands of sparks, that form true bundles of fire and of other bright lighting spots, and the seemingly mirrored stars. There emerges close before us a small spot in a sharp greenish light, which becomes ever larger and larger and finally becomes a bright shining sunlike figure."</i><sup id="cite_ref-cmog_invertebrates_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmog_invertebrates-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>This sense of wonder would fuel his later work, but in the meantime and upon his return to <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>, Leopold focused on his family business - a family which had grown by one as, in 1854, Leopold married Caroline Riegel<sup id="cite_ref-heraldica_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica-7">[7]</a></sup> - producing glass eyes, costume ornaments, lab equipment, and other such fancy goods and specialty items that only a master <a href="/wiki/Lampworking" title="Lampworking">lampworker</a> could accomplish.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_herbaria_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_herbaria-10">[10]</a></sup> However, like anyone, he did have free time, and his hobby was to make glass models of plants – as opposed to invertebrates. This would, many years later, become a base for the fabled <i>Ware Collection of Blaschka Glass Models of Plants</i> (otherwise known as the <a href="/wiki/Glass_Flowers" title="Glass Flowers">Glass Flowers</a>), but, for the moment, such artistry was naught but an amusing and profitless pastime done between his various commissions.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_herbaria_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_herbaria-10">[10]</a></sup> Yet, unsurprisingly, given their stunning quality, this amusing hobby – itself born out of seeking consolation in nature upon his first wife's death – attracted attention. Aristocratic attention, as it turned out, specifically the eyes of Prince Camille de <a href="/wiki/House_of_Rohan" title="House of Rohan">Rohan</a> who arranged to meet with Leopold at <a href="/wiki/Sychrov_Castle" title="Sychrov Castle">Sychrov Castle</a> in 1857 - the same year that the Leopold &amp; Caroline's son Rudolf was born.<sup id="cite_ref-heraldica_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica-7">[7]</a></sup> Being something of a naturalist himself, the Prince commissioned Leopold to craft 100 glass <a href="/wiki/Orchid" class="mw-redirect" title="Orchid">orchids</a> for his private collection <sup id="cite_ref-designmuseum_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-designmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup> and, being more than a little impressed by the mastery Leopold's work, in 1862, "the prince exhibited about 100 models of orchids and other exotic plants, which he displayed on two artificial tree trunks in his palace in Prague,"<sup id="cite_ref-cmog_invertebrates_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmog_invertebrates-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-heraldica_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heraldica-7">[7]</a></sup> a fateful act which brought the skill of the Blaschkas to the attention of another - man whom the Prince had actually once introduced to Leopold: a certain Professor <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Reichenbach" title="Ludwig Reichenbach">Ludwig Reichenbach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sciencefriday_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencefriday-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blaschka_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Blaschka_1.jpg/220px-Blaschka_1.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Blaschka_1.jpg/330px-Blaschka_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Blaschka_1.jpg/440px-Blaschka_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1120" data-file-height="700" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blaschka_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Blaschka model of sea anemones</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The director of the natural history museum in Dresden, Prof. Reichenbach, was enchanted by the botanical models and positive that Leopold held the key to ending his own inability to properly showcase marine invertebrates, for in the 19th century the only practiced method of showcasing them was to take a live specimen and place it in a sealed jar of alcohol.<sup id="cite_ref-cmog_archive_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmog_archive-12">[12]</a></sup> This of course killed it but, more importantly, time and their lack of hard parts eventually rendered them into little more than colorless floating blobs of jelly. Neither pretty nor a terribly effective teaching tool, the Dresden museum director desired something more, specifically 3D colored models of marine invertebrates that were both lifelike and able to stand the test of time<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_herbaria_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_herbaria-10">[10]</a></sup> and thus, in 1863<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup> Reichenbach convinced and commissioned Leopold to produce twelve model <a href="/wiki/Sea_anemone" title="Sea anemone">sea anemones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-designmuseum_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-designmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sciencefriday_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencefriday-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-berlin_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-berlin-13">[13]</a></sup> These marine models, hailed as "an artistic marvel in the field of science and a scientific marvel in the field of art,"<sup id="cite_ref-wales_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wales-14">[14]</a></sup> were a great improvement on previous methods of presenting such creatures: drawings, pressing, photographs and <a href="/wiki/Papier-m%C3%A2ch%C3%A9" title="Papier-mâché">papier-mâché</a> or wax models.<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Knowing this and thrilled with his newly acquired set of glass sea creatures, Reichenbach advised Leopold to drop his current and generations long family business of glass fancy goods and the like in favor of selling glass marine invertebrates to museums, aquaria, universities, and private collectors<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-designmuseum_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-designmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup> - advice which prompted the swiftly and highly lucrative mail-order business that followed. Indeed, "the world had never seen anything quite like the beautiful, scientifically accurate Blaschka models"<sup id="cite_ref-atlasobscura_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlasobscura-15">[15]</a></sup> and yet they were available via so common a means as via mail-order per one's local card catalog. Not glorious, perhaps, but highly effective, and museums and universities began purchasing them en masse to put on display much as Prof. Reichenbach had - for natural history museum directors the world over had the same marine invertebrate showcasing problem.<sup id="cite_ref-cmog_invertebrates_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmog_invertebrates-9">[9]</a></sup> In short, Blaschkas Glass sea creature mail-order enterprise succeeded for two reasons: 1- there was a huge and global demand; 2- they were the only and best glass artists capable of crafting literally scientifically flawless models. Initially the designs for these were based on drawings in books, but Leopold was soon able to use his earlier drawings to produce highly detailed models of other species,<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup> and his reputation quickly spread; the task additionally furthering the training of his son and apprentice (and eventual successor), Rudolf Blaschka.<sup id="cite_ref-designmuseum_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-designmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup> Indeed, a year after the success of the glass sea anemones, the family moved to family moved to <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a> to give young Rudolf better educational opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contact_with_Harvard">Contact with Harvard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_and_Rudolf_Blaschka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contact with Harvard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg/220px-GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg/330px-GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg/440px-GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1610" data-file-height="2090" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Part of the Harvard Glass Flowers collection</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In about 1880, Rudolf began assisting his father with the models. In that year, they produced 131 Glass sea creature models for the <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a> Society of Natural History Museum (now the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Science_(Boston)" title="Museum of Science (Boston)">Museum of Science</a>). These models, along with the ones purchased by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard's</a> <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology" title="Museum of Comparative Zoology">Museum of Comparative Zoology</a>, were seen by Professor <a href="/wiki/George_Lincoln_Goodale" title="George Lincoln Goodale">George Lincoln Goodale</a>, who was in the process of setting up the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Botanical_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Botanical Museum">Harvard Botanical Museum</a>. In 1886 the Blaschkas were approached by Goodale, who had come to Dresden for the sole purpose of finding them, with a request to make a series of glass botanical models for Harvard; some reports claim that Goodale saw a few glass orchids in the room where they met, surviving from the work two decades earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup> Leopold was unwilling as his current business of selling Glass sea creatures was hugely successful but, eventually, the famed glass artists agreed to send test-models to the U.S. and, although badly damaged by U.S. <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">Customs</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-cmog_flowers_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmog_flowers-16">[16]</a></sup> but Goodale nonetheless appreciated the fragmentary craftwork and showed them widely – convinced that Blaschka glass art was a more than worthy educational investment. His reasons for wanting the models was simple: At that time, Harvard was the global center of botanical study. As such, Goodale wanted the best, but the only used method was showcasing pressed and carefully labeled specimens — a methodology that offered a twofold problem: being pressed, the specimens were two-dimensional and tended to lose their color. Hence they were hardly the ideal teaching tools.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_flowers_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_flowers-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-goodale_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodale-18">[18]</a></sup> However, having already seen Harvard's recently procured glass marine invertebrates, Professor Goodale - like Professor Reichenbach before him - realized that glass flowers would solve his problem<sup id="cite_ref-goodale_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodale-18">[18]</a></sup> – for, as glass, they were three-dimensional and would retain their color.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mary_Lee_Ware.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Mary_Lee_Ware.jpg/220px-Mary_Lee_Ware.jpg" width="220" height="312" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Mary_Lee_Ware.jpg/330px-Mary_Lee_Ware.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Mary_Lee_Ware.jpg/440px-Mary_Lee_Ware.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1446" data-file-height="2053" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mary_Lee_Ware.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mary Lee Ware – patron sponsor of the Glass Flowers</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Investments require funds, and to cover the expensive enterprise Goodale approached his former student <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lee_Ware" title="Mary Lee Ware">Mary Lee Ware</a> and her mother <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Cabot_(Lee)_Ware&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Elizabeth Cabot (Lee) Ware (page does not exist)">Elizabeth C. Ware</a>; they were independently wealthy and already liberal benefactors of Harvard's botanical department.<sup id="cite_ref-wiley_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-19">[19]</a></sup> Mary convinced her mother to agree to underwrite the consignment of the uncannily lifelike models that enchanted them both.<sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1887 the Blaschkas contracted to spend half-time producing the models for Harvard.<sup id="cite_ref-emison_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emison-20">[20]</a></sup> They continued to spend their remaining time making marine invertebrate models. New arrangements were made to send the models directly to Harvard, where museum staff could open them safely, observed by Customs staff.<sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1890 the Blaschkas signed an exclusive ten-year contract with Harvard to make <a href="/wiki/Glass_Flowers" title="Glass Flowers">glass flowers</a> for 8,800 marks per year.<sup id="cite_ref-emison_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emison-20">[20]</a></sup> They modeled a great range of plants (finally, 164 taxonomic families) and plant parts (flowers, leaves, fruits, roots). Some were shown during <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> by insects, others diseased in various ways.<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_of_the_Glass_Flowers">Production of the Glass Flowers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_and_Rudolf_Blaschka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production of the Glass Flowers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early in the making of the Glass Flowers, Mary Lee Ware engaged in correspondence with Professor Goodale regarding the making of the collection, one of which contained a remark of Leopold's regarding the false rumor that secret methods were used in the making of the Glass Flowers: <i>"Many people think that we have some secret apparatus by which we can squeeze glass suddenly into these forms, but it is not so. We have tact. My son Rudolf has more than I have, because he is my son, and tact increases in every generation."</i><sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vitreous_tract_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vitreous_tract-6">[6]</a></sup> On this trend, he also once said that <i>"One cannot hurry glass. It will take its own time. If we try to hasten it beyond its limits, it resists and no longer obeys us. We have to humor it."</i><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Blaschkas used a mixture of clear and coloured glass, sometimes supported with wire, to produce their models.<sup id="cite_ref-emison_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emison-20">[20]</a></sup> Many pieces were painted, this work being entirely given to Rudolf.<sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup> In order to represent plants which were not native to the Dresden area, the two studied the exotic plant collections at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pillnitz_Palace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pillnitz Palace (page does not exist)">Pillnitz Palace</a><sup id="cite_ref-emison_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emison-20">[20]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_der_Technischen_Universit%C3%A4t_Dresden" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanischer Garten der Technischen Universität Dresden">Dresden Botanical Garden</a>, and also grew some from seed sent from the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-emison_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emison-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1892, Rudolf was sent on a trip to the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and the U.S. to study additional plants, making extensive drawings and notes.<sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rudolf made a second trip to the U.S. in 1895. While he was overseas - and eleven years into the project - Leopold died.<sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup> Rudolf continued to work alone, slowing down production so as to achieve higher levels of botanical perfection.<sup id="cite_ref-popular_science_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popular_science-22">[22]</a></sup> By the early twentieth century, he found that he was unable to buy glass of suitably high quality, and so started making his own.<sup id="cite_ref-emison_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emison-20">[20]</a></sup> This was confirmed by Mary Lee Ware during her 1908 visit to Rudolf in a letter she wrote to the second director of the Botanical Museum, Professor <a href="/wiki/Oakes_Ames_(botanist)" title="Oakes Ames (botanist)">Oakes Ames</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-schultes_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schultes-5">[5]</a></sup> This letter appears to confirm the previous statement of Leopold's regarding his son; Miss Ware writes, <i>"One change in the character of his work and, consequently in the time necessary to accomplish results since I was last here, is very noteworthy. At that time...he bought most of his glass and was just beginning to make some, and his finish was in paint. Now he himself makes a large part of the glass and all the enamels, which he powders to use as paint."</i><sup id="cite_ref-daston_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daston-23">[23]</a></sup> This missive to Professor Ames was published in January 9, 1961 by the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Herbaria" title="Harvard University Herbaria">Harvard University Herbaria</a> - Botanical Museum Leaflets, Harvard University Vol. 19, No. 6 - under the title "How Were The Glass Flowers Made?"<sup id="cite_ref-leaflet_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leaflet-24">[24]</a></sup> Indeed, and in addition to funding and visiting, Mary took a fairly active role in the project's progress, going so far as to personally unpack each model<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_ware_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_ware-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cmog_botanical_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmog_botanical-26">[26]</a></sup> and making arrangements for Rudolph's fieldwork in the U.S. and Jamaica<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_ware_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_ware-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cmog_botanical_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmog_botanical-26">[26]</a></sup> – the purpose of such trips being to gather and study various plant specimens before returning to the old style Bohemian lamp-working table at which he (and Leopold) worked.</p>
<p>Rudolf continued making models for Harvard until 1938. By then aged 80, old and weary, he announced that he would retire.<sup id="cite_ref-life_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-life-27">[27]</a></sup> Neither he nor his father had taken on an <a href="/wiki/Apprentice" class="mw-redirect" title="Apprentice">apprentice</a>, and Rudolf left no successor - he and his wife, Frieda, being childless.<sup id="cite_ref-urania_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urania-1">[1]</a></sup> For Harvard alone, Leopold and Rudolf made approximately 4,400 models, 780 showing species at life-size, with others showing magnified details; under 75% are, as of May 21, 2016, on display at the HMNH, (the exhibit itself dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Eliot_Ware" title="Charles Eliot Ware">Dr. Charles Eliot Ware</a>, the father of Mary Ware and husband of Elizabeth Ware); the old exhibit contained 3000 models but this number was reduced for renovation purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-emison_20-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emison-20">[20]</a></sup> Unlike the Glass sea creatures – "a profitable global mail-order business"<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_flowers_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_flowers-17">[17]</a></sup> – the Glass Flowers were commissioned solely for and are unique to Harvard.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leopold_and_Rudolf_Blaschka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Ultimately, Leopold and Rudolf left a legacy of over ten thousand glass models scattered across museums the world over, mostly maritime but with the Flowers as the most famous, all continuing to inspire both scientists and glass artists alike.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> They and their spouses were buried together in the Hosterwitz cemetery in Dresden. Finally and in welcome addition, "the Corning Museum and the Botanical Museum of Harvard University jointly purchased the remaining Blaschka studio materials from Gertrud Pones, Mrs. Rudolf (Frieda) Blaschka’s niece, in 1993,"<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> thus granting the world access to their workbench and sketches as well; in part due to the pure good fortune that said studio survived the <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Dresden in World War II">Bombing of Dresden in World War II</a> (luck not shared by the original collection of glass sea anemones purchased by Professor Reichenbach).</p>

<p>For a several month period beginning in 2015 and set to end in the early summer of 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Harvard Museum of Natural History">Harvard Museum of Natural History</a> (HMNH) set up a "temporary display highlighting twenty-seven of the most popular plant models as well as some items from the Blaschka archives"<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_flowers_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_flowers-17">[17]</a></sup> while the main Glass Flowers exhibit is under renovation. This exhibit was unique because it was the first recorded time that the Glass Flowers have been jointly exhibited with the Blaschkas' earlier Glass sea creature models in a major and equal display.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_flowers_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_flowers-17">[17]</a></sup> The renovation exhibit was dismantled when, on May 21, 2016, the main Glass Flowers exhibit reopened. The models of marine invertebrates remain, however and as before, as a permanent exhibit.</p>

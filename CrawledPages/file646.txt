<p><b>Concord</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">ɔːr</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><small>locally</small> <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="/ŋ/: 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a town in <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_County,_Massachusetts" title="Middlesex County, Massachusetts">Middlesex County, Massachusetts</a>, in the United States. At the 2010 census, the town population was 17,668.<sup id="cite_ref-Census_2010_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a> considers Concord part of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Boston" title="Greater Boston">Greater Boston</a>. The town center is located near where the <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_River" title="Sudbury River">Sudbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assabet_River" title="Assabet River">Assabet</a> rivers forms the <a href="/wiki/Concord_River" title="Concord River">Concord River</a>.</p>
<p>The area which became the town of Concord was originally known as <b>Musketaquid</b>, an <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a> word for "grassy plain". It was one of the scenes of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lexington_and_Concord" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Lexington and Concord">Battle of Lexington and Concord</a>, the initial conflict in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. It developed into a remarkably rich literary center during the mid-nineteenth century. Featured were <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Hawthorne" title="Nathaniel Hawthorne">Nathaniel Hawthorne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amos_Bronson_Alcott" title="Amos Bronson Alcott">Amos Bronson Alcott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisa_May_Alcott" title="Louisa May Alcott">Louisa May Alcott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a>, all of whose homes are preserved in modern-day Concord. The now-ubiquitous <a href="/wiki/Concord_grape" title="Concord grape">Concord grape</a> was developed here.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concord,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory_and_founding">Prehistory and founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concord,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prehistory and founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Egg_Rock_Inscription.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Egg_Rock_Inscription.jpg/220px-Egg_Rock_Inscription.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Egg_Rock_Inscription.jpg/330px-Egg_Rock_Inscription.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Egg_Rock_Inscription.jpg/440px-Egg_Rock_Inscription.jpg 2x" data-file-width="495" data-file-height="371" /></a>

<p>The area which became the town of Concord was originally known as "Musketaquid", situated at the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_River" title="Sudbury River">Sudbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assabet_River" title="Assabet River">Assabet</a> rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-Musketaquid_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Musketaquid-2">[2]</a></sup> The name Musketaquid was an <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a> word for "grassy plain", fitting the area's low-lying <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kettle_hole" class="mw-redirect" title="Kettle hole">kettle holes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Native Americans had cultivated corn crops there; the rivers were rich with fish and the land was lush and arable.<sup id="cite_ref-Bulkeley_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bulkeley-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the area was largely depopulated by the <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> that swept across the Americas after the arrival of Europeans.<sup id="cite_ref-Shattuck_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shattuck-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1635, a group of settlers from Britain led by Rev. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bulkley" title="Peter Bulkley">Peter Bulkley</a> and Major <a href="/wiki/Simon_Willard_(First_generation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Simon Willard (First generation)">Simon Willard</a> negotiated a land purchase with the remnants of the local tribe. Bulkley was an influential religious leader who "carried a good number of planters with him into the woods";<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Willard was a canny trader who spoke the Algonquian language and had gained the trust of Native Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They exchanged <a href="/wiki/Wampum" title="Wampum">wampum</a>, hatchets, knives, cloth, and other useful items for the six-square-mile purchase which formed the basis of the new town, called "Concord" in appreciation of the peaceful acquisition.<sup id="cite_ref-Musketaquid_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Musketaquid-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_of_Lexington_and_Concord">Battle of Lexington and Concord</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concord,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Battle of Lexington and Concord">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">Battles of Lexington and Concord</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lexington_and_Concord" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Lexington and Concord">Battle of Lexington and Concord</a> was the first conflict in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. On April 19, 1775, a force of <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> <a href="/wiki/Regular_army" title="Regular army">regulars</a> marched from <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> to Concord to capture a cache of arms that was reportedly stored in the town. Forewarned by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere" title="Paul Revere">Paul Revere</a> and other messengers, the colonists mustered in opposition. Following an early-morning skirmish at <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Massachusetts" title="Lexington, Massachusetts">Lexington</a>, where the first shots of the battle were fired, the British expedition under the command of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Smith_(British_Army_officer)" title="Francis Smith (British Army officer)">Lt. Col. Francis Smith</a> advanced to Concord. There, colonists from Concord and surrounding towns (notably a highly drilled company from <a href="/wiki/Acton,_Massachusetts" title="Acton, Massachusetts">Acton</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Davis_(soldier)" title="Isaac Davis (soldier)">Isaac Davis</a>) <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Concord#The_North_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Concord">repulsed a British detachment</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Old_North_Bridge" title="Old North Bridge">Old North Bridge</a> and forced the British troops to retreat.<sup id="cite_ref-LOC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOC-9">[9]</a></sup> Subsequently, militia arriving from across the region <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord#Return_march" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">harried the British troops</a> on their return to Boston, culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">Siege of Boston</a> and outbreak of the war.</p>
<p>The battle was initially publicized by the colonists as an example of British brutality and aggression: one colonial <a href="/wiki/Broadside_(printing)" title="Broadside (printing)">broadside</a> decried the "Bloody Butchery of the British Troops."<sup id="cite_ref-Randolph_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Randolph-10">[10]</a></sup> A century later, however, the conflict was remembered proudly by Americans, taking on a patriotic, almost mythic status (<a href="/wiki/Shot_heard_%27round_the_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Shot heard 'round the world">"the shot heard 'round the world"</a>) in works like the "<a href="/wiki/Concord_Hymn" title="Concord Hymn">Concord Hymn</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere%27s_Ride_(poem)" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Revere's Ride (poem)">Paul Revere's Ride</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Revere_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Revere-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1894 the Lexington Historical Society petitioned the Massachusetts State Legislature to proclaim April 19 as "Lexington Day." Concord countered with “Concord Day.” Governor Greenhalge opted for a compromise: <a href="/wiki/Patriots%27_Day" title="Patriots' Day">Patriots' Day</a>. In April 1975, Concord hosted a bicentennial celebration of the battle, featuring an address at the Old North Bridge by President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ford_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ford-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_history">Literary history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concord,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Literary history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Old_Manse_(view_from_Concord_River),_Concord,_Massachusetts.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/The_Old_Manse_%28view_from_Concord_River%29%2C_Concord%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/220px-The_Old_Manse_%28view_from_Concord_River%29%2C_Concord%2C_Massachusetts.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/The_Old_Manse_%28view_from_Concord_River%29%2C_Concord%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/330px-The_Old_Manse_%28view_from_Concord_River%29%2C_Concord%2C_Massachusetts.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/The_Old_Manse_%28view_from_Concord_River%29%2C_Concord%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/440px-The_Old_Manse_%28view_from_Concord_River%29%2C_Concord%2C_Massachusetts.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>

<p>Concord has a remarkably rich literary history centered in the mid-nineteenth century around <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a> (1803–1882), who moved to the town in 1835 and quickly became its most prominent citizen.<sup id="cite_ref-EmersontoConcord_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EmersontoConcord-13">[13]</a></sup> Emerson, a successful lecturer and philosopher, had deep roots in the town: his father <a href="/wiki/William_Emerson_(minister)" title="William Emerson (minister)">Rev. William Emerson</a> (1769–1811) grew up in Concord before becoming an eminent Boston minister, and his grandfather, <a href="/wiki/William_Emerson_Sr." title="William Emerson Sr.">William Emerson Sr.</a>, witnessed the battle at the North Bridge from his house, and later became a chaplain in the Continental Army.<sup id="cite_ref-Emerson_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emerson-14">[14]</a></sup> Emerson was at the center of a group of like-minded <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">Transcendentalists</a> living in Concord.<sup id="cite_ref-WPond_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WPond-15">[15]</a></sup> Among them were the author <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Hawthorne" title="Nathaniel Hawthorne">Nathaniel Hawthorne</a> (1804–1864) and the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Amos_Bronson_Alcott" title="Amos Bronson Alcott">Amos Bronson Alcott</a> (1799–1888), the father of <a href="/wiki/Louisa_May_Alcott" title="Louisa May Alcott">Louisa May Alcott</a> (1832–1888). A native Concordian, <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a> (1817–1862), was another notable member of Emerson's circle. This substantial collection of literary talent in one small town led <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">Henry James</a> to dub Concord "the biggest little place in America."<sup id="cite_ref-Cheever_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cheever-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the products of this intellectually stimulating environment were Emerson's many essays, including <i><a href="/wiki/Self-Reliance" title="Self-Reliance">Self-Reliance</a></i> (1841), Louisa May Alcott's novel <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Women" title="Little Women">Little Women</a></i> (1868), and Hawthorne's story collection <i><a href="/wiki/Mosses_from_an_Old_Manse" title="Mosses from an Old Manse">Mosses from an Old Manse</a></i> (1846).<sup id="cite_ref-Authorama_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Authorama-17">[17]</a></sup> Thoreau famously lived in a small cabin near <a href="/wiki/Walden_Pond" title="Walden Pond">Walden Pond</a>, where he wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Walden" title="Walden">Walden</a></i> (1854).<sup id="cite_ref-NPR_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-18">[18]</a></sup> After being imprisoned in the Concord jail for refusing to pay taxes in political protest against <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slavery</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Mexican-American_War" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mexican-American War">the Mexican-American War</a>, Thoreau penned the influential essay "Resistance to Civil Government", popularly known as <i><a href="/wiki/Civil_Disobedience_(Thoreau)" title="Civil Disobedience (Thoreau)">Civil Disobedience</a></i> (1849).<sup id="cite_ref-FFF_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FFF-19">[19]</a></sup> Evidencing their strong political beliefs through actions, Thoreau and many of his neighbors served as station masters and agents on the <a href="/wiki/Underground_Railroad" title="Underground Railroad">Underground Railroad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-thoreauUR_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thoreauUR-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/The_Wayside" title="The Wayside">The Wayside</a>, a house located on Lexington Road, has been home to a number of authors.<sup id="cite_ref-Wayside_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wayside-21">[21]</a></sup> It was occupied by scientist <a href="/wiki/John_Winthrop_(1714-1779)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Winthrop (1714-1779)">John Winthrop</a> (1714–1779) when <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> was temporarily moved to Concord during the Revolutionary War.<sup id="cite_ref-Winthrop_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winthrop-22">[22]</a></sup> The Wayside was later the home of the Alcott family (who referred to it as "Hillside"); the Alcotts sold it to Hawthorne in 1852, and the family moved into the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Orchard_House" title="Orchard House">Orchard House</a> in 1858. Hawthorne dubbed the house "The Wayside" and lived there until his death. The house was purchased in 1883 by Boston publisher <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Lothrop" title="Daniel Lothrop">Daniel Lothrop</a> and his wife, Harriett, who wrote the <a href="/wiki/Five_Little_Peppers" title="Five Little Peppers">Five Little Peppers</a> series and other children's books under the pen name <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Sidney" title="Margaret Sidney">Margaret Sidney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sidney_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sidney-23">[23]</a></sup> Today, The Wayside and the Orchard House are both museums. Emerson, Thoreau, Hawthorne, and the Alcotts are buried on Authors' Ridge in Concord's <a href="/wiki/Sleepy_Hollow_Cemetery,_Concord" class="mw-redirect" title="Sleepy Hollow Cemetery, Concord">Sleepy Hollow Cemetery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-G.26M_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G.26M-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Concord maintains a lively literary culture to this day; notable authors who have called the town home in recent years include <a href="/wiki/Doris_Kearns_Goodwin" title="Doris Kearns Goodwin">Doris Kearns Goodwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lightman" title="Alan Lightman">Alan Lightman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_B._Parker" title="Robert B. Parker">Robert B. Parker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Maguire" title="Gregory Maguire">Gregory Maguire</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concord_grape">Concord grape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concord,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Concord grape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1849 <a href="/wiki/Ephraim_Bull" class="mw-redirect" title="Ephraim Bull">Ephraim Bull</a> developed the now-ubiquitous <a href="/wiki/Concord_grape" title="Concord grape">Concord grape</a> at his home on Lexington Road, where the original vine still grows. <a href="/wiki/Welch%27s" title="Welch's">Welch's</a>, the first company to sell grape juice, maintains a headquarters in Concord.<sup id="cite_ref-Welch_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welch-25">[25]</a></sup> Boston-born Ephraim Wales Bull developed the Concord grape by experimenting with seeds from some of the native species. On his farm outside Concord, down the road from the Emerson, Thoreau, Hawthorne and Alcott homesteads, he planted some 22,000 seedlings in all, before he had produced the ideal grape. Early ripening, to escape the killing northern frosts, but with a rich, full-bodied flavor, the hardy Concord grape thrives where European cuttings had failed to survive. In 1853, Mr. Bull felt ready to put the first bunches of his Concord grapes before the public—and won a prize at the Boston Horticultural Society Exhibition. From these early arbors, fame of Mr. Bull’s (“the father of the Concord grape”) Concord grape spread worldwide, bringing him up to $1,000 a cutting, but he died a relatively poor man. The inscription on his tombstone states, “He sowed--others reaped.” <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.concordgrape.org/bodyhistory.html">http://www.concordgrape.org/bodyhistory.html</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic_bottle_ban">Plastic bottle ban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concord,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Plastic bottle ban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 5, 2012, Concord became the first community in the United States to approve a ban of the sale of water in single-serving plastic bottles. The law banned the sale of <a href="/wiki/PET_bottles" class="mw-redirect" title="PET bottles">PET bottles</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Liter" class="mw-redirect" title="Liter">liter</a> or less starting on January 1, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The ban provoked significant national controversy. An editorial in the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> characterized the ban as "born of convoluted reasoning" and "wrongheaded."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Some residents stated that this ban would not do much to affect the sales of bottled water, which was still highly accessible in the surrounding areas,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and the belief that it restricted consumers' freedom of choice.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Opponents also considered the ban to represent unfair targeting of one product in particular, when other, less healthy alternatives such as soda and fruit juice were still readily available in bottled form.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Nonetheless, subsequent efforts to repeal Concord's plastic bottled water ban have failed in <a href="/wiki/Open_town_meeting" class="mw-redirect" title="Open town meeting">open town meetings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> An effort to repeal Concord's ban on the sale of plastic water bottles was resoundingly defeated at a Town Meeting. Resident Jean Hill, who led the initial fight for the ban, said, "I really feel at the age of 86 that I've really accomplished something." Town Moderator Eric Van Loon didn't even bother taking an official tally because opposition to repeal was so overwhelming. It appeared that upwards of 80 to 90 percent of the 1,127 voters at town meeting raised their ballots against the repeal measure. The issue has been bubbling in Concord for several years. In 2010, a town meeting-approved ban, which wasn't written as a bylaw, was rejected by the state attorney generals office. In 2011, a new version of the ban narrowly failed at town meeting, by a vote of 265 to 272. The ban on selling water in polyethylene terephthalate (PET) bottles of one liter or less passed in 2012 by a vote of 403 to 364, and a repeal effort in April failed by a vote of 621 to 687.</p>

<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the town has a total area of 25.9 square miles (67&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), of which 24.9 square miles (64&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) is land and 1.0 square mile (2.6&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), or 3.75%, is water. The city of <a href="/wiki/Lowell,_Massachusetts" title="Lowell, Massachusetts">Lowell</a> is 13 miles (21&#160;km) to the north, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> is 19 miles (31&#160;km) to the east, and <a href="/wiki/Nashua,_New_Hampshire" title="Nashua, New Hampshire">Nashua, New Hampshire</a>, is 23 miles (37&#160;km) to the north.</p>
<p>Massachusetts state routes <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_2" title="Massachusetts Route 2">2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_2A" title="Massachusetts Route 2A">2A</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_62" title="Massachusetts Route 62">62</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_126" title="Massachusetts Route 126">126</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_119" title="Massachusetts Route 119">119</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_111" title="Massachusetts Route 111">111</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_117" title="Massachusetts Route 117">117</a> pass through Concord.</p>
<p>Concord borders the towns of <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Massachusetts" title="Carlisle, Massachusetts">Carlisle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bedford,_Massachusetts" title="Bedford, Massachusetts">Bedford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Massachusetts" title="Lincoln, Massachusetts">Lincoln</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudbury,_Massachusetts" title="Sudbury, Massachusetts">Sudbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maynard,_Massachusetts" title="Maynard, Massachusetts">Maynard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acton,_Massachusetts" title="Acton, Massachusetts">Acton</a>.</p>
<p>The town center is located near the <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_River" title="Sudbury River">Sudbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assabet_River" title="Assabet River">Assabet</a> rivers, forming the <a href="/wiki/Concord_River" title="Concord River">Concord River</a>, which flows north to the <a href="/wiki/Merrimack_River" title="Merrimack River">Merrimack River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lowell,_MA" class="mw-redirect" title="Lowell, MA">Lowell</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">Gunpowder</a> was manufactured from 1835 to 1940 in the <a href="/wiki/American_Powder_Mills" title="American Powder Mills">American Powder Mills</a> complex extending upstream along the Assabet River.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the 2000 <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-GR2_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-44">[44]</a></sup> there were 16,993 people, 5,948 households and 4,437 families residing in the town. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 682.0 per square mile (263.3/km²). There were 6,153 housing units at an average density of 246.9 per square mile (95.3/km²). The racial makeup of the town was 91.64% <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 2.24% <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.09% <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 2.90% <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.02% <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 2.12% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.99% from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 2.80% of the population.</p>
<p>There were 13,090 households of which 37.2% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 65.5% were married couples living together, 7.2% had a female householder with no husband present, and 25.4% were non-families. 22.0% of all households were made up of individuals and 10.4% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.62 and the average family size was 3.08.</p>
<p>25.1% of the population were under the age of 18, 4.2% from 18 to 24, 25.8% from 25 to 44, 28.4% from 45 to 64, and 16.5% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 42 years. For every 100 females there were 100.3 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 101.8 males.</p>
<p>In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Median_household_income" class="mw-redirect" title="Median household income">median household income</a> was $129,960.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> About 2.1% of families and 3.9% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 3.7% of those under age 18 and 3.3% of those age 65 or over.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">Pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Concord,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town's name is usually pronounced by its residents as <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="/ŋ/: 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><small title="English pronunciation respelling"><i><b>KONG</b>-kərd</i></small></a> in a manner indistinguishable from the American pronunciation of the word "conquered".<sup id="cite_ref-Pronunciation_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pronunciation-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Speakers with a <a href="/wiki/Boston_accent" title="Boston accent">Boston accent</a> often pronounce "Concord" with the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ə]</span> in the second syllable replaced by <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[<a href="/wiki/Near-close_near-front_rounded_vowel" title="Near-close near-front rounded vowel">ʏ</a>]</span> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ˈkɒŋkʏd]</span>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

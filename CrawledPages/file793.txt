<p><b>George McClelland Whitesides</b> (born August 3, 1939) is an American <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> of chemistry at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. He is best known for his work in the areas of <a href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_self-assembly" title="Molecular self-assembly">molecular self-assembly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soft_lithography" title="Soft lithography">soft lithography</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Microfabrication" title="Microfabrication">microfabrication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microfluidics" title="Microfluidics">microfluidics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>. A prolific author and patent holder who has received many awards, he received the highest <a href="/wiki/Hirsch_index" class="mw-redirect" title="Hirsch index">Hirsch index</a> rating of all living chemists in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirsch_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_academic_career">Education and academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_M._Whitesides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_M._Whitesides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whitesides attended secondary school at <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Andover" class="mw-redirect" title="Phillips Andover">Phillips Andover</a> and graduated in 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-Andover_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andover-4">[4]</a></sup> He received his A.B. degree from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> in 1960 and earned a Ph.D. in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> from the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> in 1964, where he worked with <a href="/wiki/John_D._Roberts" title="John D. Roberts">John D. Roberts</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> At Caltech, Whitesides began working in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>. Whitesides' graduate work in <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic chemistry</a> used <a href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Density_matrices" class="mw-redirect" title="Density matrices">density matrices</a> to study <a href="/wiki/Grignard_reagents" class="mw-redirect" title="Grignard reagents">Grignard reagents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup> He used NMR spectroscopy to study rate of change of <a href="/wiki/Grignard_reagents" class="mw-redirect" title="Grignard reagents">Grignard reagents</a><sup id="cite_ref-CHM2007_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHM2007-6">[6]</a></sup> and the structure of Grignard reagents in solution. He also studied <a href="/wiki/Spin-spin_coupling" class="mw-redirect" title="Spin-spin coupling">spin-spin coupling</a> in a variety of organic compounds, using density matrix calculations to examine the spin systems that NMR analyses detect.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_at_MIT">Research at MIT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_M._Whitesides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research at MIT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whitesides began his independent career as an assistant professor at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> in 1963 and remained there until 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup> He continued his work with NMR spectroscopy and organometallic compounds, as well as working with <a href="/wiki/Polymers" class="mw-redirect" title="Polymers">polymers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CHM2007_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHM2007-6">[6]</a></sup> Collaborations with biologists at MIT were an early influence informing his later work with biological systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup> He is credited as having played a "pivotal role" in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Corey-House-Posner-Whitesides_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Corey-House-Posner-Whitesides reaction">Corey-House-Posner-Whitesides reaction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Folch2013_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Folch2013-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_at_Harvard">Research at Harvard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_M._Whitesides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research at Harvard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1982, Whitesides moved back to the Department of Chemistry at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, his alma mater, taking his laboratory with him.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup> He was the Mallinckrodt Professor of Chemistry from 1982 to 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-ResearchQuote_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ResearchQuote-8">[8]</a></sup> At Harvard, Whitesides has served as chairman of the Chemistry Department (1986–89)<sup id="cite_ref-Ullyot_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullyot-9">[9]</a></sup> and Associate Dean of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences (1989–92).<sup id="cite_ref-Birthday_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birthday-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2004, Whitesides was appointed the Woodford L. and Ann A. Flowers <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Professor" title="Harvard University Professor">University Professor</a> at Harvard,<sup id="cite_ref-Vittor2004_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vittor2004-11">[11]</a></sup> one of only 24 University Professorships at the institution as of 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-office-of-news_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-office-of-news-12">[12]</a></sup> He is cofounder and director of the Whitesides Research Group at Harvard, an active research group of graduate and postdoctoral students with a lab space covering more than 6,000 square feet (560&#160;m<sup>2</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-Andover_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andover-4">[4]</a></sup> The single primary objective of his lab is "to fundamentally change the paradigms of science."<sup id="cite_ref-BowenGino_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BowenGino-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whitesides' interests include "physical and organic chemistry, materials science, biophysics, complexity and emergence, surface science, microfluidics, optics, self-assembly, micro- and nanotechnology, science for developing economies, catalysis, energy production and conservation, origin of life, rational drug design, cell-surface biochemistry, simplicity, and infochemistry."<sup id="cite_ref-ResearchQuote_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ResearchQuote-8">[8]</a></sup> He has shifted to new research areas many times throughout his career, averaging about ten years in any particular area. Once other people successfully move into an area, he tends to look for new and more interesting problems to solve.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup> "He has done that repeatedly by asking fundamental questions of what seemed to everyone to be virtually intractable problems," according to <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_R._Knowles" title="Jeremy R. Knowles">Jeremy R. Knowles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whitesides has made scientific contributions in diverse areas, including nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR), microfluidics and nanotechnology. He is particularly well known for his work in materials and surface science. His work in surface chemistry has examined the 'self-assembly' processes of molecules arranging themselves on a surface. This work has become a basis for developments in nanoscience, electronics, pharmaceutical science and medical diagnostics.<sup id="cite_ref-Eureka_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eureka-14">[14]</a></sup> Some of his research has been visually presented through the collaboration <i>On the Surface of Things: Images of the Extraordinary in Science</i> with photographer and artist <a href="/wiki/Felice_Frankel" title="Felice Frankel">Felice Frankel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Surface_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Surface-15">[15]</a></sup> One image, a pattern of blue and green water droplets, was featured on a 1992 cover of <i>Science</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Frankel_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frankel-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early work by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_G._Nuzzo" class="mw-redirect" title="Ralph G. Nuzzo">Ralph G. Nuzzo</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_L._Allara&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="David L. Allara (page does not exist)">David L. Allara</a> on spontaneously organized molecular assemblies informed Whitesides' work on <a href="/wiki/Soft_lithography" title="Soft lithography">soft lithography</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup> Whitesides and his research group have made significant contributions by developing techniques for soft lithography and <a href="/wiki/Microcontact_printing" title="Microcontact printing">microcontact printing</a>. Both microscale and nanoscale techniques are based on printing, molding and embossing, and can be used for the fabrication of patterns and features on many different materials. Soft lithography uses a patterned elastomer as a stamp, mold, or mask to create micropatterns and microstructures.<sup id="cite_ref-SoftLitho1998_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SoftLitho1998-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NatureProtocols_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatureProtocols-18">[18]</a></sup> Such techniques have now become standard in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recent research interests include energy,<sup id="cite_ref-Bullis_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bullis-19">[19]</a></sup> the origin of life,<sup id="cite_ref-Conor_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conor-20">[20]</a></sup> and science for developing economics.<sup id="cite_ref-WorldIn2012Economist_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorldIn2012Economist-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whitesides is also known for publishing his "outline system" for writing scientific papers.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_and_public_service">Policy and public service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_M._Whitesides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Policy and public service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beyond his scientific research, Whitesides is also active in public service. He was part of the Committee on Science, Engineering, and Public Policy, which authored the National Academies' report <i>Rising Above the Gathering Storm</i> (2007). The report addressed U.S. competitiveness in science and technology.<sup id="cite_ref-Rising2006_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rising2006-23">[23]</a></sup> Two key challenges were identified as being essential to American scientific and engineering prowess: 1) creating high-quality jobs for Americans and 2) addressing the nation's need for clean, affordable, and reliable energy. The committee developed four areas of recommendations, with twenty specific proposals for implementable actions. Addressing human, financial, and informational issues, the report argued in favor of:<sup id="cite_ref-Summary_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Summary-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>improving K–12 science and mathematics education, to ensure that there is a pool of talented individuals who can work in the sciences</li>
<li>supporting long-term basic research, to ensure the ongoing development of new ideas that will support the economy and enhance quality of life</li>
<li>creating a positive environment for higher education in which America can develop, recruit, and retain students, scientists, and engineers from the United States and the rest of the world</li>
<li>encouraging innovation through economic policies that will support manufacturing and marketing</li>
</ul>
<p>In 2002, Whitesides served as the Chairman of the International Review Panel that evaluated the state of chemical research in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. Their findings were summarized what is now known as the <i>Whitesides Report</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> They identified chemical biology and materials science as important areas for new development in the United Kingdom, and argued that chemistry is an important discipline in part because its concepts, processes and materials underlie other disciplines and offer opportunities to enhance communication between disciplines.<sup id="cite_ref-RSC2008_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC2008-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whitesides has served on advisory committees for the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>. He has also served on the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a> in various capacities since 1984, including stints on the Committee on Science and Technology for Counter Terrorism and the Committee on Nanotechnology for the Intelligence Community.<sup id="cite_ref-Source_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source-27">[27]</a></sup> He is also a member of the Reliance Innovation Council formed by <a href="/wiki/Reliance_Industries_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Reliance Industries Limited">Reliance Industries Limited</a>, India.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an article in <i>Nature</i> (2011), Whitesides and <a href="/wiki/John_M._Deutch" title="John M. Deutch">John M. Deutch</a> challenged the scientific and chemical communities to become more relevant to current social and environmental issues. They criticized academic chemistry for an "increasingly incurious and risk-averse attitude" and for focusing on "familiar questions of familiar disciplines" rather than taking a broad interdisciplinary view and exploring new areas. They recommended that institutions focus on practical problems, and teach entrepreneurial skills along with basic science so as to stimulate the development of practical technologies, encouraging students to take ownership of their own research.<sup id="cite_ref-Nature2011_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature2011-29">[29]</a></sup> A similar approach is taken by the Whitesides Research Group, of which <a href="/wiki/John_A._Rogers" title="John A. Rogers">John A. Rogers</a> has said, "Chemistry was the core expertise that provided the competitive advantage, but there was no sense of chemistry as a narrowly defined discipline. It was chemistry to solve problems, not necessarily to do chemistry."<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup> The article sparked strong reactions both for and against their ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-Chemmy2011_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chemmy2011-30">[30]</a></sup> Many in the scientific community asserted that research agendas should be "disinterested" and that education must focus on fundamental research to advance.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-31">[31]</a></sup> Whitesides and Deutch argued that teaching science in ways that address current issues can still lead to foundational work and scientific breakthroughs.<sup id="cite_ref-Nature2011_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature2011-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_achievements">Awards and achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_M._Whitesides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Awards and achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whitesides is the author of more than 1200 scientific articles<sup id="cite_ref-Birthday_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birthday-10">[10]</a></sup> and is listed as an inventor on at least 134 patents.<sup id="cite_ref-HarvardPatents_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarvardPatents-32">[32]</a></sup> He ranked 5th on <a href="/wiki/Thomson_ISI" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomson ISI">Thomson ISI</a>'s list of the 1000 most cited chemists from 1981 to 1997,<sup id="cite_ref-ISI1997_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISI1997-33">[33]</a></sup> and 38th on the list from 2000 to 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomson2010_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson2010-34">[34]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Hirsch_index" class="mw-redirect" title="Hirsch index">Hirsch index</a>, a ranking which combines number of articles published and citations of those articles by others, he was the most influential living chemist in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirsch_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whitesides has co-founded over 12 companies with a combined market capitalization of over $20 billion. These companies include <a href="/wiki/Genzyme" title="Genzyme">Genzyme</a>, GelTex, Theravance, Surface Logix, Nano Terra, and WMR Biomedical.<sup id="cite_ref-Impact2011_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Impact2011-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-36">[36]</a></sup> Whitesides has mentored more than 300 graduate students, postdocs, and visiting scholars.<sup id="cite_ref-Folch2013_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Folch2013-7">[7]</a></sup> He serves on the editorial advisory boards of several scientific journals, including <i><a href="/wiki/ACS_Nano" title="ACS Nano">ACS Nano</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Angewandte_Chemie" title="Angewandte Chemie">Angewandte Chemie</a></i>, <i>Chemistry &amp; Biology</i>, and <i>Small</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Birthday_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birthday-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whitesides is a member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a>. He is also a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society" title="American Philosophical Society">American Philosophical Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rising2006_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rising2006-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2002 he became foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among other awards, Whitesides is the recipient of the <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a>'s <a href="/wiki/ACS_Award_in_Pure_Chemistry" title="ACS Award in Pure Chemistry">ACS Award in Pure Chemistry</a> (1975),<sup id="cite_ref-ACSAward_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACSAward-38">[38]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Cope_Award" title="Arthur C. Cope Award">Arthur C. Cope Award</a> (1995),<sup id="cite_ref-CopeAward_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CopeAward-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a> (1998),<sup id="cite_ref-NatSci1998_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatSci1998-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NatSciDetails_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatSciDetails-41">[41]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Prize" title="Kyoto Prize">Kyoto Prize</a> in Materials Science and Engineering (2003),<sup id="cite_ref-KyotoPrize2003_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KyotoPrize2003-42">[42]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Gabbay_Award" title="Gabbay Award">Gabbay Award</a> (2004),<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Dan_David_Prize" title="Dan David Prize">Dan David Prize</a> (2005),<sup id="cite_ref-DanDavidPrize_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DanDavidPrize-44">[44]</a></sup> the Welch Award in Chemistry (2005),<sup id="cite_ref-Henry_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-45">[45]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Chemists_Gold_Medal" title="American Institute of Chemists Gold Medal">AIC Gold Medal</a> (2007),<sup id="cite_ref-CHFAIC_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHFAIC-46">[46]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Priestley_Medal" title="Priestley Medal">Priestley Medal</a> (2007), the highest honor conferred by the ACS.<sup id="cite_ref-Move2007_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move2007-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Arnaud_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arnaud-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>More recently, George Whitesides received the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Dreyfus_Prize_in_the_Chemical_Sciences" title="Dreyfus Prize in the Chemical Sciences">Dreyfus Prize in the Chemical Sciences</a> from <a href="/wiki/The_Camille_and_Henry_Dreyfus_Foundation" title="The Camille and Henry Dreyfus Foundation">The Camille and Henry Dreyfus Foundation</a> on September 30, 2009, for his creation of new materials that have significantly advanced the field of chemistry and its societal benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ChipChair2009_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChipChair2009-49">[49]</a></sup> In November 2009, he was recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Reed_M._Izatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed M. Izatt">Reed M. Izatt</a> and James J. Christensen Lectureship.<sup id="cite_ref-BYU2009_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BYU2009-50">[50]</a></sup> Also in 2009, George Whitesides was awarded the <a href="/wiki/The_Franklin_Institute_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="The Franklin Institute Awards">2009 Benjamin Franklin Medal</a> in Chemistry by The <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Institute" title="Franklin Institute">Franklin Institute</a> in Philadelphia, for his pioneering chemical research in molecular self-assembly and innovative nanofabrication techniques that have resulted in rapid, inexpensive fabrication of ultra small devices.<sup id="cite_ref-ChipChair2009_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChipChair2009-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>He received the <a href="/wiki/Othmer_Gold_Medal" title="Othmer Gold Medal">Othmer Gold Medal</a> for outstanding contributions to progress in chemistry and science in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-CHFAll_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHFAll-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2010PRNewswire_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2010PRNewswire-52">[52]</a></sup> He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/F._Albert_Cotton#F.A._Cotton_Medal_for_Excellence_in_Chemical_Research" title="F. Albert Cotton">F. A. Cotton Medal for Excellence in Chemical Research</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a> in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Cotton2011_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cotton2011-53">[53]</a></sup> In 2011 he also received the <a href="/wiki/King_Faisal_International_Prize" title="King Faisal International Prize">King Faisal International Prize</a> in Chemistry.<sup id="cite_ref-Faisal2011_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Faisal2011-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-KACST_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KACST-55">[55]</a></sup> In 2013 he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/IRI_Medal" title="IRI Medal">IRI Medal</a> alongside <a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Langer" title="Robert S. Langer">Robert S. Langer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wang_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IRI_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IRI-57">[57]</a></sup> In 2017, he was awarded the Lifetime Achievement award by <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.xconomy.com">Xconomy</a><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_M._Whitesides&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whitesides and his wife, Barbara, have two sons, George T. and Ben. <a href="/wiki/George_T._Whitesides" title="George T. Whitesides">George Thomas Whitesides</a> is CEO of <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Galactic" title="Virgin Galactic">Virgin Galactic</a>, a firm developing commercial space vehicles.

<p><b>Samuel Webber</b> (1759 – July 17, 1810) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Clergyman" class="mw-redirect" title="Clergyman">clergyman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academic" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic">academic</a>, and president of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> from 1806 until his death in 1810.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_Webber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Webber was educated at Dummer Academy (now known as <a href="/wiki/The_Governor%27s_Academy" title="The Governor's Academy">The Governor's Academy</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a>, 1784; <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">M.A.</a>, 1787) where he distinguished himself in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hasty_Pudding_Theatricals" title="Hasty Pudding Theatricals">Hasty Pudding</a>. Webber was ordained as <a href="/wiki/Congregational_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Congregational Church">Congregational</a> minister in 1787 and two years later became <a href="/wiki/Hollis_Chair_of_Mathematics_and_Natural_Philosophy" title="Hollis Chair of Mathematics and Natural Philosophy">Hollis Professor of Mathematick and Natural Philosophy</a> at Harvard.<sup id="cite_ref-Quin1915_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quin1915-1">[1]</a></sup> He served in the commission that drew the boundaries, later recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1783)" title="Treaty of Paris (1783)">Treaty of Paris</a>, between the new <a href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="United States of America">United States of America</a> and the surrounding <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> provinces. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1789<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-2">[2]</a></sup> and also served as vice-president of the Academy. He authored <i>System of Mathematics</i>, which for many years served as the only textbook on the subject in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-appletons_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appletons-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Webber was appointed <a href="/wiki/President_of_Harvard_University" title="President of Harvard University">president</a> of Harvard in 1806. That same year he received an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Divinity" title="Doctor of Divinity">Doctor of Divinity</a> degree from that institution. He led Harvard until his death in 1810.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_Webber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Webber married Anna Winslow Green, a granddaughter of <a href="/wiki/David_Mathews" title="David Mathews">David Mathews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loyalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Loyalist">Loyalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_New_York_City" title="Mayor of New York City">Mayor of New York City</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. Webber's son, also named Samuel (September 15, 1797 <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a> – December 5, 1880 <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_New_Hampshire" title="Charlestown, New Hampshire">Charlestown, New Hampshire</a>), was a distinguished <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> and author.<sup id="cite_ref-appletons_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appletons-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

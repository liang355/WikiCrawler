<p><b>Roy Jay Glauber</b> (born September 1, 1925) is an American theoretical <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>. He is the <a href="/wiki/Mallinckrodt" title="Mallinckrodt">Mallinckrodt</a> <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">Professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">Physics</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and Adjunct Professor of Optical Sciences at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arizona" title="University of Arizona">University of Arizona</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, he was awarded one half of the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> "for his contribution to the quantum theory of <a href="/wiki/Coherence_(physics)" title="Coherence (physics)">optical coherence</a>", with the other half shared by <a href="/wiki/John_L._Hall" title="John L. Hall">John L. Hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodor_W._H%C3%A4nsch" title="Theodor W. Hänsch">Theodor W. Hänsch</a>. In this work, published in 1963, he created a model for <a href="/wiki/Photodetection" title="Photodetection">photodetection</a> and explained the fundamental characteristics of different types of light, such as <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> light (see <a href="/wiki/Coherent_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Coherent state">coherent state</a>) and light from light bulbs (see <a href="/wiki/Blackbody" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackbody">blackbody</a>). His theories are widely used in the field of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_optics" title="Quantum optics">quantum optics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Glauber2009_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glauber2009-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He currently serves on the National Advisory Board<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> of the Center for Arms Control and Non-Proliferation, the research arms of <a href="/wiki/Council_for_a_Livable_World" title="Council for a Livable World">Council for a Livable World</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_J._Glauber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Glauber was born in 1925 in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. He was a member of the 1941 graduating class of the <a href="/wiki/Bronx_High_School_of_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronx High School of Science">Bronx High School of Science</a>, and went on to do his undergraduate work at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. After his sophomore year he was recruited to work on the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, where (at the age of 18) he was one of the youngest scientists at <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a>. His work involved calculating the <a href="/wiki/Critical_mass" title="Critical mass">critical mass</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Atom_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Atom bomb">atom bomb</a>. After two years at Los Alamos, he returned to Harvard, receiving his bachelor's degree in 1946 and his PhD in 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-phd_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phd-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_J._Glauber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Glauber's recent research has dealt with problems in a number of areas of quantum optics, a field which, broadly speaking, studies the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamic" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantum electrodynamic">quantum electrodynamical</a> interactions of light and matter. He is also continuing work on several topics in high-energy collision theory, including the analysis of <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadron</a> collisions, and the statistical correlation of particles produced in high-energy reactions.</p>
<p>Specific topics of his current research include: the quantum mechanical behavior of trapped <a href="/wiki/Wave_packet" title="Wave packet">wave packets</a>; interactions of light with trapped ions; atom counting-the statistical properties of free atom beams and their measurement; algebraic methods for dealing with <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermion</a> statistics; coherence and correlations of <a href="/wiki/Bosonic_atom" class="mw-redirect" title="Bosonic atom">bosonic atoms</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensation" class="mw-redirect" title="Bose–Einstein condensation">Bose–Einstein condensation</a>; the theory of continuously monitored photon counting-and its reaction on quantum sources; the fundamental nature of "<a href="/wiki/Atomic_electron_transition" title="Atomic electron transition">quantum jumps</a>"; resonant transport of particles produced multiply in high-energy collisions; the multiple diffraction model of proton-proton and proton-antiproton scattering.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_J._Glauber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Glauber has received many honors for his research, including the <a href="/wiki/Albert_A._Michelson_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Albert A. Michelson Medal">Albert A. Michelson Medal</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Institute" title="Franklin Institute">Franklin Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> (1985),<sup id="cite_ref-MichelsonMedal_Laureates_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MichelsonMedal_Laureates-11">[11]</a></sup> the Max Born Award from the <a href="/wiki/Optical_Society_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Optical Society of America">Optical Society of America</a> (1985), the <a href="/wiki/Dannie_Heineman_Prize_for_Mathematical_Physics" title="Dannie Heineman Prize for Mathematical Physics">Dannie Heineman Prize for Mathematical Physics</a> from the <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a> (1996), and the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a>. Professor Glauber was awarded the 'Medalla de Oro del <a href="/wiki/Spanish_National_Research_Council" title="Spanish National Research Council">CSIC</a>' ('CSIC's Gold Medal') in a ceremony held in <a href="/wiki/Madrid,_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrid, Spain">Madrid, Spain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He was elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_1997" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 1997">Foreign Member of the Royal Society (ForMemRS) in 1997</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-frs_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ig_Nobel">Ig Nobel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_J._Glauber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ig Nobel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For many years before winning his Nobel Prize, Glauber was familiar to audiences of the <a href="/wiki/Ig_Nobel_Prize" title="Ig Nobel Prize">Ig Nobel Prize</a> ceremonies, where he took a bow each year as "Keeper of the Broom," sweeping the stage clean of the <a href="/wiki/Paper_airplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Paper airplane">paper airplanes</a> that have traditionally been thrown during the event. He missed the 2005 event, though, as he was being awarded his real Nobel Prize for Physics.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_J._Glauber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Glauber currently lives in <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Massachusetts" title="Arlington, Massachusetts">Arlington, Massachusetts</a>. Glauber has a son and a daughter, and five grandchildren.</p>

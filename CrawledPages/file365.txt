			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> Eleventh Edition</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b><i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> Eleventh Edition</b> (1910–11) is a 29-volume reference work, an edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>. It was developed during the encyclopaedia's transition from a British to an American publication. Some of its articles were written by the best-known scholars of the time. This edition of the encyclopedia, containing 40,000 entries, is now in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>; and many of its articles have been used as a basis for articles in <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, the outdated nature of some of its content makes its use as a source for modern scholarship problematic. Some articles have special value and interest to modern scholars as <a href="/wiki/Cultural_artifact" title="Cultural artifact">cultural artifacts</a> of the 19th and early 20th centuries.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:11Britannica.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/11Britannica.JPG/200px-11Britannica.JPG" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/11Britannica.JPG/300px-11Britannica.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/11Britannica.JPG/400px-11Britannica.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1936" data-file-height="1936" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:11Britannica.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i>, 11th edition</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The 1911 eleventh edition was assembled with the management of American publisher <a href="/wiki/Horace_Everett_Hooper" title="Horace Everett Hooper">Horace Everett Hooper</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Chisholm" title="Hugh Chisholm">Hugh Chisholm</a>, who had edited the previous edition, was appointed editor in chief, with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Alison_Phillips" title="Walter Alison Phillips">Walter Alison Phillips</a> as his principal assistant editor.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Originally, Hooper bought the rights to the 25-volume <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Ninth_Edition" class="mw-redirect" title="Encyclopædia Britannica Ninth Edition">9th edition</a> and persuaded the British newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> to issue its reprint, with eleven additional volumes (35 volumes total) as the tenth edition, which was published in 1902. Hooper's association with <i>The Times</i> ceased in 1909, and he negotiated with the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Press" title="Cambridge University Press">Cambridge University Press</a> to publish the 29-volume eleventh edition. Though it is generally perceived as a quintessentially British work, the eleventh edition had substantial American influences, not only in the increased amount of American and Canadian content, but also in the efforts made to make it more popular.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> American marketing methods also assisted sales. Some 14% of the contributors (214 of 1507) were from North America, and a New York office was established to coordinate their work.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initials of the encyclopedia's contributors appear at the end of selected articles or at the end of a section in the case of longer articles, such as that on China, and a key is given in each volume to these initials. Some articles were written by the best-known scholars of the time, such as <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Gosse" title="Edmund Gosse">Edmund Gosse</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._B._Bury" title="J. B. Bury">J. B. Bury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a>, <a href="/wiki/T._H._Huxley" class="mw-redirect" title="T. H. Huxley">T. H. Huxley</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Hopwood_Jeans" class="mw-redirect" title="James Hopwood Jeans">James Hopwood Jeans</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Michael_Rossetti" title="William Michael Rossetti">William Michael Rossetti</a>. Among the then lesser-known contributors were some who would later become distinguished, such as <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a>. Many articles were carried over from the <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Ninth_Edition" class="mw-redirect" title="Encyclopædia Britannica Ninth Edition">9th edition</a>, some with minimal updating. Some of the book-length articles were divided into smaller parts for easier reference, yet others much abridged. The best-known authors generally contributed only a single article or part of an article. Most of the work was done by journalists, <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> scholars and other scholars. The 1911 edition was the first edition of the encyclopædia to include more than just a handful of female contributors, with 34 women contributing articles to the edition.<sup id="cite_ref-thomas_1992_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomas_1992-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The eleventh edition introduced a number of changes of the format of the <i>Britannica</i>. It was the first to be published complete, instead of the previous method of volumes being released as they were ready. The <a href="/wiki/Letterpress_printing" title="Letterpress printing">print type</a> was kept in <a href="/wiki/Galley_proof" title="Galley proof">galley proofs</a> and subject to continual updating until publication. It was the first edition of <i>Britannica</i> to be issued with a comprehensive index volume in which was added a categorical index, where like topics were listed. It was the first not to include long treatise-length articles. Even though the overall length of the work was about the same as that of its predecessor, the number of articles had increased from 17,000 to 40,000. It was also the first edition of <i>Britannica</i> to include biographies of living people. Sixteen maps of the famous 9th edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Stielers_Handatlas" title="Stielers Handatlas">Stielers Handatlas</a></i> were exclusively translated to English, converted to <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">Imperial units</a>, printed in <a href="/wiki/Gotha" title="Gotha">Gotha</a>, Germany by <a href="/wiki/Justus_Perthes" title="Justus Perthes">Justus Perthes</a> and became part this edition. Later editions only included Perthes' great maps as low quality reproductions.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hooper sold the rights to <a href="/wiki/Sears_Roebuck" class="mw-redirect" title="Sears Roebuck">Sears Roebuck</a> of Chicago in 1920, completing the <i>Britannica'</i>s transition to becoming a substantially American publication.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1922, an additional three volumes (also edited by Hugh Chisholm), were published, covering the events of the intervening years, including <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. These, together with a reprint of the eleventh edition, formed the twelfth edition of the work. A similar thirteenth edition, consisting of three volumes plus a reprint of the twelfth edition, was published in 1926, so the twelfth and thirteenth editions were of course closely related to the eleventh edition and shared much of the same content. However, it became increasingly apparent that a more thorough update of the work was required.</p>
<p>The fourteenth edition, published in 1929, was considerably revised, with much text eliminated or abridged to make room for new topics. Nevertheless, the eleventh edition was the basis of every later version of the <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> until the completely new fifteenth edition was published in 1974, using modern information presentation.</p>
<p>The eleventh edition's articles are still of value and interest to modern readers and scholars, especially as a <a href="/wiki/Cultural_artifact" title="Cultural artifact">cultural artifact</a>: the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> was at its maximum, <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> was largely unchallenged, much of the world was still ruled by <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarchs</a>, and the tragedy of the modern <a href="/wiki/World_war" title="World war">world wars</a> was still in the future. They are an invaluable resource for topics omitted from modern encyclopedias, particularly for biography and the history of science and technology. As a literary text, the encyclopedia has value as an example of early 20th-century prose. For example, it employs <a href="/wiki/Literary_device" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary device">literary devices</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Pathetic_fallacy" title="Pathetic fallacy">pathetic fallacy</a> (attribution of human-like traits to impersonal forces or inanimate objects), which are not as common in modern reference texts.<sup id="cite_ref-Coleman_and_Simmons_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coleman_and_Simmons-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1917, using the pseudonym of S. S. Van Dine, the US art critic and author <a href="/wiki/Willard_Huntington_Wright" class="mw-redirect" title="Willard Huntington Wright">Willard Huntington Wright</a> published <i><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Misinforming_a_Nation" class="extiw" title="s:Misinforming a Nation">Misinforming a Nation</a></i>, a 200+ page criticism of inaccuracies and biases of the <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> eleventh edition. Wright claimed that <i>Britannica</i> was "characterized by misstatement, inexcusable omissions, rabid and patriotic prejudices, personal animosities, blatant errors of fact, scholastic ignorance, gross neglect of non-British culture, an astounding egotism, and an undisguised contempt for American progress".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amos_Urban_Shirk" title="Amos Urban Shirk">Amos Urban Shirk</a>, known for having read the eleventh and fourteenth editions in their entirety, said he found the fourteenth edition to be a "big improvement" over the eleventh, stating that "most of the material had been completely rewritten".</p>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Collison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert Collison (page does not exist)">Robert Collison</a>, in <i>Encyclopaedias: Their History Throughout The Ages</i> (1966), wrote of the eleventh edition that it "was probably the finest edition of the <i>Britannica</i> ever issued, and it ranks with the <i><a href="/wiki/Enciclopedia_Italiana" class="mw-redirect" title="Enciclopedia Italiana">Enciclopedia Italiana</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Enciclopedia_universal_ilustrada_europeo-americana" title="Enciclopedia universal ilustrada europeo-americana">Espasa</a></i> as one of the three greatest encyclopaedias. It was the last edition to be produced almost in its entirety in Britain, and its position in time as a summary of the world's knowledge just before the outbreak of World War I is particularly valuable".</p>
<p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clark" title="Kenneth Clark">Kenneth Clark</a>, in <i>Another Part of the Wood</i> (1974), wrote of the eleventh edition, "One leaps from one subject to another, fascinated as much by the play of mind and the <a href="/wiki/Idiosyncrasy" title="Idiosyncrasy">idiosyncrasies</a> of their authors as by the facts and dates. It must be the last encyclopaedia in the tradition of <a href="/wiki/Denis_Diderot" title="Denis Diderot">Diderot</a> which assumes that information can be made memorable only when it is slightly coloured by prejudice. When <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a> wrote 'Soul curled up on the window seat reading the <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i>,' he was certainly thinking of the eleventh edition." (Clark refers to Eliot's 1929 poem "<a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot%27s_Ariel_poems#.22Animula.22" title="T. S. Eliot's Ariel poems">Animula</a>".) It was one of <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a>'s favorite works, and was a source of information and enjoyment for his entire working life.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1912, mathematician <a href="/wiki/L._C._Karpinski" class="mw-redirect" title="L. C. Karpinski">L. C. Karpinski</a> criticised the eleventh edition for inaccuracies in articles on the <a href="/wiki/History_of_mathematics" title="History of mathematics">history of mathematics</a>, none of which had been written by specialists.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>English writer and former priest <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McCabe" title="Joseph McCabe">Joseph McCabe</a> claimed in <i>Lies and Fallacies of the Encyclopædia Britannica</i> (1947) that <i>Britannica</i> was censored under pressure from the Roman Catholic Church after the 11th edition.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Authorities ranging from <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a> to professors criticised the 11th edition for having <a href="/wiki/Bourgeois" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> and old-fashioned opinions on art, literature, and social sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-thomas_1992_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomas_1992-4">[4]</a></sup> A contemporary <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell</a> professor, <a href="/wiki/Edward_B._Titchener" title="Edward B. Titchener">Edward B. Titchener</a>, wrote in 1912, "the new <i>Britannica</i> does not reproduce the psychological atmosphere of its day and generation... Despite the halo of authority, and despite the scrutiny of the staff, the great bulk of the secondary articles in general psychology&#160;... are not adapted to the requirements of the intelligent reader".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Critics have charged several editions with racism and sexism.<sup id="cite_ref-thomas_1992_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomas_1992-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The eleventh edition <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/1911_Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica/Ku_Klux_Klan" class="extiw" title="s:1911 Encyclopædia Britannica/Ku Klux Klan">characterises</a> the <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Ku Klux Klan</a> as protecting the white race and restoring order to the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">American South</a> after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, citing the need to "control the negro", and "the frequent occurrence of the crime of rape by negro men upon white women".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Similarly, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/1911_Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica/Civilization" class="extiw" title="s:1911 Encyclopædia Britannica/Civilization">the "Civilization" article</a> argues for <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenics</a>, stating that it is irrational to "propagate low orders of intelligence, to feed the ranks of paupers, defectives and criminals&#160;... which to-day constitute so threatening an obstacle to racial progress".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The eleventh edition has no biography of <a href="/wiki/Marie_Curie" title="Marie Curie">Marie Curie</a>, despite her winning of the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> in 1903 and the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> in 1911, although she is mentioned briefly under the biography of her husband <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Curie" title="Pierre Curie">Pierre Curie</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <i>Britannica</i> employed a large female editorial staff that wrote hundreds of articles for which they were not given credit.<sup id="cite_ref-thomas_1992_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomas_1992-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1911_Britannica_in_the_21st_century">1911 <i>Britannica</i> in the 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1911 Britannica in the 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1911 edition is no longer restricted by <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a>, and it is therefore available in several more modern forms. While it may once have been a reliable description of the consensus of its time, for many modern readers, the <i>Encyclopedia</i> has several major errors, ethnocentric remarks, and other issues:</p>
<ul>
<li>Contemporary opinions of <a href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic groups">ethnicity</a> are included in the <i>Encyclopedia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s articles. For example, the entry for "<a href="/wiki/Negro" title="Negro">Negro</a>" states, "Mentally the negro is inferior to the white... the arrest or even deterioration of mental development [after adolescence] is no doubt very largely due to the fact that after puberty sexual matters take the first place in the negro's life and thoughts."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The article about the <a href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="American War of Independence">American War of Independence</a> attributes the success of the United States in part to "a population mainly of good English blood and instincts".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Many articles are now outdated factually, in particular those concerning science, technology, <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international</a> and <a href="/wiki/Municipal_law" title="Municipal law">municipal law</a>, and medicine. For example, the article on the vitamin deficiency disease <a href="/wiki/Beriberi" title="Beriberi">beriberi</a> speculates that it is caused by a fungus, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a> not having been discovered at the time. Articles about geographic places mention rail connections and ferry stops in towns that no longer employ such transport (though this in itself can be useful for those looking for historical information).</li>
<li>Even where the facts might still be accurate, new information, theories and perspectives developed since 1911 have substantially changed the way the same facts might be interpreted. For example, the modern interpretation of the history of the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> is now very different from that of 1911; readers of the eleventh edition who want to know about the social customs and political life of the tribe and its warriors are told to look up the entry for their king, <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">Alaric I</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The eleventh edition of <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> has become a commonly quoted source, both because of the reputation of the <i>Britannica</i> and because it is now in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> and has been made available on the Internet. It has been used as a source by many modern projects, including <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> and the <i>Project Gutenberg Encyclopedia</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gutenberg_Encyclopedia"><i>Gutenberg Encyclopedia</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gutenberg Encyclopedia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><b>Project Gutenberg Encyclopedia</b></i> is the eleventh edition of the <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i>, renamed to address Britannica's trademark concerns. <a href="/wiki/Project_Gutenberg" title="Project Gutenberg">Project Gutenberg</a>'s offerings are summarized below in the <a href="#External_links">External links</a> section and include text and graphics. <a href="/wiki/Distributed_Proofreaders" title="Distributed Proofreaders">Distributed Proofreaders</a> are currently working on producing a complete electronic edition of the 1911 <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i>.</p>

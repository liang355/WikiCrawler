<p>The <b>Association of American Universities</b> (<b>AAU</b>) is a binational <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organization</a> of leading <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university">research universities</a> devoted to maintaining a strong system of academic <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> and education. It consists of 60 <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (both <a href="/wiki/State_university_system_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="State university system (United States)">public</a> and <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private</a>) and two universities in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_of_American_Universities&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AAU was founded in 1900 by a group of fourteen <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a>-granting universities in the United States to strengthen and standardize American doctoral programs. The presidents of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a> sent a letter of invitation to nine other universities to meet at <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> in February, 1900 to promote and raise standards.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Today, 62 universities in the U.S. and Canada are members and the primary purpose of the organization is to provide a forum for the development and implementation of institutional and national policies, in order to promote strong programs in academic research and scholarship and undergraduate, graduate, and professional education.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_of_American_Universities&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The largest attraction of the AAU for many schools, especially nonmembers, is prestige. For example, in 2010 the chancellor of nonmember <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University" title="North Carolina State University">North Carolina State University</a> described it as "the pre-eminent research-intensive membership group. To be a part of that organization is something N.C. State aspires to."<sup id="cite_ref-fain20100421_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fain20100421-3">[3]</a></sup> A spokesman for nonmember <a href="/wiki/University_of_Connecticut" title="University of Connecticut">University of Connecticut</a> called it "perhaps the most elite organization in higher education. You'd probably be hard-pressed to find a major research university that didn't want to be a member of the AAU."<sup id="cite_ref-hine20100613_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hine20100613-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2012, the new elected chancellor of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a>, a nonmember of AAU, reaffirmed the framework goal of elevating the campus to AAU standards which inspire them to become a member in the near future, and called it a distinctive status.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Because of the lengthy and difficult entrance process, <a href="/wiki/Board_of_trustees" class="mw-redirect" title="Board of trustees">boards of trustees</a>, state legislators, and <a href="/wiki/Donation" title="Donation">donors</a> often see membership as evidence of the quality of a <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fain20100421_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fain20100421-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The AAU acts as a <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbyist</a> at its headquarters in the city of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> for research and higher education funding and for policy and regulatory issues affecting research universities. The association holds two meetings annually, both in Washington. Separate meetings are held for <a href="/wiki/University_president" class="mw-redirect" title="University president">university presidents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Provost_(education)" title="Provost (education)">provosts</a>, and other officials. Because the meetings are private they offer the opportunity for discussion without media coverage. Prominent government officials, businessmen, and others often speak to the groups.<sup id="cite_ref-fain20100421_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fain20100421-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_of_American_Universities&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2004, AAU members accounted for 58%<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> of U.S. universities' research grants and contract income and 52% of all <a href="/wiki/Doctorates" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctorates">doctorates</a> awarded in the United States. Since 1999, 43% of all <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> winners and 74% of winners at U.S. institutions have been affiliated with an AAU university. Approximately two thirds of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> 2006 Class of Fellows are affiliated with an AAU university. The faculties at AAU universities include 2,993 members of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academies" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academies">United States National Academies</a> (82% of all members): the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Institute of Medicine">Institute of Medicine</a> (2004).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Undergraduate students: 1,044,759; 7% nationally</li>
<li>Undergraduate degrees awarded: 235,328; 17% nationally</li>
<li>Graduate students: 418,066; 20% nationally</li>
<li>Master’s degrees awarded: 106,971; 19% nationally</li>
<li>Professional degrees awarded: 20,859; 25% nationally</li>
<li>Doctorates awarded: 22,747; 52% nationally</li>
<li>Postdoctoral fellows: 30,430; 67% nationally</li>
<li>Students studying abroad: 57,205</li>
<li>National Merit/Achievement Scholars (2004): 5,434; 63% nationally</li>
<li>Faculty: approximately 72,000</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">Membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_of_American_Universities&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AAU membership is by invitation only, which requires an affirmative vote of three fourths of current members. Invitations are considered periodically, based in part on an assessment of the breadth and quality of university programs of research and graduate education, as well as undergraduate education. The association ranks its members using four criteria: research spending, the percentage of faculty who are members of the National Academies, faculty awards, and <a href="/wiki/Citation" title="Citation">citations</a>. Two thirds of members can vote to revoke membership for poor rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-abourezk20110429_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abourezk20110429-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-selingo20110429_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-selingo20110429-9">[9]</a></sup> As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Association_of_American_Universities&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> annual dues are $80,500.<sup id="cite_ref-fain20100421_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fain20100421-3">[3]</a></sup> All 60 US members of the AAU are also classified as <a href="/wiki/List_of_research_universities_in_the_United_States" title="List of research universities in the United States">Highest Research Activity (R1) Universities</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Classification_of_Institutions_of_Higher_Education" title="Carnegie Classification of Institutions of Higher Education">Carnegie Classification of Institutions of Higher Education</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advocacy">Advocacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_of_American_Universities&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Advocacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AAU supported the <a href="/wiki/Research_and_Development_Efficiency_Act_(H.R._5056;_113th_Congress)" class="mw-redirect" title="Research and Development Efficiency Act (H.R. 5056; 113th Congress)">Research and Development Efficiency Act (H.R. 5056; 113th Congress)</a> arguing that the legislation "can lead to a long-needed reduction in the regulatory burden currently imposed on universities and their faculty members who conduct research on behalf of the federal government."<sup id="cite_ref-AAUsupport1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAUsupport1-14">[14]</a></sup> According to the AAU, "too often federal requirements" for accounting for federal grant money "are ill-conceived, ineffective, and/or duplicative."<sup id="cite_ref-AAUsupport1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAUsupport1-14">[14]</a></sup> This wastes the researchers' times and "reduces the time they can devote to discovery and innovation and increases institutional compliance costs."<sup id="cite_ref-AAUsupport1_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAUsupport1-14">[14]</a></sup> AAU institutions are frequently involved in <a href="/wiki/Science_policy_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Science policy in the United States">U.S. science policy</a> debates. In 2008, AAU Vice President for Policy, Tobin Smith, co-authored a textbook on U.S. science policy.</p>

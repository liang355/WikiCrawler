<p><b>Mannheim</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈmanhaɪm]</a></span> <span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Mannheim.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/Mannheim.ogg" class="internal" title="Mannheim.ogg">listen</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:Mannheim.ogg" title="File:Mannheim.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>, <a href="/wiki/Palatine_German_language" title="Palatine German language">Palatine German</a>: <i>Monnem</i> or <i>Mannem</i>) is a city in the southwestern part of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, the third-largest in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> after <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a> with a 2015 population of approximately 305,000 inhabitants. The city is at the centre of the larger densely populated <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Neckar" title="Rhine-Neckar">Rhine-Neckar Metropolitan Region</a> which has a population of 2,400,000<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and is <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_regions_in_Germany" title="Metropolitan regions in Germany">Germany's eighth-largest metropolitan region</a>.</p>
<p>Mannheim is located at the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Neckar" title="Neckar">Neckar</a> in the northwestern corner of Baden-Württemberg. The Rhine separates Mannheim from the city of <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen" title="Ludwigshafen">Ludwigshafen</a>, just to the west of it in <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>, and the border of Baden-Württemberg with <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a> is just to the north. Mannheim is downstream along the Neckar from the city of <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>.</p>
<p>Mannheim is unusual among German cities in that its streets and avenues are laid out in a grid pattern, leading to its nickname "die Quadratestadt" ("The City of Squares"). The eighteenth century <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_Palace" title="Mannheim Palace">Mannheim Palace</a>, former home of the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-elector</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_the_Palatinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of the Palatinate">Palatinate</a>, now houses the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mannheim" title="University of Mannheim">University of Mannheim</a>.</p>
<p>The city is home to major corporations including <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Deere" title="John Deere">John Deere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_Inc." title="Caterpillar Inc.">Caterpillar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asea_Brown_Boveri" class="mw-redirect" title="Asea Brown Boveri">ABB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuchs_Petrolub" title="Fuchs Petrolub">Fuchs Petrolub</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hoffmann-La_Roche" title="Hoffmann-La Roche">Roche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reckitt_Benckiser" title="Reckitt Benckiser">Reckitt Benckiser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Pharmahandel" title="Phoenix Pharmahandel">Phoenix Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a>, and several other well-known companies. In addition, Mannheim's <a href="/wiki/SAP_Arena" title="SAP Arena">SAP Arena</a> is not only the home of the German ice hockey record champions the <a href="/wiki/Adler_Mannheim" title="Adler Mannheim">Adler Mannheim</a>, but also the well-known handball team, the <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Neckar_L%C3%B6wen" title="Rhein-Neckar Löwen">Rhein-Neckar Löwen</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> magazine, Mannheim is known for its exceptional inventive power and was ranked 11th among the Top 15 of the most inventive cities worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The New Economy Magazine elected Mannheim under the 20 cities that best represent the world of tomorrow emphasizing Mannheim's positive economic and innovative environment.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Since 2014, Mannheim has been a member of the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> Creative Cities Network and holds the title of "UNESCO City of Music".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Mannheim is a <a href="/wiki/Smart_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart City">Smart City</a>; the city's <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a> is installed with a <a href="/wiki/Power-line_communication" title="Power-line communication">Power-line communication</a> network.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city's tourism slogan is "<i>Leben. Im Quadrat.</i>" (Life. Squared.). The civic symbol of Mannheim is <i>der Wasserturm</i>, a Romanesque <a href="/wiki/Water_tower" title="Water tower">water tower</a> completed in 1886 that rises to 60 metres (200 feet) above the highest point of the <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">art nouveau</a> area <i>Friedrichsplatz</i>. Mannheim is the starting and finishing point of the <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Benz_Memorial_Route" title="Bertha Benz Memorial Route">Bertha Benz Memorial Route</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Mannheim" title="Timeline of Mannheim">Timeline of Mannheim</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name of the city was first recorded as <i>Mannenheim</i> in a legal transaction in 766, surviving in a twelfth-century copy in the <i><a href="/wiki/Lorsch_codex" title="Lorsch codex">Codex Laureshamensis</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/Lorsch_Abbey" title="Lorsch Abbey">Lorsch Abbey</a>. The name is interpreted as "the home of Manno", a short form of a <a href="/wiki/Germanic_name" title="Germanic name">Germanic name</a> such as <i>Hartmann</i> or <i>Hermann</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Mannheim remained a mere village throughout the Middle Ages.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_Age">Early Modern Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early Modern Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1606, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_IV,_Elector_Palatine" title="Frederick IV, Elector Palatine">Frederick IV, Elector Palatine</a> started building the fortress of Friedrichsburg and the adjacent city centre with its grid of streets and avenues. On January 24, 1607, Frederick IV gave Mannheim the status of a "city", whether it really was one by then or not.</p>
<p>Mannheim was mostly levelled during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirty Years War">Thirty Years War</a> around 1622 by the forces of <a href="/wiki/Johan_t%27Serclaes,_Count_of_Tilly" class="mw-redirect" title="Johan t'Serclaes, Count of Tilly">Johan Tilly's troops</a>. After being rebuilt, it was again severely damaged by the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French Army</a> in 1689 during the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years%27_War" title="Nine Years' War">Nine Years' War</a>.</p>
<p>After the rebuilding of Mannheim that began in 1698, the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_the_Palatinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of the Palatinate">Electorate of the Palatinate</a> was moved from Heidelberg to Mannheim in 1720 when <a href="/wiki/Karl_III_Philip,_Elector_Palatine" class="mw-redirect" title="Karl III Philip, Elector Palatine">Karl III Philip, Elector Palatine</a> began construction of Mannheim Palace and the <a href="/wiki/Jesuit_Church_(Mannheim)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit Church (Mannheim)">Jesuit Church</a>; they were completed in 1760.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_and_19th_centuries">18th and 19th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 18th and 19th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During the eighteenth century, Mannheim was the home of the "<a href="/wiki/Mannheim_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Mannheim School">Mannheim School</a>" of <a href="/wiki/European_classical_music" class="mw-redirect" title="European classical music">classical music composers</a>. Mannheim was said to have one of the best court orchestras in Europe under the leadership of the conductor <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Grua" title="Carlo Grua">Carlo Grua</a>. The royal court of the Palatinate left Mannheim in 1778. Two decades later, in 1802, Mannheim was removed from the Palatinate and given to the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Baden" title="Grand Duchy of Baden">Grand Duchy of Baden</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mannheim_Wasserturm_110808.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Mannheim_Wasserturm_110808.jpg/180px-Mannheim_Wasserturm_110808.jpg" width="180" height="271" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Mannheim_Wasserturm_110808.jpg/270px-Mannheim_Wasserturm_110808.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Mannheim_Wasserturm_110808.jpg/360px-Mannheim_Wasserturm_110808.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="4288" /></a>

Wasserturm <i>(water tower)</i>, Mannheim's landmark</div>

<p>In 1819, <a href="/wiki/Norwich_Duff" title="Norwich Duff">Norwich Duff</a> wrote of Mannheim:</p>
<div class="quotebox" style="margin: auto; width:750px; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 90%; background-color: #e3e5e5;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div style="margin:0px 2em;">Mannheim is in the Duchy of Baden and situated at the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neckar" title="Neckar">Neckar</a> over both of which there is a bridge of boats. This is the third town of this name having been twice burnt. The houses are large, and the streets broad and at right angles to each other, and is one of the most airy clean towns I have seen in Germany. It was formerly fortified, but the fortifications were raised in 1806 and gardens fill their places. There is a large <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau" title="Château">château</a> here belonging to the Grand Duke and a very good garden; part of the château was destroyed when the town was bombarded and has never since been repaired, the other part is occupied by the <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phanie_de_Beauharnais" title="Stéphanie de Beauharnais">Grand Duchess, widow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Karl,_Grand_Duke_of_Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Karl, Grand Duke of Baden">late Grand Duke</a> who was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I,_Grand_Duke_of_Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwig I, Grand Duke of Baden">his uncle</a> having left only three daughters. She is the sister of <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Beauharnais" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene Beauharnais">Eugene Beauharnais</a> [sic; in fact, she was his second cousin]. There is a cathedral, a theatre which is considered good, an observatory, a gallery of pictures at the château, and some private collections. About two kilometres (one point two miles) below the town the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Army" title="Imperial Russian Army">Russian Army</a> crossed the Rhine in 1813. Population 18,300.</div>
<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">“</div>
<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">”</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1819, <a href="/wiki/August_von_Kotzebue" title="August von Kotzebue">August von Kotzebue</a> was assassinated in Mannheim.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">climate crisis of 1816-17</a> caused famine and the death of many horses in Mannheim. That year <a href="/wiki/Karl_Drais" title="Karl Drais">Karl Drais</a> invented the first bicycle.</p>
<p>Infrastructure improvements included the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_Harbour" title="Mannheim Harbour">Rhine Harbour</a> in 1828 and construction of the <a href="/wiki/Mannheim%E2%80%93Karlsruhe%E2%80%93Basel_railway" title="Mannheim–Karlsruhe–Basel railway">first Baden railway</a>, which opened from Mannheim to Heidelberg in 1840. Influenced by the economic rise of the middle class, another golden age of Mannheim gradually began. In the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848_in_the_German_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Revolutions of 1848 in the German states">March Revolution of 1848</a>, the city was a centre for political and revolutionary activity.</p>
<p>In 1865, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engelhorn" title="Friedrich Engelhorn">Friedrich Engelhorn</a> founded the <i>Badische Anilin- und Soda-Fabrik</i> (Baden Aniline and Soda Factory, <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a>) in Mannheim, but the factory was constructed across the Rhine in Ludwigshafen because Mannheim residents feared <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> from its operations. From this dye factory, BASF has developed into the largest chemical company in the world. After opening a workshop in Mannheim in 1871 and patenting engines from 1878, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Benz" title="Karl Benz">Karl Benz</a> patented the first motor car in 1886. He was born in Mühlburg (now part of Karlsruhe).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_20th_century_and_World_War_I">Early 20th century and World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early 20th century and World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/List_of_Sch%C3%BCtte-Lanz_airships" title="List of Schütte-Lanz airships">Schütte-Lanz company</a>, founded by Karl Lanz and Johann Schütte in 1909, built 22 airships. The company's main competitor was the <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a> works.</p>
<p>When World War I broke out in 1914, Mannheim's industrial plants played a key role in Germany's war economy. This contributed to the fact that, on 27 May 1915, Ludwigshafen was the world's first civilian settlement behind the battle lines to be bombed from the air. French aircraft attacked the BASF plants, thereby killing twelve people. The precedent was set for this attack by Germany's repeated air raids against British civilian populations throughout southeastern Britain during the first half of 1915.</p>
<p>When Germany lost the war in 1918, according to the peace terms, the left bank of the Rhine was occupied by French troops. The French occupation lasted until 1930, and some of Ludwigshafen's most elegant houses were erected for the officers of the French garrison.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inter-war_period">Inter-war period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Inter-war period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the First World War, the Heinrich Lanz Company built the Bulldog, an advanced tractor, powered by heavy oil. As a result of the invention of the pre-combustion chamber by <a href="/wiki/Prosper_L%27Orange" title="Prosper L'Orange">Prosper L'Orange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benz_%26_Cie." class="mw-redirect" title="Benz &amp; Cie.">Benz &amp; Cie.</a> developed the world's first compact diesel-powered car at its motor works in Mannheim in 1923. In 1922, the <i>Grosskraftwerk Mannheim</i> (Mannheim large power station) was opened. By 1930, the city, along with its sister city of Ludwigshafen, which had developed out of the old Mannheim Rheinschanze, had a population of 385,000.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>, at least 2,262 of Mannheim's Jews were <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">despatched for extermination</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Mannheim_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Mannheim in World War II">Air raids on Mannheim</a> almost completely destroyed the city during the Second World War. Since Mannheim was an important industrial centre for <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, Mannheim was heavily damaged during <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing" title="Strategic bombing">aerial bombing</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">R.A.F.</a> and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Air_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army Air Forces">U.S. Air Force</a>. In addition to bombing the important factories, the R.A.F. razed the city center of Mannheim with nighttime <a href="/wiki/Area_bombing" class="mw-redirect" title="Area bombing">area bombing</a>. Some sources state that the first deliberate "<a href="/wiki/Terror_bombing" class="mw-redirect" title="Terror bombing">terror bombing</a>" of the war occurred at Mannheim on December 16, 1940.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Allied ground advance into Germany reached Mannheim in late March 1945, which was potentially well-defended by German forces, however, they suddenly abandoned the city and the U.S. <a href="/wiki/44th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="44th Infantry Division (United States)">44th Infantry Division</a> entered unopposed on 29 March 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> There has been a large American military presence in the Mannheim area ever since (see <i>United States military installations</i> below).</p>

<p>Rebuilding of the city began laboriously. <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_Palace" title="Mannheim Palace">Mannheim Palace</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim_water_tower&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mannheim water tower (page does not exist)">water tower</a> (<i>Wasserturm</i>) eventually were rebuilt and the <a href="/wiki/National_Theatre_Mannheim" title="National Theatre Mannheim">National Theatre</a> was replaced by a new building at a new location. At the old location there is a monument to <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Friedrich Schiller</a> and the <i>Zum Zwischen-Akt</i> pub. The housing shortage led to the development of many new residential areas.</p>
<p>In 1964, the City Hospital (<i>Städtische Krankenhaus</i>) became part of the <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg_University_Faculty_of_Medicine_in_Mannheim" title="Heidelberg University Faculty of Medicine in Mannheim">Neckar Faculty of Heidelberg University for Clinical Medicine in Mannheim</a>. In 1967, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mannheim" title="University of Mannheim">University of Mannheim</a> was established in the city.</p>
<p>In 1975, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesgartenschau" title="Bundesgartenschau">Bundesgartenschau</a> (Federal horticulture show) was celebrated in <a href="/wiki/Luisenpark" title="Luisenpark">Luisen</a> and Herzogenried parks. A number of pieces of infrastructure were developed for the show: the <a href="/wiki/Fernmeldeturm_Mannheim" title="Fernmeldeturm Mannheim">telecommunications tower</a> and a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kurt-Schumacher_Bridge_(Mannheim)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kurt-Schumacher Bridge (Mannheim) (page does not exist)">second bridge</a> across the Rhine were built, the pedestrian zone was established, the new Rosengarten conference centre was opened and the <a href="/wiki/Aerobus" title="Aerobus">Aerobus</a> was installed as a temporary transport system.</p>
<p>A number of major projects were completed in the 1980s and 1990s: a planetarium, an extension to the art gallery, the new Reiß Museum, Stadthaus, a new <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_May_Market" title="Mannheim May Market">May Market</a> ground, synagogue, mosque, <a href="/wiki/Technoseum" title="Technoseum">State Museum for Technology and Work</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl-Benz-Stadion" title="Carl-Benz-Stadion">Carl-Benz stadium</a> and the Fahrlach tunnel were opened.</p>
<p>Mannheim has lost many industrial jobs, although in the recent past the city was economically dominated by manufacturing. The city tried in the past to prevent the establishment of service providers by designating some locations as industrial areas. A prime example of the current trend is the construction of the Victoria Tower (Victoria-Turm) in 2001, one of the tallest buildings in the city, on railway land.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-reunification">Post-reunification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-reunification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mannheim celebrated its 400th anniversary with a series of cultural and other events throughout 2007. The 400th anniversary proper was in 2006, since <a href="/wiki/Frederick_IV,_Elector_Palatine" title="Frederick IV, Elector Palatine">Frederick IV, Elector Palatine</a> laid the foundations of Mannheim citadel on 17 March 1606. In preparation for the anniversary, some urban activities were implemented, beginning in 2000: the building of the <a href="/wiki/SAP_Arena" title="SAP Arena">SAP Arena</a> with access to the city’s new eastern ring road, the rehabilitation of the pedestrian zone in Breite Straße, the arsenal and the palace, the complete transformation of the old fair ground, and the new Schafweide tram line. The concept of the anniversary of the city aimed at a diverse range of events without a dominant central event.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inventions">Inventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Inventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some important inventions were made in Mannheim.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karl_Drais" title="Karl Drais">Karl Drais</a> built the first two-wheeled <i><a href="/wiki/Draisine" title="Draisine">draisine</a></i> in 1817.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karl_Benz" title="Karl Benz">Karl Benz</a> drove the first automobile on the streets of Mannheim in 1886. At his workshop in Mannheim he produced a lightweight <a href="/wiki/Benz_Patent_Motorwagen" class="mw-redirect" title="Benz Patent Motorwagen">three-wheeled vehicle</a> powered by a single cylinder <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">petrol/gasoline</a>-fueled <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">engine</a>, first shown in public during 1886. This powered tricycle subsequently came to be widely regarded as the first <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobile/motor car</a> powered by an internal-combustion engine. Karl's wife <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Benz" title="Bertha Benz">Bertha Benz</a> undertook the world’s first <a href="/wiki/Road_trip" title="Road trip">road trip</a> by automobile from Mannheim to <a href="/wiki/Pforzheim" title="Pforzheim">Pforzheim</a> in August 1888.</li>
<li>The Lanz Bulldog, a popular tractor with a rugged, simple Diesel engine was introduced in 1921.</li>
<li>Karl Benz developed the world's first compact diesel-powered car at the <a href="/wiki/Benz_%26_Cie." class="mw-redirect" title="Benz &amp; Cie.">Benz &amp; Cie.</a> motor works in Mannheim during 1923</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Julius_Hatry" title="Julius Hatry">Julius Hatry</a> built the world's first rocket plane in 1929.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_council">City council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: City council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The council has 48 seats and is elected by direct suffrage for five years. In the local elections in Baden-Württemberg, voters are allowed to take advantage of <a href="/wiki/Cumulative_voting" title="Cumulative voting">cumulative voting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panachage" title="Panachage">vote splitting</a>. Since the Second World War the SPD, except in the elections of 1999 and 2004, has received more votes than the CDU. The next municipal election will take place in 2019.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayor">Mayor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mayor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mayor is the head of the city council and chairman of the council, being selected by direct suffrage for a term of eight years. The current mayor is Peter Kurz (SPD), who was elected during 2007 with 50.53 percent on a turnout of 36.64 percent in the first round.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Theatre_Mannheim" title="National Theatre Mannheim">National Theatre Mannheim</a> was founded in 1779 and is the oldest "Stage" in Germany. In 1782 the premier of <i>Die Räuber</i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Friedrich Schiller</a>, was shown.</p>
<p>Recently, more smaller stages have opened, such as the Oststadt-Theater, the TIG7 (Theater im Quadrat G7), the Theater Oliv, the Freilichtbühne, the Theater31, the Theater ImPuls, the Theater Felina-Areal, the Mannheimer Puppenspiele, the Kleinkunstbühne Klapsmühl', Schatzkistl, and <a href="/wiki/Zeitraumexit" title="Zeitraumexit">zeitraumexit</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mannheim" title="University of Mannheim">University of Mannheim</a> is one of Germany's younger universities. Although founded in 1967, it has its origins in the 1763-established Palatine Academy of Sciences and the former Handelshochschule. Situated in <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_Palace" title="Mannheim Palace">Mannheim Palace</a>, it is Germany's leading university in business and economics and attracts students from around the world. Described by <a href="/wiki/Die_Zeit" title="Die Zeit">"Die Zeit" magazine</a> as the 'Harvard of Germany' it is seen as the alma mater of German businessmen and women.</p>

<p>The university town also houses one of the medical schools of <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg_University" title="Heidelberg University">Heidelberg University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hochschule_Mannheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Hochschule Mannheim">Hochschule Mannheim</a>, a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg_Cooperative_State_University" title="Baden-Württemberg Cooperative State University">Duale Hochschule of the State of Baden-Württemberg</a> and several musical and theatrical academies, including the Pop Academy Mannheim, the Musikhochschule and the Theaterakademie. These institutions draw a large and diverse student body.</p>
<p>Dependents of U.S. military personnel attended Mannheim Elementary School until it closed in June 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In the 1980s the school had 2,200 students.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Mannheim is located in Germany's warmest summer region, the "<a href="/wiki/Upper_Rhine_Plain" title="Upper Rhine Plain">Rhine shift</a>". In summer, temperatures sometimes rise up to 35&#160;°C (95&#160;°F) and higher. The highest recorded temperature was 39.8&#160;°C (104&#160;°F) on August 7, 2015. The daily lows during heat waves can be very high by north European standards (around 25&#160;°C / 77&#160;°F). In comparison to other regions of Germany, Mannheim has a higher humidity in summer which causes a higher heat index. Snow is rare, even in the cold months. Precipitation occurs mostly during afternoon thunderstorms during the warmer period (average days of thunderstorms in a year is 40–50). Climate in this area has mild differences between highs and lows, and there is adequate rainfall year-round. The <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_Climate_Classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Köppen Climate Classification">Köppen Climate Classification</a> subtype for this climate is "<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification#GROUP_C:_Mild_Temperate.2Fmesothermal_climates" title="Köppen climate classification">Cfb</a>" (Marine West Coast Climate/<a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">Oceanic climate</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The successor to the Karl Benz automobile manufacturing companies begun in Mannheim, <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a>, has had a large presence in Mannheim. Today, diesel engines and buses are assembled there. The Swiss <a href="/wiki/Hoffmann%E2%80%93La_Roche" class="mw-redirect" title="Hoffmann–La Roche">Hoffmann–La Roche</a> Diagnostic group (formerly known as Boehringer Mannheim) has its division headquarters in Mannheim. Additionally, the city also hosts large factories and offices of <a href="/wiki/ABB_Asea_Brown_Boveri" class="mw-redirect" title="ABB Asea Brown Boveri">ABB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alstom" title="Alstom">Alstom</a>, <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF (Ludwigshafen)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bilfinger_Berger" class="mw-redirect" title="Bilfinger Berger">Bilfinger Berger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Inc." title="Bombardier Inc.">Bombardier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuchs_Petrolub" title="Fuchs Petrolub">Fuchs Petrolub AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deere_%26_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Deere &amp; Company">John Deere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Siemens AG">Siemens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Svenska_Cellulosa_Aktiebolaget" class="mw-redirect" title="Svenska Cellulosa Aktiebolaget">SCA</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCdzucker" title="Südzucker">Südzucker</a>, and other companies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Army_locations">U.S. Army locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: U.S. Army locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army Europe">U.S. Army Europe</a> installations were located in and near Mannheim during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. The following locations provided services to and housed the "U.S. Army Garrison Mannheim" and other units of the U.S. Army. The U.S. Army Garrison Mannheim was formally deactivated on 31 May 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin_Village" title="Benjamin Franklin Village">Benjamin Franklin Village</a> (Mannheim-Käfertal), housing. Also, it was the home of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim_American_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mannheim American High School (page does not exist)">Mannheim American High School</a> and the Middle School,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> which closed on June 9, 2011. It will be vacated by 2014.</li>
<li>Coleman Barracks and <a href="/wiki/Coleman_Army_Airfield" title="Coleman Army Airfield">Coleman Army Airfield</a> (Mannheim-Sandhofen): The headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/American_Forces_Network" title="American Forces Network">American Forces Network</a>-Europe, and the home of the Army's 28th Transportation <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">Battalion</a>. Also, the location of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corrections_Facility-Europe" title="United States Army Corrections Facility-Europe">United States Army Corrections Facility-Europe</a>. It was vacated in 2015.</li>
<li>Funari Barracks (Mannheim-Käfertal), vacated in 2014.</li>
<li>Spinelli Barracks (Mannheim-Feudenheim), vacated in 2015.</li>
<li>Sullivan Barracks (Mannheim-Käfertal): formerly the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/7th_Signal_Brigade_(United_States)" title="7th Signal Brigade (United States)">U.S. Army's 7th Signal Brigade</a> and the 529th Military Police Honor Guard Company's 2nd Platoon; vacated in 2014.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Taylor_Barracks" title="Taylor Barracks">Taylor Barracks</a> (Mannheim-Vogelstang): formerly the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Signal_Brigade_(United_States)" title="2nd Signal Brigade (United States)">U.S. Army's 2nd Signal Brigade</a>; vacated in 2011.</li>
<li>Turley Barracks (Mannheim-Käfertal): in the early 1990s was home to the 181st Transportation Bn, with companies of 40th, 41st, 51st, 590th, TTP, and HHC transportation companies and also a light infantry unit called AMFL.</li>
</ul>
<p>The following locations were part of the "U.S. Army Garrison <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>" but were within the area of the city of Mannheim; They were vacated in 2010 and 2011:</p>
<ul>
<li>Friedrichsfeld Service Center (Mannheim-Friedrichsfeld)</li>
<li>Hammonds Barracks (formerly Loretto Kaserne) (Mannheim-Seckenheim)</li>
<li>Stem Kaserne (Mannheim-Seckenheim)</li>
</ul>
<p>All personnel of the U.S. Army military community left Mannheim by 2015, some of them moving to Wiesbaden. With the exception of four barracks, all other barracks formerly occupied by the U.S. military had been returned to the German state for conversion to civilian use in 2011.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Railway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mannheim_Hauptbahnhof" title="Mannheim Hauptbahnhof">Mannheim Hauptbahnhof</a> (central station) is at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Mannheim-Stuttgart_high-speed_rail_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Mannheim-Stuttgart high-speed rail line">Mannheim-Stuttgart high-speed rail line</a> and is the most important railway junction in the southwest of Germany, served by <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">ICE</a> high-speed train system with connections to <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>–<a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>–<a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>–<a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. A new <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt%E2%80%93Mannheim_high-speed_railway" title="Frankfurt–Mannheim high-speed railway">high speed line to Frankfurt</a> also is planned to relieve the existing <a href="/wiki/Mannheim%E2%80%93Frankfurt_railway" title="Mannheim–Frankfurt railway">Mannheim–Frankfurt railway</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mannheim_Harbour" title="Mannheim Harbour">Mannheim Harbour</a> is the second largest <a href="/wiki/River_port" class="mw-redirect" title="River port">river port</a> in Germany.</p>

<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt International Airport</a> is only 65&#160;km (40&#160;mi) to the north, at various times over the years there were daily passenger flights from <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_City_Airport" title="Mannheim City Airport">Mannheim City Airport</a> (<a href="/wiki/IATA_code" class="mw-redirect" title="IATA code">IATA code</a> MHG) to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken" title="Saarbrücken">Saarbrücken</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_Public_Transport">Local Public Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mannheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Local Public Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Local public transport in Mannheim includes the <a href="/wiki/RheinNeckar_S-Bahn" class="mw-redirect" title="RheinNeckar S-Bahn">RheinNeckar S-Bahn</a>, eleven <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">tram</a> lines, and numerous bus lines operated by <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Neckar-Verkehr" title="Rhein-Neckar-Verkehr">Rhein-Neckar-Verkehr</a> (Rhine-Neckar transport) (RNV).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/RheinNeckar_S-Bahn" class="mw-redirect" title="RheinNeckar S-Bahn">RheinNeckar S-Bahn</a>, established in 2003, connects most of the Rhine-Neckar area including lines into the <a href="/wiki/Palatinate_(region)" title="Palatinate (region)">Palatinate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odenwald" title="Odenwald">Odenwald</a>, and southern <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>. All S-Bahn lines run through Mannheim Hauptbahnhof, except S5. Further S-Bahn stations are at present Mannheim-Rangierbahnhof, Mannheim-Seckenheim, and Mannheim-Friedrichsfeld-Süd.</p>
<p>The <span class="nowrap">1,000&#160;mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">3&#160;ft&#160;<span class="frac">3<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span>&#160;in</span>) <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Metre_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Metre gauge">metre gauge</a></span> integrated <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Mannheim/Ludwigshafen" title="Trams in Mannheim/Ludwigshafen">Mannheim/Ludwigshafen tramway network</a> also extends to Heidelberg. It is operated by RNV, a company wholly owned by the three cities mentioned and a couple of municipalities in the Palatinate. RNV is the result of a merger on 1 October 2009 between the region's five former municipal transportation companies.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Interurban trams are operated by RNV on a triangular route between Mannheim, <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Weinheim" title="Weinheim">Weinheim</a> that was originally established by the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Rhine_Railway_Company" title="Upper Rhine Railway Company">Upper Rhine Railway Company</a> (<i>Oberrheinische Eisenbahn</i>, OEG), and the company also operates interurban trams between <a href="/wiki/Bad_D%C3%BCrkheim" title="Bad Dürkheim">Bad Dürkheim</a>, Ludwigshafen, and Mannheim. In the 1970s a proposal to build a <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit_in_Germany#U-Bahn" title="Rapid transit in Germany">U-Bahn</a> out of the Mannheim and Ludwigshafen tramways was begun, but only small sections were built due to lack of funds. The only underground station in Mannheim is the Haltestelle Dalbergstraße. U-Bahn planning now has stopped. All public transport is offered at uniform prices set by the <a href="/wiki/Verkehrsverbund_Rhein-Neckar" title="Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Neckar">Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Neckar</a> (Rhine-Neckar transport union, VRN).</p>

<p>There are two nationally renowned football clubs in Mannheim, <a href="/wiki/SV_Waldhof_Mannheim" title="SV Waldhof Mannheim">SV Waldhof Mannheim</a>, who currently are playing in the 4th tier <a href="/wiki/Regionalliga_S%C3%BCdwest" title="Regionalliga Südwest">Regionalliga Südwest</a>, but who have played in the top tier, the <a href="/wiki/Fu%C3%9Fball-Bundesliga" class="mw-redirect" title="Fußball-Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a>; and <a href="/wiki/VfR_Mannheim" title="VfR Mannheim">VfR Mannheim</a>, winner of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_German_football#Postwar_football" title="History of German football">German championship in 1949</a>, now playing in the 5th tier <a href="/wiki/Oberliga_Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Oberliga Baden-Württemberg">Oberliga Baden-Württemberg</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Adler_Mannheim" title="Adler Mannheim">Adler Mannheim</a> (formerly MERC, Mannheimer Eis- und Rollsport-Club) is an <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> team playing in the professional <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Eishockey_Liga" title="Deutsche Eishockey Liga">Deutsche Eishockey Liga</a>, having won the championship a total of six times.</p>
<p>The city is home to the <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_Tornados" title="Mannheim Tornados">Mannheim Tornados</a>, the oldest operational baseball and softball club in Germany. The Tornados play in the first division of the <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga_(baseball)" title="Bundesliga (baseball)">Baseball Bundesliga</a> and have won the championship 11 times, more than any other club.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003 the American football club MTG Rhein-Neckar Bandits was founded. The Bandits are playing in the first German Football League which is called GFL1. In the summer about 500 people watch each game.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rhein-Neckar_L%C3%B6wen" title="Rhein-Neckar Löwen">Rhein-Neckar-Loewen</a> (Lions) are a <a href="/wiki/Team_Handball" class="mw-redirect" title="Team Handball">handball</a> team (formerly SG Kronau-Oestringen) playing in the professional <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga_(handball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bundesliga (handball)">German Handball League</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/WWE" title="WWE">WWE</a> visited Mannheim in 2008 and grossed more than half a million dollars with over 6,500 fans attending the event.</p>
<p>UFC fighter <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Siver" title="Dennis Siver">Dennis Siver</a> lives and trains in Mannheim.</p>
<p>Mannheim hosted the <a href="/wiki/European_Show_Jumping_Championships" title="European Show Jumping Championships">European Show Jumping Championships</a> in 1997, and the <a href="/wiki/2007_FEI_European_Jumping_Championship" title="2007 FEI European Jumping Championship">FEI European Jumping Championships in 2007</a> <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> 14–19 August, in the MVV-riding stadium.</p>
<p>2002 <a href="/wiki/Hobby_Horse_Polo" class="mw-redirect" title="Hobby Horse Polo">Hobby Horse Polo</a> was invented in Mannheim, evoking the classical rivalry towards "polite society" in Heidelberg.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-holz_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holz-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

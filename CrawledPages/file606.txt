<p><b>Unitarian Universalism</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Liberal_religion" title="Liberal religion">liberal religion</a> characterized by a "free and responsible search for truth and <a href="/wiki/Meaning_of_life" title="Meaning of life">meaning</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Unitarian Universalist (<b>UU</b>) Church does not have a <a href="/wiki/Creed" title="Creed">creed</a>. Instead, UUs are unified by their shared search for <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_growth" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiritual growth">spiritual growth</a>. As such, UU congregations include many agnostics, theists, and atheists among their membership. The roots of UU are in <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Christianity" title="Liberal Christianity">liberal Christianity</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Universalism" title="Christian Universalism">Universalism</a>. Unitarian Universalists state that from these traditions come a deep regard for intellectual freedom and inclusive love. Congregations and members seek inspiration and derive insight from all major world religions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The beliefs of individual Unitarian Universalists range widely, including <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnosticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pantheism" title="Pantheism">pantheism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">deism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neopaganism" class="mw-redirect" title="Neopaganism">neopaganism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Daoism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious_humanism" title="Religious humanism">Humanism</a>, and many more.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association</a> (UUA) was formed in 1961 through the consolidation of the <a href="/wiki/American_Unitarian_Association" title="American Unitarian Association">American Unitarian Association</a>, established in 1825, and the <a href="/wiki/Universalist_Church_of_America" title="Universalist Church of America">Universalist Church of America</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-harvard1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1-10">[10]</a></sup> established in 1793. It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, and serves churches mostly in the United States. A group of thirty Philippine congregations is represented as a sole member within the UUA. The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Unitarian_Council" title="Canadian Unitarian Council">Canadian Unitarian Council</a> (CUC) became an independent body in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The UUA and CUC are, in turn, two of the seventeen members of the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_of_Unitarians_and_Universalists" title="International Council of Unitarians and Universalists">International Council of Unitarians and Universalists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roots_and_Congregationalist_background">Roots and Congregationalist background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Roots and Congregationalist background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unitarian Universalism was formed from the consolidation in 1961 of two historically Christian denominations, the Universalist Church of America and the American Unitarian Association,<sup id="cite_ref-harvard1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1-10">[10]</a></sup> both based in the United States; the new organization formed in this merger was the Unitarian Universalist Association.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At the time of the North American consolidation, Unitarians and Universalists had expanded beyond their roots in liberal Christian theology. Today they draw from a variety of religious traditions. Individuals may or may not self-identify as Christians or subscribe to Christian beliefs.<sup id="cite_ref-surveys_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-surveys-14">[14]</a></sup> Unitarian Universalist congregations and fellowships tend to retain some Christian traditions, such as Sunday worship with a sermon and the singing of hymns. The extent to which the elements of any particular faith tradition are incorporated into personal spiritual practice is a matter of individual choice for congregants, in keeping with a creedless, non-dogmatic approach to spirituality and faith development.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>New England Unitarians evolved from the <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim_Fathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilgrim Fathers">Pilgrim Fathers</a>' <a href="/wiki/Congregationalism_in_the_United_States" title="Congregationalism in the United States">Congregational Christianity</a>, which was originally based on a literal reading of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>. Liberalizing Unitarians rejected the <a href="/wiki/Trinitarianism" class="mw-redirect" title="Trinitarianism">Trinitarian</a> belief in the tri-personal godhead: Father, Son, and Holy Ghost/Spirit. Instead, they asserted a unitary notion of God. In addition, they rejected the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">original sin</a>, moving away from the <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Congregationalists" class="mw-redirect" title="Congregationalists">Congregationalists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>New England <a href="/wiki/Universalists" class="mw-redirect" title="Universalists">Universalists</a> rejected the <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> forefathers' emphasis on the select few, the Elect, who were reportedly saved from eternal damnation by a just God. Instead Universalists asserted that all people will eventually be reconciled with God.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-16">[16]</a></sup> Universalists rejected the <a href="/wiki/Fire_and_brimstone" title="Fire and brimstone">hellfire and damnation</a> of the evangelical preachers, who tried to revive the <a href="/wiki/Religious_fundamentalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious fundamentalism">fundamentalist</a> Christianity of the early Pilgrim fathers.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universalism">Universalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Universalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Universalism" title="Universalism">Universalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Universalism" title="Christian Universalism">Christian Universalism</a></div>
<p>Universalists claim a long history, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">Origen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Nyssa" title="Gregory of Nyssa">Gregory of Nyssa</a>, though some modern scholars question whether either of these church fathers taught the defining doctrine of Universalism (<a href="/wiki/Universal_salvation" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal salvation">universal salvation</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>This core doctrine asserts that through Christ every single human soul shall be saved, leading to the "restitution of all things" (<a href="/wiki/Apocatastasis" title="Apocatastasis">apocatastasis</a>). In 1793, Universalism emerged as a particular denomination of Christianity in the United States, eventually called the <a href="/wiki/Universalist_Church_of_America" title="Universalist Church of America">Universalist Church of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Early American advocates of <a href="/wiki/Universal_Salvation" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Salvation">Universal Salvation</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Elhanan_Winchester" title="Elhanan Winchester">Elhanan Winchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hosea_Ballou" title="Hosea Ballou">Hosea Ballou</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Murray_(minister)" title="John Murray (minister)">John Murray</a> taught that all souls would achieve salvation, sometimes after a period resembling <a href="/wiki/Purgatory" title="Purgatory">purgatory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christian_Universalism" title="Christian Universalism">Christian Universalism</a> denies the doctrine of everlasting <a href="/wiki/Damnation" title="Damnation">damnation</a>, and proclaims belief in an entirely loving <a href="/wiki/God_in_Christianity" title="God in Christianity">God</a> who will ultimately <a href="/wiki/Salvation#Redemption" title="Salvation">redeem</a> all human beings.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unitarianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a></div>
<p>Historically, various forms of <b><a href="/wiki/Nontrinitarianism" title="Nontrinitarianism">Nontrinitarianism</a></b> have appeared within Christianity. The term may refer to any belief about the nature of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a> that affirms <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> as a singular entity and rejects the doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>, as affirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">mainstream Christianity</a>: a consensus of Christian bishops at the <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">First Council of Nicaea</a> in 325. Nontrinitarianism was especially prevalent during the theological turmoils of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>. A Spanish physician, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Servetus" title="Michael Servetus">Michael Servetus</a>, studied the Bible and concluded that the concept of the Trinity, as traditionally conceived, was not biblical. His books <i>On the Errors of the Trinity</i> and <i>Christianismi Restitutio</i> caused much uproar. Servetus was eventually arrested, convicted of heresy, and burned at the stake in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> in 1553 under the order of <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term <b>Unitarian</b> entered the English language via <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hedworth" title="Henry Hedworth">Henry Hedworth</a>, who applied it to the teachings of <a href="/wiki/Laelio_Sozzini" class="mw-redirect" title="Laelio Sozzini">Laelio Sozzini</a> and the Polish <a href="/wiki/Socinians" class="mw-redirect" title="Socinians">Socinians</a>. Unitarian churches were formally established in <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> (by the <a href="/wiki/Socinianism" title="Socinianism">Socinians</a>) in the second half of the 16th Century.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris-26">[26]</a></sup> There, the first doctrines of religious freedom in Europe were established (in the course of several diets between 1557 and 1568, see <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Torda" title="Edict of Torda">Edict of Torda</a>) under the jurisdiction of <a href="/wiki/John_Sigismund_Z%C3%A1polya" title="John Sigismund Zápolya">John Sigismund</a>, king of Hungary and Prince of Transylvania, the only Unitarian monarch. The early Unitarian church not only rejected the Trinity, but also the <a href="/wiki/Pre-existence_of_Christ" title="Pre-existence of Christ">pre-existence of Christ</a> as well as, in many cases, <a href="/wiki/Predestination" title="Predestination">predestination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">original sin</a> as put forward by <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Substitutionary_atonement" title="Substitutionary atonement">substitutionary atonement</a> of Christ developed by <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm of Canterbury</a> and John Calvin. There were several different forms of <a href="/wiki/Christology" title="Christology">Christology</a> in the beginnings of the Unitarian movement; ultimately, the dominant Christology became <a href="/wiki/Psilanthropism" title="Psilanthropism">psilanthropism</a>: that Jesus was a man, but one with a unique relationship to God.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Britain">Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Unitarianism" title="History of Unitarianism">History of Unitarianism</a></div>
<p>Influenced by the teachings of the Polish <a href="/wiki/Socinians" class="mw-redirect" title="Socinians">Socinians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Clarke" title="Samuel Clarke">Samuel Clarke</a> (1675–1729) revised the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a>, removing the Trinitarian <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">Nicene Creed</a> and references to <a href="/wiki/Divinity_of_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Divinity of Jesus">Jesus as God</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Theophilus_Lindsey" title="Theophilus Lindsey">Theophilus Lindsey</a> also revised the Book of Common Prayer to allow a more Unitarian interpretation. Neither cleric was charged under the <a href="/wiki/Blasphemy_Act_1697" title="Blasphemy Act 1697">Blasphemy Act 1697</a> that made it an <i>offense for any person, educated in or having made profession of the Christian religion, by writing, preaching, teaching or advised speaking, to deny the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Trinity">Holy Trinity</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Toleration_1689" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Toleration 1689">Act of Toleration</a> (1689) gave relief to <a href="/wiki/English_Dissenters" title="English Dissenters">English Dissenters</a>, but excluded Unitarians. The efforts of Clarke and Lindsey met with substantial criticism from the more conservative clergy and laity of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>. In response, in 1774, Lindsey applied for registration of the <a href="/wiki/Essex_Street_Chapel" title="Essex Street Chapel">Essex House</a> as a <i>Dissenting place of worship</i> with the assistance of <a href="/wiki/Barrister" title="Barrister">barrister</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Lee_(Attorney-General)" title="John Lee (Attorney-General)">John Lee</a>. On the Sunday following the registration—April 17, 1774—the first true Unitarian congregation discreetly convened in the provisional Essex Street Chapel. In attendance were Lee, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> and the agent of the Massachusetts Colony, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Priestley also founded a reform congregation, but, after his home was burned down in the <a href="/wiki/Priestley_Riots" title="Priestley Riots">Priestley Riots</a>, fled with his wife to America, where he became a leading figure in the founding of the church on American soil.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once laity and clergy relaxed their vehement opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Doctrine_of_the_Trinity_Act_1813" title="Doctrine of the Trinity Act 1813">Doctrine of the Trinity Act 1813</a>, which finally allowed for protections of dissenting religions, the <a href="/wiki/British_and_Foreign_Unitarian_Association" title="British and Foreign Unitarian Association">British and Foreign Unitarian Association</a> was founded in 1825. It has its headquarters in Essex Hall, successor to Lindsey's Essex House.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Unitarian congregations in Britain today meet under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/General_Assembly_of_Unitarian_and_Free_Christian_Churches" title="General Assembly of Unitarian and Free Christian Churches">General Assembly of Unitarian and Free Christian Churches</a>. Two that have been significant in national life are the <a href="/wiki/Cross_Street_Chapel" title="Cross Street Chapel">Cross Street Chapel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newington_Green_Unitarian_Church" title="Newington Green Unitarian Church">Newington Green Unitarian Church</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_London" title="North London">north London</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Unitarianism" title="History of Unitarianism">History of Unitarianism</a></div>
<p>In the United States, the Unitarian movement began primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Congregationalism_in_the_United_States" title="Congregationalism in the United States">Congregational</a> <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> churches of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, which were part of the <a href="/wiki/State_church" class="mw-redirect" title="State church">state church</a> of Massachusetts.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> These churches, whose buildings may still be seen today in many New England town squares, trace their roots to the division of the <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> colonies into parishes for the administration of their religious needs.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In the late 18th century, conflict grew within some of these churches between Unitarian and Trinitarian factions. In 1805, Unitarians gained key faculty positions at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University#Religion_and_philosophy" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a>. In 1819 <a href="/wiki/William_Ellery_Channing" title="William Ellery Channing">William Ellery Channing</a> preached the <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">ordination</a> sermon for <a href="/wiki/Jared_Sparks" title="Jared Sparks">Jared Sparks</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, outlining the Unitarian position. The <a href="/wiki/American_Unitarian_Association" title="American Unitarian Association">American Unitarian Association</a> was founded as a separate denomination in 1825.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> By coincidence and unknown to both parties, the AUA was formed on the same day—May 26, 1825—as the <a href="/wiki/British_and_Foreign_Unitarian_Association" title="British and Foreign Unitarian Association">British and Foreign Unitarian Association</a> <sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century, under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a> (who had been a Unitarian minister)<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and other <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalists" class="mw-redirect" title="Transcendentalists">transcendentalists</a>, Unitarianism began its long journey from <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Christianity" title="Liberal Christianity">liberal Protestantism</a> to its present more pluralist form.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Integration_1825–1961"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Integration_1825.E2.80.931961">Integration 1825–1961</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Integration 1825–1961">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Schism (religion)">schism</a> in the Congregational Churches resulting in the foundation of the American Unitarian Association, some of those churches remained within the Congregational fold and became member congregations of the Congregational organization (later the <a href="/wiki/United_Church_of_Christ" title="United Church of Christ">United Church of Christ</a>), while others voted to become Unitarian. Some of the latter eventually became part of the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">UUA</a> during a consolidation of the Unitarian and Universalist churches. Universalist churches in contrast followed a different path, having begun as independent congregations beyond the bounds of the established <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> churches entirely. Today, the UUA and the United Church of Christ cooperate jointly on <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> initiatives such as the Sexuality Education Advocacy Training project.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961, the <a href="/wiki/American_Unitarian_Association" title="American Unitarian Association">American Unitarian Association</a> (AUA) was consolidated with the <a href="/wiki/Universalist_Church_of_America" title="Universalist Church of America">Universalist Church of America</a> (UCA), thus forming the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sias_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sias-37">[37]</a></sup> In the same year, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Unitarian_Council" title="Canadian Unitarian Council">Canadian Unitarian Council</a> (CUC) formed.<sup id="cite_ref-CUCHistory_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CUCHistory-38">[38]</a></sup> The Unitarian Universalist Association (UUA) was also given corporate status in May 1961 under special acts of legislature of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth of Massachusetts">Commonwealth of Massachusetts</a> and the <a href="/wiki/State_of_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="State of New York">State of New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, the CUC and UUA dissolved their financial accord, although they continue to cooperate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-CUCHistory_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CUCHistory-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beliefs">Beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The defining belief of Unitarian Universalism is that religion is a matter of individual experience, and that, therefore, only the individual can decide what to "believe." The roots of this belief can be found in the Unitarian insistence on freedom of personal conscience in matters of faith. As a result, while Unitarian Universalists have no required creed; instead, they treat as a sacred value the complete and responsible <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_thought" title="Freedom of thought">thought</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_religion" title="Freedom of religion">belief, faith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Disposition" title="Disposition">disposition</a>. Unitarian Universalists believe that each person is free to search for their own personal truth on issues, such as the existence, nature, and <a href="/wiki/Meaning_of_life" title="Meaning of life">meaning of life</a>, deities, <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Afterlife" title="Afterlife">afterlife</a>. UUs can come from any religious background, and hold beliefs and adhere to <a href="/wiki/Moral_syncretism" title="Moral syncretism">morals</a> from a variety of <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a> or <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religions</a>. They believe that what binds them together as a faith community is not a creed, but a belief in the power and sacredness of <a href="/wiki/Covenant_(religion)" title="Covenant (religion)">covenant</a> based on unconditional love. That love is enough to hold together such variety derives from their Universalist heritage which affirms a God of all-inclusive love.</p>
<p>Current concepts about <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a>, however, are diverse among UUs. While some are still <a href="/wiki/Monotheism" title="Monotheism">monotheistic</a>, often from a <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> perspective, many profess <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnosticism</a>. UUs see no contradiction in open atheists and agnostics being members of their community because of the rich Unitarian legacy of free inquiry and <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a> in matters of faith. Still other UUs subscribe to <a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">deism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pantheism" title="Pantheism">pantheism</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">polytheism</a>. Many UUs reject the idea of deities and instead speak of the "spirit of life" that binds all <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">life on earth</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seven_Principles_and_Purposes">Seven <i>Principles and Purposes</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Seven Principles and Purposes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deliberately without an official creed or dogma (per the principle of freedom of thought), many Unitarian Universalists make use of the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association#Principles_and_purposes" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Principles and Purposes</a> as a list of principles for guiding behavior. These "Principles and Purposes" are taken from the by-laws which govern the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association</a>. While these were written to govern congregations, not individuals, many UUs use them as guides for living their faith. The "Seven Principles" were created in committee and affirmed democratically by a vote of member congregations at an annual General Assembly (a meeting of delegates from member congregations). Adopted in 1960, the full <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association#Principles_and_purposes" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Principles, Purposes and Sources</a> can be found in the article on the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association#Principles_and_purposes" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association</a>. The Principles are as follows:</p>
<p>We, the member congregations of the Unitarian Universalist Association, covenant to affirm and promote:</p>
<ul>
<li>The inherent worth and dignity of every person;<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>Justice, equity and compassion in human relations;<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>Acceptance of one another and encouragement to spiritual growth in our congregations;<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>A free and responsible search for truth and meaning;<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li>The right of conscience and the use of the democratic process within our congregations and in society at large;<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>The goal of world community with peace, liberty, and justice for all;<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>Respect for the interdependent web of all existence of which we are a part.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Unitarian Universalism is often referred to by its members as a <i>living tradition</i>, and the principles and purposes have been modified over time to reflect changes in spiritual beliefs among the membership. Most recently, the last principle, adopted in 1985 and generally known as the <i>Seventh Principle</i>, "Respect for the interdependent web of all existence of which we are a part", and a sixth source (adopted in 1995), "Spiritual teachings of earth-centered traditions which celebrate the sacred circle of life and instruct us to live in harmony with the rhythms of nature" were added to explicitly include members with <a href="/wiki/Modern_Paganism" title="Modern Paganism">neopagan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pantheism" title="Pantheism">pantheist</a> spiritualities.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Six_Sources">Six <i>Sources</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Six Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unitarian Universalists place emphasis on spiritual growth and development. The official statement of Unitarian Universalist principles describes the "sources" upon which current practice is based:<sup id="cite_ref-uua.org_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uua.org-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Direct experience of that transcending mystery and wonder, affirmed in all cultures, which moves us to a renewal of the spirit and an openness to the forces which create and uphold life;</li>
<li>Words and deeds of prophetic women and men which challenge us to confront powers and structures of evil with justice, compassion, and the transforming power of love;</li>
<li>Wisdom from the <a href="/wiki/Major_religious_groups" title="Major religious groups">world's religions</a> which inspires us in our ethical and spiritual life;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> teachings which call us to respond to God's love by loving our neighbors as ourselves;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">Humanist</a> teachings which counsel us to heed the guidance of reason and the results of science, and warn us against idolatries of the mind and spirit.</li>
<li>Spiritual teachings of <a href="/wiki/Earth_religion" title="Earth religion">earth-centered traditions</a> which celebrate the sacred circle of life and instruct us to live in harmony with the rhythms of nature.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversity_of_practices">Diversity of practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diversity of practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Unitarian belief that reason, and not creed, defines the search for truth, and the Universalist belief that God embraces all people equally has led to the current Unitarian Universalist belief that truth and spiritual meaning can be found in all faiths. This is reflected in the wide-array of spiritual practices found among UUs today. Many Unitarian Universalist congregations include Buddhist-style meditation groups, Jewish <a href="/wiki/Passover_Seder" title="Passover Seder">Seder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur" title="Yom Kippur">Yom Kippur</a> and Passover dinners, <i><a href="/wiki/Iftaar" class="mw-redirect" title="Iftaar">iftaar</a></i> meals (marking the breaking of Ramadan fast for Muslims), and Christmas Eve/<a href="/wiki/Winter_Solstice#Observances" class="mw-redirect" title="Winter Solstice">Winter Solstice</a> services. Children's and youth's religious education classes teach about the divinity of the world and the sanctity of world religions. One of its more popular curricula, Neighboring Faiths (formerly Church Across the Street), takes middle and high school participants to visit the places of worship of many faith traditions including a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> temple, a <a href="/wiki/Reform_Judaism" title="Reform Judaism">Reform</a> or <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox</a> synagogue, and a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> church.</p>
<p>There is great variety among Unitarian Universalist congregations, with some favoring particular religious beliefs or forms of worship over others, with many more home to an eclectic mix of beliefs. Regardless of their orientation, most congregations are fairly open to differing beliefs, though not always with various faith traditions represented to the same degree.</p>
<p>There is also a wide variety in how congregations conceive of themselves. Congregations call themselves "churches", "societies", "fellowships", "congregations", or eschew the use of any particular descriptor (e.g. "Sierra Foothills Unitarian Universalists"). Whether a congregation is a 'fellowship' or a 'church' sometimes hinges on whether it is led by one (or more) minister(s): those without ministers being fellowships, those with ministers being churches. Many use the name "Unitarian Universalist", (and a few "Universalist Unitarian"), having gradually adopted this formulation since consolidation in 1961. Others use names that reflect their historic roots by keeping simply the designation "Unitarian" or "Universalist" (e.g. "Community Unitarian Church at White Plains"). A few congregations use neither. For some congregations, the name can be a clue to their theological orientation. For others, avoidance of the word "church" indicates a desire to distance itself from traditional Christian theology. Sometimes the use of another term may simply indicate a congregation's lay-led or relatively new status. However, some UU congregations have grown to appreciate alternative terms such as <i>fellowship</i> and retained them even though they have grown much larger or lost features sometimes associated with their use (such as, in the case of fellowships, a traditionally lay-led worship model).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also of note is that there are many more people who identify as UU on surveys than those who attend UU churches (by a factor of four in a recent survey),<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> reflecting those who have never joined (and lapsed members) but nonetheless consider themselves part of the UU movement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Approach_to_sacred_writings">Approach to sacred writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Approach to sacred writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both Unitarianism and Universalism were originally Christian denominations, and still reference Jewish and Christian texts. Today, Unitarian Universalist approach to the Christian/Jewish <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> and other sacred works is given in <i>Our Unitarian Universalist Faith: Frequently Asked Questions</i>, published by the UUA:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>We do not, however, hold the Bible—or any other account of human experience—to be either an infallible guide or the exclusive source of truth. Much biblical material is mythical or legendary. Not that it should be discarded for that reason! Rather, it should be treasured for what it is. We believe that we should read the Bible as we read other books—with imagination and a critical eye. We also respect the sacred literature of other religions. Contemporary works of science, art, and social commentary are valued as well. We hold, in the words of an old liberal formulation, that "revelation is not sealed." Unitarian Universalists aspire to truth as wide as the world—we look to find truth anywhere, universally.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In short, Unitarian Universalists respect the important religious texts of other religions. UUs believe that all religions can coexist if viewed with the concept of love for one's neighbor and for oneself. Other church members who do not believe in a particular text or doctrine are encouraged to respect it as a historically significant literary work that should be viewed with an open mind. It is intended that in this way, individuals from all religions or spiritual backgrounds could live peaceably.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elevator_speeches">Elevator speeches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Elevator speeches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, <i>UU World</i> magazine asked for contributions of "<a href="/wiki/Elevator_pitch" title="Elevator pitch">elevator speeches</a>" explaining Unitarian Universalism.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> These are short speeches that could be made in the course of an elevator ride to those who knew nothing of the religion. Here are examples of the speeches submitted:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In Unitarian Universalist congregations, we gather in community to support our individual spiritual journeys. We trust that openness to one another's experiences will enhance our understanding of our own links with the divine, with our history, and with one another.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Rev. Jonalu Johnstone, Oklahoma City, OK<sup id="cite_ref-affirmations_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-affirmations-52">[52]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Most Unitarian Universalists believe that nobody has a monopoly on all truth, or ultimate proof of the truth of everything in any one belief. Therefore, one's own truth is unprovable, as is that of others. Consequently, we should respect the beliefs of others, as well as their right to hold those beliefs. Conversely, we expect that others should respect our right to our own beliefs. Several UU's then, would likely hold as many different beliefs. Other beliefs they may hold in common are a respect for others, for nature, and for common decency, leading to a particular caring for the poor, the weak and the downtrodden. As a result, issues of justice, including social justice are held in common among most.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Gene Douglas, Harrah, OK<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It's a blessing each of us was born; It matters what we do with our lives; What each of us knows about God is a piece of the truth; We don't have to do it alone.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Laila Ibrahim, Berkeley, CA<sup id="cite_ref-affirmations_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-affirmations-52">[52]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Worship_and_ritual">Worship and ritual</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Worship and ritual">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As in theology, Unitarian Universalist <a href="/wiki/Worship" title="Worship">worship</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> are often a combination of elements derived from other faith traditions alongside original practices and symbols. In form, church services might be difficult to distinguish from those of a <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> church, but they vary widely among congregations.<sup id="cite_ref-Sias_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sias-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbols">Symbols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Symbols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common symbol of Unitarian Universalism is the <a href="/wiki/Flaming_chalice" title="Flaming chalice">flaming chalice</a>, often framed by two overlapping rings that many interpret as representing Unitarianism and Universalism (the symbol has no official interpretation). The chalice itself has long been a symbol of liberal religion, and indeed liberal Christianity (the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church_(Disciples_of_Christ)" title="Christian Church (Disciples of Christ)">Disciples of Christ</a> also use a chalice as their denomination symbol<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>). The flaming chalice was initially the logo of the Unitarian Service Committee during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>. It was created by Austrian artist Hans Deutsch, inspired by "the kind of chalice which the Greeks and Romans put on their altars. The holy oil burning in it is a symbol of helpfulness and sacrifice."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nevertheless, other interpretations have been suggested, such as the chalice used by the followers of Czech <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or its vague resemblance to a cross in some stylized representations. Many UU congregations light a chalice at the beginning of worship services. Other symbols include an <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross_variants#Modern_innovations" title="Christian cross variants">off-center cross within a circle</a> (a Universalist symbol associated with the Humiliati movement in the 1950s, a group of reformist, liturgically minded clergy seeking to revive Universalism).</p>
<p>Other symbols include a pair of open hands releasing a <a href="/wiki/Dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Dove">dove</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Services_of_worship">Services of worship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Services of worship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Religious services are usually held on Sundays and most closely resemble the form and format of Protestant worship in the Reformed tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-Sias_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sias-37">[37]</a></sup> Services at a vast majority of congregations follow a structure that focuses on a sermon or presentation by a minister, a lay leader of the congregation, or an invited speaker.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Sermons may cover a wide range of topics. Since Unitarian Universalists do not recognize a particular text or set of texts as primary or inherently superior, inspiration can be found in many different religious or cultural texts as well as the personal experiences of the minister.</p>
<p>The service also includes hymn-singing, accompanied by organ, piano, or other available instruments, and possibly led by a song leader or choir. The most recent worship songbook published by the denomination, <i>Singing the Journey</i><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> contains 75 songs and is a supplement to the older <i>Singing the Living Tradition</i> which contains readings as well.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Hymns typically sung in UU services come from a variety of sources—traditional <a href="/wiki/Hymn_tune" title="Hymn tune">hymn tunes</a> with new or adapted lyrics, spirituals, folk songs from various cultures, or original compositions by Unitarian Universalist musicians are just a few. Instrumental music is also a common feature of the typical worship service, including preludes, offertory music, postludes, or music for contemplation.</p>
<p>Pastoral elements of the service may include a time for sharing Joys and Sorrows/Concerns, where individuals in the congregation are invited to <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_use_of_lights" title="Ceremonial use of lights">light a candle</a> and/or say a few words about important events in their personal lives. Many UU services also include a time of <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Prayer" title="Prayer">prayer</a>, led by the minister or service leader, both spoken and silent. Responsive readings and stories for children are also typical. Many congregations also allow for a time at the end of the service, called "talk back", where members of the congregation can respond to the sermon with their own insights and questions, or even disagree with the viewpoint expressed by the minister or invited speaker.</p>
<p>Many UU congregations no longer observe the Christian sacraments of <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">communion</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">confirmation</a>, at least in their traditional forms or under their traditional names. Congregations that continue these practices under their more traditional names are often <a href="/wiki/Federated_congregation" title="Federated congregation">federated churches</a> or members of the Council of Christian Churches within the Unitarian Universalist Association (CCCUUA), or may have active chapters associated with the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Christian_Fellowship" title="Unitarian Universalist Christian Fellowship">Unitarian Universalist Christian Fellowship</a> or similar covenant groups.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> "Child dedications" often replace more traditional infant baptisms (though it should be noted that such "dedications" are sometimes practiced even in "orthodox" Christian communities that do not baptize infants for theological reasons). Annual celebrations of <a href="/wiki/Water_Communion" title="Water Communion">Water Communion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flower_Communion" title="Flower Communion">Flower Communion</a> may replace or supplement Christian-style communion (though many pluralist and Christian-oriented congregations may celebrate or otherwise make provisions for communion on Christian holy days).<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Confirmation may be replaced by a "<a href="/wiki/Coming_of_Age_(Unitarian_Universalism)" title="Coming of Age (Unitarian Universalism)">Coming of Age</a>" program, in which teenagers explore their individual religious identity, often developing their own credo. After they have completed exploring their spiritual beliefs, they write a speech about it which they then personally deliver to the congregation.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_politics_of_Unitarians_and_Universalists">Historical politics of Unitarians and Universalists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Historical politics of Unitarians and Universalists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Universalist_Church_of_America" title="Universalist Church of America">Universalist Church of America</a></div>
<p>In the 19th century, Unitarians and Universalists were active in <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">abolitionism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Women's movement">women's movement</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Temperance_movement_in_the_United_States" title="Temperance movement in the United States">temperance movement</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Social_reform" class="mw-redirect" title="Social reform">social reform</a> movements. The <a href="/wiki/Rochester_Women%27s_Rights_Convention_of_1848" title="Rochester Women's Rights Convention of 1848">second women's rights convention</a> was held at the <a href="/wiki/First_Unitarian_Church_of_Rochester" title="First Unitarian Church of Rochester">First Unitarian Church of Rochester, New York</a>. Additionally, four <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">Presidents of the United States</a> were Unitarians: <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Quincy_Adams" title="John Quincy Adams">John Quincy Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millard_Fillmore" title="Millard Fillmore">Millard Fillmore</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<p>Historically, Unitarian Universalists have often been active in political causes, notably the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/LGBT_social_movements" title="LGBT social movements">LGBT rights movement</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> movement, and the <a href="/wiki/Feminist_movement" title="Feminist movement">feminist movement</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">Susan B. Anthony</a>, a Unitarian and <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quaker</a>, was extremely influential in the women's <a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">suffrage</a> movement. Unitarian Universalists and Quakers still share many principles. It is therefore common to see Unitarian Universalists and Quakers working together.</p>
<p>UU's were and are still very involved in the fight to end <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a> in the United States. <a href="/wiki/John_Haynes_Holmes" title="John Haynes Holmes">John Haynes Holmes</a>, a Unitarian minister and social activist at The Community Church of New York—Unitarian Universalist were among the founders of both the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Colored_People" class="mw-redirect" title="National Association for the Advancement of Colored People">National Association for the Advancement of Colored People</a> (NAACP) in 1909 and the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a> (ACLU), chairing the latter for a time. <a href="/wiki/James_J._Reeb" class="mw-redirect" title="James J. Reeb">James J. Reeb</a>, a minister at <a href="/wiki/All_Souls_Church,_Unitarian_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="All Souls Church, Unitarian (Washington, D.C.)">All Souls Church, Unitarian</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Christian_Leadership_Conference" title="Southern Christian Leadership Conference">Southern Christian Leadership Conference</a>, was clubbed in <a href="/wiki/Selma,_Alabama" title="Selma, Alabama">Selma, Alabama</a> on March 8, 1965, and died two days later of massive head trauma. Two weeks after his death, <a href="/wiki/Viola_Liuzzo" title="Viola Liuzzo">Viola Liuzzo</a>, a Unitarian Universalist civil rights activist, was murdered by white supremacists after her participation in the protest march from Selma to Montgomery, Alabama. The <a href="/wiki/Selma_to_Montgomery_marches" title="Selma to Montgomery marches">Selma to Montgomery marches</a> for voting rights are best known as <a href="/wiki/Bloody_Sunday_(1965)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bloody Sunday (1965)">Bloody Sunday</a>, although technically that refers only to March 7, the most violent day of the three.</p>
<p>The past head of the Unitarian Universalist Association 2001–2009, Rev. <a href="/wiki/William_G._Sinkford" title="William G. Sinkford">William G. Sinkford</a>, is <a href="/wiki/African-American" class="mw-redirect" title="African-American">African-American</a>, making Unitarian Universalism one of the first traditionally white denominations to be headed by a member of a racial minority.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Modern_liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Modern liberalism in the United States">political liberals</a> make up a clear majority of Unitarian Universalists, the UU movement aspires to diversity, and officially welcomes congregants regardless of their political views. Politically conservative Unitarian Universalists point out that neither religious liberalism nor the Principles and Purposes of the UUA require liberal politics. Like the beliefs of Unitarian Universalists, politics are decided by individuals, not by congregations or the denomination.</p>
<p>Several congregations have undertaken a series of organizational, procedural and practical steps to become acknowledged as a "<a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalism_and_LGBT_topics#Welcoming_Congregation" class="mw-redirect" title="Unitarian Universalism and LGBT topics">Welcoming Congregation</a>": a congregation which has taken specific steps to welcome and integrate gay, lesbian, bisexual &amp; transgender (LGBT) members. UU ministers perform <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_unions" class="mw-redirect" title="Same-sex unions">same-sex unions</a> and now <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriages" class="mw-redirect" title="Same-sex marriages">same-sex marriages</a> where legal (and sometimes when not, as a form of civil protest). On June 29, 1984, the Unitarian Universalists became the first major church "to approve religious blessings on homosexual unions."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Unitarian Universalists have been in the forefront of the work to make same-sex marriages legal in their local states and provinces, as well as on the national level. Gay men, bisexuals, and lesbians are also regularly <a href="/wiki/Ordained" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordained">ordained</a> as ministers, and a number of gay, bisexual, and lesbian ministers have, themselves, now become legally married to their partners. In May 2004, <a href="/wiki/Arlington_Street_Church_(Boston)" class="mw-redirect" title="Arlington Street Church (Boston)">Arlington Street Church</a>, in Boston, Massachusetts, was the site of the first state-sanctioned same-sex marriage in the United States. The official stance of the UUA is for the legalization of same-sex marriage—"Standing on the Side of Love". In 2004 UU minister Rev. Debra Haffner of <a href="/wiki/The_Religious_Institute_on_Sexual_Morality,_Justice,_and_Healing" title="The Religious Institute on Sexual Morality, Justice, and Healing">The Religious Institute on Sexual Morality, Justice, and Healing</a> published <i>An Open Letter on Religious Leaders on Marriage Equality</i> to affirm same-sex marriage from a multi-faith perspective. In December 2009, Washington, D.C. Mayor <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Fenty" title="Adrian Fenty">Adrian Fenty</a> signed the bill to legalize same-sex marriage for the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a> in <a href="/wiki/All_Souls_Church,_Unitarian_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="All Souls Church, Unitarian (Washington, D.C.)">All Souls Church, Unitarian (Washington, D.C.)</a></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalists_for_Polyamory_Awareness" title="Unitarian Universalists for Polyamory Awareness">Unitarian Universalists for Polyamory Awareness</a> engages UU ministers and other leaders to educate them on <a href="/wiki/Polyamory" title="Polyamory">polyamory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> At the 2015 <a href="/wiki/General_Assembly_(Unitarian_Universalist_Association)" title="General Assembly (Unitarian Universalist Association)">UUA General Assembly</a>, the Association's non-discrimination rule was amended to include the category of "family and relationship structures";<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> the UUA has yet to take specific follow-up action on this, however.</p>
<p>Many congregations are heavily involved in projects and efforts aimed at supporting environmental causes and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>. These are often termed "seventh principle" activities because of the seventh principle quoted above.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External">External</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: External">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_formal_creed">Lack of formal creed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Lack of formal creed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The lack of formal creed has been a cause for criticism among some who argue that Unitarian Universalism is thus without religious content.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In May 2004, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> <a href="/wiki/Comptroller" title="Comptroller">Comptroller</a> <a href="/wiki/Carole_Keeton_Strayhorn" title="Carole Keeton Strayhorn">Carole Keeton Strayhorn</a> ruled that Unitarian Universalism was not a "religion" because it "does not have one system of belief", and stripped the Red River Unitarian Universalist Church in <a href="/wiki/Denison,_Texas" title="Denison, Texas">Denison, Texas</a>, of its tax-exempt status. However, within weeks, Strayhorn reversed her decision.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Confusion_with_other_groups">Confusion with other groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Confusion with other groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are separate movements and organizations who hold to classical Unitarian or <a href="/wiki/Christian_Universalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Universalist">Christian Universalist</a> Christian theology and do not belong to the Unitarian Universalist Association or consider themselves UUs. The <a href="/wiki/American_Unitarian_Conference" title="American Unitarian Conference">American Unitarian Conference</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Universalist_Association" title="Christian Universalist Association">Christian Universalist Association</a> are the two most significant organizations representing these theological beliefs today. Christians who hold these beliefs tend to consider themselves the true Unitarians or Universalists and heirs of the theological legacy of the original <a href="/wiki/American_Unitarian_Association" title="American Unitarian Association">American Unitarian Association</a> or <a href="/wiki/Universalist_Church_of_America" title="Universalist Church of America">Universalist Church of America</a>, and they do not wish to be confused with UUs and UUism. The <a href="/wiki/Unity_Church" title="Unity Church">Unity Church</a> is another denomination that is often confused with Unitarian Universalism.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal">Internal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Internal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_of_reverence">Language of reverence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Language of reverence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the presidency of the Rev. William Sinkford, debate within the UU movement has roiled over his call to return to or create an authentic UU "language of reverence." Sinkford has suggested that UUs have abandoned traditional religious language, thereby abandoning words with potential power to others who will then dictate their meanings in the public sphere. He has suggested that Unitarian Universalists regain their proper seat at the interfaith table by making this language their own. Others have reacted to this call by believing it to be part of an effort to return UU congregations to more orthodox Christian worship patterns. Sinkford has denied this, citing the words of UU humanists as examples of what he means by the "language of reverence." The debate seems<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (November 2010)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> part and parcel of an attendant effort at increasing biblical literacy among Unitarian Universalists, including the publication of a book by the UUA's <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Press" title="Beacon Press">Beacon Press</a> written by former UUA President John Buehrens.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The book is titled <i>Understanding the Bible: An Introduction for Skeptics, Seekers, and Religious Liberals</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and is meant as a kind of handbook to be read alongside the Bible itself. It provides interpretative strategies, so that UUs (among others) might be able to engage in public debate about what the Bible says from a liberal religious perspective, rather than relinquishing to religious conservatives, and other more literal interpretations, all control over the book's contents and significance in matters of public and civic import. Also an important work by Rev. Buehrens, along with Forrest Church, is <i>A Chosen Faith: An Introduction to Unitarian Universalism</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> in which the authors explore the many sources of the living tradition of their chosen faith.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Borrowing_from_other_religions">Borrowing from other religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Borrowing from other religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The "borrowing" of religious rituals from other faith traditions by Unitarian Universalists was discussed at the UU General Assembly in 2001 during a seminar titled "Cultural Appropriation: Reckless Borrowing or Appropriate Cultural Sharing" by the Religious Education Dept, UUA.<sup id="cite_ref-CulturApp_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CulturApp-74">[74]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Of particular discussion was the borrowing rituals and practices that are sacred to specific tribes or using spiritual practices without real context.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When UUs pick and choose from these things, it trivializes their spiritual practices. The specificity [of their use] is so complete, that visiting Native Americans do not participate in another tribe's rituals, and to do so would be perceived as foolish. I would not even practice the rituals of my own tribe, because I am not an elder or spiritual leader. If this is true of her own people, then the use of these things by others who share no cultural context is seen not only as particularly foolish and inappropriate. Not all of this usage is inappropriate, though. Some taped music, written prayers, that kind of thing, might be all right, but it's not right to fool around with it. If it's not in context, if the user is not walking with us, if the user is not part of our struggle, then it is presumptuous.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Reverend Danielle Di Bona, 2001 General Assembly<sup id="cite_ref-CulturApp_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CulturApp-74">[74]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Australia_and_New_Zealand_Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Australia and New Zealand Unitarian Universalist Association">Australia and New Zealand Unitarian Universalist Association</a> (ANZUUA) is a UU organization which serves as the organizing body for Unitarian and Universalist congregations in Australia and New Zealand.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Larger_Fellowship" title="Church of the Larger Fellowship">Church of the Larger Fellowship</a> (CLF) exists to serve UUs remote from any physical congregation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Canadian_Unitarians_for_Social_Justice" title="Canadian Unitarians for Social Justice">Canadian Unitarians for Social Justice</a> (CUSJ) is a Canadian UU social justice organization that is an associate member of the CUC.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Canadian_Unitarian_Council" title="Canadian Unitarian Council">Canadian Unitarian Council</a> (CUC) is the national body for UU congregations in Canada. They were a member of the UUA up until July 2002.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Canadian_Unitarian_Universalist_Women%27s_Association" title="Canadian Unitarian Universalist Women's Association">Canadian Unitarian Universalist Women's Association</a> (CUUWA) is a Canadian UU women's rights organization that is an associate member of the CUC.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Covenant_of_Unitarian_Universalist_Pagans" title="Covenant of Unitarian Universalist Pagans">Covenant of Unitarian Universalist Pagans</a> (CUUPS) is an association of UUs who define themselves as <a href="/wiki/Pagan" class="mw-redirect" title="Pagan">Pagans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neopaganism" class="mw-redirect" title="Neopaganism">Neopagans</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/European_Unitarian_Universalists" title="European Unitarian Universalists">European Unitarian Universalists</a> (EUU) is a network connecting Unitarian Universalists and English-speaking UU fellowships in Europe.</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.faitharchitects.org/">Faith Architects</a> equips UU young adults (age 18–35, inclusive) with the tools necessary to build and fortify UU young adult communities. They do this through a program called the Scaffold Series which consists of curricula in three different focus areas: Community Building, Social justice, and Worship.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Council_of_Unitarians_and_Universalists" title="International Council of Unitarians and Universalists">International Council of Unitarians and Universalists</a> (ICUU) represents Unitarian, Universalist, and UU churches worldwide. The UUA and CUC are both members of this organization.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leather_%26_Grace" title="Leather &amp; Grace">Leather &amp; Grace ~ Unitarian Universalists for BDSM Awareness</a> (L&amp;G) is an organization of UUs who identify with or support the <a href="/wiki/BDSM" title="BDSM">BDSM</a>/<a href="/wiki/Kink_(sexual)" title="Kink (sexual)">kink</a> community.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Promise_the_Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Promise the Children">Promise the Children</a> is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. Promise the Children's mission is to help UUs advocate for and with children and youth. Promise the Children is also an Independent Affiliate of the UUA.</li>
<li>Religious Youth Empowerment, Inc. (RYE) is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. RYE is a nonprofit created by bridged YRUUers whose goals are to empower and fund the UU youth and help network between UU youth of different UUA districts as well as between UU youth and UU young adults. RYE is currently not yet affiliated with the UUA.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></li>
<li>Unitarian Bahai Association (UBA) and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://unitarianbahai.angelfire.com/">Unitarian Bahai Fellowship</a> (UBF) are associations for UUs who define themselves as Bahai.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association</a> (UUA) is the largest association of Unitarian, Universalist, and UU congregations in the world, and the most well-known. It operates mainly within the United States. A few Unitarian and UU congregations in other countries, such as San Miguel de Allende (Mexico), Puerto Rico,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Auckland (New Zealand),<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> and a few others are also members of the UUA. Currently, the UUA represents 1,078 member congregations[11] that collectively include more than 217,000 members.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Buddhist_Fellowship" title="Unitarian Universalist Buddhist Fellowship">Unitarian Universalist Buddhist Fellowship</a> (UUBF) is an association of UUs who define themselves as Buddhists.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Christian_Fellowship" title="Unitarian Universalist Christian Fellowship">Unitarian Universalist Christian Fellowship</a> (UUCF) is an association of UUs who define themselves as Christians.</li>
<li>Unitarian Universalist Faith Alliance and Ministries<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> (UUFAM) is an independent UU religious organization, covenanting community, ordaining body and faith based post-denomination. UUFAM intends to make application, in the near future, with the UUA, as an emerging/covenanting community within Unitarian Universalism. UUFAM affirms Judeo-Christian spirituality, and Deist concepts as a way of worship. Knowledge and wisdom from global truths complement their values. UUFAM advocates "sacred eco spirituality" (sacred space/ creation spirituality) as an expression of faith. Faith groups meet for worship at meaningful times and locations, where they celebrate the awe and mystery of the divine. Music, art, dance and literature are pathways of one's journey. Reverence is given to Unitarian/ Universalist history and philosophy during service.The UUFAM Mission Statement advocates Peace and Justice by supporting public safety officials, and the military. "We recall to mind their dedication to God, country and humanity." UUFAM 's goals are embodied in the virtues of faith, hope and love. UUFAM brings to light a new "conscious awareness" of Judeo, Christian and Deist theology, by gathering in smaller fellowships, without the need to build "brick &amp; mortar" edifices as a way of promoting the UU faith. UUFAM promotes awareness of their faith on their website, and social networks. In essence, UUFAM is a UU Judeo-Christian/ Deist assembly who gather in fellowship, be it in person or via the net.</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://uuja.org/">Unitarian Universalists for Jewish Awareness</a> (UUJA) is an association of UUs who define themselves as Jews.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalists_for_Polyamory_Awareness" title="Unitarian Universalists for Polyamory Awareness">Unitarian Universalists for Polyamory Awareness</a> is an association of UUs who support officially recognizing <a href="/wiki/Polyamory" title="Polyamory">polyamory</a> as a valid lifestyle.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Service_Committee" title="Unitarian Universalist Service Committee">Unitarian Universalist Service Committee</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Nonsectarian" title="Nonsectarian">nonsectarian</a> organization devoted to promoting human rights and social justice worldwide.</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://huumanists.org">UU Humanist Association</a> is an association of UUs who define themselves as Humanists.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Young_Religious_Unitarian_Universalists" title="Young Religious Unitarian Universalists">Young Religious Unitarian Universalists</a> (YRUU) is a term used within the UUA and CUC. YRUU was an organization at the North American continental level primarily run by youth, ranging in age from 14 to 20, with mentoring adult partners. The North American continental organization of YRUU ended in 2008, but the term is still used by certain active youth groups and conferences at the congregational and regional/district levels. It was created in 1981 and 1982, at two conferences, Common Ground 1 &amp; 2. Common Ground was called to form a UUA-controlled replacement for <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Religious_Youth" title="Liberal Religious Youth">Liberal Religious Youth</a> (LRY), the UU youth organization that preceded YRUU. LRY was dissolved by the UUA, and its assets absorbed by it.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_members">Number of members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unitarian_Universalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Number of members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of December, 2015 the UUA had 1,018 UU member congregations in the United States and 1,043 UU member congregations when including two congregations in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a>, 19 in Canada, six in other countries,<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> plus 28 multi-denominational member congregations: 17 in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">MA</a>, four in <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">IL</a>, three in <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">NH</a>, two in <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">VT</a>, and one each in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">ME</a> and <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">D.C.</a> Seven of the ten US states with the most congregations are also among the most populous states; the state with the most congregations and members is Massachusetts; Vermont is No. 1 relative to its total population. A map using <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 U.S. Census</a> data showing the relative number of congregations per 1 million people is posted here.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> And as of September 2014 there are 46 UU congregations and emerging groups in Canada affiliated with the CUC.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> In 2015, there were 156,620 adult congregational members and 47,623 children enrolled in religious education programs.</p>
<p>At the time of the merger between Universalists and Unitarians, membership (both US and Canadian) was perhaps half a million.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (July 2014)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Membership rose after the merger but then fell in the 1970s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1956, Sam Wells wrote that "Unitarians and Universalists are considering merger which would have total U.S. membership of 160,000 (500,000 in world)".<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> In 1965 Conkin wrote that "In 1961, at the time of the merger, membership [in the United States] was 104,821 in 651 congregations, and the joint membership soared to its historically highest level in the mid-1960s (an estimated 250,000) before falling sharply back in the 1970s [...]".<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> According to the 2008 <i>Yearbook of American &amp; Canadian Churches</i>, the Unitarian Universalist Association of Congregations claimed 214,738 members in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Estimates from the 1990s put world membership between 120,000 and 600,000.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/American_Religious_Identification_Survey" class="mw-redirect" title="American Religious Identification Survey">American Religious Identification Survey</a> reported 629,000 members describing themselves as Unitarian Universalist in 2001, an increase from 502,000 reported in a similar survey in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The highest concentrations are in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and around <a href="/wiki/Seattle,_Washington" class="mw-redirect" title="Seattle, Washington">Seattle, Washington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. Religious Landscape Survey, conducted in 2007 by the Pew Forum on Religion and Public Life and featuring a sample size of over 35,000, puts the proportion of American adults identifying as Unitarian Universalist at 0.3%.<sup id="cite_ref-r1_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r1-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2001 Canadian census done by Statistics Canada put Canadian Unitarians at 17,480,<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> and the September 1, 2007 membership statistics from the CUC show they had at that time 5,150 official members.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> In 2015, the CUC reported 3,804 members.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Louis Menand</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span><span title="/iː/ or /ɪ/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> born January 21, 1952) is an American critic and essayist, best known for his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Metaphysical_Club:_A_Story_of_Ideas_in_America" title="The Metaphysical Club: A Story of Ideas in America">The Metaphysical Club</a></i> (2001), an intellectual and cultural history of late 19th and early 20th century America.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Menand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Menand was born in <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_New_York" title="Syracuse, New York">Syracuse</a>, New York, and raised around <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, Massachusetts. His mother, Catherine (Shults) Menand, was a historian, who wrote a biography of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Adams" title="Samuel Adams">Samuel Adams</a>. His father, Louis Menand III, taught political science at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>. His grandfather and great-grandfather owned the <a href="/wiki/Louis_Menand_House" title="Louis Menand House">Louis Menand House</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Menands,_New_York" title="Menands, New York">Menands</a>, New York, and listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-nris_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-2">[2]</a></sup> The village of Menands is named after his great-grandfather, a 19th-century horticulturalist.</p>
<p>A graduate of <a href="/wiki/Pomona_College" title="Pomona College">Pomona College</a>, Menand attended <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a> for one year (1973–1974) before he left to earn <a href="/wiki/M.A." class="mw-redirect" title="M.A.">M.A.</a> (1975) and <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> (1980) degrees in English from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>.</p>
<p>He thereafter taught at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> and held staff positions at <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Republic" title="The New Republic">The New Republic</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>. He served as Distinguished Professor of English at the Graduate Center of the <a href="/wiki/City_University_of_New_York" title="City University of New York">City University of New York</a> before accepting a post at Harvard in 2003.</p>
<p>He published his first book <i>Discovering Modernism: T. S. Eliot and His Context</i>, in 1987. His long-anticipated second book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Metaphysical_Club:_A_Story_of_Ideas_in_America" title="The Metaphysical Club: A Story of Ideas in America">The Metaphysical Club: A Story of Ideas in America</a></i> (2001), includes detailed biographical material on <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Wendell_Holmes,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr.">Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_James" title="William James">William James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce" title="Charles Sanders Peirce">Charles Sanders Peirce</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Dewey" title="John Dewey">John Dewey</a>, and documents their roles in the development of the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Pragmatism" title="Pragmatism">pragmatism</a>. It received the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_History" title="Pulitzer Prize for History">Pulitzer Prize for History</a>, the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Francis_Parkman_Prize" title="Francis Parkman Prize">Francis Parkman Prize</a>, and The Heartland Prize for Non-Fiction. In 2002 Menand published <i>American Studies</i>, a collection of essays on prominent figures in American culture.</p>
<p>He is the Robert M. and Anne T. Bass Professor of English and American Literature and Language at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. His principal field of academic interest is 19th and 20th century American cultural history. He contributes to <i>The New Yorker</i>, for which he remains a staff writer, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Review_of_Books" title="The New York Review of Books">The New York Review of Books</a></i>, among other publications.</p>

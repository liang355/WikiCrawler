<p><b>The European Library</b> is an Internet service that allows access to the resources of 49 European <a href="/wiki/National_libraries" class="mw-redirect" title="National libraries">national libraries</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and an increasing number of research libraries. Searching is free and delivers metadata records as well as digital objects, mostly free of charge. The objects come from institutions located in countries which are members of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a> and range from catalogue records to full-text books, magazines, journals and audio recordings. Over 200 million records are searchable, including 24 million pages of full-text content and more than 7 million digital objects. Thirty five different languages are represented among the searchable objects.</p>
<p>The European Library ceases operation on 2016-12-31.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_concept">History and concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European Library of today has evolved from a number of earlier projects.</p>
<p>Its starting point was in 1997 when the GABRIEL (Gateway and Bridge to Europe's National Libraries) project set out to establish a joint web portal of European national libraries. At a basic level, the portal provided information about each library's collections and access to their online public access catalogues (OPACs).</p>

<tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">“</td>
<td style="padding:0 10px;">The European Library exists to open up the universe of knowledge, information and cultures of all Europe's national libraries.</td>
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:'Times New Roman',serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">”</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px; font-size:smaller; line-height:1em; text-align:right;"><cite>—&#160; European Library Mission<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></cite></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>GABRIEL was followed by the TEL (The European Library) project, which ran from 2001–2004 and created a framework for access to key national and deposit collections within Europe. The project was part-funded under the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Framework_Programme" class="mw-redirect" title="Fifth Framework Programme">Fifth Framework Programme</a> of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>. The national libraries involved in the TEL project were those of Finland, Germany, Italy (Florence), Italy (Rome), Netherlands, Portugal, Slovenia, Switzerland and United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>This led to the launch of TheEuropeanLibrary.org portal on 17 March 2005.</p>
<p>Between 2005–2007, the TEL-ME-MOR project helped to incorporate 10 more national libraries from new European Union member states as full partners of The European Library. By the beginning of 2008, a further nine national libraries within the European Union and the European Free Trade Association had joined the service.</p>
<p>The European Library took a further step towards its enlargement with the EDLproject, during which national libraries continued to join The European Library. The project also focused on multilingualism, undertook the first steps towards a European Metadata Registry and created a roadmap for potential digitization efforts in the national libraries.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_European_Library_and_Europeana">The European Library and Europeana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The European Library and Europeana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European Library provided much of the organization and support required to launch <a href="/wiki/Europeana" title="Europeana">Europeana</a> – a European Commission initiative that makes millions of digital objects from libraries, museums and archives accessible to the public via the Europeana website.</p>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> called for Europeana to be established, the Conference of European National Librarians (CENL), under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_the_Netherlands" title="National Library of the Netherlands">National Library of the Netherlands</a> and CENL's service The European Library, were asked to submit for a project under the <a href="/w/index.php?title=EContentplus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="EContentplus (page does not exist)">eContentplus</a> programme. Work began on a prototype in 2007 and Europeana was launched in November 2008.</p>
<p>Europeana now operates independently but The European Library continues to work closely with Europeana. The European Library is the aggregator of digital content from national libraries for Europeana and delivers digital content from national libraries on a monthly basis to Europeana.</p>
<p>As of July 2012, The European Library was the second biggest content provider to Europeana,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> with 3.45 million items added to the Europeana database.</p>
<p>Some human and technical resources are also shared between Europeana and The European Library.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_exhibitions">Virtual exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Virtual exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to its search engine, The European Library pulls together themes from the collections of Europe's national libraries<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and displays them in virtual exhibitions. These exhibitions unite geographically disparate objects in a single online space, offering Pan-European sources on the topic.</p>
<ul>
<li>Manuscripts and Princes in Medieval and Renaissance Europe:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> 34 manuscripts from the royal collections of the Carolingian Emperors, French King Charles V and his family, and the Aragonese kings of Naples.</li>
<li>Travelling Through History:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Centuries worth of travel memorabilia from 13 European libraries, including photographs, sounds, maps and books.</li>
<li>Reading Europe:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Almost 1,000 books selected by <a href="/wiki/National_libraries" class="mw-redirect" title="National libraries">national libraries</a>, with curatorial information and full-text versions in most cases.</li>
<li>A Roma Journey:<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Texts, photographs, paintings and recordings of traditional songs showing the heritage of the <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani people</a> in Europe.</li>
<li>Napoleonic Wars:<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A selection of portraits, military maps, city plans, letters, books and other material from the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>.</li>
<li>Treasures of Europe's National Libraries:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A collection of objects selected by Europe's national libraries as some of the most outstanding items in their collections.</li>
<li>National Library Buildings:<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Images of the National Libraries that are partners of The European Library.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financing_and_ownership">Financing and ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Financing and ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The European Library is financed by its owners, the Conference of European National Librarians (CENL). The portal is maintained by The European Library Office located in the premises of the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_the_Netherlands" title="National Library of the Netherlands">Dutch Royal Library</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a>. Its programme director is Jill Cousins.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

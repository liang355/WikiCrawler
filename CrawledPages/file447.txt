<p>The <b>metropolitan regions in Germany</b> are eleven<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> densely populated areas in the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Republic of Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a>. They comprise the major German cities and their surrounding <a href="/wiki/Catchment_area_(human_geography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Catchment area (human geography)">catchment areas</a> and form the political, commercial and cultural centres of the country. The eleven metropolitan regions in Germany were organised into political units for planning purposes.</p>
<p>Using a narrower definition of <a href="/wiki/Metropolis" title="Metropolis">metropolises</a>, only four cities surpass the threshold of at least one million inhabitants within its administrative borders, namely: <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>.</p>
<p>For urban centres outside metropolitan areas, that generate a similar attraction at smaller scale for their region, the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Regiopolis" title="Regiopolis">Regiopolis</a> and respectively <i>regiopolitan area</i> or <i>regio</i> was introduced by German professors in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_five">Big five</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_regions_in_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Big five">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The five most important regions, collectively often called the "<i>Big Five</i>",<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> are frequently compared with other European metropolitan areas for investments and market development.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They are (from north to south): <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_Metropolitan_Region" title="Hamburg Metropolitan Region">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Metropolitan_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Berlin Metropolitan Region">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>/<a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> (or collectively <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr_Metropolitan_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine-Ruhr Metropolitan Region">Rhine-Ruhr</a>), <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt/Rhine-Main_Metropolitan_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt/Rhine-Main Metropolitan Region">Frankfurt/Rhine-Main</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. Globalization and World Cities Study Group (<a href="/wiki/GaWC" class="mw-redirect" title="GaWC">GaWC</a>) considers Frankfurt "α" (Alpha) <a href="/wiki/Global_city" title="Global city">Global Cities</a>, while the others are considered "β" (Beta) global cities.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, for its political and cultural importance</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, for its importance in global logistics</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, for its business, science and technology community</li>

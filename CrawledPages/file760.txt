<p><b>Magenta</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ʒ/: 's' in 'pleasure'">ʒ</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a> that is variously defined as purplish-<a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> reddish-<a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a>, purplish-<a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mauve" title="Mauve">mauvish</a>-<a href="/wiki/Crimson" title="Crimson">crimson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> On computer screens, it is made by mixing equal amounts of blue and red.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Color_wheel" title="Color wheel">color wheels</a> of the <a href="/wiki/RGB_color_model" title="RGB color model">RGB (additive)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtractive_color" title="Subtractive color">CMY (subtractive)</a> color models, it is located midway between <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Complementary_color" class="mw-redirect" title="Complementary color">complementary color</a> of <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>. It is one of the four colors of ink used in color printing and by an <a href="/wiki/Inkjet_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Inkjet printer">inkjet printer</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">cyan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">yellow</a>, and black, to make all the other colors. The tone of magenta used in <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> is called "printer's magenta" (Magenta (CMYK)).</p>
<p>Magenta was first made and patented in 1859 by the French chemist François-Emmanuel Verguin, who first called it <i>fuschine</i>. It was renamed to celebrate the French victory at the the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Magenta" title="Battle of Magenta">Battle of Magenta</a> fought between the French and Austrians on June 4, 1859, near the Italian town of <a href="/wiki/Magenta,_Lombardy" title="Magenta, Lombardy">Magenta</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball214_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball214-4">[4]</a></sup> A virtually identical color, called roseine, was created in 1860 by two British chemists, Chambers Nicolson and George Maule.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Web_colors" title="Web colors">web color</a> magenta is also called <a href="/wiki/Fuchsia_(color)" title="Fuchsia (color)">fuchsia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_optics_and_color_science">In optics and color science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magenta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In optics and color science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magenta is an <a href="/wiki/Spectral_color#Non-spectral_colors" title="Spectral color">extra-spectral color</a>, meaning that it is not found in the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> of light. Rather, it is physiologically and psychologically perceived as the mixture of red and violet/blue light, with the absence of green.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the RGB color system, used to create all the colors on a television or computer display, magenta is a secondary color, made by combining equal amounts of red and blue light at a high intensity. In this system, magenta is the <a href="/wiki/Complementary_color" class="mw-redirect" title="Complementary color">complementary color</a> of green, and combining green and magenta light on a black screen will create white.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/CMYK_color_model" title="CMYK color model">CMYK color model</a>, used in color printing, it is one of the three primary colors, along with cyan and yellow, used to print all the rest of the colors. If magenta, cyan, and yellow are printed on top of each other on a page, they make black. In this model, magenta is the complementary color of green, and these two colors have the highest contrast and the greatest harmony. If combined, green and magenta ink will look dark gray or black. The magenta used in color printing, sometimes called process magenta, is a darker shade than the color used on computer screens.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Line_of_purples" title="Line of purples">purple</a> hue in terms of <a href="/wiki/Color_theory" title="Color theory">color theory</a>, magenta is evoked by light having less power in green <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> than in blue/violet and red wavelengths (<a href="/wiki/Complementary_color" class="mw-redirect" title="Complementary color">complements</a> of magenta have wavelength 500–530 <a href="/wiki/Nanometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanometer">nm</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Munsell_color_system" title="Munsell color system">Munsell color system</a>, magenta is called <i>red–purple</i>.</p>
<p>If the spectrum is wrapped to form a color wheel, magenta (additive secondary) appears midway between <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> and violet. Violet and red, the two components of magenta, are at opposite ends of the visible spectrum and have very different wavelengths. The additive secondary color magenta, as noted above, is made by combining violet and red light at equal intensity; it is not on the actual spectrum.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuchsia_and_magenta">Fuchsia and magenta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magenta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fuchsia and magenta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In optics, <a href="/wiki/Fuchsia_(color)" title="Fuchsia (color)">fuchsia</a> and magenta are essentially the same color. The <a href="/wiki/Web_colors" title="Web colors">web colors</a> fuchsia and magenta are completely identical, and are made by mixing exactly the same proportions of blue and red light. In design and printing, there is a little more variation. The French version of fuchsia in the <a href="/wiki/RGB_color_model" title="RGB color model">RGB color model</a> and in printing contains a higher proportion of red than the American version of fuchsia. <a href="/wiki/Fuchsia" title="Fuchsia">Fuchsia</a> flowers themselves, which inspired both colors, have a variety of colors, from fuchsia to purple to red and pink.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magenta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Fuchsine_and_magenta_dye_(1859)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuchsine_and_magenta_dye_.281859.29">Fuchsine and magenta dye (1859)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magenta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fuchsine and magenta dye (1859)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The color magenta was the result of the industrial chemistry revolution of the mid-nineteenth century, which began with the invention by <a href="/wiki/William_Perkin" class="mw-redirect" title="William Perkin">William Perkin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mauveine" title="Mauveine">mauveine</a> in 1856, which was the first synthetic <a href="/wiki/Aniline_dye" class="mw-redirect" title="Aniline dye">aniline dye</a>. The enormous commercial success of the dye and the new color it produced, <a href="/wiki/Mauve" title="Mauve">mauve</a>, inspired other chemists in Europe to develop new colors made from aniline dyes.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball214_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball214-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In France, François-Emmanuel Verguin, the director of the chemical factory of Louis Rafard near <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>, tried many different formulae before finally in late 1858 or early 1859, mixing aniline with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>, producing a reddish-purple dye which he called "<a href="/wiki/Fuchsine" title="Fuchsine">fuchsine</a>", after the color of the flower of the fuchsia plant. He quit the Rafard factory and took his color to a firm of paint manufacturers, Francisque and Joseph Renard, who began to manufacture the dye in 1859.</p>
<p>In the same year, two British chemists, Chambers Nicolson and George Maule, working at the laboratory of the paint manufacturer George Simpson, located in Walworth, south of London, made another aniline dye with a similar red-purple color, which they began to manufacture in 1860 under the name "roseine". In 1860 they changed the name of the color to "magenta", in honor of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Magenta" title="Battle of Magenta">Battle of Magenta</a> fought between the French and Austrians at <a href="/wiki/Magenta,_Lombardy" title="Magenta, Lombardy">Magenta, Lombardy</a> the year before, and the new color became a commercial success.<sup id="cite_ref-Ball214_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ball214-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>Starting in 1935 the family of <a href="/wiki/Quinacridone" title="Quinacridone">quinacridone</a> dyes was developed. These have colors ranging from red to violet, so nowadays a quinacridone dye is often used for magenta. Various tones of magenta—light, bright, brilliant, vivid, rich, or deep—may be formulated by adding varying amounts of white to quinacridone artist's paints.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>Another dye used for magenta is <a href="/wiki/Lithol_Rubine_BK" title="Lithol Rubine BK">Lithol Rubine BK</a>. One of its uses is as a food coloring.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Color_printing" title="Color printing">color printing</a>, the color called <b>process magenta</b>, <b>pigment magenta</b>, or <b>printer's magenta</b> is one of the three primary pigment colors which, along with <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">yellow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">cyan</a>, constitute the three <a href="/wiki/Primary_colors#Subtractive_primaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary colors">subtractive primary colors</a> of pigment. (The secondary colors of pigment are blue, green, and red.) As such, the hue magenta is the <a href="/wiki/Complementary_color" class="mw-redirect" title="Complementary color">complement</a> of <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>: magenta <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a> absorb green light; thus magenta and green are opposite colors.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/CMYK" class="mw-redirect" title="CMYK">CMYK</a> printing process was invented in the 1890s, when newspapers began to publish color <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">comic strips</a>.</p>
<p>Process magenta is not an <a href="/wiki/RGB" class="mw-redirect" title="RGB">RGB</a> color, and there is no fixed conversion from CMYK primaries to RGB. Different formulations are used for printer's ink, so there may be variations in the printed color that is pure magenta ink. A typical formulation of <i>process magenta</i> is shown in the color box at right.</p>

On the <a href="/wiki/File:RBG_color_wheel.svg" title="File:RBG color wheel.svg">RGB color wheel</a>, magenta is the color between <a href="/wiki/Rose_(color)" title="Rose (color)">rose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Violet_(color)" title="Violet (color)">violet</a>, and halfway between <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>.</p>

<p>Magenta (fuchsia)<br />
#ff00ff</p>

<p>This color is called <i>magenta</i> in <a href="/wiki/X11_color_names" title="X11 color names">X11</a> and <i>fuchsia</i> in <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a>. In the RGB color model, it is created by combining equal intensities of red and blue light. The two web colors magenta and <b>fuchsia</b> are exactly the same color. Sometimes the web color magenta is called <i>electric magenta</i> or <i>electronic magenta</i>.</p>
<p>While the magenta used in printing and the web color have the same name, they have important differences. Process magenta (the color used for magenta printing ink—also called printer's or pigment magenta) is much less vivid than the color magenta achievable on a computer screen. CMYK printing technology cannot accurately reproduce on paper the color on the computer screen. When the web color magenta is reproduced on paper, it is called fuchsia and it is physically impossible for it to appear on paper as vivid as on a computer screen.</p>
<p>Colored pencils and <a href="/wiki/Crayon" title="Crayon">crayons</a> called "magenta" are usually colored the color of <i>process magenta</i> (<i>printer's magenta</i>) shown above.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_science_and_culture">In science and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magenta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In science and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art">In art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magenta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Shades of magenta began to appear in art soon after it was introduced. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gauguin" title="Paul Gauguin">Paul Gauguin</a> (1848-1903) used a shade of magenta in 1890 in his portrait of Marie Lagadu, and in some of his South Seas paintings.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Henri_Matisse" title="Henri Matisse">Henri Matisse</a> and the members of the <a href="/wiki/Fauvist" class="mw-redirect" title="Fauvist">Fauvist</a> movement used magenta and other non-traditional colors to surprise viewers, and to move their emotions through the use of bold colors.</li>
<li>Since the mid-1960s, water based fluorescent magenta paint has been available to paint <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_art" title="Psychedelic art">psychedelic</a> <a href="/wiki/Black_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Black light">black light</a> <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">paintings</a>. (Fluorescent <a href="/wiki/Cerise_(color)" title="Cerise (color)">cerise</a>, fluorescent <a href="/wiki/Chartreuse_yellow" class="mw-redirect" title="Chartreuse yellow">chartreuse yellow</a>, fluorescent blue, and fluorescent green.)</li>
</ul>
<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">
<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px">
<div style="width: 155px">
<div class="thumb" style="width: 150px;">
<div style="margin:15px auto;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bouguereau-Psyche.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Bouguereau-Psyche.jpg/72px-Bouguereau-Psyche.jpg" width="72" height="120" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Bouguereau-Psyche.jpg/107px-Bouguereau-Psyche.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Bouguereau-Psyche.jpg/143px-Bouguereau-Psyche.jpg 2x" data-file-width="598" data-file-height="1000" /></a></div>
</div>
<div class="gallerytext">
<p>Magenta, along with mauve, made with the newly discovered <a href="/wiki/Aniline_dyes" class="mw-redirect" title="Aniline dyes">aniline dyes</a>, became a popular fashion color in the second half of the nineteenth century. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_botany">In botany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magenta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In botany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Magenta is a common color for flowers, particularly in the tropics and sub-tropics. Because magenta is the complementary color of green, magenta flowers have the highest contrast with the green foliage, and therefore are more visible to the animals needed for their pollination.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_politics">In politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magenta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The color magenta is used to symbolize <a href="/wiki/Anti-racism" title="Anti-racism">anti-racism</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>-based anti-racism Magenta Foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Danish politics the magenta is the color of <a href="/wiki/Det_Radikale_Venstre" class="mw-redirect" title="Det Radikale Venstre">Det Radikale Venstre</a>, the Danish social-liberal party.</li>
</ul>

			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Glass Flowers</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg/350px-GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg" width="350" height="454" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg/525px-GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg/700px-GlassFlowers1HMNH.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1610" data-file-height="2090" /></a>

Blaschka glass model of part of a <a href="/wiki/Cashew" title="Cashew">cashew</a> tree</div>

<p><b>The Ware Collection of Blaschka Glass Models of Plants</b> (or simply the <i><b>Glass Flowers</b></i>) is a collection of highly realistic <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botanical</a> models at the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Harvard Museum of Natural History">Harvard Museum of Natural History</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>.</p>
<p>Created by <a href="/wiki/Leopold_and_Rudolf_Blaschka" title="Leopold and Rudolf Blaschka">Leopold and Rudolf Blaschka</a> from 1887 through 1936 at their studio in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hosterwitz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hosterwitz (page does not exist)">Hosterwitz</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Dresden,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Dresden, Germany">Dresden, Germany</a>, the collection was commissioned by <a href="/wiki/George_Lincoln_Goodale" title="George Lincoln Goodale">George Lincoln Goodale</a>, the first director of Harvard's <a href="/wiki/Botanical_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanical Museum">Botanical Museum</a>, and was financed by <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lee_Ware" title="Mary Lee Ware">Mary Lee Ware</a> and her mother <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Cabot_(Lee)_Ware&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Elizabeth Cabot (Lee) Ware (page does not exist)">Elizabeth C. Ware</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It includes 847 life-size models (representing 780 species and varieties of plants in 164 families) and some 3,000 detail models such as of plant parts and anatomical sections. The collection comprises approximately 4,400 individual glass models representing over 830 plant species.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_Flowers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rudolf,_Leopold_and_Caroline_Blaschka_in_garden_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Rudolf%2C_Leopold_and_Caroline_Blaschka_in_garden_cropped.jpg/150px-Rudolf%2C_Leopold_and_Caroline_Blaschka_in_garden_cropped.jpg" width="150" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Rudolf%2C_Leopold_and_Caroline_Blaschka_in_garden_cropped.jpg/225px-Rudolf%2C_Leopold_and_Caroline_Blaschka_in_garden_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Rudolf%2C_Leopold_and_Caroline_Blaschka_in_garden_cropped.jpg/300px-Rudolf%2C_Leopold_and_Caroline_Blaschka_in_garden_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="994" data-file-height="1377" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rudolf,_Leopold_and_Caroline_Blaschka_in_garden_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rudolf (standing) and Leopold Blaschka</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Blaschkas had a thriving business making glass models of <a href="/wiki/Marine_invertebrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine invertebrate">marine invertebrates</a> (see <span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable"><a href="/wiki/Glass_sea_creatures" title="Glass sea creatures">Glass sea creatures</a></span>), and after seeing their work Goodale went to Dresden in 1886 to ask them to make series of botanical models for Harvard. Leopold was hesitant but agreed to make some sample models which, though damaged in customs,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> convinced Goodale of their value in botanical teaching, which at the time used pressed specimens&#160;– two-dimensional and tending to fade.<sup id="cite_ref-hmnh.harvard.edu_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hmnh.harvard.edu-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nasonline.org_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nasonline.org-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nasonline.org_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nasonline.org-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>To fund the project Goodale approached his former student Mary Lee Ware and her mother, Elizabeth <a href="/wiki/Cabot_family" title="Cabot family">C.</a> Ware, who were already liberal benefactors of Harvard's botanical department.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The original arrangement (in 1887) provided that the Blaschkas would work half time on the project, but in 1890 a new arrangement called for them to work full-time.<sup id="cite_ref-Schultes.2C_Richard_Evans_1982_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schultes.2C_Richard_Evans_1982-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The work continued until 1936, at which point Leopold and Elizabeth had both died.<sup id="cite_ref-nasonline.org_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nasonline.org-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The collection is formally dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Eliot_Ware" title="Charles Eliot Ware">Dr. Charles Eliot Ware</a>, the deceased father and husband of Mary and Elizabeth Ware, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-hmnh.harvard.edu_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hmnh.harvard.edu-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_models">The models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_Flowers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ware_Dedication_plaque.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Ware_Dedication_plaque.jpg/220px-Ware_Dedication_plaque.jpg" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Ware_Dedication_plaque.jpg/330px-Ware_Dedication_plaque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Ware_Dedication_plaque.jpg/440px-Ware_Dedication_plaque.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1611" data-file-height="1542" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ware_Dedication_plaque.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"In memory of physician Charles Eliot Ware (1814–1887), a graduate of this university. These models were presented by his wife and daughter who survived him. He sincerely cherished and deeply loved native plants as friends."</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The models are glass with wire supports (internal or external), glue, a variety of organic media,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (March 2017)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and paint or enamel coloring.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <i>Boston Globe</i> has called them "anatomically perfect and, given all the glass-workers who've tried and failed, unreproducible."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is often said that the Blaschkas employed secret techniques now lost; in fact their techniques were common at the time, but their skill, enthusiasm, and meticulous study and observation of their subjects in life were extraordinary, which Leopold ascribed to familial tradition, in a letter to Mary Lee Ware: "Many people think that we have some secret apparatus by which we can squeeze glass suddenly into these forms<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... The only way to become a glass modeler of skill, I have often said to people, is to get a good great-grandfather who loved glass."</p>
<p>The Blaschkas' primary technique was <a href="/wiki/Lampworking" title="Lampworking">lampworking</a>, in which glass is melted over a flame fed by air from a foot-powered bellows, then shaped using tools to pinch, pull or cut; forms were blown as well.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Their old-fashioned Bohemian lamp-working table is part of the museum exhibit. Over the years Rudolf brought more and more of the entire production process under his personal control, eventually even manufacturing his own glass and colorants.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Botanist Donald Schnell has called the models "enchanting", and relates his surprise at finding that the models faithfully depict an unpublished detail of a bee's behavior while pollinating a particular plant&#160;– a detail which he had privately hypothesized.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Whitehouse and Small wrote that "the superiority in design and construction of the Blaschka models surpasses all modern model making to date and the skill and art of the Blaschkas rests in peace for eternity."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The Glass Flowers draw some 210,000 visitors annually. During <a href="/wiki/Harvard%27s_Tercentenary_celebration" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard's Tercentenary celebration">Harvard's Tercentenary celebration</a> in 1936, a <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> reporter wrote: "Tercentenary or no, the chief focus of interest remains the famous glass flowers, the first of which was put on exhibition in 1893, and which with additions at intervals since, have never failed to draw exclamations of wonder or disbelief from visitors."<sup id="cite_ref-NY_Times_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_response">Public response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_Flowers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At least two poems feature the flowers:</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Doty" title="Mark Doty">Mark Doty</a> (winner of the <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award" title="National Book Award">National Book Award</a> for Poetry in 2008), "The Ware Collection of Glass Flowers and Fruit, Harvard Museum," in <i>My Alexandria</i>, 1993,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<div class="poem">
<p>"He’s built a perfection out of hunger,<br />
fused layer upon layer, swirled until<br />
what can’t be tasted, won’t yield,<br />
almost satisfies, an art<br />
mouthed to the shape of how soft things are,<br />
how good, before they disappear."</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marianne_Moore" title="Marianne Moore">Marianne Moore</a> wrote in a poem, "Silence",</p>
<blockquote>
<div class="poem">
<p>My father used to say,<br />
"Superior people never make long visits,<br />
have to be shown Longfellow's grave,<br />
or the glass flowers at Harvard."</p>
</div>
</blockquote>

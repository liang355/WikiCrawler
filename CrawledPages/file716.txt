<p><i><b>Academic Ranking of World Universities</b></i> (<b>ARWU</b>), also known as <b>Shanghai Ranking</b>, is an annual publication of <a href="/wiki/University_ranking" class="mw-redirect" title="University ranking">university rankings</a> by Shanghai Ranking Consultancy.<sup id="cite_ref-about_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup> The league table was originally compiled and issued by <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Jiao_Tong_University" title="Shanghai Jiao Tong University">Shanghai Jiao Tong University</a> in 2003, the first global ranking with multifarious indicators,<sup id="cite_ref-theguardian_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian-2">[2]</a></sup> after which a board of international advisories was established to provide suggestions.<sup id="cite_ref-ABSCBN_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABSCBN-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ARWUconsultants_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARWUconsultants-4">[4]</a></sup> The publication currently includes global league tables for institutions and a whole and for a selection of individual subjects, alongside independent regional <i>Greater China Ranking</i> and <i>Macedonian HEIs Ranking</i>. ARWU is regarded as one of the three most influential and widely observed university measures, alongside <i><a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education_World_University_Rankings" title="Times Higher Education World University Rankings">Times Higher Education World University Rankings</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-thetelegraph_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thetelegraph-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-New_world_university_ranking_puts_Harvard_back_on_top_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_world_university_ranking_puts_Harvard_back_on_top-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-edmont_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmont-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It is praised for its <a href="/wiki/Objective_approach" title="Objective approach">objective methodology</a> but draws some condemnation for narrowly focusing on raw research power, undermining <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> and quality of instruction.<sup id="cite_ref-thetelegraph_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thetelegraph-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-edmont_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmont-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EU_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HEPI_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HEPI-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>ARWU is praised by several media and institutions for its methodology and influence. A survey on higher education published by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> in 2005 commented ARWU as "the most widely used annual ranking of the world's research universities."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> In 2010, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicle_of_Higher_Education" title="The Chronicle of Higher Education">The Chronicle of Higher Education</a></i> called ARWU "the best-known and most influential global ranking of universities".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup> EU Research Headlines reported the ARWU's work on 31 December 2003: "The universities were carefully evaluated using several indicators of research performance."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup> Chancellor of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>, Chris Patten and former Vice-Chancellor of <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">Australian National University</a>, Ian Chubb, said: "the methodology looks fairly solid ... it looks like a pretty good stab at a fair comparison." and "The SJTU rankings were reported quickly and widely around the world… (and they) offer an important comparative view of research performance and reputation." respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> Philip G. Altbach named ARWU's 'consistency, clarity of purpose, and transparency' as significant strengths.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> Whilst ARWU has originated in China, the ranking have been praised for being unbiased towards Asian institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Like all other rankings, ARWU has criticism. It is condemned for "relying too much on award factors" thus undermining the importance of quality of instruction and <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-thetelegraph_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thetelegraph-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-edmont_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmont-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ioanides_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ioanides-21">[19]</a></sup> A 2007 paper published in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Scientometrics_(journal)" title="Scientometrics (journal)">Scientometrics</a></i> found that the results from the Shanghai rankings could not be reproduced from raw data using the method described by Liu and Cheng.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> A 2013 paper in the same journal finally showed how the Shanghai ranking results could be reproduced.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup> In a report from April 2009, J-C. Billaut, D. Bouyssou and Ph. Vincke analyse how the ARWU works, using their insights as specialists of Multiple Criteria Decision Making (MCDM). Their main conclusions are that the criteria used are not relevant; that the aggregation methodology has a number of major problems; and that insufficient attention has been paid to fundamental choices of criteria.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup> The ARWU researchers themselves, N.C Liu and Y Cheng, think that the quality of universities cannot be precisely measured by mere numbers and any ranking must be controversial. They suggest that university and college rankings should be used with caution and their methodologies must be understood clearly before reporting or using the results. ARWU has been criticised by the European Commission as well as some EU member states for "favour[ing] Anglo-Saxon higher education institutions". For instance, ARWU is repeatedly criticised in France, where it triggers an annual controversy, focusing on its ill-adapted character to the French academic system<sup id="cite_ref-EU2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU2-25">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> and the unreasonable weight given to research often performed decades ago.<sup id="cite_ref-lemonde_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lemonde-27">[25]</a></sup> It is also criticised in France for its use as a motivation for fusing universities into larger ones.<sup id="cite_ref-diplo_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diplo-28">[26]</a></sup> Indeed, a further criticism has been that the metrics used are not independent of university size, e.g. number of publications or award winners will mechanically add as universities are grouped, independently of research (or teaching) quality; thus a merger between two equally-ranked institutions will almost double the merged institutions score and give it a higher ranking, without any change in quality.<sup id="cite_ref-HEPI_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HEPI-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Bayreuth</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[baɪˈʁɔʏt]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayreuth.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/41/Bayreuth.ogg" class="internal" title="Bayreuth.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <small>Upper Franconian:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ba(ː)ˈɾaɪ̯t]</a></span>) is a sizeable town in northern <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, Germany, on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Main" title="Red Main">Red Main</a> river in a valley between the <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Jura" title="Franconian Jura">Franconian Jura</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fichtelgebirge_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Fichtelgebirge Mountains">Fichtelgebirge Mountains</a>. The town's roots date back to 1194. In the early 21st century, it is the capital of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Franconia" title="Upper Franconia">Upper Franconia</a> and has a population of 72,148 (2015). It is world-famous for its annual <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festival" title="Bayreuth Festival">Bayreuth Festival</a>, at which performances of operas by the 19th-century German composer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> are presented.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_and_Early_Modern_Period">Middle Ages and Early Modern Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Middle Ages and Early Modern Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayreuth_1900.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Bayreuth_1900.jpg/220px-Bayreuth_1900.jpg" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Bayreuth_1900.jpg/330px-Bayreuth_1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Bayreuth_1900.jpg/440px-Bayreuth_1900.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3485" data-file-height="2465" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayreuth_1900.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bayreuth around 1900</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The town is believed to have been founded by the counts of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Andechs" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Andechs">Andechs</a> probably around the mid-12th century,<sup id="cite_ref-Tours_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tours-2">[2]</a></sup> but was first mentioned in 1194 as <i>Baierrute</i> in a document by Bishop <a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_VI_of_Andechs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Otto VI of Andechs (page does not exist)">Otto II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Bamberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric of Bamberg">Bamberg</a>. The syllable <i>-rute</i> may mean <i>Rodung</i> or "clearing", whilst <i>Baier-</i> indicates immigrants from the Bavarian region.</p>
<p>Already documented earlier, were villages later merged into Bayreuth: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seulbitz_(Bayreuth)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Seulbitz (Bayreuth) (page does not exist)">Seulbitz</a> (in 1035 as the royal <a href="/wiki/Salian_dynasty" title="Salian dynasty">Salian</a> estate of Silewize in a document by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Conrad_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Conrad II, Holy Roman Emperor">Conrad II</a>) and St. Johannis (possibly 1149 as <i>Altentrebgast</i>). Even the district of <a href="/wiki/Altstadt" title="Altstadt">Altstadt</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/Altenstadt,_Upper_Bavaria" title="Altenstadt, Upper Bavaria">Altenstadt</a>) west of the town centre must be older than the town of Bayreuth itself. Even older traces of human presence were found in the hamlets of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meyernberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Meyernberg (page does not exist)">Meyernberg</a>: pieces of pottery and wooden crockery were dated to the 9th century based on their decoration.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Bayreuth was previously (1199) referred to as a <i>villa</i> (village), the term <i>civitas</i> ("town") appeared for the first time in a document published in 1231. One can therefore assume that Bayreuth was awarded its <a href="/wiki/Town_charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Town charter">town charter</a> between 1200 and 1230. The town was ruled until 1248 by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Andechs" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Andechs">counts of Andechs-Merania</a>. After they died out in 1260 the burgraves of Nuremberg from the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">House of Hohenzollern</a> took over the inheritance. Initially, however, their residence and the centre of the territory was the castle of <a href="/wiki/Plassenburg" title="Plassenburg">Plassenburg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kulmbach" title="Kulmbach">Kulmbach</a>. The town of Bayreuth developed slowly and was affected time and again by disasters.</p>
<p>As early as 1361 Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV</a> had conferred on Burgrave <a href="/wiki/Frederick_V_of_Nuremberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick V of Nuremberg">Frederick V</a> the right to mint coins for the towns of Bayreuth and Kulmbach.</p>
<p>Bayreuth was first published on a map in 1421.</p>
<p>In February 1430, the <a href="/wiki/Hussites" title="Hussites">Hussites</a> devastated Bayreuth and the town hall and churches were razed. <a href="/wiki/Matth%C3%A4us_Merian" title="Matthäus Merian">Matthäus Merian</a> described this event in 1642 as follows: <i>"In 1430 the Hussites from Bohemia attacked / Culmbach and Barreut / and committed great acts of cruelty / like wild animals / against the common people / and certain individuals. / The priests / monks and nuns they either burnt at the stake / or took them onto the ice of lakes and rivers / (in Franconia and Bavaria) and doused them with cold water / and killed them in a deplorable way / as Boreck reported in the Bohemian Chronicle, page 450"</i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1528, less than ten years after the start of <a href="/wiki/The_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="The Reformation">the Reformation</a>, the lords of the Frankish margrave territories switched to the <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a> faith.</p>
<p>In 1605 a great fire, caused by negligence, destroyed 137 of the town's 251 houses. In 1620 <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> broke out and, in 1621, there was another big fire in the town. The town also suffered during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirty Years War">Thirty Years War</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayreuth_Schloss.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Bayreuth_Schloss.jpg/220px-Bayreuth_Schloss.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Bayreuth_Schloss.jpg/330px-Bayreuth_Schloss.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Bayreuth_Schloss.jpg/440px-Bayreuth_Schloss.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2160" data-file-height="1440" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayreuth_Schloss.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Old Castle</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A turning point in the town's history came in 1603 when Margrave <a href="/wiki/Christian_of_Brandenburg-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian of Brandenburg-Bayreuth">Christian</a>, the son of the elector, <a href="/wiki/John_George_of_Brandenburg" class="mw-redirect" title="John George of Brandenburg">John George of Brandenburg</a>, moved the aristocratic residence from the castle of <a href="/wiki/Plassenburg" title="Plassenburg">Plassenburg</a> above <a href="/wiki/Kulmbach" title="Kulmbach">Kulmbach</a> to Bayreuth. The first Hohenzollern palace was built in 1440-1457 under Margrave <a href="/wiki/John_of_Brandenburg-Kulmbach" class="mw-redirect" title="John of Brandenburg-Kulmbach">John the Alchemist</a>. It was the forerunner of today's Old Palace (<i>Altes Schloss</i>) and was expanded and renovated many times. The development of the new capital stagnated due to the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, but afterwards many famous <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> buildings were added to the town. After Christian's death in 1655 his grandson, <a href="/wiki/Christian_Ernest_of_Brandenburg-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Ernest of Brandenburg-Bayreuth">Christian Ernest</a>, followed him, ruling from 1661 until 1712. He was an educated and well-travelled man, whose tutor had been the statesman <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Friedrich_von_Blumenthal" title="Joachim Friedrich von Blumenthal">Joachim Friedrich von Blumenthal</a>. He founded the Christian-Ernestinum Grammar School and, in 1683, participated in the liberation of <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> which had been besieged by the Turks. To commemorate this feat, he had the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Margrave_Fountain_(Bayreuth)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Margrave Fountain (Bayreuth) (page does not exist)">Margrave Fountain</a> built as a monument on which he is depicted as the victor of the Turks; it now stands outside the New Palace (<i>Neues Schloss</i>). During this time, the outer ring of the town wall and the castle chapel (<i>Schlosskirche</i>) were built.</p>

<p>His successor, the Crown Prince and later Margrave, George William, began in 1701 to establish the then independent town of St Georgen am See (today, the district of St Georgen) with its castle, the so-called <i>Ordensschloss</i>, a town hall, a prison and a small barracks. In 1705 he founded the Order of Sincerity (<a href="/wiki/Ordre_de_la_Sinc%C3%A9rit%C3%A9" title="Ordre de la Sincérité">Ordre de la Sincérité</a>), which was renamed in 1734 to the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Red_Eagle" title="Order of the Red Eagle">Order of the Red Eagle</a> and had the monastery church built, which was completed in 1711. In 1716 a princely <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> factory was established in St. Georgen.</p>
<p>The first 'castle' in the park of the <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_Museum_(Bayreuth)" title="Hermitage Museum (Bayreuth)">Hermitage</a> was built at this time by Margrave George William (1715–1719).</p>
<p>In 1721 the town council acquired the palace of Baroness Sponheim (today's Old Town Hall or <i>Altes Rathaus</i>) as a replacement for the town hall built in 1440 in the middle of the market place and destroyed by fire.</p>
<p>In 1735 a nursing home, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Gravenreuth" title="Gravenreuth">Gravenreuth</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Christoph_von_Gravenreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Georg Christoph von Gravenreuth (page does not exist)">Stift</a>, was founded by a private foundation in St. Georgen. The cost of the building exceeded the funds of the foundation, but Margrave Frederick came to their aid.</p>
<p>Bayreuth experienced its Golden Age during the reign (1735–1763) of Margrave <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III_of_Brandenburg-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick III of Brandenburg-Bayreuth">Frederick</a> and Margravine <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmina_of_Prussia_(1709-1758)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelmina of Prussia (1709-1758)">Wilhelmina</a> of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Margravate_of_Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Margravate of Bayreuth (page does not exist)">Bayreuth</a>, the favourite sister of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick II of Prussia">Frederick the Great</a>. During this time, under the direction of court architects, Joseph Saint-Pierre and <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Gontard" title="Carl von Gontard">Carl von Gontard</a>, numerous courtly buildings and attractions were created: the <a href="/wiki/Margravial_Opera_House" title="Margravial Opera House">Margravial Opera House</a> with its richly furnished baroque theatre (1744–1748), the New 'Castle' and Sun Temple (1749–1753) at the Hermitage, the New Palace with its courtyard garden (1754 ff) to replace the Old Palace which had burned down through the carelessness of the margrave, and the magnificent row of buildings in today's <i>Friedrichstraße</i>. There was even a unique version of the <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">rococo</a> architectural style, the so-called Bayreuth Rococo which characterised the aforementioned buildings, especially their interior design.</p>
<p>The old, sombre gatehouses were demolished because they impeded transport and were an outmoded form of defence. The walls were built over in places. Margrave Frederick successfully kept his principality out of the wars being waged by his brother-in-law, Frederick the Great, at this time, and, as a result, brought a time of peace to the Frankish kingdom.</p>

<p>1742 saw the founding of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Erlangen-Nuremberg" title="University of Erlangen-Nuremberg">Frederick Academy</a>, which became a university in 1743, but was moved that same year to <a href="/wiki/Erlangen" title="Erlangen">Erlangen</a> after serious riots because of the adverse reaction of the population. The university has remained there to the present today. From 1756 to 1763 there was also an Academy of Arts and Sciences.</p>
<p>Roman Catholics were given the right to set up a prayer room and Jewish families settled here again. In 1760 the synagogue was opened and in 1787 the Jewish cemetery was dedicated.</p>
<p>Countess Wilhelmina died in 1758 and, although, Margrave Frederick married again, the marriage was only short-lived and without issue. After his death in 1763, many artists and craftsmen migrated to Berlin and Potsdam, to work for King Frederick the Great, because Frederick's successor, Margrave <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Christian_of_Brandenburg-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick Christian of Brandenburg-Bayreuth">Frederick Christian</a> had little understanding of art. He also lacked the means due to the elaborate lifestyle of his predecessor, because the buildings and the salaries of the mainly foreign artists had swallowed up a lot of money. For example, the court - which under George Frederick Charles had comprised around 140 people - had grown to about 600 employees by the end of the reign of Margrave Frederick.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> By 1769 the principality was close to bankruptcy.</p>
<p>In 1769 Margrave <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alexander_of_Brandenburg-Ansbach-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Alexander of Brandenburg-Ansbach-Bayreuth">Charles Alexander</a>, from the Ansbach line of Frankish Hohenzollerns, followed the childless Frederick Christian and Bayreuth was reduced to a secondary residence. Charles Alexander continued to live in Ansbach and rarely came to Bayreuth.</p>
<p>In 1775 the Brandenburg Pond (<i>Brandenburger Weiher</i>) in St.Georgen was drained.</p>
<p>Following the abdication of the last Margrave, Charles Alexander, from the principalities of <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Ansbach" title="Principality of Ansbach">Ansbach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Bayreuth" title="Principality of Bayreuth">Bayreuth</a> on 2 December 1791 its territories became part of a Prussian province. The Prussian Minister <a href="/wiki/Karl_August_von_Hardenberg" title="Karl August von Hardenberg">Karl August von Hardenberg</a> took over its administration at the beginning of 1792.</p>
<p>The town centre still possesses the typical structure of a Bavarian street market: the settlement is grouped around a road widening into a square; the Town Hall was located in the middle. The church stood apart from it and on a small hill stood the castle. Some sixty years later the town (at that time a tiny village) became subordinate to the <a href="/wiki/Hohenzollern" class="mw-redirect" title="Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a> state, and when this state was divided, Bayreuth ended up in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=County_of_Kulmbach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="County of Kulmbach (page does not exist)">County of Kulmbach</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1804, the author <a href="/wiki/Jean_Paul" title="Jean Paul">Jean Paul Richter</a> moved from <a href="/wiki/Coburg" title="Coburg">Coburg</a> to Bayreuth, where he lived until his death in 1825.</p>
<p>The rule of the Hohenzollerns over the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Kulmbach-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Principality of Kulmbach-Bayreuth">Principality of Kulmbach-Bayreuth</a> ended in 1806 after the defeat of Prussia by Napoleonic France. During the French occupation from 1806 to 1810 Bayreuth was treated as a province of the French Empire and had to pay high <a href="/w/index.php?title=War_contributions&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="War contributions (page does not exist)">war contributions</a>. It was placed under the administration of Comte <a href="/wiki/Camille_de_Tournon" class="mw-redirect" title="Camille de Tournon">Camille de Tournon</a>, who wrote a detailed inventory of the former Principality of Bayreuth. On 30 June 1810 the French army handed over the former principality to what was now the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Kingdom of Bavaria</a>, which it had bought from <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon</a> for 15 million francs. Bayreuth became the capital of the Bavarian district of <a href="/wiki/Mainkreis_(Bavaria)" title="Mainkreis (Bavaria)">Mainkreis</a>, which later transferred into Obermainkreis and was finally renamed as the province of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Franconia" title="Upper Franconia">Upper Franconia</a>.</p>
<p>As Bavaria was opened up by the railways, the main line from Nuremberg to Hof went past Bayreuth, running via Lichtenfels, Kulmbach and Neuenmarkt-Wirsberg to Hof. Bayreuth was first given a railway connexion in 1853, when the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth%E2%80%93Neuenmarkt-Wirsberg_railway" title="Bayreuth–Neuenmarkt-Wirsberg railway">Bayreuth–Neuenmarkt-Wirsberg railway</a> was built at the town's expense. It was followed in 1863 by the <a href="/wiki/Weiden%E2%80%93Bayreuth_railway" title="Weiden–Bayreuth railway">line to Weiden</a>, in 1877 by the <a href="/wiki/Schnabelwaid%E2%80%93Bayreuth_railway" title="Schnabelwaid–Bayreuth railway">railway to Schnabelwaid</a>, in 1896 by the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth%E2%80%93Warmensteinach_railway" title="Bayreuth–Warmensteinach railway">branch line to Warmensteinach</a>, in 1904 by the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth%E2%80%93Hollfeld_railway" title="Bayreuth–Hollfeld railway">branch to Hollfeld</a> and in 1909 by the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Altstadt%E2%80%93Kulmbach_railway" title="Bayreuth Altstadt–Kulmbach railway">branch via Thurnau to Kulmbach</a>, known as the <i>Thurnauer Bockala</i> (which means something like "Thurnau Goat").</p>

<p>On 17 April 1870 <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> visited Bayreuth, because he had read about the Margrave Opera House, whose great stage seemed fitting for his works. However, the orchestra pit could not accommodate the large number of musicians required, for example, for the <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_the_Nibelung" class="mw-redirect" title="Ring of the Nibelung">Ring of the Nibelung</a> and the ambience of the auditorium seemed inappropriate for his piece.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> So, he toyed with the idea of building his own festival hall (the <i>Festspielhaus</i>) in Bayreuth. The town supported him in this project and made a piece of land available to him, an undeveloped area outside the town between the railway station and Hohe Warte, the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gr%C3%BCner_H%C3%BCgel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grüner Hügel (page does not exist)">Grüner Hügel</a></i> ("Green Hill"). At the same time Wagner acquired a property at <i>Hofgarten</i> to build his own house, <i><a href="/wiki/Wahnfried" title="Wahnfried">Wahnfried</a></i>. On 22 May 1872 the cornerstone for the Festival Hall was laid and, on 13 August 1876, it was officially opened (see <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festival" title="Bayreuth Festival">Bayreuth Festival</a>). Planning and construction were in the hands of the Leipzig architect, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Br%C3%BCckwald&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Otto Brückwald (page does not exist)">Otto Brückwald</a>, who had already made a name for himself in the building of theatres in Leipzig and Altenburg.</p>
<p>In 1886, the composer <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a> died in Bayreuth while visiting his daughter <a href="/wiki/Cosima_Liszt" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosima Liszt">Cosima Liszt</a>, Wagner's widow. Both Liszt and Wagner are buried in Bayreuth; however Wagner did not die there. Rather he died in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in 1883, but his family had his body brought to Bayreuth for burial.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="To_the_end_of_the_Weimar_Republic_(1900–1933)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="To_the_end_of_the_Weimar_Republic_.281900.E2.80.931933.29">To the end of the Weimar Republic (1900–1933)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: To the end of the Weimar Republic (1900–1933)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The new century also brought several innovations of modern technology: in 1892, the first electric street lights; in 1908 a municipal electricity station, and, in the same year, the first cinema.</p>
<p>In 1914–15, one section of the northern arm of the Red Main was straightened and widened after areas along the river had been flooded during a period of high water in 1909.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> had ended in 1918, the <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_council" title="Workers' council">Workers' and Soldiers' Council</a> took power briefly in Bayreuth. On 17 February 1919, there was a three-day coup, the so-called <i>Speckputsch</i>, a brief interlude of excitement in the otherwise rather staid town.</p>
<p>In a series of <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkisch_movement" title="Völkisch movement"><i>völkisch</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalist</a> "Deutscher Tag" (German Days), the <a href="/wiki/NSDAP" class="mw-redirect" title="NSDAP">NSDAP</a> organised the event in Bayreuth on 30 September 1923. More than 5,000 military and civilian people gathered (equivalent to 15% of the inhabitants), although <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Defence" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of Defence">Minister of Defence</a> <a href="/wiki/Otto_Gessler" title="Otto Gessler">Otto Gessler</a> had forbidden the participation of <a href="/wiki/Reichswehr" title="Reichswehr">Reichswehr</a> units.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Among the guests were mayor Albert Preu as well as <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Wagner" title="Siegfried Wagner">Siegfried</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winifred_Wagner" title="Winifred Wagner">Winifred Wagner</a>, who invited keynote speaker <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> to <a href="/wiki/Wahnfried" title="Wahnfried">Wahnfried</a> house. There he met writer <a href="/wiki/Houston_Stewart_Chamberlain" title="Houston Stewart Chamberlain">Houston Stewart Chamberlain</a>, son-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">anti-semitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">race theorist</a>. Also on that day, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Schemm" title="Hans Schemm">Hans Schemm</a> met Hitler for the first time.</p>
<p>In 1932, the provinces of Upper and Middle Franconia were merged and <a href="/wiki/Ansbach" title="Ansbach">Ansbach</a> was chosen as the seat of government. As a small compensation, Bayreuth was given the merged state insurance agency for Upper and Middle Franconia. Unlike the provincial merger, the merger of those institutions was never reversed.</p>
<h4><span id="The_Nazi_era_(1933–1945)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Nazi_era_.281933.E2.80.931945.29">The Nazi era (1933–1945)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Nazi era (1933–1945)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Being a stronghold of right-wing parties since the 1920s, Bayreuth became a center of <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazi</a> ideology. In 1933, it was made capital of the Nazi <a href="/wiki/Gau_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gau (country subdivision)">Gau</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Ostmark" class="mw-redirect" title="Bavarian Ostmark">Bavarian Ostmark</a> (<i>Bayerische Ostmark</i>, in 1943 <i>Gau Bayreuth</i>). Nazi leaders often visited the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festival" title="Bayreuth Festival">Wagner festival</a> and tried to turn Bayreuth into a Nazi model town. It was one of several places in which town planning was administered directly from Berlin, due to Hitler's special interest in the town and in the festival. Hitler loved the music of Richard Wagner, and he became a close friend of <a href="/wiki/Winifred_Wagner" title="Winifred Wagner">Winifred Wagner</a> after she took over the festival. Hitler frequently attended Wagner performances in the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festival_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Bayreuth Festival Hall">Bayreuth Festival Hall</a>.</p>
<p>Bayreuth was to have received a so-called <i>Gauforum</i>, a combined government building and marching square built to symbolise the centre of power in the town. Bayreuth's first <a href="/wiki/Gauleiter" title="Gauleiter">Gauleiter</a> was <a href="/wiki/Hans_Schemm" title="Hans Schemm">Hans Schemm</a>, who was also the head (<i>Reichswalter</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/National_Socialist_Teachers_League" title="National Socialist Teachers League">National Socialist Teachers League</a>, NSLB, which was located in Bayreuth. In 1937 the town was connected to the new <i><a href="/wiki/Reichsautobahn" title="Reichsautobahn">Reichsautobahn</a></i>.</p>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Nazi_dictatorship" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi dictatorship">Nazi dictatorship</a> the <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Community" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish Community">Jewish Community</a> in <i>Münzgasse</i> was desecrated and looted on <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a> but, due to its proximity to the Opera House it was not razed. Inside the building, which is once again used by a Jewish community as a synagogue, a plaque next to the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> Shrine recalls the persecution and murder of Jews in the <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">Shoah</a>, which took the lives of at least 145 Jews in Bayreuth.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the Second World War, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_subcamps_of_Flossenb%C3%BCrg" title="List of subcamps of Flossenbürg">subcamp</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp" title="Flossenbürg concentration camp">Flossenbürg concentration camp</a> was based in the town,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in which prisoners had to participate in physical experiments for the <a href="/wiki/V-2" class="mw-redirect" title="V-2">V-2</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wieland_Wagner" title="Wieland Wagner">Wieland Wagner</a>, the grandson of the composer, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>, was the deputy civilian director there in late 1944 and early April 1945<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>. Shortly before the war's end branches of the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Court_(Germany)" title="People's Court (Germany)">People's Court</a> (<i>Volksgerichtshof</i>) were to have been set up in Bayreuth.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5, 8 and 11 April 1945 about one third of the town, including many public buildings and industrial installations were destroyed by heavy air strikes, along with 4,500 houses. 741 people were also killed. On 14 April, the U.S. Army occupied the town.</p>
<h4><span id="Post-war_era_and_Reconstruction_(1945–2000)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_era_and_Reconstruction_.281945.E2.80.932000.29">Post-war era and Reconstruction (1945–2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Post-war era and Reconstruction (1945–2000)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After the war Bayreuth tried to part with its ill-fated past. It became part of the <a href="/wiki/American_Zone_of_Occupation" class="mw-redirect" title="American Zone of Occupation">American Zone</a>. The American military government set up a <a href="/wiki/DP_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="DP camp">DP camp</a> to accommodate <a href="/wiki/Displaced_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Displaced person">displaced persons</a> (DP), many of whom were <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DP_camps_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DP_camps-14">[14]</a></sup> The camp was supervised by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Relief_and_Rehabilitation_Administration" title="United Nations Relief and Rehabilitation Administration">UNRRA</a>.</p>
<p>The housing situation was very difficult at first: there were about 55,000 inhabitants in the town, many more than before the war began. This increase was primarily due to the high number of refugees and expellees. Even in 1948 more than 11,000 refugees were counted. In addition, because many homes had been destroyed due to the war, thousands of people were living in temporary shelters, even the festival restaurant next to the Festival Hall housed some 500 people.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1945, 1,400 men were conscripted by the town council for "essential work" (clean-up work on damaged buildings and the clearing of roads). A significant number of historic buildings were demolished post-war but cultural life was soon back on track: in 1947 <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a> festival weeks were held in the Opera House, from which the Franconian Festival Weeks developed. In 1949 the Festival Hall was used for the first time again and there was a gala concert with the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Philharmonic" title="Vienna Philharmonic">Vienna Philharmonic</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Knappertsbusch" title="Hans Knappertsbusch">Hans Knappertsbusch</a>. In 1951, the first post-war Richard Wagner Festival took place under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Wieland_Wagner" title="Wieland Wagner">Wieland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Wagner" title="Wolfgang Wagner">Wolfgang Wagner</a>.</p>
<p>In 1949 Bayreuth became the seat of the government of Upper Franconia again.</p>
<p>In 1971 the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_State_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Bavarian State Parliament">Bavarian State Parliament</a> decided to establish the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bayreuth" title="University of Bayreuth">University of Bayreuth</a> and, on 3 November 1975, it opened for lectures and research. There are now about 10,000 students in the town.</p>
<p>In May 1972, a serious accident occurred at the folk festival in the town, when an overcrowded carriage derailed and several people were thrown out. Four died and five were injured, some seriously. At that time, it was the worst disaster on a roller coaster since the Second World War.</p>
<p>In 1979, US Army serviceman <a href="/wiki/Roy_Chung" title="Roy Chung">Roy Chung</a> disappeared from the area and allegedly defected to <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> via <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>.</p>
<p>In 1999 the world gliding championship took place at Bayreuth municipal airport.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, Bayreuth chose its first <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">CSU</a> member and mayor, the lawyer, Michael Hohl, and, in 2007, a <a href="/wiki/Youth_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Youth Parliament">Youth Parliament</a>, consisting of 12 young people, aged 14–17 years, was elected for the first time. The end of October saw the opening of the long-planned bus station and its associated office building on the newly created <i>Hohenzollernplatz</i>.</p>

<p>The town is best known for its association with the composer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>, who lived in Bayreuth from 1872 until his death in 1883. Wagner's villa, "<a href="/wiki/Wahnfried" title="Wahnfried">Wahnfried</a>", was constructed in Bayreuth under the sponsorship of <a href="/wiki/King_Ludwig_II_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="King Ludwig II of Bavaria">King Ludwig II of Bavaria</a>, and was converted after World War II into a Wagner Museum. In the northern part of Bayreuth is the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festspielhaus" title="Bayreuth Festspielhaus">Festival Hall</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Opera_house" title="Opera house">opera house</a> specially constructed for and exclusively devoted to the performance of Wagner's <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>. The premieres of the final two works of Wagner's <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Ring_des_Nibelungen" title="Der Ring des Nibelungen">Ring Cycle</a></i> ("<a href="/wiki/Siegfried_(opera)" title="Siegfried (opera)">Siegfried</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6tterd%C3%A4mmerung" title="Götterdämmerung">Götterdämmerung</a>"); the cycle as a whole; and of <i><a href="/wiki/Parsifal" title="Parsifal">Parsifal</a></i> took place here.</p>
<p>Every summer, Wagner's operas are performed at the Festspielhaus during the month-long Richard Wagner Festival, commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festival" title="Bayreuth Festival">Bayreuth Festival</a>. The Festival draws thousands each year, and has persistently been sold out since its inauguration in 1876. Currently, waiting lists for tickets can stretch for 10 years or more.</p>
<p>Owing to Wagner's relationship with the then unknown philosopher <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a>, the first Bayreuth festival is cited as a key turning point in Nietzsche's philosophical development. Though at first an enthusiastic champion of Wagner's music, Nietzsche ultimately became hostile, viewing the festival and its revellers as symptom of cultural decay and bourgeois decadence —an event which led him to turn his eye upon the moral values esteemed by society as a whole - "Nietzsche clearly preferred to see Bayreuth fail than succeed by mirroring a society gone wrong."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bayreuth lies on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Main" title="Red Main">Red Main</a> river, the southern of the two headstreams of the <a href="/wiki/River_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="River Main">River Main</a>, between the <a href="/wiki/Fichtelgebirge_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Fichtelgebirge Mountains">Fichtelgebirge Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Switzerland" title="Franconian Switzerland">Franconian Switzerland</a>. The town is also part of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Metropolitan_Region" title="Nuremberg Metropolitan Region">Nuremberg Metropolitan Region</a>.</p>

<p>In 1955 Bayreuth took on sponsorship for displaced <a href="/wiki/Sudeten_Germans" title="Sudeten Germans">Sudeten Germans</a> from the town of <a href="/wiki/Franzensbad" class="mw-redirect" title="Franzensbad">Franzensbad</a> in Okres Cheb.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Coat of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Margrave Albert Achilles, who was also Elector of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Brandenburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, presented the town Bayreuth in December 1457 with the coat of arms that it still bears today. Two fields show the black and white coat of arms of the Hohenzollerns. The black lion on gold with a red and white border was the municipal coat of arms of the burgraves of Nuremberg. Along the two diagonals are two <i>Reuten</i>, small triangular shovels with a slightly bent shaft. They represent the ending <i>-reuth</i> in the town's name." <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_places_of_interest">Culture and places of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Culture and places of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Margravial_Opera_House" title="Margravial Opera House">Margravial Opera House</a> was opened in 1748 and is one of the finest Baroque theatres in Europe. It is both a museum and the oldest working <i>tableau</i> in Bayreuth.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festspielhaus" title="Bayreuth Festspielhaus">Festival Hall</a> dates to the 19th century and is now used solely for the <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Festival" title="Bayreuth Festival">Bayreuth Festival</a>. Only works by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> are put on.</p>
<p>The Bayreuth Town House (<i>Stadthaus</i>), likewise, does not have its own ensemble. It is regularly used by the <i>Theater Hof</i> as well as the <i>Tourneetheater</i>.</p>
<p>The only two theatres with their own ensemble are the <i>Studiobühne Bayreuth</i> and amateur dramatic society, <i>Brandenburg Kulturstadl</i>. The venues of the studio theatre in Bayreuth are the domicile of the theatre in the <i>Röntgenstraße</i>, the ruins of the Bayreuth Hermitage and the courtyard of Bayreuth piano manufacturer, <a href="/wiki/Steingraeber_%26_S%C3%B6hne" title="Steingraeber &amp; Söhne">Steingraeber &amp; Söhne</a>.</p>

<li>The Richard Wagner Museum at <a href="/wiki/Wahnfried" title="Wahnfried">Wahnfried</a> House was the residence of Richard Wagner and his family's home until 1966. Since 1976 it has been a museum with attached national archives and a research centre for the Richard Wagner Foundation in Bayreuth</li>
<li>The Jean Paul Museum in the former residence of Richard Wagner's daughter, Eva Chamberlain, with <a href="/wiki/Autograph" title="Autograph">autographs</a>, first editions of works, portraits and other pictorial material</li>
<li>The Franz Liszt Museum in the house where <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a> died, with about 300 photographs, scripts and printed papers from the collection of the Munich pianist, Ernst Burger, which were bought by the town of Bayreuth. In addition there is a <i>Stummklavier</i>, made by the <a href="/wiki/Rud._Ibach_Sohn" title="Rud. Ibach Sohn">Ibach company</a> of Haus Wahnfried, letters and first editions of Franz Liszt. Biographic information boards, a mould of the font from Liszt's birthplace <a href="/wiki/Raiding,_Austria" title="Raiding, Austria">Raiding, Austria</a> and Liszt busts by Antonio Galli enhance the collection. Visits are accompanied by the music of Franz Liszt</li>
<li>The Historical Museum in the Old Latin School on <i>Kirchplatz</i>. On the ground floor it portrays the history and development of Bayreuth from the late Middle Ages to the 20th century with a model of the town in the year 1763. On the first floor are divisions covering the art and cultural history of Bayreuth's margravial period (17th and 18th centuries). Another division portrays arts and crafts in Bayreuth and the surrounding area with examples of faience pottery, glass products from the Fichtelgebirge and stone pottery from <a href="/wiki/Creu%C3%9Fen" title="Creußen">Creußen</a>. <li>The British American Tobacco's Historical Collection in the old Lord Mayor's rooms of the Old Town Hall</li>
<li>The German Typewriter Museum with a collection of over 400 historic typewriters from the Research and Training Centre for Shorthand and Word Processing in Bayreuth</li>
<li>A branch of the Bavarian State Painting Collection was opened in the New Palace in August 2007. 80 works from Dutch and German painters of the late 17th century and 18th century are displayed.</li>
<li>The Archaeological Museum in the Italian Building of the New Palace was founded in 1827 by the Historic Society. Its eight exhibition rooms include artefacts such as <a href="/wiki/New_Stone_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="New Stone Age">New Stone Age</a> stone axes, 80 pottery jars from the <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Hallstatt era">Hallstatt era</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> bronze jewellery. The discoveries on display, which all come from eastern Upper Franconia, especially <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Switzerland" title="Franconian Switzerland">Franconian Switzerland</a> and the region around Bayreuth, date from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Stone_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Stone Age">Old Stone Age</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. In the experimental field there is a reconstructed loom, a rock drill and an original <i>Schiebemühle</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brauerei_Gebr._Maisel" title="Brauerei Gebr. Maisel">Maisel's Brewery</a> and Cooper's Museum, which teaches everything about the production of <a href="/wiki/Weizen_beer" class="mw-redirect" title="Weizen beer">Weizen beer</a> on a 2,400&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (25,833&#160;sq&#160;ft) layout, making it the largest brewery in the world,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> not least due to its collection of over 5,500 beer glasses and mugs).</li>
<li>The Upper Franconia Prehistory Museum portrays the history of life in Upper Franconia since the beginning of the world. Exhibitions are constantly changing; currently the life-size dinosaurs attract especial interest.</li>

<p>In the town centre is the Court Garden (<i>Hofgarten</i>) of the New Palace. Near the Festival Hall is the Festival Park. On the southern edge of the town lie the Botanical Gardens of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bayreuth" title="University of Bayreuth">University of Bayreuth</a>. On the <i>Königsallee</i>, east of the town centre, is the relatively small Miedel Garden.</p>
<p>The best known park in Bayreuth is that of the 'Eremitage' (Hermitage) in the district of <a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Johannis_(Bayreuth)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="St. Johannis (Bayreuth) (page does not exist)">St. Johannis</a>. With a total area of almost 50 hectares it is the largest park in Bayreuth.</p>
<p>Bayreuth has been chosen to host the Bavarian Country Garden Show in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> For this reason another park is planned on the Main water meadows between the Volksfestplatz and the A9 motorway.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The oldest surviving cemetery is the Town Cemetery (<i>Stadtfriedhof</i>) with a large number of gravestones of famous people. On the southern edge of the town is the Southern Cemetery (<i>Südfriedhof</i>) and crematorium. The districts of St. Johannis and St. Georgen have their own cemeteries. On <i>Nürnberger Straße</i>, in the east of the town, is an Israeli cemetery.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over 60 clubs offer just under one hundred sports. The most successful club in the town presently is the Bayreuth Air Sports Community with its Gliding Team: 2002 and 2015 the pilots won the Federal Gliding League, in 2015 they have won even the IGC-World League.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The street hockey team of the Hurricans Bayreuth have been German runners-up three times (1998/2004/2006) and champions five times (1996/1997/2001/2005/2007). The basketball team of <a href="/wiki/BBC_Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC Bayreuth">BBC Bayreuth</a> plays in the <a href="/wiki/Basketball_Bundesliga" title="Basketball Bundesliga">Basketball Bundesliga</a> (division 1), the HaSpo Bayreuth handball team, the footballers of <a href="/wiki/SpVgg_Bayreuth" title="SpVgg Bayreuth">SpVgg Bayreuth</a> and the volleyball players of <a href="/w/index.php?title=BSV_Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="BSV Bayreuth (page does not exist)">BSV Bayreuth</a> each play in their respective Bavarian League. The ice hockey team, <a href="/w/index.php?title=EHC_Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="EHC Bayreuth (page does not exist)">EHC Bayreuth</a>, has also just entered the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bavarian_League&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bavarian League (page does not exist)">Bavarian League</a>.</p>
<p>Bayreuth had its sporting heyday in the late 1980s and early 90s. The basketball team, <a href="/wiki/Steiner_Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Steiner Bayreuth">Steiner Bayreuth</a>, were twice German Cup winners (1987/1988 and 1988/1989) and in the 1988/1989 season they also won the German championship. The hockey team of Bayreuth's swimming club (SCC) was twice champions of Second Division South and also played for a year in the Hockey League. At the time that the <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis" title="Table tennis">table tennis</a> team of Steiner Bayreuth was also first class<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> (since 1983 2nd Division, in 1984/85, 1986/87 and 1987/88 1st Division,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> 1988 relegated<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and the team has played for many years in the 2nd Football Division. The table tennis players of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=1._Bayreuth_FC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="1. Bayreuth FC (page does not exist)">1. Bayreuth FC</a> played in the 1st Division from 1994 to 1997.</p>
<p>In 1999 the World <a href="/wiki/Glider_(sailplane)" title="Glider (sailplane)">Glider</a> Championships took place in Bayreuth.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>From <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bayreuth Hauptbahnhof">Bayreuth Central Station</a> (<i>Hauptbahnhof</i>) railway lines run north <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth%E2%80%93Neuenmarkt-Wirsberg_railway" title="Bayreuth–Neuenmarkt-Wirsberg railway">to Neuenmarkt-Wirsberg</a>, and from there to Bamberg and over the <a href="/wiki/Schiefe_Ebene" title="Schiefe Ebene">Schiefe Ebene</a> to Hof, east <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth%E2%80%93Warmensteinach_railway" title="Bayreuth–Warmensteinach railway">to Weidenberg</a>, southeast <a href="/wiki/Weiden%E2%80%93Bayreuth_railway" title="Weiden–Bayreuth railway">to Weiden</a> and south <a href="/wiki/Schnabelwaid%E2%80%93Bayreuth_railway" title="Schnabelwaid–Bayreuth railway">to Schnabelwaid</a> with connections to <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Cheb_railway" title="Nuremberg–Cheb railway">Pegnitz Valley Railway</a>. The lines around Bayreuth are all single-tracked and non-electrified.</p>
<p>Since 23 May 1992 <a href="/wiki/Tilting_train" title="Tilting train">tilting</a> <a href="/wiki/DB_Class_610" title="DB Class 610">Class 610</a> <a href="/wiki/Diesel_multiple_units" class="mw-redirect" title="Diesel multiple units">diesel multiple units</a> have worked the Pegnitz Valley route. These were bought by the former <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bundesbahn" title="Deutsche Bundesbahn">Deutsche Bundesbahn</a> specifically for the winding track.</p>
<p>Since a 2006/2007 timetable change, Bayreuth has no longer been connected to the DB's long-distance network. However, the <a href="/wiki/Franken-Sachsen-Express" class="mw-redirect" title="Franken-Sachsen-Express">Franken-Sachsen-Express</a> still provides a direct connection to Dresden (since December 2007, every two hours). This service is worked by <a href="/wiki/DBAG_Class_612" title="DBAG Class 612">Class 612</a> diesel multiple units. There are also <a href="/wiki/Regional-Express" title="Regional-Express">Regional-Express</a> links via <a href="/wiki/Lichtenfels,_Bavaria" title="Lichtenfels, Bavaria">Lichtenfels</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>, and via Lichtenfels and <a href="/wiki/Kronach" title="Kronach">Kronach</a> to <a href="/wiki/Saalfeld" title="Saalfeld">Saalfeld</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_public_transport">Local public transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Local public transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ZOH_Bayreuth.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/ZOH_Bayreuth.jpg/220px-ZOH_Bayreuth.jpg" width="220" height="104" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/ZOH_Bayreuth.jpg/330px-ZOH_Bayreuth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/ZOH_Bayreuth.jpg/440px-ZOH_Bayreuth.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4840" data-file-height="2298" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ZOH_Bayreuth.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The central bus station (ZOH) at the <i>Hohenzollernplatz</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The town bus routes are operated by Bayreuth Transport and Public Baths (BVB) (<i>Bayreuther Verkehrs- und Bäder GmbH</i>). Sometimes private bus operators run services on behalf of the transport companies. The 15 routes (lines 301-315) operate from Monday to Friday at 20 or 30-minute intervals; on Saturday and Sunday the interval is extended to 30 minutes. Late evening services (from about 20 to 12 pm during the week and to 1 am at weekends), on Sunday mornings a simplified network of six lines (lines 321-326) runs buses at 30-minute intervals. Some lines then operate like an on-call taxi service. The network is star-shaped. Originally, the central station was at the market square in <i>Maximilianstrasse</i>. Since 27 October 2007 the Central Bus Station (ZOH) has been at <i>Hohenzollernplatz</i> at the junction of <i>Kanalstraße</i> on the <i>Hohenzollernring</i>. At this stop there are also bus stops for local buses to facilitate transfers.</p>
<p>Regional rail is operated by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Omnibusverkehr_Franken&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Omnibusverkehr Franken (page does not exist)">Omnibusverkehr Franken</a>. From 1 January 2010 public transport from the town and district of Bayreuth was integrated into the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Regional_Transport_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nuremberg Regional Transport Network (page does not exist)">Nuremberg Regional Transport Network</a> (<i>Verkehrsverbund Großraum Nürnberg</i>).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycling">Cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In most places there is a signed <a href="/wiki/Segregated_cycle_facilities" class="mw-redirect" title="Segregated cycle facilities">cycle path</a> network. In the centre of Bayreuth itself, cycling is fairly straightforward due to the relatively flat <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, something which encourages the use bicycles as an everyday means of transport. Because of the proximity of the 600 kilometre long <a href="/w/index.php?title=Main_Cycle_Path&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Main Cycle Path (page does not exist)">Main Cycle Path</a>, Bayreuth is also a destination for many tourist <a href="/wiki/Cycle_route" class="mw-redirect" title="Cycle route">cycle routes</a>. Because of the long service intervals of the Bayreuth town bus system and its long overnight pause, students use bicycles as their everyday mode of transport. Bicycles may be carried for a fee on <a href="/wiki/DB_Regio" title="DB Regio">DB Regio</a> trains leaving Bayreuth and in the VGN's buses.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_transport">Air transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Air transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The local airport supports Bayreuth's commercial aviation traffic, individual business travel, general aviation and air sports. By 2002 even the airline from Frankfurt to Hof stopped in Bayreuth three times a day.</p>
<p>The airfield at Bindlacher Berg is also one of the most important bases for gliding in Germany. For example, the World Championships took place here in 1999. For the air sports community in Bayreuth, the airport is a departure point for glider flights taking part in the national Bundesliga competition league. The local gliding club also provides instruction in flying gliders and light aircraft.</p>
<p>See also: <i><a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Bayreuth Airport">Bayreuth Airport</a></i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Garrison">Garrison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bayreuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Garrison">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For centuries Bayreuth was also a <a href="/wiki/Garrison" title="Garrison">garrison town</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Army" title="Prussian Army">Prussian Army</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bavarian_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Bavarian Army">Royal Bavarian Army</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Reichswehr" title="Reichswehr">Reichswehr</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/US_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="US Army">US Army</a>, German Army (<i><a href="/wiki/Bundeswehr" title="Bundeswehr">Bundeswehr</a></i>) and the German Border Police (<i><a href="/wiki/Bundesgrenzschutz" title="Bundesgrenzschutz">Bundesgrenzschutz</a></i>). In the early 1990s, following the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> the garrison tradition of the town came to an end when the <i>Bundeswehr's</i> Margrave Barracks (<i>Markgrafenkaserne</i>) and the Röhrensee Barracks (<i>Röhrenseekaserne</i>), used by the US Army and the BGS (<i>Grenzschutzabteilung Süd 3</i>), were closed.</p>

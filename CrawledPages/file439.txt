<p><b>Reutlingen</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈʁɔʏtlɪŋən]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Reutlingen.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1b/De-Reutlingen.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Reutlingen.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is the capital of the eponymous <a href="/wiki/Reutlingen_(district)" title="Reutlingen (district)">district of Reutlingen</a>. As of April 2008, it has a population of 109,828.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Reutlingen has a <a href="/wiki/Reutlingen_University" title="Reutlingen University">university of applied sciences</a>, which was founded in 1855, originally as a weavers' school. Today Reutlingen is home to an established textile industry and also houses machinery, leather goods and steel manufacturing facilities. It has the narrowest street in the world, <a href="/wiki/Spreuerhofstra%C3%9Fe" title="Spreuerhofstraße">Spreuerhofstraße</a> (width 31&#160;cm).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Reutlingen is located about 35&#160;km (22&#160;mi) south of the State capital of Baden-Württemberg, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>. It lies in the Southwest corner of Germany, right next to the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_Jura" title="Swabian Jura">Swabian Jura</a>, and that is why it is often called <i>The gate to the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_Alps" class="mw-redirect" title="Swabian Alps">Swabian Jura</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Das Tor zur Schwäbischen Alb</span>). The <a href="/wiki/Echaz" title="Echaz">Echaz</a> river, a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Neckar" title="Neckar">Neckar</a>, flows through the town centre.</p>
<p>Along with the old <a href="/wiki/College_town" title="College town">university town</a> of <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCbingen" title="Tübingen">Tübingen</a> (about 15&#160;km (9.3&#160;mi) to the west), Reutlingen is the centre of the <a href="/wiki/Neckar-Alb" title="Neckar-Alb">Neckar-Alb</a> region. It is also part of the larger <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart_Metropolitan_Region" title="Stuttgart Metropolitan Region">Stuttgart Metropolitan Region</a>.</p>

<p>The first settlements in the area are believed to date from the 4th or 5th century. Some time around 1030, Count Egino started to build a castle on top of the <i>Achalm</i>, one of the largest mountains in Reutlingen district (about 706 m). One of the towers of this castle was rebuilt in the 19th century and is open to visitors. The name <i>Reutlingen</i> was first mentioned in writing in the so-called <i>Bempflingen Treaty</i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Bempflinger Vertrag</span>) which is dated approximately 1089–90.</p>
<p>Around 1180, Reutlingen received market rights and, between 1220 and 1240 it was promoted to <a href="/wiki/German_town_law" title="German town law">city status</a> and city-walls and fortifications were built. Shortly thereafter, from 1247–1343, the town's landmark, the <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Church,_Reutlingen" title="St. Mary's Church, Reutlingen">St. Mary's Church</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Marienkirche</span>) was built.</p>
<p>In 1377 Reutlingen was the scene of a victory by the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_League" title="Swabian League">Swabian League</a>, formed in the previous year by 14 Swabian cities, led by <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a>, over the <a href="/wiki/Count_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Count of Württemberg">Count of Württemberg</a>. In 1519, a later Swabian League came to Reutlingen's help when <a href="/wiki/Ulrich,_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Ulrich, Duke of Württemberg">Ulrich, Duke of Württemberg</a> attempted to seize the city; the League landed a crushing blow, conquering Württemberg and selling it to <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a>. In 1495 and 1516 the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">jews</a> were exiled from the town.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a result of such struggles, Reutlingen became an <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Free_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Free City">Imperial City</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, free from allegiance to the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Württemberg">Duke of Württemberg</a>. In 1530, Reutlingen's city council signed the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a>, and in 1580 and the Formula of Concord, key documents of <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a>. In 1803, in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a>, Reutlingen lost its independence in the <a href="/wiki/German_Mediatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="German Mediatisation">German Mediatisation</a>, being restored to <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Electorate of Württemberg">Württemberg</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_period">Modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reutlingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The worst disaster in the history of Reutlingen happened in 1726, when a major fire swept through the city, destroying 80% of all residential houses and almost all public buildings, and making 1,200 families homeless. The impact of this fire, which lasted three days, is still visible today.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the wings of the <a href="/wiki/V-1_(flying_bomb)" class="mw-redirect" title="V-1 (flying bomb)">V-1 flying bomb</a> were manufactured in Reutlingen, making the city the target of several allied bombing raids.</p>
<p>On 24 July 2016 a Syrian <a href="/wiki/Asylum_seeker" title="Asylum seeker">asylum seeker</a> killed a pregnant woman in a <a href="/wiki/2016_Reutlingen_knife_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="2016 Reutlingen knife attack">machete attack</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mutscheltag">Mutscheltag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reutlingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mutscheltag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Mutschel" title="Mutschel">Mutscheltag</a> (the first Thursday after <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">Epiphany</a>), townspeople gather in halls and homes to play games of dice, the winner of which earns parts or whole <a href="/wiki/Mutschel" title="Mutschel">Mutschel</a> loaves of bread. The Mutschelspiele (Mutschel games) consist of small games scored by tally marks, and are won both independently and by grand total at the end of the hour or night. This tradition is unique to the city of Reutlingen.</p>

<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Church,_Reutlingen" title="St. Mary's Church, Reutlingen">Church of the Virgin Mary</a></i>, built in <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> style in the 13th–14th centuries. Nearby is a statue of emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a>.</li>
<li><i>Marktbrunnen</i> ("Market Fountain", 16th century), surmounted by the statue of emperor <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian II, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian II</a>.</li>
<li><i>Spitalhof</i>, built as a hospital in the 14th century. Damaged by a fire, it was largely rebuilt in the 18th century.</li>
<li><i>Church of St. Nicholas</i>, built in the 14th century as a chapel.</li>
<li><i>Gerber- und Färberbrunnen</i> ("Tanners' and Dyers' Fountain"), 1920.</li>
<li><i>City Hall</i>, built in 2013.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Spreuerhofstra%C3%9Fe" title="Spreuerhofstraße">Spreuerhofstraße</a></i>, the world's narrowest street.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="University">University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reutlingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Reutlingen_University" title="Reutlingen University">Reutlingen University</a> is a university of applied sciences, focusing on hands-on learning, which is apparent in their mandatory internship for all business majors. The university is an internationally friendly school with 111 cooperative campuses worldwide. Classes are generally taught in German; however, in some Bachelor programs and in the Master's programs classes are taught in English.</p>

<p><i><b>John Harvard</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Bronze_sculpture" title="Bronze sculpture">sculpture in bronze</a> by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Chester_French" title="Daniel Chester French">Daniel Chester French</a> in <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Yard" title="Harvard Yard">Harvard Yard</a>, Cambridge, Massachu­setts honoring <a href="/wiki/John_Harvard_(clergyman)" title="John Harvard (clergyman)">John Harvard</a> (1607–1638), whose deathbed<sup id="cite_ref-HMagJanFeb2000_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HMagJanFeb2000-3">[2]</a></sup> bequest to the "schoale or Colledge" recently undertaken by the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony" title="Massachusetts Bay Colony">Massachu­setts Bay Colony</a> was so gratefully received that it was consequently ordered "that the Colledge agreed upon formerly to bee built at Cambridg shalbee called <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard Colledge</a>."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-charter_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charter-4">[3]</a></sup> There being nothing to indicate what John Harvard had looked like, French used a Harvard student <a href="/wiki/Collateral_descendant" class="mw-redirect" title="Collateral descendant">collaterally descended</a> from an early Harvard president as inspiration.</p>
<p>The statue's inscription‍—‌<small><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;"><span class="nowrap">JOHN HARVARD<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>&#160;•</span> <span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span class="nowrap">FOUNDER<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>&#160;•</span> 1638</span></small>‍—‌is the subject of an <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/arch#English-knowing" class="extiw" title="wikt:arch">arch</a> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/polemic#English-attack" class="extiw" title="wikt:polemic">polemic</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ST_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ST-5">[4]</a></sup> traditionally recited for visitors, questioning whether John Harvard justly merits the honorific <i>founder</i>. According to a Harvard official, the founding of the college was not the act of one but the work of many; John Harvard is therefore considered not <i>the</i> founder, but rather <i>a</i><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>founder, of the school, though the timeliness and generosity of his contribution have made him the most honored of these.</p>
<p>Tourists often rub the toe of <i>John Harvard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s left shoe for luck, in the mistaken belief that doing so is a Harvard student tradition.</p>

<p><i>The New York Times</i> described the statue at its unveiling:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The young clergyman is represented sitting, holding an open [book] on his knee. The costume is the simple clerical garb of the seventeenth century<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... low shoes, long, silk hose, loose knee breeches, and a tunic belted at the waist, while a long cloak, thrown back, falls in broad, picturesque folds.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT18_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT18-13">[b]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>John Harvard's gift to the school was <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>780 and‍—‌perhaps more importantly<sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-potter1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-potter1-14">[12]</a></sup>‍—‌his 400-volume scholar's library:<sup id="cite_ref-potter2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-potter2-15">[13]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Partly under the chair, within easy reach, lie a pile of books.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT18_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT18-13">[b]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>That he had died of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, at about age thirty, was one of the few things known about John Harvard at the time of the statue's composition; as dedication orator <a href="/wiki/George_Edward_Ellis" title="George Edward Ellis">George Edward Ellis</a> put it:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Gently touched by the weakness which was wasting his immature <span class="nowrap">life,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[c]</a></sup></span> he rests for a moment from his <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/converse#English-noun_discourse" class="extiw" title="wikt:converse">converse</a> with wisdom on the printed page, and raises his contemplative eye to the spaces of all wisdom.<sup id="cite_ref-gazes_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gazes-1">[a]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Historian <a href="/wiki/Laurel_Ulrich" class="mw-redirect" title="Laurel Ulrich">Laurel Ulrich</a> suggests that <i>John Harvard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s general composition may have been inspired by <a href="/wiki/Clio_(Hendrik_Goltzius)" title="Clio (Hendrik Goltzius)">Hendrik Goltzius' engraving of Clio</a>, and that the figure's collar, buttons, tassel, and mustache may have been taken from a portrait of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a> Governor <a href="/wiki/Edward_Winslow" title="Edward Winslow">Edward Winslow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ireland_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ireland-10">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>On June 27, 1883, at the <a href="/wiki/History_and_traditions_of_Harvard_commencements" title="History and traditions of Harvard commencements">Commencement Day</a> dinner of Harvard alumni a letter was read<sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup> from "a generous benefactor, General <a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_James_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Samuel James Bridge (page does not exist)">Samuel James Bridge</a>, an adopted alumnus of the college":<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-MassHist_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MassHist-11">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[d]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">To the <a href="/wiki/President_and_Fellows_of_Harvard_College" title="President and Fellows of Harvard College">President and Fellows of Harvard College</a>.</span><br />
<i>Gentlemen,</i> — I have the pleasure of offering you an ideal statue in bronze, representing your founder, the Rev. John Harvard, to be designed by Daniel C.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>French of Concord<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... I am assured that the same can be in place by June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1, 1884.<sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Bridge specified an "ideal" statue because there was then (as now)<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-HMagJanFeb2000_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HMagJanFeb2000-3">[2]</a></sup> nothing to indicate what John Harvard had looked like; thus when French began work in September he used Harvard student <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Hoar" title="Sherman Hoar">Sherman Hoar</a> as inspiration for the figure's face. "In looking about for a type of <a href="/wiki/Pilgrims_(Plymouth_Colony)" title="Pilgrims (Plymouth Colony)">the early comers to our shores</a>," he wrote, "I chose a lineal descendant of them for my model in the general structure of the face. He has more of what I want than anybody I know."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-AZ_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AZ-20">[16]</a></sup> (Through his father <a href="/wiki/Ebenezer_Rockwood_Hoar" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebenezer Rockwood Hoar">Ebenezer Rockwood Hoar</a>‍—‌chairman<sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup> of Harvard's <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Board_of_Overseers" title="Harvard Board of Overseers">Board of Overseers</a>‍—‌Sherman Hoar was descended from a brother of Harvard's fourth president <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Hoar" title="Leonard Hoar">Leonard Hoar</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[e]</a></sup> as well as from <a href="/wiki/Roger_Sherman" title="Roger Sherman">Roger Sherman</a>, a signer of the Declaration of Independence and the U.S. Constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-ireland_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ireland-10">[9]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>The commission weighed heavily on French even as the figure neared completion. "I am sometimes scared by the importance of this work. It is a subject that one might not have in a lifetime," wrote the sculptor‍—‌who thirty years later would create the <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln_(1920_statue)" title="Abraham Lincoln (1920 statue)">statue of Abraham Lincoln</a> for the Lincoln Memorial‍—‌"and a failure would be inexcusable. As a general thing, my model looks pretty well to me, but there are dark days."<sup id="cite_ref-richman_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-richman-22">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>French's final model was ready the following May and realized in bronze by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry-Bonnard_Bronze_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Henry-Bonnard Bronze Company (page does not exist)">Henry-Bonnard Bronze Company</a> over the next several months. The cost was reportedly more than $20,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1984_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1984-23">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The statue was installed‍—‌"looking wistfully into the western sky", said Harvard president <a href="/wiki/Charles_William_Eliot" title="Charles William Eliot">Charles W. Eliot</a><sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup>‍—‌at the western end of Memorial Hall on the triangular city block then known as the Delta <span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">(see <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Hall_(Harvard_University)" title="Memorial Hall (Harvard University)">Memorial Hall</a>)</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-move_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-move-24">[19]</a></sup> At its October 15, 1884 unveiling<sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup> Ellis gave "a singularly felicitous address, telling the story of the life of John Harvard, who passes so mysteriously across the page of our early history."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-bacon_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bacon-25">[20]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Harvard_statue_old_site_Memorial_Hall_3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/John_Harvard_statue_old_site_Memorial_Hall_3.jpg/350px-John_Harvard_statue_old_site_Memorial_Hall_3.jpg" width="350" height="244" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/John_Harvard_statue_old_site_Memorial_Hall_3.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="348" /></a>

<center>Original site west of <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Hall_(Harvard_University)" title="Memorial Hall (Harvard University)">Memorial Hall</a></center>
</div>
</div>

<p>In 1920 French wrote<sup id="cite_ref-alumni_1924_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alumni_1924-26">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lowell_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lowell-27">[22]</a></sup> to Harvard president <a href="/wiki/Abbott_Lawrence_Lowell" title="Abbott Lawrence Lowell">Abbott Lawrence Lowell</a> desiring that the statue be relocated; in 1924<sup id="cite_ref-ST_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ST-5">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1984_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1984-23">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-move_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-move-24">[19]</a></sup> it was moved from Memorial Hall (then the college dining hall‍—‌a <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Lampoon" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Lampoon">Harvard Lampoon</a></i> drawing showed <i>John Harvard</i> dismounting his <a href="/wiki/Plinth" title="Plinth">plinth</a>, chair in tow, and holding his nose because he "couldn't stand the smell of 'Mem' any longer")<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to its current location on the west side of Harvard Yard's <a href="/wiki/University_Hall_(Harvard_University)" title="University Hall (Harvard University)">University Hall</a>, facing <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Hall_(Harvard_University)" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Hall (Harvard University)">Harvard Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Hall_(Harvard_University)" title="Massachusetts Hall (Harvard University)">Massachusetts Hall</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Gate" title="Johnston Gate">Johnston Gate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bunting_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bunting-28">[f]</a></sup> Later that year the <i>Lampoon</i> imagined the frustrations of the metallic and immobile <i>John Harvard</i> surrounded by Harvard under­graduates—<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1984_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1984-23">[18]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>Great men arise<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>/<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Before my eyes<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>/<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>From yonder pile I founded</i><br />
<i>While I must sit<span class="nowrap"><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>/<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span></span>Quite out of it<span class="nowrap"><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>/<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span></span>My jealousy unbounded</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>—though twelve years later <a href="/wiki/David_McCord" title="David McCord">David McCord</a> portrayed the founder as satisfied in his stationarity:<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-bethell_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bethell-29">[23]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><i>"Is that you, John Harvard?"<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>/<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>I said to his statue.</i><br />
<i>"Aye, that's me," said John,<span class="nowrap"><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>/<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span></span>"And after you're gone."</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Sometime in the 1990s tour guides began encouraging visitors to emulate a "student tradition"‍—‌nonexistent‍—‌of rubbing the toe of <i>John Harvard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s left shoe for luck, so that while the statue as a whole is darkly weathered the toe now "gleams almost throbbingly bright, as though from an excruciating inflammation of the bronze."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-rubforluck_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rubforluck-30">[g]</a></sup> It is, however, traditional for seniors, as they process to graduation exercises on Commencement Day (<span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">see <a href="/wiki/History_and_traditions_of_Harvard_commencements" title="History and traditions of Harvard commencements">History and traditions of Harvard commencements</a></span>), to remove their caps as they pass.<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1984_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1984-23">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-regalia_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-regalia-31">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The statue is depicted on the United States Postal Service's 1986 John Harvard stamp (part of its <a href="/wiki/Great_Americans_series" title="Great Americans series">Great Americans series</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-stamp_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-32">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seals_and_inscriptions">Seals and inscriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(statue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seals and inscriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JohnHarvardShinyFoot24June07.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/JohnHarvardShinyFoot24June07.jpg/260px-JohnHarvardShinyFoot24June07.jpg" width="260" height="380" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/JohnHarvardShinyFoot24June07.jpg/390px-JohnHarvardShinyFoot24June07.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/JohnHarvardShinyFoot24June07.jpg/520px-JohnHarvardShinyFoot24June07.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1574" data-file-height="2303" /></a>

<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:23em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F5F6CE;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>The facts as to John Harvard's relation to the found­ing of the Col­lege<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... are entirely compati­ble with the inscription on John Harvard's statue. There is no myth to be destroyed.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;">Jerome Davis Greene<sup id="cite_ref-Greene_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greene-34">[h]</a></sup></cite></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lincoln_statue.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Lincoln_statue.jpg/260px-Lincoln_statue.jpg" width="260" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Lincoln_statue.jpg/390px-Lincoln_statue.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Lincoln_statue.jpg/520px-Lincoln_statue.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1112" data-file-height="1004" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lincoln_statue.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln_(1920_statue)" title="Abraham Lincoln (1920 statue)">French's 1920 <i>Abraham Lincoln</i></a>. <a href="/wiki/Laurel_Ulrich" class="mw-redirect" title="Laurel Ulrich">Laurel Ulrich</a> notes that "statues of great men are not often seated".<sup id="cite_ref-ireland_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ireland-10">[9]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The monument's six-foot<sup id="cite_ref-HarvIllus1899_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarvIllus1899-16">[14]</a></sup> granite <a href="/wiki/Plinth" title="Plinth">plinth</a> is by Boston architect <a href="/wiki/C._Howard_Walker" class="mw-redirect" title="C. Howard Walker">C.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Howard Walker</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1984_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1984-23">[18]</a></sup> On its southern side (the side to the viewer's right), in bronze, is the seal of John Harvard's alma mater, the University of Cambridge's <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_College,_Cambridge" title="Emmanuel College, Cambridge">Emmanuel College</a>; on the northern side is what Ellis called "that most felicitously chosen of all like <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/device#English-heraldry" class="extiw" title="wikt:device">devices</a>, the three open books and the <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/veritas#Latin-truth" class="extiw" title="wikt:veritas">veritas</a></i> of Harvard. The pupil of the one institution was the founder of the other, transferring learning from its foreign home to this once wilderness scene."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-MassHist_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MassHist-11">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[i]</a></sup> On the rear are the words <small><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">GIVEN BY<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>&#160;• <span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span>SAMUEL JAMES BRIDGE<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>&#160;•  <span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span>JUNE<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>17, 1883</span></small>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[j]</a></sup></p>
<p>The face of the plinth is inscribed (in letters originally <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilt</a>)<sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup> <small><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;"><span class="nowrap">JOHN HARVARD<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>&#160;•</span> <span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span style="white-space:nowrap"> </span><span class="nowrap">FOUNDER<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>&#160;•</span> 1638</span></small>‍—‌words "hardly read before some smartass guide breezily informs the unsuspecting visitor that this is, after all, the 'Statue of the Three Lies<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>" (as Douglas Shand-Tucci put it)<sup id="cite_ref-ST_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ST-5">[4]</a></sup> because (as is ritually related to freshmen and <span class="nowrap">visitors):<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-toes_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toes-12">[11]</a></sup></span></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>the statue is not a likeness of <small><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">JOHN HARVARD</span></small>;</li>
<li>it was the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_General_Court" title="Massachusetts General Court">Great and General Court of the Massachu­setts Bay Colony</a>‍—‌not John Harvard‍—‌which first voted "to give <span class="nowrap">400£</span> towards a schoale or Colledge", preempting any claim for John Harvard as <small><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">FOUNDER</span></small>; and</li>
<li>the Court's vote came in 1636, not in the inscription's <small><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">1638</span></small>‍—‌the latter being merely the year of John Harvard's bequest to the school.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>However (Shand-Tucci continues) "the idea of the three lies is at best a fourth, and by far the greater falsehood,"<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-ST_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ST-5">[4]</a></sup> as detailed in a 1934 letter to the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Harvard_Crimson" title="The Harvard Crimson">Harvard Crimson</a></i> from the secretary of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Corporation">Harvard Corporation</a> and director of the school's then-upcoming <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Tercentenary_celebration" title="Harvard Tercentenary celebration">Tercenten­ary Celebration</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The facts as to John Harvard's relation to the founding of the College are not at all in dispute nor can it be said that the statue in front of University Hall does any violence to them. No likeness of John Harvard having been preserved, the statue [is an "ideal" representa­tion].</p>
<p>If the founding of a university must be dated to a split second of time, then the founding of Harvard should perhaps be fixed by the fall of the president's gavel in announcing the passage of the vote of October 28, 1636 <i>[see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Harvard_University" title="History of Harvard University">History of Harvard University</a>].</i> But if the founding is to be regarded as a process rather than as a single event [then John Harvard, by virtue of his bequest "at the very threshold of the College's existence and going further than any other contribution made up to that time to ensure its permanence"] is clearly entitled to be considered a founder. The General Court<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... acknowledged the fact by bestowing his name on the College.</p>
<p>These are all familiar facts and it is well that they should be understood by the sons of Harvard. They are entirely compatible with the inscription on John Harvard's statue. There is no myth to be destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-Greene_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greene-34">[h]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pranks">Pranks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(statue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pranks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The work became the target of pranks soon after its unveiling.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1884_tarring">1884 tarring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(statue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1884 tarring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1884 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Harvard_Crimson" title="The Harvard Crimson">The Harvard Crimson</a></i> reported that, "Some ingenious persons covered the John Harvard statue last night with a coat of tar. The same persons presum­ably, marked a large '87 on the wall at the entrance of the chapel,"<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[28]</a></sup> and in 1886 the <i>Crimson</i> mentions a further incident: "A graduate contribu­tor to the Advocate suggests that the editors of the college papers ferret out the authors of the small disturbances, such as the painting of the John Harvard statue."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1890_painting">1890 painting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(statue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1890 painting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following a May 31, 1890 Harvard athletic victory, front-page headlines in the <i>Boston Morning Globe</i> declared: "Vandalism at Harvard; statue of John Harvard and college buildings daubed with red paint by drunken students; seniors and faculty indig­nant<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... Riotous Mob Ruled the Campus."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The next day the <i>Globe</i> further reported that a Harvard student observing graffiti-removal efforts "declared that no Harvard man ever daubed the impious phrase, 'To <span class="nowrap">h—l</span> with Yale.' He was of the opinion that a Harvard man would at least soften the profanity by varnishing it with Latin or Greek<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... Two detectives who were requested to ferret out the perpetrators paid little heed to the discussion on swear words, but kept their eyes on several impressions that had been made on the paint when it was fresh. One thought they were made by a dog's paws, and as several students kept dogs the suspicion was magnified to the importance of a clue. A student, however, told the detectives that according to his view the impressions were made by barefoot boys walking on tip-toe."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Out-of-state newspapers reporting the outrage, and to a greater or lesser degree following the subsequent investigation, included (among many others):</p>
<ul>
<li><i>The World</i> (New York, New York; June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>2, p.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>2): "A Jocular Outrage<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>— Harvard Students Exceed Decency in Celebrating."</li>
<li><i>Evening Gazette</i> (Sterling, Illinois; June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>2, p.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>4): "Harvard Students on an Outra­geous Tear.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>— Slathers of Red Paint Used.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>— The Fine Statue of the College Founder Ruined by the Crazy <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/scapegrace#reckless_person" class="extiw" title="wikt:scapegrace">Scapegraces</a>."</li>
<li><i>Fort Wayne Sentinel</i> (Fort Wayne, Indiana; June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>2, p.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>5): "The faculty will expel the criminals and persecute [<i>sic</i>] them if found."</li>
<li><i>The Philadelphia Record</i>: "Painted Harvard Red<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>— Disgraceful Antics of Rum-Crazed Students.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>— Cambridge is Horri­fied.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>— The Faculty Bent on Venge­ance<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... Last night the whole college celebrated a wild orgie [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>]<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... There were suppers, bonfires, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fish-horn" class="extiw" title="wikt:fish-horn">fish-horns</a> and a general pandemo­ni­um; but, save the insane acts of two of the students, who, overcome with enthusi­asm, deliber­ately threw their dress coats into the bonfire while dancing around the blaze, no great overt act was then commit­<span class="nowrap">ted ...</span> It was during the small hours that the vandals were <span class="nowrap"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/abroad#English-unconfined" class="extiw" title="wikt:abroad">abroad</a> ...</span> [<i>John Harvard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s] face, hands, books, and shoes were bright crimson, and his clothes striped like a zebra."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-record_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-record-42">[32]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Despite a mass meeting of outraged Harvard men (who insisted the culprits must be outsid­ers or, failing that, freshmen), the hiring of detec­tives, and an apparently facetious report that Harvard President <a href="/wiki/Charles_W._Eliot" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles W. Eliot">Charles W. Eliot</a> was unavaila­ble for comment because he had "gone out in the woods to cut <a href="/wiki/Switch_(rod)" class="mw-redirect" title="Switch (rod)">switches</a>" (all <i>Globe,</i> June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>3), on June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>22 an anonymous contribu­tor (<i>Globe</i>, p.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>20) intimated that while "the faculty claim that they have not found out any of the men who did the 'fine art' work<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... I saw the ringleader on class day showing two very pretty girls around the 'yard'."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_incidents">Other incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(statue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 1934 Harvard athletes were suspected in the disappearance of <a href="/wiki/Yale" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale">Yale</a>'s "ugly bulldog mascot", <a href="/wiki/Handsome_Dan" title="Handsome Dan">Handsome Dan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-times_honors_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times_honors-43">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard_alumni_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_alumni-45">[35]</a></sup> The dog was recovered a few days later, though not before the <i>Harvard Lampoon</i> had photographed him licking <i>John Harvard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s boots,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[37]</a></sup> which had been smeared with hamburger.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard_alumni_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard_alumni-45">[35]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Man_bites_dog_(journalism)" title="Man bites dog (journalism)">"Dog licks man"</a>, a <i>Crimson</i> headline read.)<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>"Some years ago some students painted [the statue] <a href="/wiki/Crimson" title="Crimson">crimson</a> and our cops caught them red-handed", Deputy Chief of the Harvard University Police Jack W. Morse told <i>The Harvard Crimson</i> in 1984, adding "I've been waiting a long time to use that."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1984_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1984-23">[18]</a></sup> (Crimson is Harvard's school color.)<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the work's hundredth anniversary approached, <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Lampoon" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Lampoon">Harvard Lampoon</a></i> president <a href="/wiki/Conan_O%27Brien" title="Conan O'Brien">Conan O'Brien</a> predicted that "we'll probably stuff it with cottage cheese, maybe also with some chives."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-Crimson1984_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson1984-23">[18]</a></sup> "I think it’s creative but I wish students would direct their creative energies elsewhere," a Harvard maintenance official said in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-crime_2002_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crime_2002-50">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="&quot;Idealization&quot;_dispute"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Idealization.22_dispute">"Idealization" dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Harvard_(statue)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: &quot;Idealization&quot; dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The challenge of creating an idealized represen­ta­tion of John Harvard was discussed by Ellis at the October 1883 meeting of the Massachu­setts Historical Society:<sup id="cite_ref-MassHist_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MassHist-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>A very exacting demand is to be made upon the genius and skill of the artist<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... The work must be wholly ideal, guided by a few suggestive hints, all of which are in harmony with grace, delicacy, dignity, and reverential regard. There is necessarily much that is unsatisfactory in a wholly idealized represen­ta­tion by art of an historical person of whose form, features, and <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lineament#English-facial" class="extiw" title="wikt:lineament">lineaments</a> there are no certifications. But the few facts [known with certainty] concerning Harvard are certainly helpful to the artist.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>But Society president <a href="/wiki/Robert_Charles_Winthrop" title="Robert Charles Winthrop">Robert Charles Winthrop</a> harshly disapproved:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It must be altogether a fancy sketch, a <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Tragedy_of_Hamlet,_Prince_of_Denmark/Act_3#counterfeit_presentment" class="extiw" title="s:The Tragedy of Hamlet, Prince of Denmark/Act 3">'counterfeit presentment,'</a>‍—‌to use Shake­speare's phrase,‍—‌and in more senses of the word than one<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... [S]uch attempts to make portrait statues of those of whom there are not only no portraits, but no records or recollec­tions, are of very doubtful desirea­ble­ness<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... Such a course tends to the confusing and confounding of historic truth and leaves posterity unable to decide what is authentic and what is mere inven­tion<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... It seems to me of very questionable expediency to get up a fictitious likeness of him and make up a figure according to our ideas of the man.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>A year later, in his oration<sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup> before the unveiling of what he called "a <i><a href="/wiki/Simulacrum" title="Simulacrum">simula­crum</a></i><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... a conception of what Harvard might have been in body and lineament, from what we know that he was in mind and in soul", Ellis answered Winthrop's criticism:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This exquisite moulding in bronze serves a purpose for the eye, the thought, and sentiment, through the ideal, in lack of the real<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... It is by no means without allowed and approved precedent, that, in the lack of authentic portraitures of such as are to be commemorated, an ideal representa­tion supplies the vacancy of a reality. It is one of the fair issues between poetry and prose.</p>
<p>The wise, the honored, the fair, the noble, and the saintly, are never grudged some finer touches of the artist in tint or feature, which etherialize their beauty, or magnify their elevation, as expressed in the actual body,‍—‌the eye, the brow, the lip, the moulding of the mortal clay. To flatter is not always to falsify.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Should there ever appear, however,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>some authentic portrai­ture of John Harvard, the pledge may here and now be ventured, that some generous friend, such as, to the end, shall never fail our Alma Mater, notwithstanding her chronic poverty, will provide that this bronze shall be liquified again, and made to tell the whole known truth so as by fire.</p>
</blockquote>

<p><span class="reference-text">"If I remember aright," French was quoted in 1899 as saying, John Har­vard "is described as being 'rever­end, godly, and a lover of learn­ing,' and it is known that he died at an early age (about thirty) of <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/consumption#English-TB" class="extiw" title="wikt:consumption">consumption</a>, which gave a clue to his phy­sique." (French's daughter wrote<sup id="cite_ref-ST_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ST-5">[4]</a></sup> of the figure's "beautiful, wasted hand<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... the hands were thin and nervous"; Shand-Tucci<sup id="cite_ref-ST_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ST-5">[4]</a></sup> mentions the "scrawny calves.") French continued, "It may possibly be of interest that my regular model for the statue, except the face, was a young Englishman, a graduate of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a>, who was temporar­ily embarrassed financially and took this means of earning his bread."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-HarvIllus1899_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarvIllus1899-16">[14]</a></sup></span></p>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-19"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-19">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Hodges_Choate" title="Joseph Hodges Choate">Joseph Hodges Choate</a>, presiding at the dinner, "referred to the giver as 'a pious worshipper at Harvard's shrine, turning his face towards Mecca;' and, when the letter was read, the applause of the company compelled Mr. Bridge to make a silent acknowledgement".<sup class="reference nowrap"><a href="#M">[M]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-21"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-21">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Nourse, Henry Stedman (1899). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~malchist/mypage40.htm"><i>The Hoar Family in America</i></a>.</cite><span title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AJohn+Harvard+%28statue%29&amp;rft.aufirst=Henry+Stedman&amp;rft.aulast=Nourse&amp;rft.btitle=The+Hoar+Family+in+America&amp;rft.date=1899&amp;rft.genre=book&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Ffreepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com%2F~malchist%2Fmypage40.htm&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook" class="Z3988"><span style="display:none;">&#160;</span></span> (See family tree at end of transcription.) "Leonard Hoar, designated in his father's will to be the scholar of the family and a teacher in the church," became in 1672 the first Harvard president to have also been a Harvard graduate. "In <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Sewall" title="Samuel Sewall">Sewall's Diary</a>, June<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>15, 1674, is an account of the flogging of an undergraduate before the assembled students in the Library, President Hoar prefacing and closing the exercises with prayer. But this was not a very unusual discipline in those days and Dr. Hoar is not charged with undue severity."</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-bunting-28"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-bunting_28-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Bunting, Bainbridge. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=nlaE3-XfmJwC"><i>Harvard: An Architectural History</i></a>. p.&#160;290, n.14:</cite><span title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AJohn+Harvard+%28statue%29&amp;rft.aufirst=Bainbridge&amp;rft.aulast=Bunting&amp;rft.btitle=Harvard%3A+An+Architectural+History&amp;rft.genre=book&amp;rft.pages=290%2C+n.14&amp;rft_id=https%3A%2F%2Fbooks.google.com%2Fbooks%3Fid%3DnlaE3-XfmJwC&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook" class="Z3988"><span style="display:none;">&#160;</span></span> "The transfer of the statue from its original site on the Delta to a position on axis with Charles McKim's Johnston Gate was intended to give a sense of large-scale planning to the Yard and also to ameliorate the awkwardness of the central portion of Bulfinch's facade of University Hall."</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-rubforluck-30"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-rubforluck_30-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-rubforluck_30-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Though noting that "students <i>do</i> rub bronze body parts [including noses and 'pedal extremeties'] at many schools and colleges", and that Dean of Students <a href="/wiki/Archie_Epps" title="Archie Epps">Archie Epps</a> confessed to having once "insinuated himself into a group of tourists admiring the statue and whispered, 'I wonder if you'd get good luck if you rubbed his foot<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>", <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Magazine" title="Harvard Magazine">Harvard Magazine</a></i> attributed persistence of the Harvard rub-for-luck faux tradition to the "mythmaking" of tour guides, who "assure their flocks that undergraduates have <i>traditionally</i> rubbed John Harvard's foot for luck (before exams or a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mixer#English-socialevent" class="extiw" title="wikt:mixer">mixer</a>). They invite the tourists to do the same, and the tourists, being game and having paid their nickel, rub with gusto."</span>
<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div>
Based on the estimate of a professor of <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a> that "the shoe can endure 10 million rubs before it is utterly consumed", <i>Harvard Magazine</i> concluded that "the situation is grave": if 20,000 visitors per year each contrib­ute "three brisk rubs (conservative estimates, surely), in 166 years John's toes will be history."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-toes_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toes-12">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li id="cite_note-Greene-34"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-Greene_34-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-Greene_34-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Excerpted from <cite class="citation news"><a href="/wiki/Jerome_Davis_Greene" title="Jerome Davis Greene">Greene, Jerome Davis</a> (December 11, 1934). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.thecrimson.harvard.edu/article/1934/12/11/dont-quibble-sybll-ped-note-the-crimson/">"Don't Quibble Sybil — The Mail" (Letter to the editor)"</a>. <i>Harvard Crimson</i>.</cite><span title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AJohn+Harvard+%28statue%29&amp;rft.atitle=Don%27t+Quibble+Sybil+%E2%80%94+The+Mail%22+%28Letter+to+the+editor%29&amp;rft.aufirst=Jerome+Davis&amp;rft.aulast=Greene&amp;rft.date=1934-12-11&amp;rft.genre=article&amp;rft.jtitle=Harvard+Crimson&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.thecrimson.harvard.edu%2Farticle%2F1934%2F12%2F11%2Fdont-quibble-sybll-ped-note-the-crimson%2F&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal" class="Z3988"><span style="display:none;">&#160;</span></span> ("Don't quibble, Sybil" is a line from Noël Coward's 1930 <i><a href="/wiki/Private_Lives" title="Private Lives">Private Lives</a></i>.) Greene's scold to "the sons of Harvard" opens, "The quibble over the question whether John Harvard was entitled to be called the Founder of Harvard College seems to me one of the least profitable. The destruction of myths is a legitimate sport, but its only justification is the establish­ment of truth in place of error." Greene was responding to a November 26 <i>Crimson</i> item iconoclas­tically headlined <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.thecrimson.com/article/1934/11/26/memorial-society-honors-founder-of-college/">"Memorial Society Honors Founder of College In the Name and Image of Two Other Men<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>— College Founded By Grant of the Massachu­setts General Court in the Year 1636"</a>&#160;: "When the members of the Memorial Society place a wreath on the statue of John Harvard today, expecting to honor the memory and the image of the founder of Harvard College, they will be honoring the likeness of another man and the name of a man who was not the legal founder of the college."</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-36"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-36">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The modern design of the Harvard College seal features the syllables <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;"><span class="nowrap">ve&#160;•</span> <span class="nowrap">ri&#160;•</span> tas</span> ("truth") superimposed on three books opened face up, with their pages to the viewer. The seal on the plinth, however, is an earlier design (the "Quincy seal", itself based on a long-forgotten 17th-century design rediscovered by president <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Quincy_III" title="Josiah Quincy III">Josiah Quincy</a> in the 1830s)<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-seals_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seals-35">[27]</a></sup> in which the third book is opened face <i>down,</i> with the letters <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">tas</span> over cover, spine, cover.</span></li>

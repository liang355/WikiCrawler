<p><b>Ludwigshafen am Rhein</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈluːtvɪçsˌhafn̩ am ʁaɪ̯n]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Ludwigshafen_(Rhein).ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/53/De-Ludwigshafen_%28Rhein%29.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Ludwigshafen (Rhein).ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> opposite <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>. With Mannheim, <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> and the surrounding region, it forms the <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Neckar_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine Neckar Area">Rhine Neckar Area</a>.</p>
<p>Known primarily as an industrial city, Ludwigshafen is the home of chemical giant <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a>, as well as other companies. Among its cultural facilities are the <a href="/wiki/Staatsphilharmonie_Rheinland-Pfalz" title="Staatsphilharmonie Rheinland-Pfalz">Staatsphilharmonie Rheinland-Pfalz</a>. It is the birthplace of the former <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Chancellor" title="Chancellor">chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a> and the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Bloch" title="Ernst Bloch">Ernst Bloch</a>.</p>
<p>The city is a <a href="/wiki/Global_city" title="Global city">global city</a> with Sufficiency status.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">antiquity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celtic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> tribes settled in the Rhine Neckar area. During the 1st century B.C. the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> conquered the region, and a Roman <a href="/wiki/Auxiliaries_(Roman_military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Auxiliaries (Roman military)">auxiliary</a> fort was constructed near the present suburb of Rheingönheim.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> saw the foundation of some of Ludwigshafen's future suburbs, including <a href="/wiki/Oggersheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Oggersheim">Oggersheim</a>, Maudach, <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen-Oppau" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwigshafen-Oppau">Oppau</a> and Mundenheim; most of the area, however, remained swampland, its development hindered by seasonal flood of the Rhine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Rheinschanze">The Rheinschanze</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Rheinschanze">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RheinschanzeMannheim.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/RheinschanzeMannheim.jpg/220px-RheinschanzeMannheim.jpg" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/RheinschanzeMannheim.jpg/330px-RheinschanzeMannheim.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/RheinschanzeMannheim.jpg/440px-RheinschanzeMannheim.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1154" data-file-height="709" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RheinschanzeMannheim.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rheinschanze c. 1750</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Rhine Neckar region was part of the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-elector</a> of the <i>Kurpfalz</i>, or <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_the_Palatinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of the Palatinate">Electorate of the Palatinate</a>, one of the larger states within the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. The foundation of the new capital of the Kurpfalz, Mannheim, was a decisive influence on the development of the area as a whole. Parallel to the foundation of Mannheim in 1606, a fortress (<i>die Rheinschanze</i>) was built by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_IV,_Elector_Palatine" title="Frederick IV, Elector Palatine">Frederick IV, Elector Palatine</a> on the other side of the Rhine to protect the City of Mannheim, thus forming the nucleus of the city of Ludwigshafen itself.</p>
<p>In the 17th century the region was devastated and depopulated during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, and also in King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a>´s wars of conquest in the later part of the century.</p>
<p>It was only in the 18th century that the settlements around the Rheinschanze began to prosper, profiting from the proximity of the capital Mannheim. Oggersheim in particular gained some importance, after the construction of both a small palace serving as secondary residence for the Elector, and the famous pilgrimage church, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallfahrtskirche&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wallfahrtskirche (page does not exist)">Wallfahrtskirche</a>. For some weeks in 1782, the great German writer and playwright <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Friedrich Schiller</a> lived in Oggersheim, on flight from his native <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Württemberg">Württemberg</a>).</p>
<p>War returned to the Ludwigshafen area with the armies of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. The palace at Oggersheim was burned down, Mannheim besieged several times, and all the area west of the Rhine annexed by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> from 1798 to 1813. The <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_the_Palatinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of the Palatinate">Electorate of the Palatinate</a> was split up. The eastern bank of the Rhine with Mannheim and <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> was given to <a href="/wiki/Baden" title="Baden">Baden</a>, while the western bank (including the Ludwigshafen area) was granted to <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, following the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Sixth_Coalition" title="War of the Sixth Coalition">Wars of Liberation</a> (1813-1815), in which the French were expelled. The Rhine had become a frontier and the Rheinschanze, cut off politically from Mannheim, lost its function as the neighbouring city's military bulwark.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation_of_Ludwigshafen">Foundation of Ludwigshafen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foundation of Ludwigshafen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ludwig_I..jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Ludwig_I..jpg/220px-Ludwig_I..jpg" width="220" height="307" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Ludwig_I..jpg/330px-Ludwig_I..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Ludwig_I..jpg/440px-Ludwig_I..jpg 2x" data-file-width="551" data-file-height="768" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ludwig_I..jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig I of Bavaria">Ludwig I of Bavaria</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1808, during the French occupation, Carl Hornig of Mannheim purchased the fortress from the French authorities and turned it into a way station for passing river traffic. Later, the Rheinschanze with its winter-proof harbour basin (created by a flood in 1824) was used as trading post. Hornig died in 1819, but <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Heinrich_Scharpff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johann Heinrich Scharpff (page does not exist)">Johann Heinrich Scharpff</a>, a businessman from <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a>, continued Hornig's plans, which were then turned over to his son-in-law, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philipp_Markus_Lichtenberger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Philipp Markus Lichtenberger (page does not exist)">Philipp Markus Lichtenberger</a>, in 1830. Their activities marked the beginning of the civilian use of the Rheinschanze.</p>
<p>The year 1844 was the official birth of Ludwigshafen, when Lichtenberger sold this property to the state of Bavaria (Bayern), and the military title of the fortress was finally removed. The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavarian</a> king, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwig I">Ludwig I</a>, set forth plans to rename the settlement after himself and to start construction of an urban area as a Bavarian rival to Mannheim on the opposite bank.</p>
<p>During the failed German revolution of 1848 rebels captured Ludwigshafen, but they were bombarded from Mannheim (rumours said the Mannheimers didn't aim at the revolutionaries, but on the rival harbour's infrastructure), and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussian</a> troops quickly expelled the revolutionaries. On December 27, 1852, <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_II_of_Bavaria" title="Maximilian II of Bavaria">King Maximilian II</a> granted Ludwigshafen am Rhein political freedom and as on November 8, 1859, the settlement gained city status.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_and_growth_of_population">Industry and growth of population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industry and growth of population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1866.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1866.JPG/220px-BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1866.JPG" width="220" height="109" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1866.JPG/330px-BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1866.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1866.JPG/440px-BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1866.JPG 2x" data-file-width="525" data-file-height="260" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1866.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
BASF, 1866</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At its founding Ludwigshafen was still a very modest settlement with just 1,500 inhabitants. Real growth began with <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>, and gained enormous momentum in Ludwigshafen due to its ideal transport facilities. In addition to its excellent position and harbor facilities on the Rhine, a railway connecting Ludwigshafen with the <a href="/wiki/Saar_(river)" title="Saar (river)">Saar</a> coalfields was completed in 1849.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The year 1865 was an important date in the history of independent Ludwigshafen. After several discussions, <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> decided to move its factories from Mannheim to the <a href="/wiki/Hemshof" title="Hemshof">Hemshof</a> district, which belonged to Ludwigshafen. From then on, the city's rapid growth and wealth were linked to BASF's success and its expansion into becoming one of the world's most important chemical companies. Ludwigshafen also became home to several other rapidly growing chemical companies, including <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Raschig" title="Friedrich Raschig">Friedrich Raschig</a> GmbH, the <a href="/wiki/Reckitt_Benckiser#Benckiser" title="Reckitt Benckiser">Benckiser</a> company (founded by Johann Benckiser), Giulini Brothers, Grünzweig&amp;Hartmann AG, and the Knoll AG.</p>
<p>With more jobs available, the population of Ludwigshafen increased rapidly. In 1899 the city was governing more than 62,000 residents (compared to 1,500 in 1852).</p>
<p>This population explosion looked quite “American” to contemporaries; it determined Ludwigshafen's character as a “worker's city”, and created problematic shortages of housing and real estate. The solution was the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Municipal" class="mw-redirect" title="Municipal">municipal</a> area and the incorporation of the two nearest villages, Friesenheim and Mundenheim, in the years 1892 and 1899. In the area between the city centre and those two suburbs new quarters (“North” and “South”) were built after (then) modern urban development plans. Because the ground was marshy and too low to be protected from Rhine floods, all the new houses were built on raised ground, sometimes as high as 5 metres above the original ground. Visitors can see the original ground level in many backyards of Ludwigshafen, which are sometimes two floors below street level.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I_through_World_War_II">World War I through World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: World War I through World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> (1914-1918), Ludwigshafen's industrial plants played a key role in Germany's war economy, producing chemical ingredients for munitions, as well as much of the <a href="/wiki/Poison_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Poison gas">poison gas</a> used on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>. This contributed to Ludwigshafen having the dubious honour, on May 27, 1915, of being the target of the world's first strategic aerial bombardment. French aircraft attacked the BASF plants, killing twelve people and setting the precedent for the age to come.</p>
<p>When the war was lost by Germany in 1918, the left bank of the Rhine was occupied by French troops, in accordance with the terms of the peace agreement. The French occupation lasted until 1930, and some of Ludwigshafen's most elegant houses were erected for the officers of the French garrison.</p>
<p>The economic recovery of the 1920s was marred by one of the worst <a href="/wiki/Oppau_explosion" title="Oppau explosion">industrial explosions</a> in history when, on Sept. 21, 1921, a BASF storage silo in Oppau blew up, killing more than 500 people, injuring a further 2,000, and destroying countless buildings.</p>
<p>Despite this setback, Ludwigshafen reached a population of 100,000 in 1922, thus gaining “<a href="/wiki/Town_privileges" title="Town privileges">City</a>” status. It prospered until the worldwide economic crisis of 1929, which brought unemployment, labor trouble, political strife, and the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Nazis" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazis">Nazis</a>.</p>
<p>The Nazi party had few followers and votes in working-class-dominated Ludwigshafen, after 1933, when they had come to power in Germany, the Nazis succeeded in enforcing their policies in Ludwigshafen. Many small houses with gardens were built, especially in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gartenstadt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gartenstadt (page does not exist)">Gartenstadt</a>. Further, similar to Nazi plans in other cities (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>), they aimed at creating a ”Greater Ludwigshafen” by agglomerating smaller towns and villages in the vicinity. Thus Oggersheim, Oppau, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edigheim&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Edigheim (page does not exist)">Edigheim</a>, Rheingönheim, and Maudach became suburbs of Ludwigshafen, raising its population to 135,000. The Ludwigshafen <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> was destroyed in 1938 and its <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> population of 1,400 <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> was deported in 1940.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Oil_Campaign_of_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil Campaign of World War II">Oil Campaign of World War II</a>, the Allies conducted <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Ludwigshafen_and_Oppau_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Ludwigshafen and Oppau in World War II">bombing of Ludwigshafen and Oppau</a>. Thirteen thousand Allied bombers hit the city in 121 separate raids during the war, of which 56 succeeded in hitting the <a href="/wiki/IG_Farben" title="IG Farben">IG Farben</a> plant. Those 56 raids dropped 53,000 bombs each containing 250 to 4,000 pounds of high explosives, plus 2.5 million 4-pound magnesium incendiary bombs (the bombers also dropped millions of leaflets warning the civilians to evacuate the city, plus counterfeit ration coupons). Repairs took longer and longer as spare parts became more difficult to find. By December 1944, so much damage had been done to vital utilities that output dropped to nearly zero. Follow-up raids every week ended production permanently. By war's end most dwellings were destroyed or damaged; 1,800 people had died, and 3,000 were injured.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Allied ground advance into Germany reached Ludwigshafen in March 1945. The US <a href="/wiki/12th_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="12th Armored Division (United States)">12th Armored Division</a> and <a href="/wiki/94th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="94th Infantry Division (United States)">94th Infantry Division</a> captured Ludwigshafen against determined German resistance in house-to-house and block-to-block urban combat during 21–24 March 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_rebuilding">Post-war rebuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-war rebuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">Post-war</a>, Ludwigshafen was part of the French occupation zone, becoming part of the newly founded <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">Bundesland</a> (state) of Rheinland-Pfalz and thus part of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Republic of Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a>. Reconstruction of the devastated city and revival of the economy was supported by the Allies, especially by American aid. In 1948, the “Pasadena Shares Committee” sent packages of blankets, clothing, food, and medicines to help the residents of post-war Ludwigshafen. Many friendships started to form, so that in 1956, Ludwigshafen am Rhein and <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> became sister cities.</p>
<p>Large parts of the city were literally ruined, which were rebuilt in the architectural style of the 1950s and 1960s. The most important projects were the Hochstraßen (highways on stilts), the revolutionary new main station (then the most modern station in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>), several tower blocks and a whole new suburb, the satellite quarter <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pfingstweide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pfingstweide (page does not exist)">Pfingstweide</a> north of Edigheim.</p>
<p>The city's economic wealth allowed social benefits and institutions to be introduced. The population number reached its all-time climax in 1970 with more than 180,000 inhabitants, thus surpassing even the capital of Rheinland-Pfalz, <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>, for a while.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_crisis">Financial crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Financial crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1970s, a plan to reform the composition of the German Bundesländer, which would have created a new state around a united Mannheim-Ludwigshafen as capital with more than half a million inhabitants, failed.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, further ambitious projects were financed in Ludwigshafen, first of all the 15-floor city hall with its linked-up shopping centre (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rathaus_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rathaus Center (page does not exist)">Rathaus Center</a></i>). The last (up to now) new incorporated suburb was <a href="/wiki/Ruchheim" title="Ruchheim">Ruchheim</a> in 1974.</p>
<p>But then a process began that accelerated during the 1980s and 1990s and caused the financial near-collapse of Ludwigshafen. The enormous maintenance costs of the buildings and institutions introduced during the “fat time”, new tax regulations that cut down the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trade_tax&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Trade tax (page does not exist)">trade tax</a> profits from the local industries, and thousands of dismissals in BASF were the main causes for the city's crisis. Loss of population due to the loss of working places and general economic trends, such as the oil crises, further worsened Ludwigshafen's financial situation at the end of the 20th century.</p>
<p>The negative aspects of industrial success became obvious when examinations revealed the bad state of air and the Rhine due to <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>. There had always been some stench or dirt all over the city, caused by BASF and other plants, and as long as the industry had prospered, people had accepted it. Besides that, the concrete constructions that had been so modern after the war and had a formative influence on today's cityscape were increasingly considered as obsolete.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_Ludwigshafen">Contemporary Ludwigshafen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Contemporary Ludwigshafen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In recent years, many efforts have been made to enhance Ludwigshafen's image in the media. The city administration has cut down its deficit by cutting down social payments and maintenance, pollution has been (not least by BASF) restricted, the formerly rotten Hemshof quarter has been restored.</p>
<p>In 2008, a fire broke out in a building where many ethnic Turks lived.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> 9 people died, all of them Turks and 5 of them children. It was believed to be an arsonist attack, however this was found to be not true.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the most annoying faults<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of Ludwigshafen – at least for many of the city's inhabitants - was its comparative lack of high-quality shopping possibilities. It has attempted to repair this deficiency by creating a second large shopping mall on the southern tip of the city centre (the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walzm%C3%BChle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Walzmühle (page does not exist)">Walzmühle</a> near <a href="/w/index.php?title=Berliner_Platz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Berliner Platz (page does not exist)">Berliner Platz</a>) with affiliated railway station (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen-Mitte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ludwigshafen-Mitte (page does not exist)">Ludwigshafen-Mitte</a>). In addition, another shopping mall on the banks of the Rhine, the Rhein-Galerie, was completed in September 2010.</p>
<p>Ludwigshafen has enormous importance as an industrial city.</p>

<p>The city centre of Ludwigshafen is comparatively small and dominated by post-war buildings. Its northern and southern boundaries are the Hochstraßen (highways on stilts), the Rhine is in the East and the <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen_(Rhein)_Hauptbahnhof" title="Ludwigshafen (Rhein) Hauptbahnhof">main station</a> is located in the West of downtown Ludwigshafen, at a walking distance of about 15 minutes from the central pedestrian precinct <i>Bismarckstraße</i> that forms, together with the shopping mile <i>Ludwigsstraße</i>, the main North-South Axis, connecting the so-called “North Pole” with the <i>Rathaus Center</i> and the “South Pole” with Berliner Platz, the Walzmühle shopping centre and <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen_(Rhein)_Mitte_station" title="Ludwigshafen (Rhein) Mitte station">Ludwigshafen (Rhein) Mitte station</a>. The main East-West connections are the <i>Bahnhofsstraße</i> and <i>Kaiser-Wilhelm-Straße</i>. The Pfalzbau, Staatsphilharmonie, Wilhelm-Hack-Museum and the half-destroyed monument <a href="/wiki/Lutherkirche,_Wiesbaden" title="Lutherkirche, Wiesbaden">Lutherkirche</a> are main features of downtown Ludwigshafen.</p>

<p>The <i>Südliche Innenstadt</i> or “southern city centre” (ca. 29,000 inhabitants) includes the real city centre as described above and the <i>Stadtteil Süd</i> or “South” quarter. “South” has some of the most attractive<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (February 2014)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> residential areas, especially the <i>Parkinsel</i> area. Other sub-quarters of “South” are the <i>Musikantenviertel</i> or the <i>Malerviertel</i>. In a few years, there will be one more highly prized residential area (“<i>Rheinufer Süd</i>”) on the Rhine near the Walzmühle on former industrial estates.</p>

<p>The <i>Nördliche Innenstadt</i> (ca. 22,000 inhabitants) includes the Hemshof, “North” and “West” districts. Hemshof and “North” represent the “old town” of Ludwigshafen, they are known for their very high proportion of foreign inhabitants, making them culturally diverse. ”West” (also called <i>Valentin-Bauer-Siedlung</i>) is located between main station and main cemetery.</p>

<p><i>Friesenheim</i> (ca. 18,000 inhabitants) is located north of Hemshof and is one of the two (the other one being Mundenheim) “mother villages” of Ludwigshafen, because they were responsible for the administration of Ludwigshafen prior to its independence. Helmut Kohl was born in Friesenheim. Its western district, the <i>Froschlache</i>, boasts four impressive tower blocks.</p>

<p><i>Oppau</i> (ca. 10,000 inhabitants) in the North is dominated by the nearby BASF and had once been a town of its own prior to its incorporation into Ludwigshafen. In its history, it has been afflicted by several catastrophes like the explosion of 1921 and the flood of 1882.</p>

<p><i>Oggersheim</i> (ca. 23,000 inhabitants) is one of the most important suburbs, being much like a town for itself (which it was in the Middle Ages). <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a> owned a bungalow in southern Oggersheim. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Tramway from the 60`s in regular service</div>

<p>Although Ludwigshafen itself has no airfield, it is well connected with several airports in the region. There are small airfields near <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bad_D%C3%BCrkheim" title="Bad Dürkheim">Bad Dürkheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a>, a medium-sized regional <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a> in Mannheim and the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt International Airport</a> in about an hour driving distance.</p>
<p>Ludwigshafen is the most important German harbour west of the Rhine. The local industry depends on shipping their raw materials and products on the river. The harbour of Ludwigshafen consists of several basins in the South of the city near Mundenheim (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Luitpoldhafen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Luitpoldhafen (page does not exist)">Luitpoldhafen</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaiserw%C3%B6rthhafen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kaiserwörthhafen (page does not exist)">Kaiserwörthhafen</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mundenheimer_Altrheinhafen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mundenheimer Altrheinhafen (page does not exist)">Mundenheimer Altrheinhafen</a>), the wharfs along the river parallel to the city centre and the BASF, and, finally, of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landeshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Landeshafen (page does not exist)">Landeshafen</a> basin in the North that connects the BASF.</p>
<p>Ludwigshafen has excellent <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> (motorway/highway) connections to all directions. Most important are the A 650 in West-East direction, the A 61 in North-South direction. But there are also A 6, A 65 and B 9 to be mentioned.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen_Hauptbahnhof" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwigshafen Hauptbahnhof">Ludwigshafen Hauptbahnhof</a> is a huge station, its impressive pylon bridge pier serving as the city's landmark. The extraordinary architecture of the station complex is caused by the need to connect three joining tracks (to <a href="/wiki/Frankenthal" title="Frankenthal">Frankenthal</a>/Worms/Mainz, to <a href="/wiki/Neustadt_an_der_Weinstra%C3%9Fe" title="Neustadt an der Weinstraße">Neustadt</a>/Speyer and to Mannheim) and to work in the underground <a href="/wiki/Stra%C3%9Fenbahn" class="mw-redirect" title="Straßenbahn">Straßenbahn</a> station and the massive road bridge above the concourse. Other railway stations are at Oggersheim, Mundenheim, Rheingönheim, and, the new more central <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen_(Rhein)_Mitte_station" title="Ludwigshafen (Rhein) Mitte station">Ludwigshafen (Rhein) Mitte</a>, near Berliner Platz. Since 2003, the <a href="/wiki/S-Bahn_Rhein-Neckar" class="mw-redirect" title="S-Bahn Rhein-Neckar">S-Bahn Rhein-Neckar</a> suburban train system runs successfully in the region.</p>
<p>Ludwigshafen's public transport system is run by the VBL (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Verkehrsbetriebe_Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Verkehrsbetriebe Ludwigshafen (page does not exist)">Verkehrsbetriebe Ludwigshafen</a>) and the holding companies <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Neckar-Verkehr" title="Rhein-Neckar-Verkehr">Rhein-Neckar-Verkehr</a> (RNV) and VRN. There is an integrated <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Mannheim/Ludwigshafen" title="Trams in Mannheim/Ludwigshafen">Mannheim/Ludwigshafen tramway network</a>; four tram lines (4,6,7,8) cross the Rhine bridges between the two cities and one other line (10) runs through Ludwigshafen only. In late 2008 Line 4 was extended and replaced Line 14 (also known as "Rhein-Haardt-Bahn"). Line 4 now serves as a long-distance line, which runs from Bad Dürkheim to Ludwigshafen, Mannheim and <a href="/wiki/Heddesheim" title="Heddesheim">Heddesheim</a>. The bus network consists of about ten municipal lines and further regional lines.</p>
<p>A rather strange feature of Ludwigshafen's public transport system is the existence of four underground tram stations (Rathaus, Danziger Platz (closed since late 2008), Hauptbahnhof, Hemshofstraße). They go back to the 1970s, when a common underground network in Mannheim and Ludwigshafen was planned. The rash construction of these first stations in Ludwigshafen became superfluous when Mannheim cancelled the project due to its enormous costs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Region_and_neighbours">Region and neighbours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Region and neighbours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The twin cities of Mannheim and Ludwigshafen closely cooperate in many areas; although they are separated by the <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>/Rhineland Palatinate boundary, this frontier is mainly an administrative one. Many Ludwigshafeners shop and go out in Mannheim's inner city, as it is within easy reach. In the reverse case, some Mannheimers work in Ludwigshafen and many <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mannheim" title="University of Mannheim">University of Mannheim</a> students choose Ludwigshafen as residence because of its cheaper rents.</p>
<p>The surroundings of Ludwigshafen on the left bank of the Rhine are called Pfalz and are the easternmost part of the <a href="/wiki/Palatinate_(region)" title="Palatinate (region)">Palatinate region</a>. The administrative district around Ludwigshafen is called <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Pfalz-Kreis" title="Rhein-Pfalz-Kreis">Rhein-Pfalz-Kreis</a>. North of Ludwigshafen, there is the industrial town of Frankenthal. In the western vicinity of Ludwigshafen, there are several villages producing enormous amounts of vegetables, thus securing the Rheinpfalz the title of “Germany's vegetable garden”. The district south of Ludwigshafen is dominated by the Rhine and the <a href="/wiki/Altrhein" class="mw-redirect" title="Altrhein">Altrhein</a> arms (lakes marking the earlier course of the river) and the ancient town of <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a> with its magnificent imperial cathedral, a noteworthy and remarkable city.</p>
<p>The regions with some more distance to Ludwigshafen include the beautiful <a href="/wiki/German_Wine_Route" title="German Wine Route">German Wine Route</a> region with Germany's biggest coherent winegrowing area and the <a href="/wiki/Palatinate_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Palatinate forest">Palatinate forest</a>, the biggest coherent forest of Europe 50 kilometres (31 miles) in the West, the French region <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a> and the German Schwarzwald (<a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a>) hills in the South, <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Odenwald" title="Odenwald">Odenwald</a> hills in the East and the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Rhine_Main_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt Rhine Main Area">Rhein-Main</a> region with the city of Frankfurt about 100 kilometres (62 miles) in the North.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pfalzbau" title="Pfalzbau">Pfalzbau</a> is a theatre and concert hall with regional importance. The Staatsphilharmonie Rheinland-Pfalz keeps its own <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_orchestra" class="mw-redirect" title="Symphonic orchestra">symphonic orchestra</a>, and there is a production company that stages operas 25 nights per year.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the Hemshof district, there are smaller theatres playing regional dialect plays.</p>
<p>The Wilhelm-Hack-Museum is the municipal art museum, with collections spanning from ancient to contemporary art. It is known for the emblematic <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Joan Miró">Miró</a> mural covering an entire façade, called the "<a href="/wiki/Mir%C3%B3_Wall" title="Miró Wall">Miró Wall</a>" (Miró-Wand in German). The mural is a work of art by the Spanish artist <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Joan Miró">Joan Miró</a>, with the collaboration of his long-time colleague, the ceramist, <a href="/wiki/Joan_Gardy_Artigas" title="Joan Gardy Artigas">Joan Gardy Artigas</a>, and is made of 7,200 ceramic tiles. It has been subject to degradation due to air pollution since it was installed in 1979.</p>
<p>Several small museums in Ludwigshafen focus on the city's history, first of all the Stadtmuseum in the <i>Rathaus Center</i>, but also the Schillerhaus Oggersheim, K.O. Braun-Museum in Oppau or the Frankenthaler Kanal Museum in the North.</p>
<p>The Fachhochschule Ludwigshafen (technical college) specialises in economics and has an affiliated Ostasieninstitut (East Asia Institute). There is also the Evangelische Fachhochschule Ludwigshafen, specialising in social sciences.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> is by far the most important industrial company in Ludwigshafen, there are many other firms. Trade and industry in Ludwigshafen have about 90,000 employees in total, with an annual total turnover of nearly 17 billion euros.</p>
<p>BASF is the world's leading chemical company, employing 110,000 people at all and about 35,000 (a few years ago, the employee total was about 55,000) of them in the Ludwigshafen plant, which is also the largest chemical plant in the world. The company's main products are fertilizers, dye, coolants and many other chemical substances.. Among the other chemical companies with plants in Ludwigshafen rank BK Giulini, <a href="/wiki/Abbott_Laboratories" title="Abbott Laboratories">Abbott</a>, Raschig and <a href="/wiki/Reckitt_%26_Colman" class="mw-redirect" title="Reckitt &amp; Colman">Benckiser</a>. Other important branches of industry are mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, IT and brewery (Mayerbräu Oggersheim).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Ludwigshafen is one of the German cities that has never had a professional football club. This is all the more surprising, because Ludwigshafen is a typical "workers' city" and has quite a large stadium, the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCdweststadion" title="Südweststadion">Südweststadion</a>, built from debris from World War II with a capacity of around 40,000. Several international matches and some <a href="/wiki/Fu%C3%9Fball-Bundesliga" class="mw-redirect" title="Fußball-Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a> matches when <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Kaiserslautern" title="1. FC Kaiserslautern">1. FC Kaiserslautern</a> or <a href="/wiki/Waldhof_Mannheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Waldhof Mannheim">Waldhof Mannheim</a> used it as alternative stadium during the past decades have been held there.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Town_F.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Huddersfield Town F.C.">Huddersfield Town</a> left-back <a href="/wiki/Dominik_Werling" title="Dominik Werling">Dominik Werling</a> was born in Ludwigshafen.</p>
<p>Formerly the most successful Ludwigshafen football club was <a href="/wiki/FSV_Oggersheim" title="FSV Oggersheim">FSV Oggersheim</a>, whose team experienced short-term success when gaining promotion to the <a href="/wiki/Regionalliga" title="Regionalliga">Regionalliga</a> (3rd Division) at the end of the 2006-07 season. However, the club found itself outclassed, and as the financial situation grew worse after two poor seasons, the club withdrew to 11th tier local level play in 2010-11.</p>
<p>As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Arminia_Ludwigshafen" title="Arminia Ludwigshafen">Arminia Ludwigshafen</a> is the highest-classed football club from the city, competing in the <a href="/wiki/Oberliga_Rheinland-Pfalz/Saar" title="Oberliga Rheinland-Pfalz/Saar">Oberliga Rheinland-Pfalz/Saar</a> (V).</p>
<p>An athletics hall has been constructed near the Stadium a few years ago.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/TSG_Friesenheim" title="TSG Friesenheim">TSG Friesenheim</a> plays in the German 1st handball division since summer 2010.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature">Nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwigshafen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There are several municipal parks in Ludwigshafen: First of all the Ebertpark in the North quarter and Friesenheim. It was created for the South German Horticulture Exhibition in 1925 with the Friedrich-Ebert-Halle, a multi-purpose hall.</p>
<p>The official Stadtpark, or municipal park, is somewhat remote from the city centre (yet easy to reach by the #10 tram), because it is situated on the Parkinsel, or park island, on a bank of the Rhine.</p>
<p>The Friedenspark is closer to the city centre, being located just north of the main station and west of the city hall. It is the youngest of Ludwigshafen's parks, having been created on a former industrial area.</p>
<p>Further, there are numerous smaller parks that are just a bit larger than a towel in the suburbs, for example the Stadtpark Oggersheim, Riedsaumpark, Alwin-Mittasch-Platz and Friesenpark in Friesenheim, Stadtpark Oppau, Bürgerpark Pfingstweide or Zedtwitzpark Mundenheim.</p>
<p>The Maudacher Bruch in the West between Maudach, Gartenstadt and Oggersheim, is a very extensive, horse-shoe shaped area, including the Michaelsberg (126m), a mountain built of debris and wreckage after World War II. Due to excessive extraction of ground water from chemical companies the ground water level drops and the diversity of nature is no longer preserved. The Kief´scher Weiher in the South is connected with the Rhine and serves as yacht harbour, being surrounded by weekend camping areas.</p>

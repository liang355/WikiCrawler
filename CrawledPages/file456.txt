<p><b>Memmingen</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a> in <a href="/wiki/Swabia_(Bavaria)" title="Swabia (Bavaria)">Swabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, Germany. It is the economic, educational and administrative centre of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Danube-Iller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Danube-Iller (page does not exist)">Danube-Iller</a> region. To the west the town is flanked by the <a href="/wiki/Iller" title="Iller">Iller</a>, the river that marks the <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> border. To the north, east and south the town is surrounded by the district of <a href="/wiki/Unterallg%C3%A4u" title="Unterallgäu">Unterallgäu</a> (Lower Allgäu).</p>
<p>With about 42,000 inhabitants, Memmingen is the 5th biggest town in the administrative region of Swabia. The origins of the town go back to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. The old town, with its many courtyards, castles and patricians' houses, palaces and fortifications is one of the best preserved in southern Germany. With good transport links by road, rail and air, it is the transport hub for <a href="/wiki/Upper_Swabia" title="Upper Swabia">Upper</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Swabia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Central Swabia (page does not exist)">Central Swabia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Allg%C3%A4u" title="Allgäu">Allgäu</a>.</p>
<p>Due to its proximity to the <a href="/wiki/Allg%C3%A4u" title="Allgäu">Allgäu</a> region, Memmingen is often called the Gateway to the Allgäu (<i>Tor zum Allgäu</i>). The town motto is <i>Memmingen&#160;– Stadt mit Perspektiven</i> ("Memmingen - a town with perspectives"). In recent times it has been frequently referred to as <i>Memmingen&#160;– Stadt der Menschenrechte</i> (Memmingen - the town of human rights). This alludes to the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Articles_of_the_Black_Forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve Articles of the Black Forest">Twelve Articles</a>, considered to be the first written set of <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Europe" title="Human rights in Europe">human rights in Europe</a>, which were penned in Memmingen in 1525. Every four years there is the <i>Wallensteinfestspiel</i>, with about 4,500 participants, the biggest historical reenactment in Europe. It commemorates the invasion of <a href="/wiki/Wallenstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Wallenstein">Wallenstein</a> and his troops in 1630.</p>

<p>It is believed that on the site of present-day Memmingen in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> times there was a small military town, probably called <i>Cassiliacum</i>. In the 5th century an <a href="/wiki/Alemans" class="mw-redirect" title="Alemans">Alemanic</a> settlement was established and in the 7th century there was here a palace belonging to the king of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>.</p>
<p>Memmingen was linked to <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, Austria and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> by the <i>salt road</i> to <a href="/wiki/Lindau" title="Lindau">Lindau</a>. Another important route through Memmingen was the <i>Italian road</i> from Northern Germany to Switzerland and Italy. Both roads helped Memmingen gain importance as a trading centre. In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the place was known as <i>Mammingin</i>; in 1158 the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Welf" title="House of Welf">Welfian</a> Duke <a href="/wiki/Welf_VI" title="Welf VI">Welf VI</a> founded the town of Memmingen. In 1286 it became an <a href="/wiki/Free_Imperial_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Imperial City">Imperial City</a>, responsible only to the <i><a href="/wiki/Kaiser" title="Kaiser">Kaiser</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Christoph_Schappeler" title="Christoph Schappeler">Christoph Schappeler</a>, the preacher at St. Martin's in Memmingen during the early 16th century, was an important figure during the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War" title="German Peasants' War">German Peasants' War</a>. His support for peasant rights helped to draw peasants to Memmingen. The city first followed the <a href="/wiki/Tetrapolitan_Confession" title="Tetrapolitan Confession">Tetrapolitan Confession</a>, and then the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a>.</p>
<p><i>The Twelve Articles: The Just and Fundamental Articles of All the Peasantry and Tenants of Spiritual and Temporal Powers by Whom They Think Themselves Oppressed</i> was written (probably by Schappeler and <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Lotzer" title="Sebastian Lotzer">Sebastian Lotzer</a>) in early 1525. This was a religious petition borrowing from <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Luther's</a> ideas to appeal for peasant rights. Within two months of its publication in Memmingen, 25,000 copies of the tract were in circulation around Europe. These are the first known set of human rights documents in the world.</p>
<p>In the 1630s Memmingen was at centre stage during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, and the Imperial <a href="/wiki/Generalissimo" title="Generalissimo">generalissimo</a> <a href="/wiki/Wallenstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Wallenstein">Wallenstein</a> was quartered in the town when he was dramatically dismissed from service. From 1632 Memmingen was briefly garrisoned by the Swedish army, and became a base of operations for Swedish troops in <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabia</a>. In September 1647 the Imperialists besieged the Swedish garrison, under Colonel Sigismund Przyemski. Two months later the town surrendered.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the reorganization of Germany in 1802, Memmingen became part of Bavaria. The 19th century saw the slow economic deterioration of the town, which was halted only with the building of <a href="/wiki/Neu-Ulm%E2%80%93Kempten_railway" title="Neu-Ulm–Kempten railway">a railway</a> following the course of the River Iller.</p>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> Memmingen has been a developing town, with a rate of economic growth above the average for Bavaria.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_main_attractions">Culture and main attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Culture and main attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Every year Memmingen celebrates the <i>Fischertag</i> (Fisherman's day), recreating medieval traditions. Men who were born in Memmingen or live there for at least ten years, jump into the river that flows through the town and try to catch trout. Every four years Memmingen re-enacts the events around the visit of Wallenstein in 1630. The next Wallenstein Festival will occur during the summer of 2016.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The theatre has a long tradition in Memmingen. By the Middle Ages some chroniclers were already recording different theatre performances. In 1937 the <i>Landestheater Schwaben</i> (State Theatre of Swabia) or LTS was founded in the city. In 1945, after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the LTS was one of the first theatres in <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> to begin putting on performances again. The performances take place in the Rooms of the City Theatre, the theatre at the <i>Schweizerberg</i> (cabaret stage), in the <i>Kaminwerk</i> cultural centre or in rooms at the boroughs of Memmingen. The Schweizerberg Theatre will be closed at the end of 2010. It will move to new premises in the <i>Elsbethen</i> area, behind the City Theatre, where a new cabaret stage, rehearsing rooms, workshops, depots, management rooms, the foyer and some guest rooms will be built.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Another theatre was founded by Helmut Wolfseher and members of the <i>Alternative Kleinkunst e.V.</i> (Alternative Cabaret Society), <i>Parterretheater im Künerhaus (PIK)</i>. This theatre is specifically for amateur actors and young talented musicians. The <i>Kaminwerk</i> also puts on major plays by amateur actors. The municipal hall is for <i>Volksschauspieler</i> or other artists.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MeWoKunsthalle2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/MeWoKunsthalle2.jpg/220px-MeWoKunsthalle2.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/MeWoKunsthalle2.jpg/330px-MeWoKunsthalle2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/MeWoKunsthalle2.jpg/440px-MeWoKunsthalle2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3888" data-file-height="2592" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

MeWo-Kunsthalle (art hall)</div>

<p>The biggest museum in Memmingen is the Town Museum at the <i>Hermannsbau</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The town's history is described in its historical rooms. There is also a section covering the history of the Jewish community in Memmingen, whose members were killed or forced into exile in 1939. Part of the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> from the destroyed <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> is on display there. The <i><a href="/wiki/Brunt%C3%A1l" title="Bruntál">Freudenthal</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hrub%C3%BD_Jesen%C3%ADk" title="Hrubý Jeseník">Altvater</a> Homeland Museum</i> for refugees who have settled in Memmingen is also part of the town museum. It is one of 43 homeland museums recognised by the Ministry of the Interior. A foundation, founded and administered by the town, takes responsibility for the museum. The <i>Strigel-</i> and <i>Antoniter-Museum</i> at the <i>Antonierkloster</i> present wood carvings and paintings by the <i>Strigel</i> family of artists as well as a permanent exhibition on the work of the <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Brothers_of_St._Anthony" title="Hospital Brothers of St. Anthony">Hospital Brothers of St. Anthony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The museum was established in 1996. With donations from the <i>Memminger Wohnungsbaugenossenschaft (MeWo)</i> the <i>MEWO-Kunsthalle</i> was opened in 2005 in the old post office next to the station. The museum holds the estates of the Memmingen artists <a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Unold&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Max Unold (page does not exist)">Max Unold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Josef_Madlener" title="Josef Madlener">Josef Madlener</a> and presents a wide selection of contemporary art exhibitions.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The size and scope of this art gallery is unique in region. The former <i>Kreuzherren</i> monastery is used for changing exhibitions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The organ concerts in the churches of St. Martins and St. Josef are famous in the region. Chamber music would be performed in the former Kreuzherren monastery and also in some other buildings in Memmingen.</p>
<p>There are several Pubs, Restaurants, Wine taverns and Cafés and also some discothèques in an around the City. The cultural centre <i>Kaminwerk</i> (Chimney factory) is for concerts, theater, program cinema, readings and special parties.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Memmingen,_der_Ulmer_Tor_Dm%3DD-7-64-000-229_foto6_2014-07-28_14.36.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Memmingen%2C_der_Ulmer_Tor_Dm%3DD-7-64-000-229_foto6_2014-07-28_14.36.jpg/220px-Memmingen%2C_der_Ulmer_Tor_Dm%3DD-7-64-000-229_foto6_2014-07-28_14.36.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Memmingen%2C_der_Ulmer_Tor_Dm%3DD-7-64-000-229_foto6_2014-07-28_14.36.jpg/330px-Memmingen%2C_der_Ulmer_Tor_Dm%3DD-7-64-000-229_foto6_2014-07-28_14.36.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Memmingen%2C_der_Ulmer_Tor_Dm%3DD-7-64-000-229_foto6_2014-07-28_14.36.jpg/440px-Memmingen%2C_der_Ulmer_Tor_Dm%3DD-7-64-000-229_foto6_2014-07-28_14.36.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3281" data-file-height="4370" /></a>

<p>Memmingen has considerable tourist interest, mainly because large areas of the medieval old town survived World War II. There are ten city gates and towers and about two kilometres of the city wall. The old town contains many interesting houses of patricians, some in the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> style. They are picturesque Streets with the <i>Stadtbach</i> (town river) beside. The medieval market place, surrounded by the town hall, which is built in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a> style, the <i>Großzunft</i>(Guildhouse) and the painted <i>Steuerhaus</i> (tax house). Also famous is St. Martin's church, built in <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">gothic style</a> with its more-than-500-year-old <a href="/wiki/Choir_(architecture)" title="Choir (architecture)">Choir</a> and the 1996 restored <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Brothers_of_St._Anthony" title="Hospital Brothers of St. Anthony">Hospital Brothers of St. Anthony</a> monastery (<i>Antonierkloster</i>), the oldest, best conserved and biggest of these kind. The probably oldest church in town <i>Unser Frauen</i> (Church of Our Lady) or also called <i>Frauenkirche</i> with significant frescos of the 15th and 16th centuries. Also the <i>Seven Roof House</i>, the baroque <i>Kreuzherren</i> monastery, the renovated whorehouse of the city, the <i>Salzstadel</i> (salt barn), the <i>Kramerzunft</i> (shopkeepers guild, also called the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Articles_of_the_Black_Forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve Articles of the Black Forest">Twelve-Article-House</a> are sights in Memmingen). Not so well known is the <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_tower" title="Bismarck tower">Bismarck tower</a> in the west of Memmingen. Beside the tower is the 2007 build new soccer stadium.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks">Parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green areas were created all along the city wall. The old ditches where filled up and replaced with green areas or parks with partially over 150-year-old trees. The name of the parks are (starting clockwise at the <i>Ulmer Gate</i>): Hubergarten, Zollergarten, Ratzengraben/Zollergraben, Kohlschanze, Reichshain, Kaisergraben, Hohe Wacht, Westertorplatz, Grimmelschanze. Nearby every residential area has its own smaller parks. There is also the city park in the <i>New World</i>, the old <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landesgartenschau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Landesgartenschau (page does not exist)">Landesgartenschau</a> place. Also the old and the forest cemetery, which are both used as parks.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cemeteries">Cemeteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cemeteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Memmingen_Alter_Friedhof.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Memmingen_Alter_Friedhof.jpg/220px-Memmingen_Alter_Friedhof.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/84/Memmingen_Alter_Friedhof.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="320" data-file-height="240" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Memmingen_Alter_Friedhof.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Old cemetery</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There were four cemeteries in Memmingen in the Middle Ages. They were around the St. Martin's Church and the Church of Our Lady, also at the Kreuzherren monastery and the <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Scottish_mission" title="Hiberno-Scottish mission">Scottish monastery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They were abandoned in 1530. The replacement was the <i>Old cemetery</i> at the former Scottish monastery. This cemetery was abandoned in 1930. The closing of the Old Cemetery has involved a new Cemetery. It was founded in the east of Memmingen as a Forest Cemetery. More cemeteries are in the districts Amendingen, Steinheim, Buxach, Volkratshofen, Ferthofen and Dickenreishausen. In the east of the city is also a Jewish cemetery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Memmingen is located at the western border of Bavaria at the river Iller, 50&#160;km south of <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a>, and 100&#160;km west of Munich. The landscape or region beginning with Memmingen is called <i><a href="/wiki/Unterallg%C3%A4u" title="Unterallgäu">Unterallgäu</a></i> and forms a part of the region <a href="/wiki/Mittelschwaben" title="Mittelschwaben">Mittelschwaben</a> who is next to <a href="/wiki/Oberschwaben" class="mw-redirect" title="Oberschwaben">Oberschwaben</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allg%C3%A4u" title="Allgäu">Allgäu</a>. Memmingen is also sometimes called the <i>Gate to the Allgäu</i>. It is reached by the A7 and the A96 motorways and <a href="/wiki/Memmingen_station" title="Memmingen station">Memmingen station</a> is on railways connecting Munich and Lindau and the <a href="/wiki/Neu-Ulm%E2%80%93Kempten_railway" title="Neu-Ulm–Kempten railway">Ulm–Oberstdorf railway</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Lord Mayor has been from the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party of Germany</a> since 1966, the biggest party in the city council is traditionally the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">Christian Social Union</a>.</p>
<p>The city politics is mostly dominated by a coalition of bigger parties ("coalition of the reasoned") from CSU, SPD, Christlicher Rathausblock Memmingen (Christian Town Hall Party Memmingen) and the <a href="/wiki/Free_Voters" title="Free Voters">Free Voters</a>. The smaller parties of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_Democratic_Party" title="Ecological Democratic Party">Ecological Democratic Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Alliance '90/The Greens</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Free Democratic Party</a> make up the opposition.</p>
<p>There was a hefty dispute between the parties in 2005, concerning financial participation in the <a href="/wiki/Memmingen_Airport" title="Memmingen Airport">Memmingen Airport</a>. The Ecological Democratic Party and the Greens initiated a referendum to inhibit financial support for the airport, but this vote met with no success.</p>
<p>At the top of the city government is the Lord Mayor, who is elected directly by the people. He is the representative of the town and the leader of municipality. As second representatives, the majors are elected from the members of the city council. Historically the CSU, as biggest party, appoints the second major. The third major is appointed by the third biggest party. The second biggest party, the SPD, traditionally declines to appoint the third major, because they already appoint the Lord Mayor.</p>
<p>Memmingen is building, alongside the double centre <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a>/<a href="/wiki/Neu-Ulm" title="Neu-Ulm">Neu-Ulm</a>, the second economical centre in Upper Swabia. It thus leads the central supply function for the adjoining cities and districts.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_Finances">City Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: City Finances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With €512 per capita, Memmingen is one of the cities in Germany with the lowest level of debt<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (The German average is more than €1,300). The city had 2007 a management budget (<i>Verwaltungshaushalt</i>) of €94,925,160 and an asset budget (<i>Vermögenshaushalt</i>) of €19,490,860. The income from trade taxes amounted to about €40 million, the income tax assignment to about €20 million. The local rates were last changed in 2003. The city has many charitable foundations, with roots partly going back to the Middle Ages (such as the <i>Unterhospitalstiftung</i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_Arms_and_Flag">Coat of Arms and Flag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memmingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Coat of Arms and Flag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Blazon" title="Blazon">Blazon</a>: <i>Split from gold and silver, in front a half, reinforced in red, black eagle. Backward a red pawcross</i></p>
<p>The city's colours, handed down since 1488, are Black, Red, White. The flag is a banner flag with cross bar.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amendingen" class="mw-redirect" title="Amendingen">Amendingen</a> and Eisenburg have their own historical coats of arms.</p>

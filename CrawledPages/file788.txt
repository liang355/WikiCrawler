<p><b>Steven Arthur Pinker</b> (born September 18, 1954) is a Canadian-born American <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_Psychology" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognitive Psychology">cognitive psychologist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> author. He is Johnstone Family Professor in the Department of Psychology at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, and is known for his advocacy of <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Computational_theory_of_mind" title="Computational theory of mind">computational theory of mind</a>.</p>
<p>Pinker's academic specializations are <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">visual cognition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psycholinguistics" title="Psycholinguistics">psycholinguistics</a>. His experimental subjects include mental imagery, shape recognition, visual attention, children's language development, regular and irregular phenomena in language, the neural bases of words and <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a>, and the psychology of cooperation and communication, including <a href="/wiki/Euphemism" title="Euphemism">euphemism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Innuendo" title="Innuendo">innuendo</a>, emotional expression, and common knowledge. He has written two technical books that proposed a general theory of <a href="/wiki/Language_acquisition" title="Language acquisition">language acquisition</a> and applied it to children's learning of verbs. In particular, his work with <a href="/wiki/Alan_Prince" title="Alan Prince">Alan Prince</a> published in 1989 critiqued the <a href="/wiki/Connectionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Connectionist">connectionist</a> model of how children acquire the past tense of English verbs, arguing instead that children use default rules such as adding "-ed" to make regular forms, sometimes in error, but are obliged to learn irregular forms one by one.</p>
<p>In his popular books, he has argued that the human faculty for language is an <a href="/wiki/Instinct" title="Instinct">instinct</a>, an innate behavior shaped by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adapted</a> to our communication needs. He is the author of seven books for a general audience. Five of these, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Language_Instinct" title="The Language Instinct">The Language Instinct</a></i> (1994), <i><a href="/wiki/How_the_Mind_Works" title="How the Mind Works">How the Mind Works</a></i> (1997), <i><a href="/wiki/Words_and_Rules" title="Words and Rules">Words and Rules</a></i> (2000), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blank_Slate" title="The Blank Slate">The Blank Slate</a></i> (2002), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stuff_of_Thought" title="The Stuff of Thought">The Stuff of Thought</a></i> (2007), describe aspects of the field of psycholinguistics and cognitive science, and include accounts of his own research. In the sixth book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Better_Angels_of_Our_Nature" title="The Better Angels of Our Nature">The Better Angels of Our Nature</a></i> (2011), Pinker makes the case that violence in human societies has, in general, steadily declined with time, and identifies six major causes of this decline. His seventh book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sense_of_Style" title="The Sense of Style">The Sense of Style</a></i> (2014), is intended as a general <a href="/wiki/Style_guide" title="Style guide">style guide</a> that is informed by modern science and psychology, offering advice on how to produce more comprehensible and unambiguous writing in nonfiction contexts and explaining why so much of today's academic and popular writing is difficult for readers to understand.</p>
<p>Pinker has been named as one of the world's most influential intellectuals by various magazines. He has won awards from the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">American Psychological Association</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Institution of Great Britain">Royal Institution</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_Neuroscience_Society" title="Cognitive Neuroscience Society">Cognitive Neuroscience Society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Humanist_Association" title="American Humanist Association">American Humanist Association</a>. He delivered the <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Lectures" title="Gifford Lectures">Gifford Lectures</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> in 2013. He has served on the editorial boards of a variety of journals, and on the advisory boards of several institutions. He has frequently participated in public debates on science and society and is a regular contributor to the online science and culture digest <i><a href="/wiki/3_Quarks_Daily" title="3 Quarks Daily">3 Quarks Daily</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Pinker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pinker was born in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, in 1954, to a middle-class Jewish family. His parents were Roslyn and Harry Pinker.<sup id="cite_ref-google_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google-3">[3]</a></sup> His grandparents immigrated to Canada from <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bessarabia" title="Bessarabia">Bessarabia</a> in 1926.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His father, a lawyer, first worked as a manufacturer's representative, while his mother was first a home-maker then a guidance counselor and high-school vice-principal. He has two younger siblings. His brother Robert is a policy analyst for the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">Canadian government</a>, while his sister, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Pinker" title="Susan Pinker">Susan Pinker</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">psychologist</a> and writer who authored <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sexual_Paradox" title="The Sexual Paradox">The Sexual Paradox</a></i> and <i>The Village Effect</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-siblings_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-siblings-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Pinker married Nancy Etcoff in 1980 and they divorced in 1992; he married Ilavenil Subbiah in 1995 and they too divorced.<sup id="cite_ref-wives_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wives-8">[8]</a></sup> His third wife, whom he married in 2007, is the novelist and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Goldstein" title="Rebecca Goldstein">Rebecca Goldstein</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Blagg_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blagg-9">[9]</a></sup> He has two stepdaughters: the novelist <a href="/wiki/Yael_Goldstein_Love" title="Yael Goldstein Love">Yael Goldstein Love</a> and the poet Danielle Blau.</p>
<p>Pinker graduated from <a href="/wiki/Dawson_College" title="Dawson College">Dawson College</a> in 1973. He received a Bachelor of Arts in <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> from <a href="/wiki/McGill_University" title="McGill University">McGill University</a> in 1976, and earned his Doctorate of Philosophy in <a href="/wiki/Experimental_psychology" title="Experimental psychology">experimental psychology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1979 under <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Kosslyn" title="Stephen Kosslyn">Stephen Kosslyn</a>. He did research at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT) for a year, after which he became an <a href="/wiki/Assistant_professor" title="Assistant professor">assistant professor</a> at Harvard and then <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>.</p>
<p>From 1982 until 2003, Pinker taught at the Department of Brain and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognitive Sciences">Cognitive Sciences</a> at MIT, was the co-director of the Center for <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">Cognitive Science</a> (1985-1994), and eventually became the director of the Center for <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_neuroscience" title="Cognitive neuroscience">Cognitive Neuroscience</a> (1994-1999),<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> taking a one-year sabbatical at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara" title="University of California, Santa Barbara">University of California, Santa Barbara</a>, in 1995–96. As of 2003, he is the Johnstone Family Professor of Psychology at Harvard; from 2008 to 2013 he also held the title of Harvard College Professor in recognition of his dedication to teaching.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He currently gives lectures as a visiting professor at the <a href="/wiki/New_College_of_the_Humanities" title="New College of the Humanities">New College of the Humanities</a>, a private college in London.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>About his Jewish background Pinker has said, "I was never religious in the theological sense&#160;... I never outgrew my conversion to <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a> at 13, but at various times was a serious <a href="/wiki/Secular_Jewish_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Secular Jewish culture">cultural Jew</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Dougla_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dougla-14">[14]</a></sup> As a teenager, he says he considered himself an <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchist</a> until he witnessed <a href="/wiki/Civil_unrest" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil unrest">civil unrest</a> following <a href="/wiki/Murray-Hill_riot" title="Murray-Hill riot">a police strike in 1969</a>, when:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>As a young teenager in proudly peaceable Canada during the romantic 1960s, I was a true believer in Bakunin's anarchism. I laughed off my parents' argument that if the government ever laid down its arms all hell would break loose. Our competing predictions were put to the test at 8:00&#160;A.M. on October 17, 1969, when the Montreal police went on strike... This decisive empirical test left my politics in tatters (and offered a foretaste of life as a scientist).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Pinker identifies himself as an <a href="/wiki/Equity_feminism" title="Equity feminism">equity feminist</a>, which he defines as "a moral doctrine about equal treatment that makes no commitments regarding open empirical issues in psychology or biology".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He reported the result of a test of his political orientation that characterized him as "neither leftist nor rightist, more <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarian</a> than <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-genome_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-genome-17">[17]</a></sup> He describes himself as having "experienced a primitive tribal stirring" after his genes were shown to trace back to the Middle East, noting that he "found it just as thrilling to zoom outward in the diagrams of my genetic lineage and see my place in a family tree that embraces all of humanity".<sup id="cite_ref-Forward_12_Aug_11_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forward_12_Aug_11-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinker also identifies himself as an <a href="/wiki/Atheist" class="mw-redirect" title="Atheist">atheist</a>. In the 2007 interview with the <a href="/wiki/Point_of_Inquiry" title="Point of Inquiry">Point of Inquiry</a> podcast, Pinker states that he would "defend atheism as an empirically supported view." He sees theism and atheism as competing empirical hypotheses, and states that "we're learning more and more about what makes us tick, including our moral sense, without needing the assumption of a deity or a soul. It's naturally getting crowded out by the successive naturalistic explanations."<sup id="cite_ref-Point_of_Inquiry_Interview_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Point_of_Inquiry_Interview-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_theory">Research and theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Pinker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research and theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pinker's research on visual cognition, begun in collaboration with his thesis adviser, Stephen Kosslyn, showed that mental images represent scenes and objects as they appear from a specific vantage point (rather than capturing their intrinsic three-dimensional structure), and thus correspond to the neuroscientist <a href="/wiki/David_Marr_(neuroscientist)" title="David Marr (neuroscientist)">David Marr</a>'s theory of a "two-and-a-half-dimensional sketch."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He also showed that this level of representation is used in visual attention, and in <a href="/wiki/Object_recognition" class="mw-redirect" title="Object recognition">object recognition</a> (at least for asymmetrical shapes), contrary to Marr's theory that recognition uses viewpoint-independent representations.</p>
<p>In psycholinguistics, Pinker became known early in his career for promoting <a href="/wiki/Computational_learning_theory" title="Computational learning theory">computational learning theory</a> as a way to understand <a href="/wiki/Language_acquisition" title="Language acquisition">language acquisition</a> in children. He wrote a tutorial review of the field followed by two books that advanced his own theory of language acquisition, and a series of experiments on how children acquire the passive, dative, and locative constructions. These books were <i>Language Learnability and Language Development</i> (1984), in Pinker's words "outlin[ing] a theory of how children acquire the words and grammatical structures of their mother tongue",<sup id="cite_ref-LongBiog_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LongBiog-21">[21]</a></sup> and <i>Learnability and Cognition: The Acquisition of Argument Structure</i> (1989), in Pinker's words "focus[ing] on one aspect of this process, the ability to use different kinds of verbs in appropriate sentences, such as intransitive verbs, transitive verbs, and verbs taking different combinations of complements and indirect objects".<sup id="cite_ref-LongBiog_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LongBiog-21">[21]</a></sup> He then focused on verbs of two kinds that illustrate what he considers to be the processes required for human language: retrieving whole words from memory, like the past form of the <a href="/wiki/Regular_and_irregular_verbs" title="Regular and irregular verbs">irregular verb</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> "bring", namely "brought"; and using rules to combine (parts of) words, like the past form of the regular verb "walk", namely "walked".<sup id="cite_ref-LongBiog_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LongBiog-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1988 Pinker and <a href="/wiki/Alan_Prince" title="Alan Prince">Alan Prince</a> published an influential critique of a <a href="/wiki/Connectionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Connectionist">connectionist</a> model of the acquisition of the past tense (a textbook problem in language acquisition), followed by a series of studies of how people use and acquire the past tense. This included a monograph on children's <a href="/wiki/Regularization_(linguistics)" title="Regularization (linguistics)">regularization</a> of irregular forms and his popular 1999 book, <i><a href="/wiki/Words_and_Rules" title="Words and Rules">Words and Rules: The Ingredients of Language</a></i>. Pinker argued that language depends on two things, the associative remembering of sounds and their meanings in words, and the use of rules to manipulate symbols for <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a>. He presented evidence against connectionism, where a child would have to learn all forms of all words and would simply retrieve each needed form from memory, in favour of the older alternative theory, the use of words and rules combined by <a href="/wiki/Generative_phonology" class="mw-redirect" title="Generative phonology">generative phonology</a>. He showed that mistakes made by children indicate the use of default rules to add suffixes such as "-ed": for instance 'breaked' and 'comed' for 'broke' and 'came'. He argued that this shows that irregular verb-forms in English have to be learnt and retrieved from memory individually, and that the children making these errors were predicting the regular "-ed" ending in an open-ended way by applying a mental rule. This rule for combining verb stems and the usual suffix can be expressed as<sup id="cite_ref-WRessay_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WRessay-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>V<sub>past</sub> → V<sub>stem</sub> + d</p>
</blockquote>
<p>where V is a verb and d is the regular ending. Pinker further argued that since the ten most frequently occurring English verbs (be, have, do, say, make ...) are all irregular, while 98.2% of the thousand least common verbs are regular, there is a "massive correlation" of frequency and irregularity. He explains this by arguing that every irregular form, such as 'took', 'came' and 'got', has to be committed to memory by the children in each generation, or else lost, and that the common forms are the most easily memorized. Any irregular verb that falls in popularity past a certain point is lost, and all future generations will treat it as a regular verb instead.<sup id="cite_ref-WRessay_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WRessay-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1990, Pinker, with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bloom_(psychologist)" title="Paul Bloom (psychologist)">Paul Bloom</a>, published the paper "Natural Language and Natural Selection", arguing that the human language faculty must have evolved through <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The article provided arguments for a continuity based view of language evolution, contrary to then current discontinuity based theories that see language as suddenly appearing with the advent of <i>Homo sapiens</i> as a kind of evolutionary accident. This discontinuity based view was prominently argued by two of the main authorities, linguist Noam Chomsky and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-journey_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journey-25">[25]</a></sup> The paper became widely cited and created renewed interest in the evolutionary prehistory of language, and has been credited with shifting the central question of the debate from "did language evolve?" to "how did language evolve".<sup id="cite_ref-journey_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journey-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The article also presaged Pinker's argument in <i>The Language Instinct</i>.</p>
<p>Pinker's research includes delving into human nature and what science says about it. In his interview on the <a href="/wiki/Point_of_Inquiry" title="Point of Inquiry">Point of Inquiry</a> podcast in 2007, he provides the following examples of what he considers defensible conclusions of what science says human nature is:</p>
<ul>
<li>The sexes are not statistically identical; "their interests and talents form two overlapping distributions". Any policy that wants to provide equal outcomes for both men and women will have to discriminate against one or the other.</li>
<li>"Individuals differ in personality and intelligence."</li>
<li>"People favor themselves and their families over an abstraction called society."</li>
<li>Humans are "systematically self deceived. Each one of us thinks of ourselves as more competent and benevolent than we are."</li>
<li>"People crave status and power"</li>
</ul>
<p>He informs the listeners that one can read more about human nature in his book, <i>Blank Slate</i>.</p>
<p>Pinker also speaks about <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a> in the podcast and believes that this area of science is going to pay off. He cites the fact that there are many areas of study, such as beauty, religion, play, and sexuality, that were not studied 15 years ago. It is thanks to evolutionary psychology that these areas are being studied.<sup id="cite_ref-Point_of_Inquiry_Interview_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Point_of_Inquiry_Interview-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularization_of_science">Popularization of science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Pinker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popularization of science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_cognition_and_natural_language">Human cognition and natural language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Pinker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Human cognition and natural language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/The_Language_Instinct" title="The Language Instinct">The Language Instinct</a>, <a href="/wiki/Words_and_Rules" title="Words and Rules">Words and Rules</a>, <a href="/wiki/How_the_Mind_Works" title="How the Mind Works">How the Mind Works</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Blank_Slate" title="The Blank Slate">The Blank Slate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Stuff_of_Thought" title="The Stuff of Thought">The Stuff of Thought</a></div>
<p>Pinker's 1994 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Language_Instinct" title="The Language Instinct">The Language Instinct</a></i> was the first of several books to combine <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive science</a> with <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_genetics" class="mw-redirect" title="Behavioral genetics">behavioral genetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a>. It introduces the science of language and popularizes <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a>'s theory that language is an innate faculty of mind, with the controversial twist that the faculty for language evolved by natural selection as an adaptation for communication. Pinker criticizes several widely held ideas about language – that it needs to be taught, that people's <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a> is poor and getting worse with new ways of speaking, the <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_relativity" title="Linguistic relativity">Sapir–Whorf hypothesis</a> that language limits the kinds of thoughts a person can have, and that <a href="/wiki/Great_Ape_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Ape language">other great apes can learn languages</a>. Pinker sees language as unique to humans, evolved to solve the specific problem of communication among social hunter-gatherers. He argues that it is as much an <a href="/wiki/Instinct" title="Instinct">instinct</a> as specialized adaptative behavior in other species, such as a <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spider</a>'s web-weaving or a <a href="/wiki/Beaver" title="Beaver">beaver</a>'s dam-building.</p>
<p>Pinker states in his introduction that his ideas are "deeply influenced"<sup id="cite_ref-LIintro_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LIintro-27">[27]</a></sup> by Chomsky; he also lists scientists whom Chomsky influenced to "open up whole new areas of language study, from child development and speech perception to neurology and genetics"<sup id="cite_ref-LIintro_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LIintro-27">[27]</a></sup> — <a href="/wiki/Eric_Lenneberg" title="Eric Lenneberg">Eric Lenneberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Armitage_Miller" title="George Armitage Miller">George Miller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_William_Brown" class="mw-redirect" title="Roger William Brown">Roger Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morris_Halle" title="Morris Halle">Morris Halle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Liberman" title="Alvin Liberman">Alvin Liberman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LIintro_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LIintro-27">[27]</a></sup> Brown mentored Pinker through his thesis; Pinker stated that Brown's "funny and instructive"<sup id="cite_ref-BrownObit_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrownObit-28">[28]</a></sup> book <i>Words and Things</i> (1958) was one of the inspirations for <i>The Language Instinct</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BrownObit_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrownObit-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reality of Pinker's proposed language instinct, and the related claim that grammar is innate and genetically based, has been contested by many linguists. One prominent opponent of Pinker's view is <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Sampson" title="Geoffrey Sampson">Geoffrey Sampson</a> whose 1997 book, <i><a href="/wiki/Educating_Eve" title="Educating Eve">Educating Eve: The 'Language Instinct' Debate</a></i> has been described as the "definitive response" to Pinker's book.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sampson_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sampson-31">[31]</a></sup> Sampson argues that while it may seem attractive to argue the nature side of the 'nature versus nurture' debate, the nurture side may better support the creativity and nobility of the human mind. Sampson denies there is a language instinct, and argues that children can learn language because people can learn anything.<sup id="cite_ref-Sampson_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sampson-31">[31]</a></sup> Others have sought a middle ground between Pinker's nativism and Sampson's culturalism.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowley_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowley-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The assumptions underlying the <a href="/wiki/Psychological_nativism" title="Psychological nativism">nativist</a> view have also been criticised in <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Elman" title="Jeffrey Elman">Jeffrey Elman</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Rethinking_Innateness" title="Rethinking Innateness">Rethinking Innateness</a>: A Connectionist Perspective on Development</i>, which defends the connectionist approach that Pinker attacked. In his 1996 book <i>Impossible Minds</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Machine_intelligence" class="mw-redirect" title="Machine intelligence">machine intelligence</a> researcher <a href="/wiki/Igor_Aleksander" title="Igor Aleksander">Igor Aleksander</a> calls <i>The Language Instinct</i> excellent, and argues that Pinker presents a relatively soft claim for innatism, accompanied by a strong dislike of the 'Standard Social Sciences Model' or SSSM (Pinker's term), which supposes that development is purely dependent on culture. Further, Aleksander writes that while Pinker criticises some attempts to explain language processing with neural nets, Pinker later makes use of a neural net to create past tense verb forms correctly. Aleksander concludes that while he doesn't support the SSSM, "a cultural repository of language just seems the easy trick for an efficient evolutionary system armed with an iconic <a href="/wiki/State_machine" class="mw-redirect" title="State machine">state machine</a> to play."<sup id="cite_ref-Aleksander_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aleksander-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two other books, <i><a href="/wiki/How_the_Mind_Works" title="How the Mind Works">How the Mind Works</a></i> (1997) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blank_Slate" title="The Blank Slate">The Blank Slate</a></i> (2002), broadly surveyed the mind and defended the idea of a complex human nature with many mental faculties that are <a href="/wiki/Adaptive" class="mw-redirect" title="Adaptive">adaptive</a> (Pinker is an ally of <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Dennett" title="Daniel Dennett">Daniel Dennett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a> in many disputes surrounding <a href="/wiki/Adaptationism" title="Adaptationism">adaptationism</a>). Another major theme in Pinker's theories is that human cognition works, in part, by combinatorial symbol-manipulation, not just associations among sensory features, as in many connectionist models. On the debate around <i>The Blank Slate</i>, Pinker called <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Sowell" title="Thomas Sowell">Thomas Sowell</a>'s book <i>A Conflict of Visions</i> "wonderful",<sup id="cite_ref-Sailer_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sailer-34">[34]</a></sup> and explained that "The Tragic Vision" and the "Utopian Vision" are the views of human nature behind <a href="/wiki/Right-wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Right-wing">right-</a> and left-wing ideologies.<sup id="cite_ref-Sailer_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sailer-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Words_and_Rules" title="Words and Rules">Words and Rules: the Ingredients of Language</a></i> (1999), Pinker argues from his own research that regular and irregular phenomena are products of computation and memory lookup, respectively, and that language can be understood as an interaction between the two.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> "Words and Rules" is also the title of an essay by Pinker outlining many of the topics discussed in the book.<sup id="cite_ref-WRessay_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WRessay-23">[23]</a></sup> Critiqueing the book from the perspective of <a href="/wiki/Generative_linguistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Generative linguistics">Generative linguistics</a> Charles Yang, in the <i><a href="/wiki/London_Review_of_Books" title="London Review of Books">London Review of Books</a></i>, writes that "this book never runs low on <a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">hubris</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hyperbole" title="Hyperbole">hyperbole</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-LRB_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LRB-36">[36]</a></sup> The book's topic, the English past tense, is in Yang's view unglamorous, and Pinker's attempts at compromise risk being in no man's land between rival theories. Giving the example of German, Yang argues that irregular nouns in that language at least all belong to classes, governed by rules, and that things get even worse in languages that attach prefixes and suffixes to make up long 'words': they can't be learnt individually, as there are untold numbers of combinations. "All Pinker (and the connectionists) are doing is turning over the rocks at the base of the intellectual landslide caused by the Chomskian revolution."<sup id="cite_ref-LRB_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LRB-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stuff_of_Thought" title="The Stuff of Thought">The Stuff of Thought</a></i> (2007), Pinker looks at a wide range of issues around the way words related to thoughts on the one hand, and to the world outside ourselves on the other. Given his evolutionary perspective, a central question is how an intelligent mind capable of abstract thought evolved: how a mind adapted to <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a> life could work in the modern world. Many quirks of language are the result.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinker is critical of theories about the <a href="/wiki/Evolution_of_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Evolution of language">evolutionary origins of language</a> that argue that linguistic cognition might have evolved from earlier musical cognition. He sees language as being tied primarily to the capacity for logical reasoning, and speculates that human proclivity for music may be a <a href="/wiki/Spandrel_(biology)" title="Spandrel (biology)">spandrel</a> — a feature not adaptive in its own right, but that has persisted through other traits that are more broadly practical, and thus selected for. In <i>How the Mind Works</i>, Pinker reiterates <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>'s view that music is not in itself an important cognitive phenomenon, but that it happens to stimulate important auditory and spatio-motor cognitive functions. Pinker compares music to "auditory cheesecake", stating that "As far as biological cause and effect is concerned, music is useless". This argument has been rejected by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Levitin" title="Daniel Levitin">Daniel Levitin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Carroll_(scholar)" title="Joseph Carroll (scholar)">Joseph Carroll</a>, experts in <a href="/wiki/Music_cognition" class="mw-redirect" title="Music cognition">music cognition</a>, who argue that music has had an important role in the evolution of human cognition.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Interview_with_Daniel_Levitin_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Interview_with_Daniel_Levitin-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Steven_Pinker.E2.80.99s_Cheesecake_For_The_Mind_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steven_Pinker.E2.80.99s_Cheesecake_For_The_Mind-43">[43]</a></sup> In his book <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Your_Brain_On_Music" class="mw-redirect" title="This Is Your Brain On Music">This Is Your Brain On Music</a></i>, Levitin argues that music could provide adaptive advantage through <a href="/wiki/Sexual_selection" title="Sexual selection">sexual selection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_bonding" title="Human bonding">social bonding</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_development" title="Cognitive development">cognitive development</a>; he questions the assumption that music is the antecedent to language, as opposed to its progenitor, noting that many species display music-like habits that could be seen as precursors to human music.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinker has also been critical of "<a href="/wiki/Whole_language" title="Whole language">whole language</a>" reading instruction techniques, stating in <i><a href="/wiki/How_the_Mind_Works" title="How the Mind Works">How the Mind Works</a></i>, "...the dominant technique, called 'whole language,' the insight that [spoken] language is a naturally developing human instinct has been garbled into the evolutionarily improbable claim that <i>reading</i> is a naturally developing human instinct."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In the appendix to the 2007 reprinted edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Language_Instinct" title="The Language Instinct">The Language Instinct</a></i>, Pinker cited <i>Why Our Children Can't Read</i> by cognitive psychologist <a href="/wiki/Diane_McGuinness" title="Diane McGuinness">Diane McGuinness</a> as his favorite book on the subject and noted:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>One raging public debate involving language went unmentioned in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Language_Instinct" title="The Language Instinct">The Language Instinct</a></i>: the "reading wars," or dispute over whether children should be explicitly taught to read by decoding the sounds of words from their spelling (loosely known as "<a href="/wiki/Phonics" title="Phonics">phonics</a>") or whether they can develop it instinctively by being immersed in a text-rich environment (often called "whole language"). I tipped my hand in the paragraph in [the sixth chapter of the book] which said that language is an instinct but reading is not.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Like most psycholinguists (but apparently unlike many school boards), I think it's essential for children to be taught to become aware of speech sounds and how they are coded in strings of letters.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Better_Angels_of_Our_Nature"><i>The Better Angels of Our Nature</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Pinker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Better Angels of Our Nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Better_Angels_of_Our_Nature" title="The Better Angels of Our Nature">The Better Angels of Our Nature</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mars_(Hausbuch_1480).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mars_%28Hausbuch_1480%29.png/330px-Mars_%28Hausbuch_1480%29.png" width="330" height="342" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mars_%28Hausbuch_1480%29.png/495px-Mars_%28Hausbuch_1480%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Mars_%28Hausbuch_1480%29.png/660px-Mars_%28Hausbuch_1480%29.png 2x" data-file-width="1331" data-file-height="1379" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mars_(Hausbuch_1480).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Violence in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle ages">middle ages</a>: detail from "Mars" in <i>Das Mittelalterliche Hausbuch</i>, c. 1475 – 1480. The image is used by Pinker in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Better_Angels_of_Our_Nature" title="The Better Angels of Our Nature">The Better Angels of Our Nature</a></i>, with the comment "as the <i>Housebook</i> illustrations suggest, [the knights] did not restrict their killing to other knights".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <i>The Better Angels of Our Nature</i>, published in 2011, Pinker argues that violence, including tribal warfare, homicide, cruel punishments, child abuse, animal cruelty, domestic violence, lynching, pogroms, and international and civil wars, has decreased over multiple scales of time and magnitude. Pinker considers it unlikely that human nature has changed. In his view, it is more likely that human nature comprises inclinations toward violence and those that counteract them, the "better angels of our nature". He outlines six 'major historical declines of violence' that all have their own socio/cultural/economic causes:<sup id="cite_ref-http:.2F.2Fiai.tv.2Fvideo.2Fthe-decline-of-violence_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http:.2F.2Fiai.tv.2Fvideo.2Fthe-decline-of-violence-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>"The Pacification Process" – The rise of organized systems of government has a correlative relationship with the decline in violent deaths. As states expand they prevent tribal feuding, reducing losses.</li>
<li>"The Civilizing Process" – Consolidation of centralized states and kingdoms throughout Europe results in the rise of criminal justice and commercial infrastructure, organizing previously chaotic systems that could lead to raiding and mass violence.</li>
<li>"The Humanitarian Revolution" – The 18th - 20th century abandonment of institutionalized violence by the state (breaking on the wheel, burning at the stake). Suggests this is likely due to the spike in literacy after the invention of the printing press thereby allowing the proletariat to question conventional wisdom.</li>
<li>"The Long Peace" – The powers of 20th Century believed that period of time to be the bloodiest in history. This to a largely peaceful 65-year period post World War I and World War II. Developed countries have stopped warring (against each other and colonially), adopted democracy, and this has led a massive decline (on average) of deaths.</li>
<li>"The New Peace" – The decline in organized conflicts of all kinds since the end of the Cold War.</li>
<li>"The Rights Revolutions" – The reduction of systemic violence at smaller scales against vulnerable populations (racial minorities, women, children, homosexuals, animals).</li>
</ol>
<p>The book was welcomed by many critics and reviewers, who found its arguments convincing and its synthesis of a large volume of historical evidence compelling.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> It also aroused criticism on a variety of grounds, such as whether deaths per capita was an appropriate metric, Pinker's atheism, lack of moral leadership, excessive focus on Europe (though the book covers other areas), the interpretation of historical data, and its image of indigenous people.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English_writing_style_in_the_21st_century">English writing style in the 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Pinker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: English writing style in the 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his seventh popular book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sense_of_Style" title="The Sense of Style">The Sense of Style: The Thinking Person's Guide to Writing in the 21st Century</a></i> (2014), Pinker attempts to provide a writing style guide that is informed by modern science and psychology, offering advice on how to produce more comprehensible and unambiguous writing in nonfiction contexts and explaining why so much of today's academic and popular writing is difficult for readers to understand.</p>
<p>In a November 2014 episode of the <a href="/wiki/Point_of_Inquiry" title="Point of Inquiry">Point of Inquiry</a> podcast, host Lindsay Beyerstein, asked Pinker how his style guide was different from the many guides that already exist. His answer,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Thinking Person's Guide because I don't issue dictates from on high as most manuals do but explain why the various guidelines will improve writing, what they do for language, what they do for the reader's experience, in the hope that the users will apply the rules judiciously knowing what they are designed to accomplish, rather than robotically.<sup id="cite_ref-SofS01_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SofS01-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>He also indicated that the 21st century was applicable because language and usage change over time and it has been a long time since <a href="/wiki/William_Strunk_Jr." title="William Strunk Jr.">William Strunk</a> wrote <a href="/wiki/Elements_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Elements of Style">Elements of Style</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SofS01_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SofS01-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_debate">Public debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Pinker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Public debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pinker is a frequent participant in public debates surrounding the contributions of science to contemporary society. Social commentators such as Ed West, author of <i>The Diversity Illusion</i>, consider Pinker important and daring in his willingness to confront taboos, as in <i>The Blank Slate</i>. This doctrine (the <i><a href="/wiki/Tabula_rasa" title="Tabula rasa">tabula rasa</a></i>), writes West, remained accepted "as fact, rather than fantasy"<sup id="cite_ref-WestTelegraph_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WestTelegraph-67">[67]</a></sup> a decade after the book's publication. West describes Pinker as "no polemicist, and he leaves readers to draw their own conclusions".<sup id="cite_ref-WestTelegraph_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WestTelegraph-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2005, Pinker defended <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">Lawrence Summers</a>, president of Harvard University, whose comments about a <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender gap</a> in mathematics and science angered much of the faculty. Pinker noted that Summers's remarks, properly understood, were hypotheses about overlapping statistical distributions of men's and women's talents and tastes, and that in a university such hypotheses ought to be the subject of empirical testing rather than dogma and outrage.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edge.org" class="mw-redirect" title="Edge.org">Edge.org</a> ran a debate between Pinker and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Spelke" title="Elizabeth Spelke">Elizabeth Spelke</a> on gender and science.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, Pinker wrote a mixed review of <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Gladwell" title="Malcolm Gladwell">Malcolm Gladwell</a>'s essays in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> criticizing his analytical methods.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Gladwell replied, disputing Pinker's comments about the importance of <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_quotient" title="Intelligence quotient">IQ</a> on teaching performance and by analogy, the effect, if any, of draft order on quarterback performance in the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Advanced_NFL_Stats" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced NFL Stats">Advanced NFL Stats</a> addressed the issue statistically, siding with Pinker and showing that differences in methodology could explain the two men's differing opinions.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/David_Shenk" title="David Shenk">David Shenk</a> criticized Pinker for siding with the "nature" argument and for "never once acknowledg[ing] <a href="/wiki/Gene-environment_interaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Gene-environment interaction">gene-environment interaction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Epigenetics" title="Epigenetics">epigenetics</a>" in an article on <a href="/wiki/Nature_versus_nurture" title="Nature versus nurture">nature versus nurture</a> in <i>The New York Times</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Pinker responded to a question about epigenetics as a possibility for the <a href="/wiki/The_Better_Angels_of_Our_Nature" title="The Better Angels of Our Nature">decline in violence</a> in a lecture for the <a href="/wiki/BBC_World_Service" title="BBC World Service">BBC World Service</a>. Pinker said it was unlikely since the decline in violence happened too rapidly to be explained by genetic changes.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Helga Vierich and Cathryn Townsend wrote a critical review of Pinker’s sweeping "Civilizational” explanations for patterns of human violence and warfare in response to a lecture he gave at Cambridge University in September 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>Steven Pinker is also noted for having identified the rename of Phillip Morris to <a href="/wiki/Altria" title="Altria">Altria</a> as an "egregious example" of phonesthesia - with the company attempting to "switch its image from bad people who sell addictive carcinogens to a place or state marked by altruism and other lofty values".<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_distinctions">Awards and distinctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Pinker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Awards and distinctions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Pinker was named one of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a>'s</i> 100 most influential people in the world in 2004<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> and one of <i><a href="/wiki/Prospect_(magazine)" title="Prospect (magazine)">Prospect</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Policy" title="Foreign Policy">Foreign Policy</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Top_100_Public_Intellectuals_Poll" class="mw-redirect" title="Top 100 Public Intellectuals Poll">100 top public intellectuals</a> in both years the poll was carried out, 2005<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> and 2008;<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> in 2010 and 2011 he was named by <i>Foreign Policy</i> to its list of top global thinkers.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> In 2016, he was elected to the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>His research in cognitive psychology has won the Early Career Award (1984) and Boyd McCandless Award (1986) from the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">American Psychological Association</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Troland_Research_Awards" title="Troland Research Awards">Troland Research Award</a> (1993) from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, the Henry Dale Prize (2004) from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Institution of Great Britain">Royal Institution of Great Britain</a>, and the George Miller Prize (2010) from the <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_Neuroscience_Society" title="Cognitive Neuroscience Society">Cognitive Neuroscience Society</a>. He has also received honorary doctorates from the universities of <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University" title="Newcastle University">Newcastle</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Surrey" title="University of Surrey">Surrey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv_University" title="Tel Aviv University">Tel Aviv</a>, <a href="/wiki/McGill_University" title="McGill University">McGill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simon_Fraser_University" title="Simon Fraser University">Simon Fraser University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Troms%C3%B8" title="University of Tromsø">University of Tromsø</a>. He was twice a finalist for the <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>, in 1998 and in 2003. On May 13, 2006, he received the <a href="/wiki/American_Humanist_Association" title="American Humanist Association">American Humanist Association</a>'s Humanist of the Year award for his contributions to public understanding of human evolution.<sup id="cite_ref-AHA-PressRelease_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AHA-PressRelease-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pinker has served on the editorial boards of journals such as Cognition, Daedalus, and PLOS One, and on the advisory boards of institutions for scientific research (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Allen_Institute_for_Brain_Science" title="Allen Institute for Brain Science">Allen Institute for Brain Science</a>), free speech (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Foundation_for_Individual_Rights_in_Education" title="Foundation for Individual Rights in Education">Foundation for Individual Rights in Education</a>), the popularization of science (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/World_Science_Festival" title="World Science Festival">World Science Festival</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Committee_for_Skeptical_Inquiry" title="Committee for Skeptical Inquiry">Committee for Skeptical Inquiry</a>), peace (e.g., the Peace Research Endowment), and secular humanism (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_from_Religion_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom from Religion Foundation">Freedom from Religion Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Secular_Coalition_for_America" title="Secular Coalition for America">Secular Coalition for America</a>).</p>
<p>Since 2008, he has chaired the Usage Panel of the <a href="/wiki/American_Heritage_Dictionary" class="mw-redirect" title="American Heritage Dictionary">American Heritage Dictionary</a>, and wrote the essay on usage for the fifth edition of the Dictionary, which was published in 2011.</p>

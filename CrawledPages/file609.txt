<p><b>Thomas Reid</b> <a href="/wiki/FRSE" class="mw-redirect" title="FRSE">FRSE</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 26 April 1710 – 7 October 1796) was a religiously trained Scottish philosopher, a contemporary of <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a> as well as "Hume's earliest and fiercest critic".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_School_of_Common_Sense" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish School of Common Sense">Scottish School of Common Sense</a> and played an integral role in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a>. The early part of his life was spent in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> and he graduated from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Aberdeen" title="University of Aberdeen">University of Aberdeen</a>. He began his career as a <a href="/wiki/Minister_(Christianity)" title="Minister (Christianity)">Minister</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> but ceased to be a Minister (or called 'Reverend') when he was given a professorship at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Aberdeen" title="King's College, Aberdeen">King's College, Aberdeen</a> in 1752. He obtained his doctorate and wrote <i>An Inquiry Into the Human Mind on the Principles of Common Sense</i> (published in 1764). He and his colleagues founded the 'Aberdeen Philosophical Society' which was popularly known as the 'Wise Club' (a literary-philosophical association).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Shortly after the publication of his first book, he was given the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Professor_of_Moral_Philosophy,_Glasgow" class="mw-redirect" title="Professor of Moral Philosophy, Glasgow">Professorship of Moral Philosophy</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a> when he was called to replace <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>. He resigned from this position in 1781, after which he prepared his university lectures for publication in two books: <i>Essays on the Intellectual Powers of Man</i> (1785) and <i>Essays on the Active Powers of the Human Mind</i> (1788). Reid was buried at Blackfriars Church in the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Glasgow University">Glasgow College</a> and when the university moved to Gilmorehill in the west of Glasgow, his tombstone was inserted in the main building. See separate article on <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Reid%27s_tombstone" title="Thomas Reid's tombstone">Thomas Reid's tombstone</a>.</p>
<p>Reid believed that <a href="/wiki/Common_sense" title="Common sense">common sense</a> (in a special philosophical sense of <i><a href="/wiki/Sensus_communis" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensus communis">sensus communis</a></i>) is, or at least should be, at the foundation of all philosophical inquiry.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He disagreed with Hume, who asserted that we can never know what an external world consists of as our knowledge is limited to the ideas in the mind, and <a href="/wiki/George_Berkeley" title="George Berkeley">George Berkeley</a>, who asserted that the external world is merely ideas in the mind. By contrast, Reid claimed that the foundations upon which our <i>sensus communis</i> are built justify our belief that there is an external world.</p>
<p>In his day and for some years into the 19th century, he was regarded as more important than Hume.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He advocated <a href="/wiki/Direct_realism" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct realism">direct realism</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Na%C3%AFve_realism" title="Naïve realism">common sense realism</a>, and argued strongly against the <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">Theory of Ideas</a> advocated by <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a>, and (in varying forms) nearly all <a href="/wiki/17th-century_philosophy" title="17th-century philosophy">Early Modern philosophers</a> who came after them. He had a great admiration for Hume and had a mutual friend send Hume an early manuscript of Reid's <i>Inquiry.</i> Hume responded that the "deeply philosophical" work "is wrote in a lively and entertaining matter," but that "there seems to be some defect in method," and criticized Reid for implying the presence of innate ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span id="Thomas_Reid's_theory_of_common_sense"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Thomas_Reid.27s_theory_of_common_sense">Thomas Reid's theory of common sense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Reid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Thomas Reid's theory of common sense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His theory of knowledge had a strong influence on his theory of morals. He thought <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a> was an introductory part to practical ethics: When we are confirmed in our common beliefs by philosophy, all we have to do is to act according to them, because we know what is right. His moral philosophy is reminiscent of Roman <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">stoicism</a> in its emphasis on the agency of the subject and self-control. He often quotes <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>, from whom he adopted the term "<a href="/wiki/Sensus_communis" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensus communis">sensus communis</a>". Reid's answer to Hume's sceptical and naturalist arguments was to enumerate a set of principles of <i>common sense</i> (<i>sensus communis</i>) which constitute the foundations of rational thought. Anyone who undertakes a philosophical argument, for example, must implicitly presuppose certain beliefs like, "I am talking to a real person," and "There is an external world whose laws do not change," among many other positive, substantive claims. For Reid, the belief in the truth of these principles is not rational; rather, reason itself demands these principles as prerequisites, as does the innate "constitution" of the human mind. It is for this reason (and possibly a mocking attitude toward Hume and Berkeley) that Reid sees belief in the principles of common sense as a litmus test for sanity. For example, in <i>The Intellectual Powers of Man</i> he states, "For, before men can reason together, they must agree in first principles; and it is impossible to reason with a man who has no principles in common with you." One of the first principles he goes on to list is that "qualities must necessarily be in something that is figured, coloured, hard or soft, that moves or resists. It is not to these qualities, but to that which is the subject of them, that we give the name body. If any man should think fit to deny that these things are qualities, or that they require any subject, I leave him to enjoy his opinion as a man who denies first principles, and is not fit to be reasoned with."</p>
<p>Reid also made positive arguments based in phenomenological insight to put forth a novel mixture of <a href="/wiki/Direct_realism" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct realism">direct realism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_language_philosophy" title="Ordinary language philosophy">ordinary language philosophy</a>. In a typical passage in the <i>Intellectual Powers</i> he asserts that when he has a conception of a centaur, the thing he conceives is an animal, and no idea is an animal; therefore, the thing he conceives is not an idea, but a centaur. This point relies both on an account of the subjective experience of conceiving an object and also on an account of what we mean when we use words. Because Reid saw his philosophy as publicly accessible knowledge, available both through introspection and the proper understanding of how language is used, he saw it as the philosophy of common sense.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploring_Sense_and_Language">Exploring Sense and Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Reid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exploring Sense and Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reid started out with a 'common sense' based on a direct experience of an external reality, but then proceeded to explore in two directions - external to the senses, and internal to human language - to find a more rational basis.</p>
<p>In the case of the latter, Reid saw this as based on an innate capacity pre-dating human consciousness, and acting as an instrument for that consciousness. Also, language then becomes a means of examining the original form of human cognition. Reid notes that current human language contains two distinct elements: first, the acoustic element and secondly the meanings which seem to have nothing to do with the sounds as such. This state of language, which he calls 'artificial', cannot be the primeval one, which he terms 'natural', wherein sound was not an abstract sign, but a concrete gesture or natural sign. Reid looks to the way a child learns language, by imitating sounds, becoming aware of them long before it understands the meaning accorded to the various groups of sounds in the artificial state of contemporary adult speech. If, says Reid, the child were to understand immediately the conceptual content of the words it hears, it would never learn to speak at all. Here Reid distinguishes between natural and artificial signs.</p>
<dl>
<dd>'It is by natural signs chiefly that we give force and energy to language; and the less language has of them, it is the less expressive and persuasive. ... Artificial signs signify, but they do not express; they speak to the intellect, as algebraic characters may do, but the passions and the affections and the will hear them not: these continue dormant and inactive, till we speak to them in <i>the language of nature</i>, to which they are all attention and obedience.' (p. 52) <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>As regards the former, Reid was led to his critical distinction between 'sensation' and 'perception'. While we become aware of an object through the senses, the content of that perception is not identical with the sum total of the sensations caused in our consciousness. Thus, while we tend to focus on the object perceived, we pay no attention to the process leading from sensation to perception, which contains the knowledge of the thing as real. How, then, do we receive the conviction of the latter's existence? Reid's answer is, by entering into an immediate intuitive relationship with it, as a child does. In the case of the adult, the focus is on perceiving, but with the child it is on receiving of the sensations in their living nature. For Reid, the perception of the child is different from the adult, and he states that man must become like a child to get past the artificial perception of the adult, which leads to <a href="/wiki/David_Hume_(philosopher)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Hume (philosopher)">Hume's</a> view that what we perceive is an illusion. Also, the artist provides a key to the true content of <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">sense experience</a>, as he engages the 'language of nature'.</p>
<dl>
<dd>'It were easy to show, that the fine arts of the musician, the painter, the actor, and the orator, so far as they are expressive... are nothing else but the language of nature, which we brought into the world with us, but have unlearned by disuse and so find the greatest difficulty in recovering it. (p. 53)<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>That without a natural knowledge of the connection between these [natural] signs and the things signified by them, language could never have been invented and established among men; and, That the fine arts are all founded upon this connection, which we may call the natural language of mankind." (p. 59) <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Thus, for Reid, common sense was based on the innate capacity of man in an earlier epoch to directly participate in nature, and one we find to some extent in the child and artist, but one that from a philosophical and scientific perspective, we must re-awaken at a higher level in the human mind above nature.</p>
<p>Reid also claimed that this discovery of the link between the natural sign and the thing signified was the basis of natural philosophy and science, as pointed out by <a href="/wiki/Bacon" title="Bacon">Bacon</a> in his new method of discovery of the innate laws of nature.</p>
<dl>
<dd>The great Lord Verulam had a perfect comprehension of this, when we called it an interpretation of nature. No man ever more distinctly understood, or happily expressed the nature and foundation of the philosophic art. What is all we know of mechanics, astronomy, and optics, but connections established by nature and discovered by experience or observation, and consequences deduced from them? …What we commonly call natural causes might, with more propriety, be called natural signs, and what we call effects, the things signified…[as]all we can certainly affirm, is, that nature hath established a constant conjunction between them and the things called their effects." (p. 59)<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Reid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been claimed that his reputation waned after attacks on the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_School_of_Common_Sense" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish School of Common Sense">Scottish School of Common Sense</a> by <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a> (although Kant, only 14 years Reid's junior, also bestowed much praise on Scottish philosophy - Kant attacked the work of Reid, but admitted he had never actually read the works of Thomas Reid) and by <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a>. But Reid's was the philosophy taught in the colleges of North America during the 19th century and was championed by <a href="/wiki/Victor_Cousin" title="Victor Cousin">Victor Cousin</a>, a French philosopher. Justus Buchler has shown that Reid was an important influence on the American philosopher <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce" title="Charles Sanders Peirce">Charles Sanders Peirce</a>, who shared Reid's concern to revalue common sense and whose work links Reid to <a href="/wiki/Pragmatism" title="Pragmatism">pragmatism</a>. To Peirce, conceptions of truth and the real involve the notion of a community without definite limits (and thus potentially self-correcting as far as needed), and capable of a definite increase of knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Common sense is socially evolved, open to verification much like scientific method, and constantly evolving, as evidence, perception, and practice warrant, albeit with a slowness that Peirce came only in later years to see, at which point he owned his "adhesion, under inevitable modification, to the opinion of...Thomas Reid, in the matter of Common Sense"<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> (Peirce called his version "critical common-sensism"). By contrast, on Reid's concept, the <i>sensus communis</i> is not a social evolutionary product but rather a precondition of the possibility that humans could reason with each other. The work of Thomas Reid influenced the work of Noah Porter and James McCosh in the 19th century United States and is based upon the claim of universal principles of objective truth, Pragmatism is not the development of the work of the Scottish "Common Sense" School - it is the negation of it. There are clear links between the work of the Scottish Common Sense School and the work of the Oxford Realist philosophers Harold Prichard and Sir William David Ross in the 20th century.</p>
<p>Reid's reputation has revived in the wake of the advocacy of common sense as a philosophical method or criterion by <a href="/wiki/G._E._Moore" title="G. E. Moore">G. E. Moore</a> early in the 20th century, and more recently because of the attention given to Reid by contemporary philosophers, in particular philosophers of religion in the school of <a href="/wiki/Reformed_epistemology" title="Reformed epistemology">Reformed epistemology</a> such as <a href="/wiki/William_Alston" title="William Alston">William Alston</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Plantinga" title="Alvin Plantinga">Alvin Plantinga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Wolterstorff" title="Nicholas Wolterstorff">Nicholas Wolterstorff</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> seeking to rebut charges that theistic belief is irrational where it has no doxastic foundations (that is, where that belief is not inferred from other adequately grounded beliefs).</p>
<p>He wrote a number of important philosophical works, including <i>Inquiry into the Human Mind on the Principles of Common Sense</i> (1764, Glasgow &amp; London), <i>Essays on the Intellectual Powers of Man</i> (1785) and <i>Essays on the Active Powers of Man</i> (1788). In 1844, <a href="/wiki/Schopenhauer" class="mw-redirect" title="Schopenhauer">Schopenhauer</a> praised Reid for explaining that the perception of external objects does not result from the raw data that is received through the five senses:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Thomas Reid's excellent book, <i>Inquiry into the Human Mind</i>... affords us a very thorough conviction of the inadequacy of the <i><a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">senses</a></i> for producing the objective <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> of things, and also of the non-empirical origin of the intuition of <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>. Reid refutes Locke's teaching that perception is a product of the <i>senses</i>. This he does by a thorough and acute demonstration that the collective sensations of the senses do not bear the least resemblance to the world known through perception, and in particular by showing that Locke's five primary qualities (extension, figure, solidity, movement, number) cannot possibly be supplied to us by any sensation of the senses...</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><i><a href="/wiki/The_World_as_Will_and_Representation" title="The World as Will and Representation">The World as Will and Representation</a></i>, Vol. II, Ch. 2</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_philosophical_positions">Other philosophical positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Reid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other philosophical positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though known mainly for his epistemology, Reid is also noted for his views in the <a href="/wiki/Action_theory_(philosophy)" title="Action theory (philosophy)">theory of action</a> and the metaphysics of <a href="/wiki/Personal_identity" title="Personal identity">personal identity</a>. Reid held an <a href="/wiki/Incompatibilism" title="Incompatibilism">incompatibilist</a> or libertarian notion of freedom, holding that we are capable of free actions of which we are the cause, and for which we are morally appraisable.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Regarding personal identity, he rejected Locke's account that self-consciousness in the form of memory of one's experiences was the basis of a person's being identical with their self over time. Reid held that continuity of memory was neither necessary nor sufficient to make one numerically the same person at different times.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

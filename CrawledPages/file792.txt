<p><b>Dudley Robert Herschbach</b> (born June 18, 1932) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. He won the 1986 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> jointly with <a href="/wiki/Yuan_T._Lee" title="Yuan T. Lee">Yuan T. Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_C._Polanyi" class="mw-redirect" title="John C. Polanyi">John C. Polanyi</a> "for their contributions concerning the dynamics of chemical elementary processes."<sup id="cite_ref-NobelPress_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NobelPress-1">[1]</a></sup> Herschbach and Lee specifically worked with molecular beams, performing <a href="/wiki/Crossed_molecular_beam" title="Crossed molecular beam">crossed molecular beam</a> experiments that enabled a detailed molecular-level understanding of many elementary reaction processes. Herschbach is a member of the Board of Sponsors of the <i><a href="/wiki/Bulletin_of_the_Atomic_Scientists" title="Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists">Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dudley_R._Herschbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Herschbach was born in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> on June 18, 1932. The eldest of six children, he grew up in a rural area. He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Campbell_High_School_(California)" title="Campbell High School (California)">Campbell High School</a>, where he played football. Offered both athletic and academic scholarships to Stanford University, Herschbach chose the academic. His freshman advisor, <a href="/wiki/Harold_S._Johnston" title="Harold S. Johnston">Harold S. Johnston</a>, hired him as a summer research assistant, and taught him chemical kinetics in his senior year. His master's research involved calculating Arrhenius A-factors for gas-phase reactions.<sup id="cite_ref-James_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-2">[2]</a></sup> Herschbach received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">B.S.</a> in mathematics in 1954 and an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">M.S.</a> in chemistry in 1955 from <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NobelBio_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NobelBio-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Herschbach then attended <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> where he earned a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">A.M.</a> in physics in 1956 and a <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> in chemical physics in 1958 under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Bright_Wilson" title="Edgar Bright Wilson">Edgar Bright Wilson</a>. At Harvard, Herschbach examined tunnel splitting in molecules, using microwave spectroscopy.<sup id="cite_ref-James_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-2">[2]</a></sup> He was awarded a three-year Junior Fellowship in the Society of Fellows at Harvard, lasting from 1957 to 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson2011_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson2011-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dudley_R._Herschbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1959, Herschbach joined the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California_at_Berkeley" class="mw-redirect" title="University of California at Berkeley">University of California at Berkeley</a>, where he was appointed an Assistant Professor of Chemistry and became an Associate Professor in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-NobelBio_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NobelBio-3">[3]</a></sup> At Berkeley, he and graduate students George Kwei and James Norris constructed a cross-beam instrument large enough for reactive scattering experiments involve alkali and various molecular partners. His interest in studying elementary chemical processes in molecular-beam reactive collisions challenged an often-accepted belief that "collisions do not occur in crossed molecular beams". The results of his studies of K + CH<sub>3</sub>I were the first to provide a detailed view of an elementary collision, demonstrating a direct rebound process in which the KI product recoiled from an incoming K atom beam. Subsequent studies of K + Br<sub>2</sub> resulted in the discovery that the hot-wire surface ionization detector they were using was potentially contaminated by previous use, and had to be pre-treated to obtain reliable results. Changes to the instrumentation yielded reliable results, including the observation that the K + Br<sub>2</sub> reaction involved a stripping reaction, in which the KBr product scattered forward from the incident K atom beam. As the research continued, it became possible to correlate the electronic structure of reactants and products with the reaction dynamics.<sup id="cite_ref-James_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963, Herschbach returned to <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> as a professor of chemistry. There he continued his work on molecular-beam reactive dynamics, working with graduate students Sanford Safron and Walter Miller on the reactions of alkali atoms with alkali halides. In 1967, Yuan T. Lee joined the lab as a postdoctoral student, and Herschbach, Lee, and graduate students Doug MacDonald and Pierre LeBreton began to construct a "supermachine" for studying collisions such as Cl + Br<sub>2</sub> and hydrogen and halogen reactions.<sup id="cite_ref-James_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>His most acclaimed work, for which he won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1986 with <a href="/wiki/Yuan_T._Lee" title="Yuan T. Lee">Yuan T. Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_C._Polanyi" class="mw-redirect" title="John C. Polanyi">John C. Polanyi</a>, was his collaboration with Yuan T. Lee on crossed molecular beam experiments. Crossing collimated beams of gas-phase reactants allows partitioning of energy among translational, rotational, and vibrational modes of the product molecules—a vital aspect of understanding reaction <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(physics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynamics (physics)">dynamics</a>. For their contributions to reaction dynamics, Herschbach and Lee are considered to have helped create a new field of research in chemistry.<sup id="cite_ref-NobelPress_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NobelPress-1">[1]</a></sup> Herschbach is a pioneer in molecular stereodynamics, measuring and theoretically interpreting the role of angular momentum and its vector properties in chemical reaction dynamics.<sup id="cite_ref-James_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DOE_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the course of his life's work in research, Herschbach has published over 400 scientific papers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Herschbach has applied his broad expertise in both the theory and practice of <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> to diverse problems in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_physics" title="Chemical physics">chemical physics</a>, including theoretical work on dimensional scaling. One of his studies demonstrated that <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> is in fact spontaneously formed at high pressure and high temperature environments such as those deep in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>; this finding is an exciting indication of abiogenic <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> formation, meaning that the actual amount of hydrocarbons available on earth might be much larger than conventionally assumed under the assumption that all hydrocarbons are <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> His recent work also includes a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Steven_Brams" title="Steven Brams">Steven Brams</a> studying <a href="/wiki/Approval_voting" title="Approval voting">approval voting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-voting_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-voting-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hershbach's teaching ranges from graduate seminars on <a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">chemical kinetics</a> to an introductory undergraduate course in general chemistry that he taught for many years at Harvard, and described as his "most challenging assignment."<sup id="cite_ref-NobelBio_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NobelBio-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pauling_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauling-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Herschbach has been a strong proponent of science education and science among the general public, and frequently gives lectures to students of all ages, imbuing them with his infectious enthusiasm for science and his playful spirit of discovery. Herschbach has also lent his voice to the animated television show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i> for the episode "<a href="/wiki/Treehouse_of_Horror_XIV" title="Treehouse of Horror XIV">Treehouse of Horror XIV</a>" where he is seen presenting the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> to <a href="/wiki/Professor_Frink" title="Professor Frink">Professor Frink</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Simpsons_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpsons-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Oct. 2010, Herschbach participated in the <a href="/wiki/USA_Science_and_Engineering_Festival" title="USA Science and Engineering Festival">USA Science and Engineering Festival</a>'s Lunch with a Laureate program where middle and high school students will get to engage in an informal conversation with a Nobel Prize–winning scientist over a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brown-bag_seminar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brown-bag seminar (page does not exist)">brown-bag lunch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Herschbach is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/USA_Science_and_Engineering_Festival" title="USA Science and Engineering Festival">USA Science and Engineering Festival</a>'s Advisory Board.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Furthermore, Herschbach has participated in the Distinguished Lecture Series of the <a href="/wiki/Research_Science_Institute" title="Research Science Institute">Research Science Institute</a> (RSI), a summer research program for high school students held at <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CEEMIT_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CEEMIT-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although still an active research professor at Harvard, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Texas_A%26M_University" title="Texas A&amp;M University">Texas A&amp;M University</a> faculty September 1, 2005 as a Professor of Physics, teaching one semester per year in the chemical physics program.<sup id="cite_ref-Texas_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Texas-14">[14]</a></sup> As of 2010, he holds the title of Professor Emeritus at Harvard, and remains well known for his involvement as a lecturer and mentor in the Harvard research community. He and his wife Georgene Herschbach also served for several years as the co-Masters of <a href="/wiki/Currier_House_(Harvard_College)" title="Currier House (Harvard College)">Currier House</a>, where they were highly involved in undergraduate life in addition to their full-time duties.<sup id="cite_ref-James_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crimson2011_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson2011-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_service">Public service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dudley_R._Herschbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Public service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He is a board member of the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Arms_Control_and_Non-Proliferation" class="mw-redirect" title="Center for Arms Control and Non-Proliferation">Center for Arms Control and Non-Proliferation</a> and was the chairman of the board for <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Science_%26_the_Public" title="Society for Science &amp; the Public">Society for Science &amp; the Public</a> from 1992-2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Lupton_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lupton-15">[15]</a></sup> Herschbach is a member of the Board of Sponsors of The <a href="/wiki/Bulletin_of_the_Atomic_Scientists" title="Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists">Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Atomic_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atomic-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2003 he was one of 22 Nobel Laureates who signed the <a href="/wiki/Humanism_and_Its_Aspirations" title="Humanism and Its Aspirations">Humanist Manifesto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is also an <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Scout_(Boy_Scouts_of_America)" title="Eagle Scout (Boy Scouts of America)">Eagle Scout</a> and recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Eagle_Scout_Award" title="Distinguished Eagle Scout Award">Distinguished Eagle Scout Award</a> (DESA).<sup id="cite_ref-Lupton_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lupton-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_Endorsement_for_President_of_USA">2016 Endorsement for President of USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dudley_R._Herschbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2016 Endorsement for President of USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Professor Herschbach publicly endorsed <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> for President in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>Herschbach's wife, Georgene Herschbach, served as the Associate Dean of Harvard College for Undergraduate Academic Programs.<sup id="cite_ref-Georgene_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Georgene-20">[20]</a></sup> Prior to retirement in 2009, she chaired Harvard College's influential Committee on Undergraduate Education.<sup id="cite_ref-Dingman_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dingman-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dudley_R._Herschbach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Herschbach is a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society" title="American Philosophical Society">American Philosophical Society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Chemical_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Chemical Society">Royal Chemical Society</a> of Great Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-James_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-2">[2]</a></sup> In addition to the Nobel Prize in Chemistry, he has received a wide variety of national and international awards. These include the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a>, the <a href="/wiki/ACS_Award_in_Pure_Chemistry" title="ACS Award in Pure Chemistry">ACS Award in Pure Chemistry</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Linus Pauling Medal">Linus Pauling Medal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Irving_Langmuir_Award" title="Irving Langmuir Award">Irving Langmuir Award</a><sup id="cite_ref-Texas_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Texas-14">[14]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Chemists_Gold_Medal" title="American Institute of Chemists Gold Medal">American Institute of Chemists Gold Medal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CHF_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CHF-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <b>John F. Kennedy School of Government</b> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> (also known as <b>Harvard Kennedy School</b> and <b>HKS</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Public_policy_school" title="Public policy school">public policy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Public_administration_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Public administration school">public administration school</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The school offers <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degrees" class="mw-redirect" title="Master's degrees">master's degrees</a> in <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">public administration</a>, and <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">international development</a>, grants several <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctoral degrees</a>, and many executive education programs. It conducts research in subjects relating to <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international affairs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. It has close ties to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> (CIA).<sup id="cite_ref-chroniclebartletttakingstock_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chroniclebartletttakingstock-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The School's primary campus is located on <a href="/wiki/Memorials_to_John_F._Kennedy#Roads_and_bridges" class="mw-redirect" title="Memorials to John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, United States. The main buildings overlook the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a>, southwest of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Yard" title="Harvard Yard">Harvard Yard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Square" title="Harvard Square">Harvard Square</a>, on the site of a former <a href="/wiki/Red_Line_(MBTA)" title="Red Line (MBTA)">MBTA Red Line</a> trainyard. The School is adjacent to the public riverfront John F. Kennedy Memorial Park.</p>
<p>In 2015, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Elmendorf" title="Douglas Elmendorf">Douglas Elmendorf</a>, the former director of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> who had previously served as a Harvard faculty member, was named Dean of the Harvard Kennedy School and Don K. Price Professor of Public Policy.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> From 2004 to 2015, the School's Dean was <a href="/wiki/David_T._Ellwood" title="David T. Ellwood">David Ellwood</a>, who was also the Scott M. Black Professor of Political Economy at HKS. Previously, Ellwood was an assistant secretary in the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Health and Human Services">Department of Health and Human Services</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Clinton</a> Administration.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A major $120m expansion and renovation of the campus began in 2015. The project is expected to be completed in late 2017 with an official opening expected in early 2018. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graduate_School_of_Public_Administration">Graduate School of Public Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Graduate School of Public Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harvard Kennedy School was originally the Harvard Graduate School of Public Administration (GSPA), and was founded in 1936 with a $2 million gift (equivalent to ~$30 million in 2010) from <a href="/wiki/Lucius_N._Littauer" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucius N. Littauer">Lucius N. Littauer</a>, a graduate of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Its shield was designed to express the national purpose of the school and was modeled after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Seal_of_the_United_States" title="Great Seal of the United States">U.S. shield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The School drew its initial faculty from Harvard's existing government and economics departments, and welcomed its first students in 1937.</p>
<p>The School's original home was in the Littauer Center north of Harvard Yard, now the home of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Faculty_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences">Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences</a> (FAS) Economics Department. The first students at the Graduate School were so-called "Littauer Fellows," participating in a one-year course listing which later developed into the school's mid-career <a href="/wiki/Master_in_Public_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Master in Public Administration">Master in Public Administration</a> program. In the 1960s, the School began to develop today's public policy degree and course curriculum in the Master in Public Policy program.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaming_and_move">Renaming and move</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renaming and move">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1966, the School was renamed for President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>. By 1978, the faculty—notably presidential scholar and adviser <a href="/wiki/Richard_Neustadt" title="Richard Neustadt">Richard Neustadt</a>, foreign policy scholar and later dean of the School <a href="/wiki/Graham_Allison" class="mw-redirect" title="Graham Allison">Graham Allison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Zeckhauser" title="Richard Zeckhauser">Richard Zeckhauser</a>, and Edith Stokey—had orchestrated the consolidation of the School's programs and research centers in the present campus. Under the terms of Littauer's original grant, the current HKS campus also features a building called Littauer.</p>
<p>In addition to playing a critical role in the development of the School's modern era, Neustadt, who at the time served as the Assistant Dean, was also the founding Director of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Institute_of_Politics" title="Harvard Institute of Politics">Harvard Institute of Politics</a> (IOP), created in 1966 in honor of President Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The IOP has been housed on the Kennedy School campus since 1978, and today the Institute puts on a series of programs, speeches and study groups for Harvard undergraduates and graduate students. The <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John F. Kennedy, Jr.">John F. Kennedy, Jr.</a> Forum in the new Littauer building is both the site of IOP forum events as well as a major social gathering place between HKS courses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_Expansion">Campus Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Campus Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012 the school announced a $500m fundraising campaign of which over $120m was to be used to significantly expand the campus adding 91,000 square feet of space that will include six new classrooms, a new kitchen, and dining facility, offices and meeting spaces, a new student lounge and study space, more collaboration and active learning spaces as well as a redesigned central courtyard. Groundbreaking commenced on May 7, 2015 and is expected to be complete in late 2017 and open officially in 2018 <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>. The school has stated that the new space will be used exclusively to cater to the needs of the existing class sizes and that there will not be a major expansion in the size of the student body. A new canteen, locker rooms, and study spaces have already been opened but major thoroughfares, the courtyard, and walkway along Eliot St. remain under construction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Degrees">Degrees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Degrees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently, Harvard Kennedy School offers four master's degree programs.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The two-year <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Public_Policy" title="Master of Public Policy">Master of Public Policy</a> (MPP) program focuses on <a href="/wiki/Policy_analysis" title="Policy analysis">policy analysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a>, ethics, statistics and negotiations in the public sector.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also three separate <a href="/wiki/Master_in_Public_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Master in Public Administration">Master in Public Administration</a> (MPA) programs: a one-year Mid-Career Program (MC/MPA), intended for professionals more than seven years after college graduation; a two-year MPA program intended for professionals who have an additional graduate degree and are more recently out of school; and a two-year International Development track (MPA/ID) focused on development studies with a strong emphasis on economics and quantitative analysis.</p>
<p>Among the members of the Mid-Career MPA class are the Mason Fellows, who are public and private executives from developing countries. Mason Fellows typically constitute about 50% of the incoming class of Mid Career MPA candidates. The Mason cohort is the most diverse at Harvard in terms of nationalities and ethnicities represented, and it is named after late Harvard Professor and Dean of the Graduate School of Public Administration, now known as the John F. Kennedy School of Government, from 1947 to 1958 <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sagendorph_Mason" title="Edward Sagendorph Mason">Edward Sagendorph Mason</a> who thought of bringing the developing world leaders to Harvard to stand on the cutting edge of development knowledge ultimately aiming for a better world.</p>
<p>In addition to the master's programs, HKS also administers four doctoral programs. PhD degrees are awarded in <a href="/wiki/Political_Economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Political Economy">Political Economy</a> and Government, Public Policy, and <a href="/wiki/Social_Policy" class="mw-redirect" title="Social Policy">Social Policy</a>, in conjunction with the Departments of Government and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">Sociology</a> in the Faculty of Arts and Sciences, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Health_Policy" class="mw-redirect" title="Health Policy">Health Policy</a>, in conjunction with FAS and the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_School_of_Public_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard School of Public Health">Harvard School of Public Health</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_and_concurrent_degrees">Joint and concurrent degrees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Joint and concurrent degrees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Harvard Kennedy School has a number of joint and concurrent degree programs, within Harvard and with other leading universities, which allow students to receive multiple degrees in a reduced period of time. Joint and current students spend at least one year in residence in Cambridge taking HKS courses. At Harvard, HKS joint degree programs are run with <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Design" title="Harvard Graduate School of Design">Harvard Graduate School of Design</a>, and concurrent programs are offered with <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Divinity_School" title="Harvard Divinity School">Harvard Divinity School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a>.</p>
<p>Beyond Harvard, HKS has concurrent degree arrangements with other law, business, and medical schools. These include: <a href="/wiki/MIT_Sloan_School_of_Management" title="MIT Sloan School of Management">MIT Sloan School of Management</a>; <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Business_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanford Business School">Stanford Business School</a>; <a href="/wiki/Tuck_School_of_Business" title="Tuck School of Business">Tuck School of Business</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a>; <a href="/wiki/The_Wharton_School" class="mw-redirect" title="The Wharton School">The Wharton School</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>; <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Law_School" title="Columbia Law School">Columbia Law School</a>; <a href="/wiki/Duke_University_School_of_Law" title="Duke University School of Law">Duke University School of Law</a>; <a href="/wiki/Georgetown_University_Law_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgetown University Law Center">Georgetown University Law Center</a>; <a href="/wiki/New_York_University_School_of_Law" title="New York University School of Law">New York University School of Law</a>; <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University_School_of_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Northwestern University School of Law">Northwestern University School of Law</a>; <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Law_School" title="Stanford Law School">Stanford Law School</a>; <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley_School_of_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="University of California, Berkeley School of Law">University of California, Berkeley School of Law</a>; <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan_Law_School" title="University of Michigan Law School">University of Michigan Law School</a>; <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania_Law_School" title="University of Pennsylvania Law School">University of Pennsylvania Law School</a>; <a href="/wiki/Yale_Law_School" title="Yale Law School">Yale Law School</a>; and <a href="/wiki/UCSF_Medical_Center" title="UCSF Medical Center">UCSF Medical Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Abroad, HKS offers a dual degree with the <a href="/wiki/Graduate_Institute_of_International_and_Development_Studies" title="Graduate Institute of International and Development Studies">Graduate Institute of International and Development Studies</a> in Geneva.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rankings">Rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harvard Kennedy School receives high rankings in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report">U.S. News &amp; World Report</a> listing of top graduate schools of public affairs. In the 2015 rankings,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> HKS is ranked third overall, and is ranked first in the subcategory of health policy, second in public policy analysis and social policy.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Kennedy's foreign affairs offerings are also ranked at or near the top of <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Policy_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign Policy (magazine)">Foreign Policy</a></i> magazine's <a href="/wiki/Inside_the_Ivory_Tower" title="Inside the Ivory Tower">Inside the Ivory Tower</a> survey, which lists the world's top twenty <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a> programs at the undergraduate, Master's and Ph.D. levels.<sup id="cite_ref-ivorytower2012_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ivorytower2012-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2012, for example, the survey ranked HKS first overall for doctoral and undergraduate programs and third overall in the Master's category.<sup id="cite_ref-tripsurvey_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tripsurvey-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>There is an active student life at HKS. Most of the activities are centered on interest-driven student 'caucuses,' the student government (Kennedy School Student Government, known as KSSG), student-edited policy journals, such as the <i>Kennedy School Review</i><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and the <i>Journal of Middle Eastern Politics and Policy</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> a student newspaper (<i>The Citizen</i>), and a number of athletic groups.</p>
<p>Elections for HKS student government are among the most lively of the activities at the School. The KSSG is led by a President, Executive Vice President, functional Vice Presidents (Academic Affairs, International Student Affairs, Professional Development, Diversity and Inclusion among others) and Class Representatives from all the graduate programs of HKS. The KSSG is also responsible for overseeing the interest caucuses at the School.</p>

<p>Students can join the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_Student_Government" title="Harvard Graduate Student Government">Harvard Graduate Student Government</a>, colloquially known as "the HGSG" and formerly known as the "Harvard Graduate Council" or "HGC", is the university-wide representative student government for the twelve graduate and professional schools of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. The HGSG is responsible for advocating student concerns to central administrators – including the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Harvard_University" title="President of Harvard University">President of Harvard University</a>, Provost, Deans of Students, and Deans for the nearly 15,000 graduate and professional students across the twelve schools, organizing large university-wide initiatives and events, administering and providing funding for university-wide student groups (USGs),<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and representing the Harvard graduate student population to other universities and external organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The HGSG has become well known for creating and executing on advocacy initiatives and events focused on the "One Harvard" movement.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The courtyard between the main Kennedy School buildings is a key attraction for students, who gather there to work on their assignments, have lunch, or relax. During the warmer months, the School frequently sponsors beer and barbecue events which give students the opportunity to socialize. During the colder months, "Quorum Calls" are held in one of the indoor atriums, to celebrate the end of each week of HKS courses with friends.</p>

Under Dean Elmendorf, the school has tried to focus its engagement across the political spectrum. This has not been without controversy. Recently, the school came under criticism for offering a fellowship to Chelsea Manning on 13 September 2017. It then publicly rescinded the offer on 15 September 2017 after CIA Director Mike Pompeo, canceled his speaking appointment and sent a letter condemning Harvard for awarding the fellowship.</p>

<p>The Kennedy School has strong ties to the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA).<sup id="cite_ref-chroniclebartletttakingstock_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chroniclebartletttakingstock-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicle_of_Higher_Education" title="The Chronicle of Higher Education">The Chronicle of Higher Education</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Goodman" title="Melvin Goodman">Melvin Goodman</a> says in <i>Whistleblower at the CIA</i> that he was "in charge of dealing with the Kennedy School in 1990, when he was still at the CIA," and that the Kennedy School conducted "CIA-sponsored research" in the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-chroniclebartletttakingstock_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chroniclebartletttakingstock-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>Spy Schools: How the CIA, FBI, and Foreign Intelligence Secretly Exploit America's Universities</i>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Golden" title="Daniel Golden">Daniel Golden</a> says CIA operatives attend the school under false names and make connections with foreign students, who are likely to become leaders in their own countries.<sup id="cite_ref-chroniclebartletttakingstock_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chroniclebartletttakingstock-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Nye" title="Joseph Nye">Joseph Nye</a>, who served as the dean from 1995 to 2004, also said CIA agents attended the school, while pretending to work for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">United States Department of State</a> instead of the CIA.<sup id="cite_ref-chroniclebartletttakingstock_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chroniclebartletttakingstock-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

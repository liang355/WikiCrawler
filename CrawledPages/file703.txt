<p><b>Renaissance art</b> is the painting, sculpture and decorative arts of that period of European history known as the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, emerging as a distinct style in Italy in about 1400, in parallel with developments which occurred in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_philosophy" title="Renaissance philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_literature" title="Renaissance literature">literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_music" title="Renaissance music">music</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Renaissance science">science</a>. Renaissance art, perceived as the noblest of ancient traditions, took as its foundation the art of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical antiquity</a>, but transformed that tradition by absorbing recent developments in the art of Northern Europe and by applying contemporary scientific knowledge. Renaissance art, with <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_Humanism" class="mw-redirect" title="Renaissance Humanism">Renaissance Humanist philosophy</a>, spread throughout Europe, affecting both artists and their patrons with the development of new techniques and new artistic sensibilities. Renaissance art marks the transition of Europe from the medieval period to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern">Early Modern</a> age.</p>

<p>In many parts of Europe, Early Renaissance art was created in parallel with <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Late Medieval art</a>.</p>
<p>The influences upon the development of Renaissance men and women in the early 15th century are those that also affected Philosophy, Literature, Architecture, Theology, Science, Government, and other aspects of society. The following list presents a summary, dealt with more fully in the main articles that are cited above.</p>
<ul>
<li>Classical texts, lost to European scholars for centuries, became available. These included Philosophy, Prose, Poetry, Drama, Science, a thesis on the Arts, and Early Christian Theology.</li>
<li>Simultaneously, Europe gained access to advanced mathematics which had its provenance in the works of Islamic scholars.</li>
<li>The advent of movable type <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> in the 15th century meant that ideas could be disseminated easily, and an increasing number of books were written for a broad public.</li>
<li>The establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Medici">Medici</a> Bank and the subsequent trade it generated brought unprecedented wealth to a single Italian city, <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cosimo_de%27_Medici_the_Elder" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosimo de' Medici the Elder">Cosimo de' Medici</a> set a new standard for patronage of the arts, not associated with the church or monarchy.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">Humanist</a> philosophy meant that man's relationship with humanity, the universe and with God was no longer the exclusive province of the Church.</li>
<li>A revived interest in the <a href="/wiki/Classical_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical literature">Classics</a> brought about the first archaeological study of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> remains by the architect <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a> and sculptor <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a>. The revival of a style of architecture based on classical precedents inspired a corresponding classicism in painting and sculpture, which manifested itself as early as the 1420s in the paintings of <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uccello" class="mw-redirect" title="Uccello">Uccello</a>.</li>
<li>The improvement of <a href="/wiki/Oil_paint" title="Oil paint">oil paint</a> and developments in oil-painting technique by Dutch artists such as <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rogier_van_der_Weyden" title="Rogier van der Weyden">Rogier van der Weyden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugo_van_der_Goes" title="Hugo van der Goes">Hugo van der Goes</a> led to its adoption in Italy from about 1475 and had ultimately lasting effects on painting practices, worldwide.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Serendipity" title="Serendipity">serendipitous</a> presence within the region of <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> in the early 15th century of certain individuals of artistic genius, most notably <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Ghiberti" title="Lorenzo Ghiberti">Ghiberti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca" title="Piero della Francesca">Piero della Francesca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michelozzo" title="Michelozzo">Michelozzo</a> formed an ethos out of which sprang the great masters of the High Renaissance, as well as supporting and encouraging many lesser artists to achieve work of extraordinary quality.<sup id="cite_ref-Hartt_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hartt-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>A similar heritage of artistic achievement occurred in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> through the talented <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Bellini" title="Jacopo Bellini">Bellini</a> family, their influential in-law <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Mantegna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giorgione" title="Giorgione">Giorgione</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hartt_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hartt-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MB_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MB-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>The publication of two treatises by <a href="/wiki/Leone_Battista_Alberti" class="mw-redirect" title="Leone Battista Alberti">Leone Battista Alberti</a>, <i>De Pitura</i> (<i>On Painting</i>), 1435, and <i><a href="/wiki/De_re_aedificatoria" class="mw-redirect" title="De re aedificatoria">De re aedificatoria</a></i> (<i>Ten Books on Architecture</i>), 1452.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Proto-Renaissance_in_Italy,_1280–1400"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-Renaissance_in_Italy.2C_1280.E2.80.931400">Proto-Renaissance in Italy, 1280–1400</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proto-Renaissance in Italy, 1280–1400">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">Italian Renaissance painting</a></div>
<p>In Italy in the late 13th and early 14th centuries, the sculpture of <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Pisano" title="Nicola Pisano">Nicola Pisano</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pisano" title="Giovanni Pisano">Giovanni Pisano</a>, working at <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pistoia" title="Pistoia">Pistoia</a> shows markedly classicising tendencies, probably influenced by the familiarity of these artists with ancient Roman <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagi</a>. Their masterpieces are the pulpits of the Baptistery and <a href="/wiki/Pisa_Cathedral" title="Pisa Cathedral">Cathedral of Pisa</a>.</p>
<p>Contemporary with Giovanni Pisano, the Florentine painter <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a> developed a manner of figurative painting that was unprecedentedly naturalistic, three-dimensional, lifelike and classicist, when compared with that of his contemporaries and teacher <a href="/wiki/Cimabue" title="Cimabue">Cimabue</a>. Giotto, whose greatest work is the cycle of the <i>Life of Christ</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Arena_Chapel" class="mw-redirect" title="Arena Chapel">Arena Chapel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a>, was seen by the 16th century biographer <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> as "rescuing and restoring art" from the "crude, traditional, Byzantine style" prevalent in Italy in the 13th century.</p>
<h3><span id="Early_Netherlandish_art,_1400–1525"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Netherlandish_art.2C_1400.E2.80.931525">Early Netherlandish art, 1400–1525</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early Netherlandish art, 1400–1525">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Early_Netherlandish_painting" title="Early Netherlandish painting">Early Netherlandish painting</a></div>

<p>The painters of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_(terminology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Netherlands (terminology)">Low Countries</a> at this period included <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a>, his brother <a href="/wiki/Hubert_van_Eyck" title="Hubert van Eyck">Hubert van Eyck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Campin" title="Robert Campin">Robert Campin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Memling" title="Hans Memling">Hans Memling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rogier_van_der_Weyden" title="Rogier van der Weyden">Rogier van der Weyden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugo_van_der_Goes" title="Hugo van der Goes">Hugo van der Goes</a>. Their painting developed independently of Early Italian Renaissance painting, and without the influence of a deliberate and conscious striving to revive antiquity. The style of painting grew directly out of the Medieval arts of tempera painting, <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a> and book <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illumination</a>. The media used was oil paint, which had long been utilised for painting leather ceremonial shields and accoutrements, because it was flexible and relatively durable. The earliest Netherlandish oil paintings are meticulous and detailed like tempera paintings. The material lent itself to the depiction of tonal variations and texture, so facilitating the observation of nature in great detail.</p>
<p>The Netherlandish painters did not approach the creation of a picture through a framework of linear perspective and correct proportion. They maintained a Medieval view of hierarchical proportion and religious symbolism, while delighting in a realistic treatment of material elements, both natural and man-made. Jan van Eyck, with his brother Hubert painted <i><a href="/wiki/Ghent_Altarpiece" title="Ghent Altarpiece">The Altarpiece of the Mystical Lamb</a></i>. It is probable that Antonello da Messina became familiar with Van Eyck's work, while in Naples or Sicily. In 1475, Hugo van der Goes' <i><a href="/wiki/Portinari_Altarpiece" title="Portinari Altarpiece">Portinari Altarpiece</a></i> arrived in Florence where it was to have a profound influence on many painters, most immediately <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Ghirlandaio" title="Domenico Ghirlandaio">Domenico Ghirlandaio</a> who painted an altarpiece imitating its elements.</p>
<h3><span id="Early_Renaissance_in_Italy,_1400–1479"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Renaissance_in_Italy.2C_1400.E2.80.931479">Early Renaissance in Italy, 1400–1479</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early Renaissance in Italy, 1400–1479">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg/220px-Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg" width="220" height="355" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg/330px-Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg/440px-Donatello_-_David_-_Floren%C3%A7a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1066" data-file-height="1719" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Donatello, <i><a href="/wiki/David_(Donatello)" title="David (Donatello)">David</a></i> (1440s?) Museo Nazionale del Bargello.</div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">Italian Renaissance painting</a></div>
<p>Although both the Pisanos and Giotto had students and followers, the first truly Renaissance artists were not to emerge in Florence until 1401 with the competition to sculpt a set of <a href="/wiki/Florence_Baptistry#Lorenzo_Ghiberti" class="mw-redirect" title="Florence Baptistry">bronze doors</a> of the Baptistery of <a href="/wiki/Florence_Cathedral" title="Florence Cathedral">Florence Cathedral</a> which drew entries from seven young sculptors including <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a> and the winner, <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Ghiberti" title="Lorenzo Ghiberti">Lorenzo Ghiberti</a>. Brunelleschi, most famous as the architect of the dome of Florence Cathedral and the Church of San Lorenzo, created a number of sculptural works, including a lifesized Crucifix in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Novella" title="Santa Maria Novella">Santa Maria Novella</a>, renowned for its naturalism. His studies of perspective are thought to have influenced the painter <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>. <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a> became renowned as the greatest sculptor of the Early Renaissance, his masterpieces being his Humanist and unusually erotic statue of <a href="/wiki/David_(Bible)" class="mw-redirect" title="David (Bible)">David</a>, one of the icons of the Florentine republic, and his great monument to <a href="/wiki/Gattamelata" class="mw-redirect" title="Gattamelata">Gattamelata</a>, the first large equestrian bronze to be created since Roman times.</p>
<p>The contemporary of Donatello, <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>, was the painterly descendant of Giotto, furthering the trend towards solidity of form and naturalism of face and gesture that he had begun a century earlier. Masaccio completed several panel paintings but is best known for the fresco cycle that he began in the <a href="/wiki/Brancacci_Chapel" title="Brancacci Chapel">Brancacci Chapel</a> with the older artist <a href="/wiki/Masolino" class="mw-redirect" title="Masolino">Masolino</a> and which had profound influence on later painters, including <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>. Masaccio's developments were carried forward in the paintings of <a href="/wiki/Fra_Angelico" title="Fra Angelico">Fra Angelico</a>, particularly in his frescos at the Convent of San Marco in Florence.</p>
<p>The treatment of the elements of perspective and light in painting was of particular concern to 15th-century Florentine painters. <a href="/wiki/Uccello" class="mw-redirect" title="Uccello">Uccello</a> was so obsessed with trying to achieve an appearance of perspective that, according to Vasari, it disturbed his sleep. His solutions can be seen in his masterpiece, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_San_Romano" title="Battle of San Romano">Battle of San Romano</a>. <a href="/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca" title="Piero della Francesca">Piero della Francesca</a> made systematic and scientific studies of both light and linear perspective, the results of which can be seen in his fresco cycle of <i><a href="/wiki/The_History_of_the_True_Cross" title="The History of the True Cross">The History of the True Cross</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/San_Francesco,_Arezzo" title="San Francesco, Arezzo">San Francesco, Arezzo</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, the painter <a href="/wiki/Antonello_da_Messina" title="Antonello da Messina">Antonello da Messina</a> began using oil paints for portraits and religious paintings at a date that preceded other Italian painters, possibly about 1450. He carried this technique north and influenced the painters of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. One of the most significant painters of Northern Italy was <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Andrea Mantegna</a>, who decorated the interior of a room, <i>the <a href="/wiki/Camera_degli_Sposi" title="Camera degli Sposi">Camera degli Sposi</a></i> for his patron <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_III_Gonzaga" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludovico III Gonzaga">Ludovico Gonzaga</a>, setting portraits of the family and court into an illusionistic architectural space.</p>
<p>The end of the Early Renaissance in Italian art is marked, like its beginning, by a particular commission that drew artists together, this time in cooperation rather than competition. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_IV" title="Pope Sixtus IV">Pope Sixtus IV</a> had rebuilt the Papal Chapel, named the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel" title="Sistine Chapel">Sistine Chapel</a> in his honour, and commissioned a group of artists, <a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">Sandro Botticelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Perugino" title="Pietro Perugino">Pietro Perugino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Ghirlandaio" title="Domenico Ghirlandaio">Domenico Ghirlandaio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_Rosselli" title="Cosimo Rosselli">Cosimo Rosselli</a> to decorate its wall with fresco cycles depicting <i>the Life of Christ</i> and <i>the Life of Moses</i>. In the sixteen large paintings, the artists, although each working in his individual style, agreed on principals of format, and utilised the techniques of lighting, linear and atmospheric perspective, anatomy, foreshortening and characterisation that had been carried to a high point in the large Florentine studios of Ghiberti, Verrocchio, Ghirlandaio and Perugino.</p>
<h3><span id="Early_Renaissance_in_France,_1375–1528"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Renaissance_in_France.2C_1375.E2.80.931528">Early Renaissance in France, 1375–1528</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early Renaissance in France, 1375–1528">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The artists of France, (including duchies such as <a href="/wiki/Burgundy_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Burgundy (region)">Burgundy</a>) were often associated with courts, providing illuminated manuscripts and portraits for the nobility as well as devotional paintings and altarpieces. Among the most famous were the <a href="/wiki/Limbourg_brothers" title="Limbourg brothers">Limbourg brothers</a>, Flemish illuminators and creators of the <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%A8s_Riches_Heures_du_Duc_de_Berry" title="Très Riches Heures du Duc de Berry">Très Riches Heures du Duc de Berry</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jean_Fouquet" title="Jean Fouquet">Jean Fouquet</a>, painter of the royal court, visited Italy in 1437 and reflects the influence of Florentine painters such as <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Uccello" title="Paolo Uccello">Paolo Uccello</a>. Although best known for his portraits such as that of <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII of France</a> Fouquet also created illuminations, and is thought to be the inventor of the portrait miniature. There were a number of artists at this date who painted famed altarpieces, that are stylistically quite distinct from both the Italian and the Flemish. These include two enigmatic figures, <a href="/wiki/Enguerrand_Quarton" title="Enguerrand Quarton">Enguerrand Quarton</a> to whom is ascribed the <i>Pieta of Villeneuve-lès-Avignon</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Hey" title="Jean Hey">Jean Hey</a>, otherwise known as "the Master of Moulins" after his most famous work, <i>the Moulins Altarpiece</i>. In these works realism and close observation of the human figure, emotions and lighting are combined with a Medieval formality, which includes gilt backgrounds.</p>
<h3><span id="High_Renaissance_in_Italy,_1475–1525"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Renaissance_in_Italy.2C_1475.E2.80.931525">High Renaissance in Italy, 1475–1525</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: High Renaissance in Italy, 1475–1525">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/High_Renaissance" title="High Renaissance">High Renaissance</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

Michelangelo, (c. 1511) <i><a href="/wiki/The_Creation_of_Adam" title="The Creation of Adam">The Creation of Adam</a></i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel_ceiling" title="Sistine Chapel ceiling">Sistine Chapel ceiling</a></div>

<p>The "universal genius" <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> was to further perfect the aspects of pictorial art (lighting, linear and atmospheric perspective, anatomy, foreshortening and characterisation) that had preoccupied artists of the Early Renaissance, in a lifetime of studying and meticulously recording his observations of the natural world. His adoption of oil paint as his primary media meant that he could depict light and its effects on the landscape and objects more naturally and with greater dramatic effect than had ever been done before, as demonstrated in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa" title="Mona Lisa">Mona Lisa</a></i>. His dissection of cadavers carried forward the understanding of skeletal and muscular anatomy, as seen in the unfinished <i>St Jerome</i>. His depiction of human emotion in <i>The Last Supper</i> set the benchmark for religious painting.</p>
<p>The art of Leonardo's younger contemporary <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> took a very different direction. Michelangelo, in neither his painting nor his sculpture demonstrates any interest in the observation of any natural object except the human body. He perfected his technique in depicting it, while in his early twenties, by the creation of the enormous marble statue of <i>David</i> and the group <i>the Pieta</i>, in <a href="/wiki/St_Peter%27s_Basilica" class="mw-redirect" title="St Peter's Basilica">St Peter's Basilica</a>, Rome. He then set about an exploration of the expressive possibilities of the human anatomy. His commission by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope Julius II</a> to paint the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Chapel_ceiling" title="Sistine Chapel ceiling">Sistine Chapel ceiling</a> resulted in the supreme masterpiece of figurative composition, which was to have profound effect on every subsequent generation of European artists.</p>
<p>Standing alongside Leonardo and Michelangelo as the third great painter of the High Renaissance was the younger <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a>, who in a short life span painted a great number of lifelike and engaging portraits, including those of Pope Julius II and his successor <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a>, and numerous portrayals of the Madonna and Christ Child, including the <a href="/wiki/Sistine_Madonna" title="Sistine Madonna">Sistine Madonna</a>.</p>
<p>In Northern Italy the High Renaissance represented by the religious paintings of <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Bellini" title="Giovanni Bellini">Giovanni Bellini</a> which include several large altarpieces of a type known as "<a href="/wiki/Sacred_Conversation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sacred Conversation">Sacred Conversation</a>" which show a group of saints around the enthroned Madonna. His contemporary <a href="/wiki/Giorgione" title="Giorgione">Giorgione</a> left a small number of enigmatic works, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(painting)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Tempest (painting)">The Tempest</a></i>, the subject of which has remained a matter of speculation. The earliest works of <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a> date from the era of the High Renaissance, including a massive altarpiece <i><a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_the_Virgin_(Titian)" title="Assumption of the Virgin (Titian)">The Assumption of the Virgin</a></i> which combines human action and drama with spectacular colour and atmosphere.</p>

<p>German Renaissance art falls into the broader category of the Renaissance in Northern Europe, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">Northern Renaissance</a>. Renaissance influences began to appear in German art in the 15th century, but this trend was not widespread. Gardner's <i>Art Through the Ages</i> identifies <a href="/wiki/Michael_Pacher" title="Michael Pacher">Michael Pacher</a>, a painter and sculptor, as the first German artist whose work begins to show Italian Renaissance influences. According to that source, Pacher's painting, <i>St. Wolfgang Forces the Devil to Hold His Prayerbook</i> (c.1481), is Late Gothic in style, but also shows the influence of the Italian artist, <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Mantegna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Artisans, such as engravers, became more concerned with aesthetics rather than just perfecting their craft. Germany had master engravers, such as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schongauer" title="Martin Schongauer">Martin Schongauer</a>, who did metal engravings in the late 1400s. Gardner relates this mastery of the graphic arts to advances in printing which occurred in Germany, and says that metal engraving began to replace the woodcut during the Renaissance.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, some artists, such as <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>, continued to do woodcuts. Both Gardner and Russell describe the fine quality of Dürer's woodcuts, with Russell stating, in <i>The World of Dürer</i>, that Dürer "elevated them into high works of art."<sup id="cite_ref-The_World_of_D.C3.BCrer_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_World_of_D.C3.BCrer-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1500s, Renaissance art in Germany became more common as, according to Gardner, "The art of northern Europe during the sixteenth century is characterized by a sudden awareness of the advances made by the Italian Renaissance and by a desire to assimilate this new style as rapidly as possible."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> One of the best known practitioners of German Renaissance art was Albrecht Dürer (1471–1528). Dürer's fascination with classical ideas led him to Italy to study art. Both Gardner and Russell recognized the importance of Durer's contribution to German art in bringing Italian Renaissance styles and ideas to Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-The_World_of_D.C3.BCrer_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_World_of_D.C3.BCrer-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Art_Through_the_Ages_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Art_Through_the_Ages-8">[8]</a></sup> Russell calls this "Opening the Gothic windows of German art,"<sup id="cite_ref-The_World_of_D.C3.BCrer_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_World_of_D.C3.BCrer-6">[6]</a></sup> while Gardner calls it Dürer's "life mission."<sup id="cite_ref-Art_Through_the_Ages_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Art_Through_the_Ages-8">[8]</a></sup> Importantly, as Gardner points out, Dürer "was the first northern artist who fully understood the basic aims of the southern Renaissance,"<sup id="cite_ref-Art_Through_the_Ages_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Art_Through_the_Ages-8">[8]</a></sup> although his style did not always reflect that. The same source says that <a href="/wiki/Hans_Holbein_the_Younger" title="Hans Holbein the Younger">Hans Holbein the Younger</a> (1497–1543) successfully assimilated Italian ideas while also keeping "northern traditions of close realism."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This is contrasted against Dürer's tendency to work in "his own native German style"<sup id="cite_ref-Art_Through_the_Ages_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Art_Through_the_Ages-8">[8]</a></sup> instead of combining German and Italian styles. Other important artists of the German Renaissance were <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Gr%C3%BCnewald" title="Matthias Grünewald">Grünewald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Altdorfer" title="Albrecht Altdorfer">Albrecht Altdorfer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Cranach_the_Elder" title="Lucas Cranach the Elder">Lucas Cranach the Elder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Bosch" title="Hieronymus Bosch">Hieronymus Bosch</a> was a painter of the Netherlands, who employed the type of fanciful forms that were often utilized to decorate borders and letters in illuminated manuscripts, combining plant and animal forms with architectonic ones. When taken from the context of the illumination and peopled with humans, these forms give Bosch's paintings a surreal quality which have no parallel in the work of any other Renaissance painter. His masterpiece is the triptych <i><a href="/wiki/The_Garden_of_Earthly_Delights" title="The Garden of Earthly Delights">The Garden of Earthly Delights</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Britain">Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Britain was very late to late to develop a Renaissance style and most <a href="/wiki/Artists_of_the_Tudor_court" title="Artists of the Tudor court">artists of the Tudor court</a> were imported foreigners, usually from the <a href="/wiki/Low_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Low countries">Low countries</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Hans_Holbein_the_Younger" title="Hans Holbein the Younger">Hans Holbein the Younger</a>, who died in England. One exception was the <a href="/wiki/Portrait_miniature" title="Portrait miniature">portrait miniature</a>, which artists including <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Hilliard" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas Hilliard">Nicholas Hilliard</a> developed into a distinct genre, well before it became popular in the rest of Europe. <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_in_Scotland#Art" title="Renaissance in Scotland">Renaissance art in Scotland</a> was similarly dependent on imported artists, and largely restricted to the court.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes_and_symbolism">Themes and symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Themes and symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Magnificatio.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Magnificatio.jpg/220px-Magnificatio.jpg" width="220" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Magnificatio.jpg/330px-Magnificatio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Magnificatio.jpg/440px-Magnificatio.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2900" data-file-height="2887" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Magnificatio.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">Sandro Botticelli</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Magnificat_Madonna" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnificat Madonna">Magnificat Madonna</a></i>, 1480–81, tempera on panel, <a href="/wiki/Uffizi" title="Uffizi">Uffizi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Renaissance artists painted a wide variety of themes. Religious <a href="/wiki/Altarpiece" title="Altarpiece">altarpieces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> cycles, and small works for private devotion were very popular. For inspiration, painters in both Italy and northern Europe frequently turned to <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_de_Voragine" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacobus de Voragine">Jacobus de Voragine</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Legend" title="Golden Legend">Golden Legend</a></i> (1260), a highly influential source book for the lives of <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a> that had already had a strong influence on Medieval artists. The rebirth of classical antiquity and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a> also resulted in many <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">Mythological</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_painting" title="History painting">history paintings</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ovidian" class="mw-redirect" title="Ovidian">Ovidian</a> stories, for example, were very popular. Decorative <a href="/wiki/Ornament_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornament (architecture)">ornament</a>, often used in painted architectural elements, was especially influenced by classical Roman motifs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>the use of <a href="/wiki/Proportion_(architecture)" title="Proportion (architecture)">proportion</a> – The first major treatment of the painting as a window into space appeared in the work of <a href="/wiki/Giotto_di_Bondone" class="mw-redirect" title="Giotto di Bondone">Giotto di Bondone</a>, at the beginning of the 14th century. True linear perspective was formalized later, by <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Filippo Brunelleschi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leon_Battista_Alberti" title="Leon Battista Alberti">Leon Battista Alberti</a>. In addition to giving a more realistic presentation of art, it moved Renaissance painters into composing more paintings.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Foreshortening" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreshortening">foreshortening</a> – The term foreshortening refers to the artistic effect of shortening lines in a drawing so as to create an illusion of depth.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sfumato" title="Sfumato">sfumato</a> – The term sfumato was coined by Italian Renaissance artist, Leonardo da Vinci, and refers to a fine art painting technique of blurring or softening of sharp outlines by subtle and gradual blending of one tone into another through the use of thin glazes to give the illusion of depth or three-dimensionality. This stems from the Italian word sfumare meaning to evaporate or to fade out. The Latin origin is fumare, to smoke.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">chiaroscuro</a> – The term chiaroscuro refers to the fine art painting modeling effect of using a strong contrast between light and dark to give the illusion of depth or three-dimensionality. This comes from the Italian words meaning light (chiaro) and dark (scuro), a technique which came into wide use in the Baroque Period.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="German_artists">German artists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: German artists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

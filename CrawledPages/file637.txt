<p>The <b>Harvard John A. Paulson School of Engineering and Applied Sciences</b> (<b>SEAS</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Engineering_education" title="Engineering education">engineering school</a> within <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Faculty_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences">Faculty of Arts and Sciences (FAS)</a>. It offers undergraduate and graduate degrees in engineering and applied sciences. On July 1, 2015, <a href="/wiki/Francis_J._Doyle_III" title="Francis J. Doyle III">Francis J. Doyle III</a> assumed the leadership of the School as its dean.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_John_A._Paulson_School_of_Engineering_and_Applied_Sciences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</tr>
<tr>
<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center"><a href="/wiki/File:Abbott_Lawrence.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Abbott Lawrence.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Abbott_Lawrence.jpg/220px-Abbott_Lawrence.jpg" width="220" height="276" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Abbott_Lawrence.jpg/330px-Abbott_Lawrence.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0e/Abbott_Lawrence.jpg 2x" data-file-width="436" data-file-height="547" /></a></td>

<p>The formation of the Lawrence Scientific School at Harvard University in 1847 marked Harvard's first major effort to provide a formal, advanced education in science and engineering.</p>
<p>The school was named for Massachusetts industrialist and entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Abbott_Lawrence" title="Abbott Lawrence">Abbott Lawrence</a>, who donated $50,000 (a then-unprecedented sum) to create the institution. While he did not attend Harvard, he had a long personal history with key faculty members such as <a href="/wiki/Louis_Agassiz" title="Louis Agassiz">Louis Agassiz</a> and enjoyed the pursuit of and understood the value in science and engineering. In the letter that accompanied his gift, Lawrence explained his rationale for forming a school:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>But where can we send those who intend to devote themselves to the practical applications of science? Our country abounds in men of action. Hard hands are ready to work upon our hard materials; and where shall sagacious heads be taught to direct those hands?</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><a href="/wiki/Abbott_Lawrence" title="Abbott Lawrence">Abbott Lawrence</a>, <sup id="cite_ref-Abbott_Lawrence_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abbott_Lawrence-3">[3]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>James Emmanuel, Jr. was the first Dean.</p>
<p>The School hosted astronomers, architects, naturalists, engineers, mathematicians and even philosophers.</p>
<p>The School’s initial success did not escape the notice of other institutions, leading <a href="/wiki/William_Greenleaf_Eliot" title="William Greenleaf Eliot">William Greenleaf Eliot</a>, president of Eliot Seminary (later renamed <a href="/wiki/Washington_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington University">Washington University</a>) to declare in 1854:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Harvard University is, at this time, gaining more credit and accomplishing greater good, by the Lawrence Scientific School than by any other agency. We need just such a school, here. Its effect would be to elevate mechanical, agricultural, and mercantile pursuits, into learned professions. It would annihilate that absurd distinction by which three pursuits, of Law, Medicine, and Theology, are called professions, and everything else, labor or trade...</p>
<div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><a href="/wiki/William_Greenleaf_Eliot" title="William Greenleaf Eliot">William Greenleaf Eliot</a>, president of Eliot Seminary, <sup id="cite_ref-Historical_Profiles_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_Profiles-4">[4]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>While the School initially thrived, by the latter decades of the 19th century the institution faced increasing competition from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT, founded in 1861) and was constrained by the uncertain views about its role and status by the long-serving Harvard President <a href="/wiki/Charles_William_Eliot" title="Charles William Eliot">Charles William Eliot</a>. Eliot was involved in at least five unsuccessful attempts to absorb his former employer (MIT) into Harvard.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-5">[5]</a></sup> As a result of such uncertainty, the Lawrence Scientific School became less of an independent entity, losing its influence and students to other parts of College and University.</p>
<p>In 1891 industrialist <a href="/wiki/Gordon_McKay" title="Gordon McKay">Gordon McKay</a> designated the Lawrence Scientific School his beneficiary. The American inventor, engineer and entrepreneur was best known for machinery that revolutionized the shoe manufacturing. In 1906, before the first payment from his bequest, Lawrence's scientific and engineering programs were incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Harvard Graduate School of Arts and Sciences">Graduate School of Arts and Sciences</a>. The School ceased to exist as an independent entity. (McKay's gift still supports over 40 endowed professorships).</p>
<p>In 1914, a merger of MIT and Harvard's Applied Science departments was formally announced<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and was to begin "when the Institute will occupy its splendid new buildings in Cambridge."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, in 1917, the arrangement with MIT was cancelled due to a decision by the State Judicial Court, so Harvard University President <a href="/wiki/Abbott_Lawrence_Lowell" title="Abbott Lawrence Lowell">Abbott Lawrence Lowell</a> moved to establish the Harvard Engineering School independently instead.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1934, the School began offering graduate-level and professional programs in engineering. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Harvard participated in the <a href="/wiki/V-12_Navy_College_Training_Program" title="V-12 Navy College Training Program">V-12 Navy College Training Program</a> to provide training for commissioned officers. In 1942 the undergraduate Department of Engineering Sciences changed to the Department of Engineering Sciences and Applied Physics to reflect an increased emphasis on applied physics. President <a href="/wiki/James_Bryant_Conant" title="James Bryant Conant">James Bryant Conant</a> created what was known as "Conant's Arsenal", a research hub for defense-related engineering projects including <a href="/wiki/Radar_jamming" class="mw-redirect" title="Radar jamming">radar jamming</a>, night vision, aerial photography, <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a>, explosives, napalm, and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bomb</a> research.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> One notable project from this era was the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_I" title="Harvard Mark I">Harvard Mark I</a> computer; one of the first programs to run on the Mark I was initiated on 29 March 1944 by <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a>, who worked on the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> at the time, and needed to determine whether implosion was a viable choice to detonate the atomic bomb that would be used a year later.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Mark I also computed and printed mathematical tables, which had been the initial goal of British inventor <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage" title="Charles Babbage">Charles Babbage</a> for his "analytical engine".</p>
<p>By 1945, Harvard income from government contracts was $33.5 million, the third highest among U.S. universities.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Between 1946 and 1949 the Graduate School of Engineering merged its faculty with the undergraduate program, the Department of Engineering Sciences and Applied Physics, into the Division of Engineering Sciences within the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Faculty_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences">Harvard Faculty of Arts and Sciences</a>.</p>
<p>In February 2007, the <a href="/wiki/President_and_Fellows_of_Harvard_College" title="President and Fellows of Harvard College">Harvard Corporation and Overseers</a> voted for the Division of Engineering and Applied Sciences to change its name to the School of Engineering and Applied Sciences (SEAS).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In September 2008, "Engineering a Renaissance”<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> was held to mark the creation of Harvard’s first new school in seven decades.</p>
<p>On March 10, 2009, <a href="/wiki/Cherry_A._Murray" title="Cherry A. Murray">Cherry A. Murray</a>, a former executive at <a href="/wiki/Bell_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="Bell Laboratories">Bell Laboratories</a> and the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> was appointed dean, effective July 1, 2009. She also became the John A. and Elizabeth S. Armstrong Professor of Engineering and Applied Sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> At the end of 2014, she resigned and returned, after a sabbatical, to teaching.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> She was reappointed as Benjamin Peirce Professor of Technology and Public Policy and Professor of Physics.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 3, 2015, the school was renamed the Harvard John A. Paulson School of Engineering and Applied Sciences following a $400 million gift by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a> alumnus <a href="/wiki/John_Paulson" title="John Paulson">John A. Paulson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nytimes1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 14, 2015, Francis J. Doyle III, a distinguished scholar in chemical engineering at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara" title="University of California, Santa Barbara">University of California, Santa Barbara</a> (UCSB) and director of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Collaborative_Biotechnologies" title="Institute for Collaborative Biotechnologies">Institute for Collaborative Biotechnologies</a>, was appointed Dean, effective August 1, 2015. Doyle succeeded <a href="/wiki/Harry_R._Lewis" title="Harry R. Lewis">Harry R. Lewis</a>, Gordon McKay Professor of Computer Science, who had served as interim dean since the end of 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics_and_research">Academics and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_John_A._Paulson_School_of_Engineering_and_Applied_Sciences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academics and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Undergraduates can pursue programs in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> (AB and as a secondary field), engineering sciences (AB and SB, both of which are ABET accredited), and <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">applied mathematics</a> (AB and as a secondary field).</p>
<p>At the graduate level, the division offers SM, ME, and PhD options covering interdisciplinary research areas including applied mathematics, applied physics, <a href="/wiki/Biological_engineering" title="Biological engineering">bioengineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a>, computational science and engineering, computer science, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a> and engineering, and <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a>. In addition, graduate students may pursue collaborative options: Engineering and Physical Biology (with the Faculty of Arts and Sciences); Science, Technology and Management (joint with HBS); Medical Engineering and Medical Physics; (Harvard/MIT Division of Health Sciences and Technology); and Systems Biology (with <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a>).</p>
<p>Faculty number approximately eighty<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> (73 <a href="/wiki/Full-time" title="Full-time">FTEs</a>) who account for nearly $40M in annual research funds (2007/8 figure). The faculty have particularly close ties (including joint appointments) with the departments of Physics, Earth and Planetary Science and Chemistry and Chemical Biology. The facilities provide 400,000 square feet (37,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) of interconnected labs, classrooms, clusters and offices in six buildings.</p>
<p>Areas of particular research focus include Applied Mathematics, Applied Physics, Bioengineering, Computer Science, Electrical Engineering, Environmental Sciences and Engineering and Mechanical Engineering.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_highlights">Research highlights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_John_A._Paulson_School_of_Engineering_and_Applied_Sciences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research highlights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_John_A._Paulson_School_of_Engineering_and_Applied_Sciences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1919 – <a href="/wiki/G._W._Pierce" title="G. W. Pierce">George Washington Pierce</a> (PhD, 1900), Rumford Professor of Physics and director of Harvard's Cruft High-Tension Electrical Laboratory invented an oscillator that enabled a given radio station to stay "fixed" at a proper frequency and allowed multiple telephone calls to occur over a single line.</li>
<li>1938 – A <a href="/wiki/Cyclotron" title="Cyclotron">cyclotron</a> was constructed at the Graduate School of Engineering's Gordon McKay Engineering Laboratory to support research in biology and medicine as well as physics. It was projected to be the world's largest such facility. In 1942, it was sent to Los Alamos for work on the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> to develop an atomic bomb.</li>
<li>1944 – Howard Aiken '37 (PhD) developed the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_I" title="Harvard Mark I">Mark I</a> series of computers, the first large-scale automatic digital computer in the US. Around the same time, a new generation of technically trained students began to share their knowledge well beyond Harvard's campus. Alumnus and donor Allen E. Puckett SB '39, SM '41 created an endowed professorship at SEAS), went on to define modern aerodynamics, served as CEO of <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes Aircraft</a> and won the National Medal of Honor in Technology.</li>
<li>1952 – <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">Nuclear Magnetic Resonance</a> (NMR), the scientific foundation for MRI (used in modern medical imaging systems), was pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Nicolaas_Bloembergen" title="Nicolaas Bloembergen">Nicolaas Bloembergen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Mills_Purcell" title="Edward Mills Purcell">Edward Purcell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Pound" title="Robert Pound">Robert Pound</a>. Purcell won the 1952 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> for this discovery and the 2003 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Medicine</a> was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lauterbur" title="Paul Lauterbur">Paul C. Lauterbur</a> (University of Illinois, Urbana) and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Mansfield" title="Peter Mansfield">Peter Mansfield</a> (University of Nottingham, School of Physics and Astronomy Nottingham, United Kingdom) for work leading to MRI imaging.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1995_to_2006">1995 to 2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_John_A._Paulson_School_of_Engineering_and_Applied_Sciences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1995 to 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Stopping light – <a href="/wiki/Lene_Hau" title="Lene Hau">Lene Hau</a> and her colleagues created a new form of matter, a <a href="/wiki/Bose-Einstein_Condensate" class="mw-redirect" title="Bose-Einstein Condensate">Bose-Einstein Condensate</a>, to slow light to 17 miles an hour and later to bring a light beam to a complete stop, then restart it again.</li>
<li>Unbreakable hyper-encryption – <a href="/wiki/Michael_O._Rabin" title="Michael O. Rabin">Michael O. Rabin</a> embedded messages in rapidly moving streams of random digital bits in ways that cannot be decoded, even with unlimited computing power.</li>
<li>Black silicon – <a href="/wiki/Eric_Mazur" title="Eric Mazur">Eric Mazur</a>'s group created a new material that efficiently traps light and has potential use in solar cells, global warming sensors and ultra-thin television screens.</li>
<li>The mathematics of nature – <a href="/wiki/L._Mahadevan" class="mw-redirect" title="L. Mahadevan">L. Mahadevan</a> and colleagues discovered how the Venus flytrap snaps up its prey in a mere tenth of a second by actively shifting the curved shape of its mouth-like leaves.</li>
<li>Atmospheric modeling – Loretta J. Mickley, Dan Jacob and colleagues found that the frequency of cold fronts bringing cool, clear air out of Canada during the summer months declined by about 20 percent. These cold fronts are responsible for breaking up the hot, stagnant air that builds up regularly in summer, generating high levels of ground level ozone pollution.</li>
<li>High speed nanowire circuits – <a href="/wiki/Donhee_Ham" title="Donhee Ham">Donhee Ham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lieber" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Lieber">Charles Lieber</a> made robust circuits from minuscule nanowires that align themselves on a chip of glass during low-temperature fabrication, creating rudimentary electronic devices that offer performance without high-temperature production or high-priced silicon.</li>
<li>Double emulsions – A new microfluidics-based device made by <a href="/wiki/David_A._Weitz" title="David A. Weitz">David A. Weitz</a> and colleagues at Harvard University and <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a> makes precisely controlled double <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsions</a> in a single step. Double emulsions—droplets inside droplets—could be useful for encapsulating products such as drugs, cosmetics, or food additives.</li>
<li>1999 – <a href="/wiki/Steven_A._Ballmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Steven A. Ballmer">Steven A. Ballmer</a> and Bill Gates financed the construction of the Maxwell Dworkin Laboratory—the building is named after Ballmer's and Gates's mothers.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="2006–"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2006.E2.80.93">2006–</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_John_A._Paulson_School_of_Engineering_and_Applied_Sciences&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2006–">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Applied physicist <a href="/wiki/Lene_Hau" title="Lene Hau">Lene Hau</a> caused a light pulse disappeared from one cold cloud then was retrieved from another cloud nearby. In the process, light was converted into matter then back into light.</li>
<li>A research team led by Mike Aziz and Earth and Planetary Sciences' Kurt House invented an engineered weathering process that might mitigate <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.</li>
<li>Bio-Engineers, including <a href="/wiki/David_Edwards_(engineer)" title="David Edwards (engineer)">David Edwards</a> and public health researchers at the School of Public Health developed a novel spray-drying method for preserving and delivering a <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> vaccine that could help prevent the related spread of <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> in the developing world.</li>
<li>Working with a team of Dutch researchers and software developers, SEAS computer scientists used a novel peer-to-peer video sharing application to explore a model for e-commerce that uses bandwidth as a global currency.</li>
<li>Rob Wood's team launched a robotic fly that could be used in everything from surveillance to chemical sensing.</li>
<li>MIT's <i><a href="/wiki/Technology_Review" class="mw-redirect" title="Technology Review">Technology Review</a></i> named the creation of light-focusing optical antennas (that could lead to DVDs that hold hundreds of movies) as one of their Top 10 emerging technologies for 2007.</li>
<li>Kit Parker's lab found that an elastic film coated with a single layer of cardiac muscle cells can semi-autonomously engage in lifelike gripping, pumping, walking and swimming.</li>
<li>Nan Sun and <a href="/wiki/Donhee_Ham" title="Donhee Ham">Donhee Ham</a> built what may be the smallest complete nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) system to date in a 0.1-kilogram (0.22&#160;lb) package.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>Engineers and applied physicists demonstrated the first room-temperature electrically pumped <a href="/wiki/Laser_diode" title="Laser diode">semiconductor laser</a> source of <a href="/wiki/Terahertz_radiation" title="Terahertz radiation">terahertz</a> (THz) radiation, also known as T-rays.</li>
<li>A team composed of Harvard students and alumni was among the winners of the World Bank's <a href="/wiki/Lighting_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Lighting Africa">Lighting Africa</a> 2008 Development Marketplace competition, held in Accra, Ghana. The innovation, <a href="/wiki/Microbial_fuel_cell" title="Microbial fuel cell">microbial fuel cell</a>-based lighting systems suitable for Sub-Saharan Africa, netted the group a $200,000 prize.</li>
<li>In collaboration with SiEnergy Systems, materials scientists at SEAS have demonstrated the first macro-scale thin-film solid-oxide fuel cell (SOFC).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>An interdisciplinary research institution that investigates digitized <a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">text corpuses</a> containing about 4% of all books ever printed in English, between 1800 and 2000. It was co-founded and co-directed by Erez Aiden and Jean-Baptiste Michel, whose prototype was instrumental in creating <a href="/wiki/Google_Ngram_Viewer" title="Google Ngram Viewer">Google Ngram Viewer</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>The <b>Arnold Arboretum</b> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Arboretum" title="Arboretum">arboretum</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Plain,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Jamaica Plain, Massachusetts">Jamaica Plain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roslindale,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Roslindale, Massachusetts">Roslindale</a> sections of <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>. It was designed by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted" title="Frederick Law Olmsted">Frederick Law Olmsted</a> and is the second largest "link" in the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_Necklace" title="Emerald Necklace">Emerald Necklace</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ArnoldArboretum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b3/ArnoldArboretum.jpg/220px-ArnoldArboretum.jpg" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b3/ArnoldArboretum.jpg/330px-ArnoldArboretum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b3/ArnoldArboretum.jpg/440px-ArnoldArboretum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1679" data-file-height="1238" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ArnoldArboretum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sign at the main gate</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Arboretum was founded in 1872 when the President and Fellows of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College#History" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> became trustees of a portion of the estate of James Arnold (1781–1868), a <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">whaling merchant</a> from <a href="/wiki/New_Bedford,_Massachusetts" title="New Bedford, Massachusetts">New Bedford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.</p>
<p>In 1842, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Bussey" title="Benjamin Bussey">Benjamin Bussey</a> (1757–1842), a prosperous Boston merchant and scientific farmer, donated his country estate <i>Woodland Hill</i> and a part of his fortune to Harvard University "for instruction in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticulture</a>, and related subjects". Bussey had inherited land from fellow patriot <a href="/wiki/Weld_Family" class="mw-redirect" title="Weld Family">Eleazer Weld</a> in 1800 and further enlarged his large estate between 1806 and 1837 by acquiring and consolidating various farms that had been established as early as the seventeenth century. Harvard used this land for the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Bussey_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Bussey Institute">Bussey Institute</a>, which was dedicated to agricultural experimentation. The first Bussey Institute building was completed in 1871 and served as headquarters for an <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a> school of agriculture.</p>
<p>Sixteen years after Bussey's death, James Arnold specified that a portion of his estate was to be used for "...the promotion of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agricultural</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">Horticultural</a> improvements". In 1872, when the <a href="/wiki/Trustee" title="Trustee">trustees</a> of the will of James Arnold transferred his estate to Harvard University, Arnold’s gift was combined with 120 acres (0.49&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of the former Bussey estate to create the Arnold Arboretum. In the deed of trust between the Arnold trustees and the College, income from Arnold’s legacy was to be used for establishing, developing and maintaining an arboretum to be known as the Arnold Arboretum, which "shall contain, as far as practicable, all the trees [and] shrubs ... either indigenous or exotic, which can be raised in the open air of <a href="/wiki/West_Roxbury" title="West Roxbury">West Roxbury</a>". The historical mission of the Arnold Arboretum is to increase knowledge of woody plants through research and to disseminate this knowledge through education.</p>
<p>In 1872 <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sprague_Sargent" title="Charles Sprague Sargent">Charles Sprague Sargent</a> was appointed director and Arnold Professor of <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">Botany</a> shortly after the establishment of the institution.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Together with the landscape architect <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted" title="Frederick Law Olmsted">Frederick Law Olmsted</a>, he developed the road and pathway system and delineated the collection areas by <a href="/wiki/Scientific_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific classification">family</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>, following the then-current and widely accepted classification system of <a href="/wiki/George_Bentham" title="George Bentham">Bentham</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Jackson_Hooker" title="William Jackson Hooker">Hooker</a>. The Hunnewell building was designed by architect <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Wadsworth_Longfellow,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander Wadsworth Longfellow, Jr.">Alexander Wadsworth Longfellow, Jr.</a> in 1892 and constructed with funds donated by <a href="/wiki/H._H._Hunnewell" title="H. H. Hunnewell">H. H. Hunnewell</a> in 1903.</p>
<p>From 1946 to 1950 the landscape architect <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_Farrand" title="Beatrix Farrand">Beatrix Farrand</a> was the landscape design consultant for the Arboretum. Her early training in the 1890s included time with Charles Sprague Sargent and Jackson Thornton Johnson, the chief propagator and superintendent .<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Today the Arboretum occupies 281 acres (114&#160;<a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">ha</a>) of land divided between four parcels, viz. the main Arboretum and the Peters Hill, Weld-Walter and South Street tracts. The collections are located primarily in the main Arboretum and on the Peters Hill tract. The Arboretum remains one of the finest examples of a landscape designed by Frederick Law Olmsted. It is known as the <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted_National_Historic_Site" title="Frederick Law Olmsted National Historic Site">Frederick Law Olmsted National Historic Site</a> and has been designated as a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a>.</p>
<p>Dr. Ned Friedman is the eighth and current Director of the Arnold Arboretum. He is also the Arnold Professor of Organismic and Evolutionary Biology at Harvard University.</p>

<p>The Arboretum is privately endowed as a department of Harvard University. The land was deeded to the City of Boston in 1882 and incorporated into the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Emerald_Necklace" title="Emerald Necklace">Emerald Necklace</a>". Under the agreement with the City, Harvard University was given a thousand-year lease on the property, and the University, as trustee, is directly responsible for the development, maintenance, and operation of the Arboretum; the City retains responsibility for water fountains, benches, roads, boundaries, and policing. The annual operating budget of $7,350,644 (fiscal year 2003) is largely derived from the endowment, which is also managed by the University. All Arboretum staff are University employees. Other income is obtained through granting agencies and contributors.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Average yearly rainfall is 43.63&#160;inches (1,102&#160;mm); average snowfall, 40.2&#160;inches (102 centimeters). Monthly mean temperature is 51.5 °F (10.8 °C); July's mean temperature is 73.5 °F (23 °C); January's is 29.6 °F (-1.3 °C). The Arboretum is located in USDA hardiness zone 6b (0 to −5 °F, −18 to −21 °C).</p>
<h2><span id="Collections_(as_of_February_2011)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections_.28as_of_February_2011.29">Collections (as of February 2011)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Collections (as of February 2011)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At present, the living collections include 14,980 individual plants (including nursery holdings) belonging to 10,117 accessions representing 3,924 taxa; with particular emphasis on the <a href="/wiki/Woody_plant" title="Woody plant">ligneous</a> species of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Historic collections include the plant introductions from eastern Asia made by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sprague_Sargent" title="Charles Sprague Sargent">Charles Sprague Sargent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Henry_Wilson" title="Ernest Henry Wilson">Ernest Henry Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Purdom" title="William Purdom">William Purdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Hers" title="Joseph Hers">Joseph Hers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Rock" title="Joseph Rock">Joseph Rock</a>. Recent introductions from Asia have resulted from the 1977 Arnold Arboretum Expedition to <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, the 1980 Sino-American Botanical Expedition to western <a href="/wiki/Hubei_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Hubei Province">Hubei Province</a>, and more recent expeditions to <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People's Republic of China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.</p>
<p>Comprehensive collections are maintained and augmented for most genera, and genera that have received particular emphasis include: <i><a href="/wiki/Acer_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Acer (genus)">Acer</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">Fagus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Carya" class="mw-redirect" title="Carya">Carya</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Forsythia" title="Forsythia">Forsythia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Taxodium" title="Taxodium">Taxodium</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus" class="mw-redirect" title="Pinus">Pinus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Metasequoia" title="Metasequoia">Metasequoia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lonicera" class="mw-redirect" title="Lonicera">Lonicera</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Magnolia" title="Magnolia">Magnolia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Malus" title="Malus">Malus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Quercus" class="mw-redirect" title="Quercus">Quercus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">Rhododendron</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Syringa" title="Syringa">Syringa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Paulownia" title="Paulownia">Paulownia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Albizia" title="Albizia">Albizia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ilex" class="mw-redirect" title="Ilex">Ilex</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gleditsia" title="Gleditsia">Gleditsia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tsuga" title="Tsuga">Tsuga</a></i>. Other comprehensive collections include the Bradley Collection of <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">Rosaceous Plants</a>, the collection of <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifers</a> and dwarf conifers, and the <a href="/wiki/Larz_Anderson_Bonsai_Collection" title="Larz Anderson Bonsai Collection">Larz Anderson Bonsai Collection</a>. Approximately 500 accessions are processed annually.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections_policy">Collections policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Collections policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ArnoldArboretumBirch20040415-01s.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/ArnoldArboretumBirch20040415-01s.jpg/170px-ArnoldArboretumBirch20040415-01s.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/ArnoldArboretumBirch20040415-01s.jpg/255px-ArnoldArboretumBirch20040415-01s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/14/ArnoldArboretumBirch20040415-01s.jpg 2x" data-file-width="320" data-file-height="427" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ArnoldArboretumBirch20040415-01s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">birch</a> tree in early spring</div>
</div>

<p>The mission of the Arnold Arboretum is to increase knowledge of the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> and biology of woody plants. Historically, this research has investigated the global distribution and evolutionary history of trees, shrubs and vines, with particular emphasis on the disjunct species of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> and North America. Today this work continues through molecular studies of the evolution and bio-geography of the floras of temperate Asia, North America and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.</p>
<p>Research activities include molecular studies of gene evolution, investigations of plant-water relations, and the monitoring of plant phenology, vegetation succession, nutrient cycling and other factors that inform studies of environmental change. Applied work in horticulture uses the collections for studies in plant propagation, plant introduction, and environmental management. This diversity of scientific investigation is founded in a continuing commitment to acquire, grow, and document the recognized species and infraspecific <a href="/wiki/Taxa" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxa">taxa</a> of <a href="/wiki/Woody_plant" title="Woody plant">ligneous plants</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> that are able to withstand the climate of the Arboretum’s 281-acre (1.14&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) Jamaica Plain/Roslindale site.</p>
<p>As a primary resource for research in plant biology, the Arboretum’s living collections are developed, curated, and managed to support scientific investigation and study. To this end, acquisition policies place priority on obtaining plants that are genetically representative of documented wild populations. For each taxon, the Arnold Arboretum aspires to grow multiple accessions of known wild provenance in order to represent significant variation that may occur across the geographic range of the species. Accessions of garden or cultivated provenance are also acquired as governed by the collections policies herein.</p>
<p>For all specimens, full documentation of both provenance and history within the collection is a critical priority. Curatorial procedures provide for complete and accurate records for each accession, and document original provenance, locations in the collections, and changes in botanical identity. <a href="/wiki/Herbarium" title="Herbarium">Herbarium</a> specimens, <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> materials, and <a href="/wiki/Digital_images" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital images">digital images</a> are gathered for the collection and maintained in Arboretum data systems and the herbarium at the Roslindale site.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Research on plant pathology and integrated pest management for maintenance of the living collections is constantly ongoing. Herbarium-based research focuses on the systematics and biodiversity of both temperate and tropical Asian forests, as well as the ecology and potential for sustainable use of their resources. The Arboretum's education programs offer school groups and the general public a wide range of lectures, courses, and walks focusing on the ecology and cultivation of plants. Its quarterly magazine, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20091007133215/http://arnoldia.arboretum.harvard.edu/static/home"><i>Arnoldia</i></a>, provides in-depth information on horticulture, botany, and garden history. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20051228104826/http://www.arboretum.harvard.edu/research/research.html">Current Research Initiatives</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_records">Plant records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Plant records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plant records are maintained on a computerized database, <i>BG-BASE</i> 6.8 (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://rbg-web2.rbge.org.uk/BG-BASE/welcome.htm#top"><i>BG-BASE</i> Inc.</a>), which was initiated in 1985 at the request of the Arnold Arboretum and the Threatened Plants Unit (TPU) of the World Conservation Monitoring Centre (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.unep-wcmc.org/">WCMC</a>). Currently the Arboretum utilizes a suite of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.esri.com/">ESRI</a> Desktop and Mobile <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.gis.com/">GIS</a> software applications to manage, analyze, query, capture, manipulate, and display geographic information. A computer-driven embosser generates records labels. All accessioned plants in the collections are labeled with accession number, botanical name, and <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a> name (when appropriate), source information, common name, and map location. Trunk and/or display labels are also hung on many accessions and include botanical and common names and nativity. Stake labels are used to identify plants located in the Leventritt Garden and Chinese Path.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grounds_maintenance">Grounds maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Grounds maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The grounds staff consists of the manager of horticulture, three <a href="/wiki/Arborists" class="mw-redirect" title="Arborists">arborists</a>, ten horticultural technologists, a foreman, and a gardener. During the summer months ten horticultural interns supplement the grounds staff. A wide array of vehicles and modern equipment, including an aerial lift truck and a <a href="/wiki/John_Deere" title="John Deere">John Deere</a> backhoe and front loader, are used in grounds maintenance. Permanent grounds staff, excluding the manager, are members of <a href="/wiki/AFL/CIO" class="mw-redirect" title="AFL/CIO">AFL/CIO</a> Local 615, Service Employees International Union (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.seiu.org/">SEIU</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nursery_and_greenhouse_facilities">Nursery and greenhouse facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nursery and greenhouse facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://arboretum.harvard.edu/plants/collections-management/propagation-distribution/">Dana Greenhouses</a>, located at 1050 Centre Street (with a mailing address of 125 Arborway), were completed in 1962. They comprise four service <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a> totaling 3,744 square feet (348 m²), the headhouse with offices, cold rooms, storage areas, and a classroom. Staffing at the greenhouse includes the manager of greenhouses and nurseries, the plant propagator, two assistants, and, during the summer months, two horticultural interns. Adjacent to the greenhouse is a shade house of 3,150 square feet (293 m²), a 12,600 cubic foot (357 m³) cold storage facility, and three irrigated, inground nurseries totaling approximately one and one-half acres (6,000 m²). Also located in the greenhouse complex is the bonsai pavilion, where the <a href="/wiki/Larz_Anderson_Bonsai_Collection" title="Larz Anderson Bonsai Collection">Larz Anderson Bonsai Collection</a> is displayed from the middle of April to the end of October. During the winter months the bonsai are held in the cold storage unit at temperatures slightly above freezing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isabella_Welles_Hunnewell_Internship_Program">Isabella Welles Hunnewell Internship Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Isabella Welles Hunnewell Internship Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Living Collections Department of the Arnold Arboretum offers a paid summer internship Program <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> that combines hands-on training in horticulture with educational courses. Intern trainees will be accepted for 12- to 24-week appointments. Ten interns will work with the grounds maintenance department and two in the Dana Greenhouses.</p>
<p>The Program supports the educational and horticultural missions of the Arnold Arboretum by providing students in the plant sciences with on-the-job training during the summer months. Interns work directly with the Arboretum's historic landscape and world-renowned scientific collection, gaining practical experience in public horticulture, collections and landscape management, and the arboreal sciences. Working alongside Arboretum staff, interns receive valuable mentoring opportunities and engage in a formal program of instruction, both in the classroom and in the field. A series of field trips scheduled throughout their summer experience offers participants valuable networking opportunities and exposure to the operations of many other botanical institutions. Fundamentally, the program provides essential support to horticulture staff in caring for the Arboretum's 281-acre landscape and living collection of more than 15,000 woody plants. The program helps identify exceptional talent, and many interns extend their terms through apprenticeships, fellowships, and full-time positions.</p>
<p>As part of the Program, interns participate in mandatory instructional sessions and field trips in order to develop a broader sense of the Arboretum’s horticultural practices as well as those of other institutions. Sessions and field trips are led by Arnold staff members and embrace an open question and answer format encouraging all to participate. Interns often bring experience and knowledge that everyone, including staff, benefits from. It is a competitive-free learning environment.</p>
<p>The Hunnewell Internship Program was endowed in 2003 by the descendants of <a href="/wiki/H._H._Hunnewell" title="H. H. Hunnewell">H. H. Hunnewell</a> and his wife, Isabella Pratt Welles Hunnewell, for whom the Program is named. The Program honors the longtime commitment of the Hunnewell family to both the Arnold Arboretum and to the advancement of botanical science and horticulture. <a href="/wiki/H._H._Hunnewell" title="H. H. Hunnewell">H. H. Hunnewell</a> was a distant cousin and friend of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sprague_Sargent" title="Charles Sprague Sargent">Charles Sprague Sargent</a>, and Hunnewell and Sargent advised and helped each other on the creation of the Arnold Arboretum and Hunnewell's <a href="/wiki/H.H._Hunnewell_Estate" class="mw-redirect" title="H.H. Hunnewell Estate">estate</a> in Wellesley, respectively.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horticultural_Apprenticeship">Horticultural Apprenticeship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Horticultural Apprenticeship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Arboretum created the horticultural apprenticeship program in 1997 to provide hands-on experience in all aspects of the development, curation, and maintenance of the Arboretum's living collections to individuals interested in pursuing a career in an arboretum or botanical garden.</p>

<p>The second Sunday in May every year is "<a href="/wiki/Lilac" class="mw-redirect" title="Lilac">Lilac</a> Sunday". This is the only day of the year that <a href="/wiki/Picnic" title="Picnic">picnicking</a> is allowed. In 2008, on the 100th anniversary of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://arboretum.harvard.edu/news-events/lilac-sunday/">Lilac Sunday</a>, the Arboretum website touted:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Of the thousands of flowering plants in the Arboretum, only one, the lilac, is singled out each year for a daylong celebration. On Lilac Sunday, garden enthusiasts from all over New England gather at the Arboretum to picnic and tour the lilac collection. On the day of the event, which takes place rain or shine, the Arboretum is open as usual from dawn to dusk.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_collections">Associated collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Associated collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Arboretum's herbarium in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Plain" title="Jamaica Plain">Jamaica Plain</a> holds specimens of cultivated plants that relate to the living collections (ca. 160,000). The Jamaica Plain herbarium, horticultural library, archives, and photographs are maintained in the Hunnewell building at 125 Arborway; however, the main portions of the herbarium and library collections are housed in Cambridge on the campus of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, at 22 Divinity Avenue.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The inventory of living collections is updated periodically and made available to sister botanical gardens and arboreta on request; it is also available on the Arboretum’s website (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.arboretum.harvard.edu/plants/inventory.html">searchable inventory</a>). <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20091007133215/http://arnoldia.arboretum.harvard.edu/static/home">Arnoldia</a></i>, the quarterly magazine of the Arnold Arboretum, frequently publishes articles relating to the living collections. Publication of a journal targeting more scientific audience, <i>Journal of the Arnold Arboretum</i>, established in 1919, was suspended in 1990,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> when it was incorporated into <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Papers_in_Botany" title="Harvard Papers in Botany">Harvard Papers in Botany</a></i> (HPB).<sup id="cite_ref-About_HPB_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About_HPB-8">[8]</a></sup> The 71 volumes are available online at the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Heritage_Library" title="Biodiversity Heritage Library">Biodiversity Heritage Library</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674766945"><i>A Reunion of Trees</i></a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> by Stephen A. Spongberg (curator emeritus) recounts the history of the introduction of many of the exotic species included in the Arobretum’s collections. <i>New England Natives</i><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> written by horticultural research archivist <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://archive.is/20121210214742/http://arboretum.harvard.edu/about/staff/sheila-connor/">Sheila Connor</a> describes many of the trees and shrubs of the New England flora and the ways New Englanders have used them since prehistoric times. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://worid-of-books.com/?id=TO7aAAAAMAAJ"><i>Science in the Pleasure Ground</i></a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> by Ida Hay (former curatorial associate) constitutes an institutional biography of the Arboretum.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutional_collaborations">Institutional collaborations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_Arboretum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Institutional collaborations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Arboretum maintains an institutional membership in the American Public Garden Association (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.aabga.org/">APGA</a>), Botanic Gardens Conservation International (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bgci.org/usa">BGCI</a>), and the International Association of Botanical Gardens and Arboreta. Additionally, members of the staff are associated with many national and international botanical and horticultural organizations. The Arboretum is also a cooperating institution with the Center for Plant Conservation (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.centerforplantconservation.org/">CPC</a>), and as an active member of the North American Plant Collections Consortium (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110219214205/http://www.publicgardens.org/content/what-napcc">NAPCC</a>), it is committed to broadening and maintaining its holdings of: <i><a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">Acer</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Carya" class="mw-redirect" title="Carya">Carya</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">Fagus</a></i>, <i>Stewartia</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Syringa" title="Syringa">Syringa</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Tsuga" title="Tsuga">Tsuga</a></i> for the purposes of plant conservation, evaluation, and research. The Arboretum is also a member of the North American China Plant Exploration Consortium (NACPEC).</p>

<p><b>Academic dishonesty</b>, <b>academic misconduct</b> or <b>academic fraud</b> is any type of <a href="/wiki/Cheating" title="Cheating">cheating</a> that occurs in relation to a formal <a href="/wiki/Academic" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic">academic</a> exercise. It can include</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">Plagiarism</a></b>: The adoption or reproduction of original creations of another author (person, collective, organization, community or other type of author, including anonymous authors) without due acknowledgment.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Fabrication_(science)" title="Fabrication (science)">Fabrication</a></b>: The falsification of data, information, or <a href="/wiki/Citation" title="Citation">citations</a> in any formal academic exercise.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Deception" title="Deception">Deception</a></b>: Providing false information to an instructor concerning a formal academic exercise—<i>e.g.,</i> giving a false excuse for missing a deadline or falsely claiming to have submitted work.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cheating#Academic" title="Cheating">Cheating</a></b>: Any attempt to obtain assistance in a formal academic exercise (like an <a href="/wiki/Test_(assessment)" title="Test (assessment)">examination</a>) without due acknowledgment (including the use of <a href="/wiki/Cheat_sheet" title="Cheat sheet">cheat sheets</a>).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">Bribery</a></b> or paid services: Giving assignment answers or test answers for money.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">Sabotage</a></b>: Acting to prevent others from completing their work. This includes cutting pages out of library books or willfully disrupting the experiments of others.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Academic_dishonesty#Professorial_misconduct" title="Academic dishonesty">Professorial misconduct</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">Professorial</a> acts that are academically fraudulent equate to academic fraud and/or grade fraud.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Impersonation" class="mw-redirect" title="Impersonation">Impersonation</a></b>: assuming a student's identity with intent to provide an advantage for the student.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Academic dishonesty has been documented in every type of educational setting from <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school" title="Elementary school">elementary school</a> to <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate school</a>. Throughout history this type of dishonesty has been met with varying degrees of approbation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In antiquity, the notion of <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> did not exist. Ideas were the common property of the literate elite. Books were published by hand-copying them. Scholars freely made digests or commentaries on other works, which could contain as much or as little original material as the author desired. There was no standard system of citation at this time. Scholars were an elite and small group who knew and generally trusted each other. This system continued through the European Middle Ages. Education was in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>. Some scholars were <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a>, who used much of their time copying <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscripts</a>. Other scholars were in urban <a href="/wiki/Universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Universities">universities</a> connected to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Academic dishonesty dates back to the first tests. Scholars note that cheating was prevalent on the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_civil_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese civil service">Chinese civil service</a> exams thousands of years ago, even when cheating carried the penalty of death for both examinee and examiner.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Before the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Language_Association" title="Modern Language Association">MLA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">APA</a> at the end of the 19th century, there were no set rules on how to properly cite quotations from others' writings, which may have caused many cases of plagiarism out of ignorance."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, cheating was widespread at college campuses in the United States, and was not considered dishonorable among students.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It has been estimated that as many as two-thirds of students cheated at some point of their college careers at the turn of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sorority" class="mw-redirect" title="Sorority">Fraternities</a> often operated so-called <a href="/wiki/Essay_mill" title="Essay mill">essay mills</a>, where term papers were kept on file and could be resubmitted over and over again by different students, often with the only change being the name on the paper.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As higher education in the U.S. trended towards <a href="/wiki/Meritocracy" title="Meritocracy">meritocracy</a>, however, a greater emphasis was put on anti-cheating policies, and the newly diverse student bodies tended to arrive with a more negative view of academic dishonesty.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Academic dishonesty occurs in all levels of education.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the United States, one study has shown that 20% of students started cheating in the first grade.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Similarly, other studies reveal that currently in the U.S., 56% of <a href="/wiki/Middle_school" title="Middle school">middle school</a> students and 70% of <a href="/wiki/High_school" class="mw-redirect" title="High school">high school</a> students have cheated.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A large-scale study in Germany found that 75% of the university students admitted that they conducted at least one of seven types of academic misconduct (such as plagiarism or falsifying data) within the previous six months.<sup id="cite_ref-tandfonline.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tandfonline.com-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Students are not the only ones to cheat in an academic setting. A study among North Carolina school teachers found that some 35% of respondents said they had witnessed their colleagues cheating in one form or another. The rise of <a href="/wiki/High-stakes_testing" title="High-stakes testing">high-stakes testing</a> and the consequences of the results on the teacher is cited as a reason why a teacher might want to inflate the results of their students.<sup id="cite_ref-Jacob-Levitt-2003_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacob-Levitt-2003-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first scholarly studies in the 1960s of academic dishonesty in <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> found that nationally in the U.S., somewhere between 50%-70% of college students had cheated at least once.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> While nationally, these rates of cheating in the U.S. remain stable today, there are large disparities between different schools, depending on the size, selectivity, and anti-cheating policies of the school. Generally, the smaller and more selective the college, the less cheating occurs there. For instance, the number of students who have engaged in academic dishonesty at small elite <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts_colleges" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal arts colleges">liberal arts colleges</a> can be as low as 15%-20%, while cheating at large <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public universities</a> can be as high as 75%.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Moreover, researchers have found that students who attend a school with an <a href="/wiki/Honor_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Honor code">honor code</a> are less likely to cheat than students at schools with other ways of enforcing <a href="/wiki/Academic_integrity" title="Academic integrity">academic integrity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As for graduate education, a recent study found that 56% of MBA students admitted cheating, along with 54% of graduate students in engineering, 48% in education, and 45% in law.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cheating in high schools is growing in the United States at an exponential rate. There is also a great difference in students' perceptions and the realty of their own ethical behavior. In a 2008 survey of 30,000 students in high school carried out by the Josephson Institute for Youth Ethics, 62 percent of students polled said they "copied another's homework two or more times in the past year."<sup id="cite_ref-Josephson_Institute_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Josephson_Institute-18">[18]</a></sup> Yet, on the same survey, 92 percent said they were "satisfied with their personal ethics and character."<sup id="cite_ref-Josephson_Institute_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Josephson_Institute-18">[18]</a></sup> Hence, there is generally a discrepancy between actual behavior and self-image of high school students' character.</p>
<p>As more students take courses and assessments online, there is a persistent perception that it is easier to cheat in an online class than a face-to-face course.<sup id="cite_ref-West_Georgia_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-West_Georgia-19">[19]</a></sup> Moreover, there are online services that offer to prepare any kind of homework of high school and college level and take online tests for students.<sup id="cite_ref-ohiomatyc_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ohiomatyc-20">[20]</a></sup> While administrators are often aware of such websites, they have been unsuccessful in curbing cheating in homework and non-proctored online tests, resorting to a recommendation by the Ohio Mathematics Association to derive at least 80% of the grade of online classes from proctored tests.<sup id="cite_ref-ohiomatyc_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ohiomatyc-20">[20]</a></sup> In addition, colleges and universities are increasingly turning to online proctoring services to oversee tens of thousands of exams per year.<sup id="cite_ref-Inside_Higher_Ed_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inside_Higher_Ed-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>While research on academic dishonesty in other countries is less extensive, anecdotal evidence suggests cheating could be even more common in countries like Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>A typology of academic misconduct has been devised by Perry (2010).<sup id="cite_ref-eprints.hud.ac.uk_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eprints.hud.ac.uk-23">[23]</a></sup> Perry's typology presents a two dimensional model of academic misconduct with one dimension measuring the degree to which rules are understood and the other dimension measuring how closely these rules are followed.<sup id="cite_ref-eprints.hud.ac.uk_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eprints.hud.ac.uk-23">[23]</a></sup> According to the typology only those students who understand the rules but fail to adhere to the rules are classified as 'cheats'.<sup id="cite_ref-eprints.hud.ac.uk_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eprints.hud.ac.uk-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bribery">Bribery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bribery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">Bribery</a></div>
<p><b>Bribery</b> is an act of giving money or gift giving that alters the behavior of the recipient. Bribery constitutes a crime and is defined by <i><a href="/wiki/Black%27s_Law_Dictionary" title="Black's Law Dictionary">Black's Law Dictionary</a></i> as the <a href="/wiki/Offer_and_acceptance" title="Offer and acceptance">offering</a>, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/giving" class="extiw" title="wikt:giving">giving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Offer_and_acceptance" title="Offer and acceptance">receiving</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Solicitation" title="Solicitation">soliciting</a> of any item of value to influence the actions of an official or other person in charge of a public or <a href="/wiki/Legal" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal">legal</a> <a href="/wiki/Duty" title="Duty">duty</a>.</p>
<p>The bribe is the gift bestowed to influence the recipient's conduct. It may be any <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a>, <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Good (economics)">good</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chose_(English_law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chose (English law)">right in action</a>, <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a>, <a href="/wiki/Promotion_(rank)" title="Promotion (rank)">preferment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Privilege_(legal_ethics)" title="Privilege (legal ethics)">privilege</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remuneration" title="Remuneration">emolument</a>, object of value, advantage, or merely a promise or undertaking to induce or influence the action, vote, or influence of a person in an official or public capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cheating">Cheating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cheating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cheating" title="Cheating">Cheating</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cheating.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Cheating.JPG/220px-Cheating.JPG" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Cheating.JPG/330px-Cheating.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Cheating.JPG/440px-Cheating.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2240" data-file-height="1488" /></a>

<p>Cheating can take the form of crib notes, looking over someone's shoulder during an exam, or any forbidden sharing of information between students regarding an exam or exercise. Many elaborate methods of cheating have been developed over the years. For instance, students have been documented hiding notes in the bathroom toilet tank, in the brims of their baseball caps, or up their sleeves. Also, the storing of information in graphing calculators, pagers, cell phones, and other electronic devices has cropped up since the information revolution began. While students have long surreptitiously scanned the tests of those seated near them, some students actively try to aid those who are trying to cheat. Methods of secretly signaling the right answer to friends are quite varied, ranging from coded sneezes or pencil tapping to high-pitched noises beyond the hearing range of most teachers. Some students have been known to use more elaborate means, such as using a system of repetitive body signals like hand movements or foot jerking to distribute answers (i.e. where a tap of the foot could correspond to answer "A", two taps for answer "B", and so on).</p>
<p>Cheating differs from most other forms of academic dishonesty, in that people can engage in it without benefiting themselves academically at all. For example, a student who illicitly <a href="/wiki/Telegraphed" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraphed">telegraphed</a> answers to a friend during a test would be cheating, even though the student's own work is in no way affected. Another example of academic dishonesty is a dialogue between students in the same class but in two different time periods, both of which a test is scheduled for that day. If the student in the earlier time period informs the other student in the later period about the test; that is considered academic dishonesty, even though the first student has not benefited himself.</p>
<p>One other method is taking advantage of time zones, particularly in exams administered worldwide. Those who take the exam first (likely in Oceania) can then post answers for those about to take the exam (in a time zone behind like Europe).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deception">Deception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Deception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deception" title="Deception">Deception</a></div>
<p>Deception is providing false information to a teacher/instructor concerning a formal academic exercise. Examples of this include taking more time on a take-home test than is allowed, giving a dishonest excuse when asking for a deadline extension, or falsely claiming to have submitted work. This type of academic misconduct is often considered softer than the more obvious forms of cheating, and otherwise-honest students sometimes engage in this type of dishonesty without considering themselves cheaters. It is also sometimes done by students who have failed to complete an assignment, to avoid responsibility for doing so.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fabrication">Fabrication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fabrication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lie#Fabrication" title="Lie">Lie §&#160;Fabrication</a></div>
<p>Fabrication is the falsification of data, information, or citations in any formal academic exercise. This includes making up citations to back up arguments or inventing quotations. Fabrication predominates in the natural sciences, where students sometimes falsify data to make experiments "work". It includes data falsification, in which false claims are made about research performed, including selective submitting of results to exclude inconvenient data to generating bogus data.</p>
<p>Bibliographical references are often fabricated, especially when a certain minimum number of references is required or considered sufficient for the particular kind of paper. This type of fabrication can range from referring to works whose titles look relevant but which the student did not read, to making up bogus titles and authors.</p>
<p>There is also the practice of <i>dry-labbing</i>—which can occur in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> or other lab courses, in which the teacher clearly expects the experiment to yield certain results (which confirm established laws), so the student starts from the results and works backward, calculating what the experimental data should be, often adding variation to the data. In some cases, the lab report is written before the experiment is conducted—in some cases, the experiment is never carried out. In either case, the results are what the instructor expects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impersonation">Impersonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Impersonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Impersonation" class="mw-redirect" title="Impersonation">Impersonation</a></div>
<p>Impersonation is a form of cheating whereby a different person than the student assigned an assignment or exam completes it. Different from regular cheating,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the academic work is totally 'outsourced' to another person or organization, usually for pay.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plagiarism">Plagiarism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Plagiarism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">Plagiarism</a></div>
<p>Plagiarism, as defined in the 1995 <i>Random House Compact Unabridged Dictionary</i>, is the "use or close imitation of the language and thoughts of another author and the representation of them as one's own <a href="/wiki/Original_work" class="mw-redirect" title="Original work">original work</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Academia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academia">academia</a>, it is seen as the adoption or reproduction of original intellectual creations (such as concepts, ideas, methods, pieces of information or expressions, etc.) of another author (person, collective, organization, community or other type of author, including anonymous authors) without due acknowledgment, in contexts where originality is acknowledged and rewarded.<sup id="cite_ref-Rughinis_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rughinis-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2010)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (August 2010)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> This can range from borrowing without <a href="/wiki/Attribution_(copyright)" title="Attribution (copyright)">attribution</a> a particularly apt phrase, to <a href="/wiki/Paraphrasing_of_copyrighted_material" title="Paraphrasing of copyrighted material">paraphrasing</a> someone else's original idea without citation, to wholesale <a href="/wiki/Contract_cheating" title="Contract cheating">contract cheating</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The modern concept of plagiarism as <a href="/wiki/Immoral" class="mw-redirect" title="Immoral">immoral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Originality" title="Originality">originality</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_(ethics)" title="Ideal (ethics)">ideal</a> emerged in Europe only in the 18th century, while in the previous centuries authors and artists were encouraged to "copy the masters as closely as possible" and avoid "unnecessary invention".<sup id="cite_ref-Lynch02_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lynch02-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Will63_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Will63-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RSC07_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSC07-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lindey52_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lindey52-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Young59_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young59-34">[34]</a></sup> The 18th century new morals have been institutionalized and enforced prominently in the sectors of academia (including academic science, education, engineering etc.) and <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a>, where plagiarism is now considered academic dishonesty and a breach of <a href="/wiki/Journalistic_ethics" class="mw-redirect" title="Journalistic ethics">journalistic ethics</a>, subject to sanctions like <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_(academia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Expulsion (academia)">expulsion</a> and other severe career damages. Not so in <a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">the arts</a>, which have resisted in their long-established tradition of copying as a fundamental practice of the <a href="/wiki/Creative_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Creative process">creative process</a>, with plagiarism being still hugely tolerated by 21st-century artists.<sup id="cite_ref-Alfrey_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alfrey-35">[35]</a></sup> Lawmaking is a professional field which is not structured around the concept of originality and for which plagiarism is less relevant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Plagiarism is not a <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> but is disapproved more on the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">moral</a> offence.<sup id="cite_ref-Lynch02_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lynch02-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Green_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green-36">[36]</a></sup> It may be a case for <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil law</a> if it is so substantial to constitute <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a>.</p>
<p>Since 2000, discussions on the subjects of <a href="/wiki/Student_plagiarism" class="mw-redirect" title="Student plagiarism">student plagiarism</a> have increased<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> with a major strand of this discussion centring on the issue of how best students can be helped to understand and avoid plagiarism.<sup id="cite_ref-ireland_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ireland-38">[38]</a></sup> Given the serious consequences that plagiarism has for students there has been a call for a greater emphasis on learning in order to help students avoid committing plagiarism.<sup id="cite_ref-ireland_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ireland-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professorial_misconduct">Professorial misconduct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Professorial misconduct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Professorial misconduct includes improper grading of students' papers and oral exams, grade fraud, deliberate negligence towards cheating or assistance in cheating. This can be done for reasons of personal bias towards students (favoritism) or a particular viewpoint (intellectual dishonesty), for a <a href="/wiki/Bribe" class="mw-redirect" title="Bribe">bribe</a>, or to improve the teacher's own perceived performance by increasing the passing rate. It is still occasionally done for matters of ego or to procure sexual favors (sexual harassment).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sabotage">Sabotage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sabotage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">Sabotage</a></div>
<p>Sabotage is when a student or professor prevents others from completing their work. This includes cutting pages out of library books, deleting data off of classmate's computer or otherwise willfully disrupting the experiments of others. Sabotage is usually only found in highly <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competitive</a>, cutthroat environments, such as at extremely elite schools where class rankings are highly prized. Poor behavior and the low level disruption of other students' learning, however, is extremely common in all educational settings. Some medical-school librarians have noted that important articles—required reading for key courses—are frequently missing from bound journals—sliced out with razor blades, scalpels, or other sharp blades. Other journals will be marked up in crayon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a variety of causes of academic misconduct. Researchers have studied the correlation of cheating to personal characteristics, demographics, contextual factors, methods of deterring misconduct, even <a href="/wiki/Kohlberg%27s_stages_of_moral_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Kohlberg's stages of moral development">stages of moral development</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incentives_to_cheat">Incentives to cheat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Incentives to cheat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some scholars contend that there are students who have a pathological urge to cheat. The writer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mallon" title="Thomas Mallon">Thomas Mallon</a> noted that many scholars had found plagiarism in literature (<a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Reade" title="Charles Reade">Charles Reade</a> being two notable examples) to often be perpetrated in a way similar to <a href="/wiki/Kleptomania" title="Kleptomania">kleptomania</a> (a psychological disease associated with uncontrollable stealing, even when it is against the interests of the thief).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> On the other hand, Mallon concludes it is probable that most "cheaters" make a rational choice to commit academic misconduct.<sup id="cite_ref-Sattler.2C_S._2013_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sattler.2C_S._2013-40">[40]</a></sup> A common reason for unethical behavior is the desire to "gain a competitive advantage in the race for position or power".<sup id="cite_ref-Vojinovic_and_Abbott.2C_2012_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vojinovic_and_Abbott.2C_2012-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Richard Fass puts forward the possibility that business scandals in the real world make students believe dishonesty is an acceptable method for achieving success in contemporary society.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Academic dishonesty, in this case, would be practice for the real world. For some students, there would be a dichotomy between success and honesty, and their decision is that: "It is not that we love honesty less, but that we love success more."<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Conversely, other scholars consider that with the recent rise in corporate ethics related dismissals in the business world, this approach to cheating may be losing its appeal, if it ever really had any.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> However, it has been shown that the expected benefits of cheating as well as student's morality plays an important role for the engagement in dishonest behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-Sattler.2C_S._2013_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sattler.2C_S._2013-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent studies have indicated that there is no clear link between academic dishonesty and academic success<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. One study showed that students given an unexpected opportunity to cheat did not improve their grades significantly from the control group.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-46">[46]</a></sup> Another study showed that students who were allowed to bring cheat sheets to a test did not improve their grades.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-46">[46]</a></sup> While this may conflict with the common perception of cheating (one survey found only 13% of males and 46% of females think that cheating does not help grades,<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-46">[46]</a></sup>) it is often apparent to professors and members of academic conduct committees when a paper has been plagiarized by its inferior quality.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the USA, on average one third of grade A students have cheated.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> And asserts that academic dishonesty acts as a shortcut, so even grade A students might be tempted to cheat. He contends that even if a plagiarized paper receives a relatively low grade, that grade is actually high, given how much time and effort went into the paper. In the study mentioned above (in which students were allowed to bring crib sheets to a test but did not improve their scores), the researcher concluded that the students used the crib notes as alternatives to studying, rather than as complements to studying, and thus spent less time preparing for the exam.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Teachers">Teachers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Teachers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The federal government of the United States has mandated high-stakes testing as part of the <a href="/wiki/No_Child_Left_Behind_Act" title="No Child Left Behind Act">No Child Left Behind Act</a>, signed into law in 2002. Schools and teachers are held accountable for the results. According to Steven Levitt and Stephen Dubner, co-authors of <i>Freakonomics</i>, teachers are known to "teach to the test": while not teaching the actual answers, they teach the questions and similar ones, and they neglect any topic that will not be tested on. Levitt also states that teachers may inflate the results of tests given in their classroom.<sup id="cite_ref-Jacob-Levitt-2003_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacob-Levitt-2003-13">[13]</a></sup> Teachers and librarians can have a significant proactive impact on doing honest work.<sup id="cite_ref-test_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-test-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographic_and_personal_causes">Demographic and personal causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Demographic and personal causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research has identified a number of demographic characteristics that appear to be important influences on cheating, including age, gender and grade point average.<sup id="cite_ref-McCabe_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCabe-49">[49]</a></sup> Older students, females, and students with higher academic achievement<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-50">[50]</a></sup> are less likely to cheat, whereas students involved with many extra-curricular activities are more likely to do so. Students involved in extra-curricular activities may be less committed to their studies, or may have more demands on their time, which interfere with their studies, creating a greater incentive to cheat. It has been found that younger students are somewhat more likely to cheat: one study finding the highest incidence of cheating occurs during sophomore year at college.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Although cheating might be expected to decline with greater moral development, one experiment found that there was no relationship between how a student performed on a <a href="/wiki/Defining_Issues_Test" title="Defining Issues Test">morality test</a> and his likelihood of cheating (that is, students at a <a href="/wiki/Kohlberg%27s_stages_of_moral_development#Pre-conventional" class="mw-redirect" title="Kohlberg's stages of moral development">pre-conventional</a> stage of morality are as likely to cheat as those at a <a href="/wiki/Kohlberg%27s_stages_of_moral_development#Post-Conventional" class="mw-redirect" title="Kohlberg's stages of moral development">post-conventional</a> stage).<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Higher academic procrastination was also found to increase the frequency of seven different forms of academic misconduct (using fraudulent excuses, plagiarism, copying from someone else in exams, using forbidden means in exams, carrying forbidden means into exams, copying parts of homework from others, and fabrication or falsification of data) as well as the variety of academic misconduct.<sup id="cite_ref-tandfonline.com_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tandfonline.com-12">[12]</a></sup> This German panel study among thousands of university students argues that academic misconduct might be a coping-strategy to overcome the negative consequences of academic procrastination such as lower performance.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (classification of human beings)">Race</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nationality" title="Nationality">nationality</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">class</a> all show little correlation with academic misconduct. There is also no correlation between how religious someone is and the likelihood that that person will cheat. A comparison between students of different <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religions</a> yielded similar results, although the study did show that <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> tend to cheat less than members of other religions.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> One of the strongest demographic correlations with academic misconduct in the United States is with language. Students who speak <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> as a second language have been shown to commit academic dishonesty more and are more likely to be caught than native speakers, since they will often not want to rewrite sources in their own words, fearing that the meaning of the sentence will be lost through poor paraphrasing skills.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a> system, <a href="/wiki/International_students" class="mw-redirect" title="International students">international students</a> make up 10% of the student body but comprise 47% of academic dishonesty cases.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Universities_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Universities in the United Kingdom">British universities</a>, students from outside of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> make up 12% of the student body but comprise 35% of academic dishonesty cases.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contextual_causes">Contextual causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Contextual causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Academic misconduct is more easily traced to the academic and social environment of students than to their background. These contextual factors can be as broad as the social milieu at school to as narrow as what instructions a teacher gives before an exam.</p>
<p>Contextual factors that individual teachers can affect often make the least difference on cheating behavior. A study found that increasing the distance between students taking an exam has little effect on academic misconduct, and that threatening students before an exam with expulsion if they cheat actually promotes cheating behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Indeed, increased exam <a href="/wiki/Proctor" title="Proctor">proctoring</a> and other methods of detecting cheating in the classroom are largely ineffective. According to one survey of American college students, while 50% had cheated at least once in the previous six months, and 7% had cheated more than five times in that period, only 2.5% of the cheaters had been caught.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> As teachers invent more elaborate methods of deterring cheating, students invent even more elaborate methods of cheating (sometimes even treating it as a game), leading to what some teachers call a costly and unwinnable <a href="/wiki/Arms_race" title="Arms race">arms race</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Increased punishment for academic misconduct also has little correlation with cheating behavior. It has been found that students with markedly different perceptions of what the severity of the punishment for cheating were all equally likely to cheat, probably indicating that they thought that increased penalties were immaterial since their cheating would never be discovered.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> However, if a professor makes clear that he disapproves of cheating, either in the syllabus, in the first class, or at the beginning of a test, academic dishonesty can drop by 12%.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Some professors may have little incentive to reduce cheating in their classes below a point that would otherwise be obvious to outside observers, as they are rated by how many research papers they publish and research grants they win for the college, and not by how well they teach.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Teachers can, however, accidentally promote cheating behavior. A study found a correlation between how harsh or unfair a professor is perceived as and academic misconduct, since students see cheating as a way of getting back at the teacher.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Also, students who see themselves in a competition, such as when the teacher is using a <a href="/wiki/Grade_curve" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade curve">grade curve</a>, are more likely to cheat.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Research has also shown a correlation between goal orientation and the occurrence of academic cheating. Students who perceive their classroom to have high mastery goals are less likely to engage in cheating than those who perceive their classroom to emphasize performance goals.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In other words, students who are encouraged to learn for the sake of learning and who exhibit an intrinsic value of education are less likely to cheat than those who are encouraged primarily by grades and other extrinsic rewards.</p>
<p>The most important contextual causes of academic misconduct are often out of individual teachers' hands. One very important factor is <a href="/wiki/Time_management" title="Time management">time management</a>. One survey reported two-thirds of teachers believed that poor time management was the principal cause of cheating.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Often social engagements are to blame. It has been found that there is a strong correlation between <a href="/wiki/Extracurricular_activities" class="mw-redirect" title="Extracurricular activities">extracurricular activities</a> and cheating, especially among athletes, even those on intramural teams.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> It has also been found that student cheating rates rise significantly the more time students spend <a href="/wiki/Playing_cards" class="mw-redirect" title="Playing cards">playing cards</a>, watching television, or having a few drinks with friends.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Relatedly, <a href="/wiki/Fraternities_and_sororities" title="Fraternities and sororities">fraternity or sorority</a> membership is also strongly correlated with academic misconduct.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the most important causes of academic misconduct is the contextual factor of an environment of peer disapproval of cheating, that is, <a href="/wiki/Peer_pressure" title="Peer pressure">peer pressure</a>. Psychologists note that all people tend to follow the <a href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Norm (sociology)">norms</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Peer_group" title="Peer group">peer group</a>, which would include norms about academic dishonesty.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Thus, students who believe that their peers disapprove of cheating are less likely to cheat. Indeed, multiple studies show that the most decisive factor in a student's decision to cheat is his perception of his peers' relationship with academic dishonesty.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> For instance, on average 69% of students cheat at colleges with low community disapproval of academic misconduct, whereas only about 23% of students cheat at colleges with strong community disapproval of academic misconduct.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-72">[72]</a></sup> Peer pressure works both ways, as a study found that there is a 41% increase in the probability of a student cheating if he or she has seen someone else cheat.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> However, even if most students strongly disapprove of cheating, there has to be a community in order for those norms to be enforced via peer pressure. For instance, larger schools, which usually have much higher cheating rates than small schools, tend to have a weaker community, being more split up into different peer groups that exert little social pressure on each other.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Another measure of a college community, how many students live on campus, further shows a significant relation with a school's cheating rate.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Relatedly, many professors argue that smaller classes reduce cheating behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_causes">Ethical causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Ethical causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>No matter what the demographic or contextual influences are on a student who decides to engage in cheating behavior, before they can cheat they must overcome their own <a href="/wiki/Conscience" title="Conscience">conscience</a>. This depends both on how strongly someone disapproves of academic dishonesty and what types of justifications the student uses to escape a sense of guilt. For instance, students who personally do not have a moral problem with academic misconduct can cheat <a href="/wiki/Guilt_(emotion)" title="Guilt (emotion)">guilt</a>-free. However, while many students have been taught and have internalized that academic dishonesty is wrong, it has been shown that on average a third of students who strongly disapprove of cheating have in fact cheated.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> People who cheat despite personal disapproval of cheating engage in something called "neutralization", in which a student rationalizes the cheating as being acceptable due to certain mitigating circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> According to psychologists of deviant behavior, people who engage in neutralization support the societal norm in question, but "conjure up" reasons why they are allowed to violate that norm in a particular case.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Neutralization is not a simple case of <i>ex post facto</i> rationalization, but is rather a more comprehensive affair, occurring before, during, and after the act of cheating.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Researchers have found four major types of neutralization of academic dishonesty, which they categorize by type of justification. <i>Denial of responsibility</i> – that is, the accusation that others are to blame or that something forced the student to cheat – is the most common form of neutralization among college students who cheated, with 61% of cheaters using this form of justification.<sup id="cite_ref-McCabe-1992_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCabe-1992-81">[81]</a></sup> <i>Condemnation of condemner</i> – that is, that the professors are hypocrites or brought it on themselves – is the second most common form of college student neutralization at 28%.<sup id="cite_ref-McCabe-1992_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCabe-1992-81">[81]</a></sup> The third most popular form of neutralization among college students is the <i>appeal to higher loyalties,</i> where the student thinks their responsibility to some other entity, usually their peers, is more important than doing what they know to be morally right. About 6.8% of cheaters in higher education use this form of neutralization.<sup id="cite_ref-McCabe-1992_81-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCabe-1992-81">[81]</a></sup> <i>Denial of injury</i> – thinking that nobody is worse off for the cheating – is the fourth most popular kind of neutralization at 4.2% of cheaters.<sup id="cite_ref-McCabe-1992_81-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCabe-1992-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Cheating in academics has a host of effects on students, on teachers, on individual schools, and on the educational system itself.</p>
<p>For instance, students who engage in neutralisation to justify cheating, even once, are more likely to engage in it in the future, potentially putting them on a road to a life of dishonesty.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Indeed, one study found that students who are dishonest in class are more likely to engage in fraud and theft on the job when they enter the workplace.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Students are also negatively affected by academic dishonesty after graduation. A university diploma is an important document in the <a href="/wiki/Labor_market" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor market">labor market</a>. Potential employers use a degree as a representation of a graduate's knowledge and ability. However, due to academic dishonesty, not all graduates with the same grades actually did the same work or have the same skills. Thus, when faced with the fact that they do not know which graduates are skilled and which are the "lemons" (see "<a href="/wiki/The_Market_for_Lemons" title="The Market for Lemons">The Market for Lemons</a>"), employers must pay all graduates based on the quality of the average graduate. Therefore, the more students who cheat, getting by without achieving the required skills or learning, the lower the quality of the average graduate of a school, and thus the less employers are willing to pay a new hire from that school. Because of this reason, all students, even those that do not cheat themselves, are negatively affected by academic misconduct.</p>
<p>Academic dishonesty also creates problems for teachers.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> In economic terms, cheating causes an underproduction of knowledge, where the professor's job is to produce knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Moreover, a case of cheating often will cause emotional distress to faculty members, many considering it to be a personal slight against them or a violation of their trust. Dealing with academic misconduct is often one of the worst parts of a career in education, one survey claiming that 77% of academics agreed with the statement "dealing with a cheating student is one of the most onerous aspects of the job".<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Academic misconduct can also have an effect on a college's reputation, one of the most important assets of any school. An institution plagued by cheating scandals may become less attractive to potential donors and students and especially prospective employers. Alternatively, schools with low levels of academic dishonesty can use their reputation to attract students and employers.</p>
<p>Ultimately, academic dishonesty undermines the academic world. It interferes with the basic mission of education, the transfer of knowledge, by allowing students to get by without having to master the knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Furthermore, academic dishonesty creates an atmosphere that is not conducive to the learning process, which affects honest students as well.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> When honest students see cheaters escape detection, it can discourage student morale, as they see the rewards for their work cheapened. Cheating also undermines academia when students steal ideas. Ideas are a professional author's "capital and identity", and if a person's ideas are stolen it retards the pursuit of knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>If never formally retracted, fraudulent publications can remain an issue for many years as articles and books remain on shelves and continue to be cited. The case of <a href="/wiki/S._Walter_Poulshock" title="S. Walter Poulshock">S. Walter Poulshock</a>, a 1960s early-career historian whose work was found to contain wholly fabricated material, was exposed in 1966 with the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Historical_Review" class="mw-redirect" title="American Historical Review">American Historical Review</a></i> providing a warning on the topic.<sup id="cite_ref-Sternstein_02_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sternstein_02-90">[90]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Luker_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luker-91">[91]</a></sup> Nonetheless, his book was never removed from the shelves of many university libraries and (together with his related thesis) was still being cited in 2013, 47 years after it was intended to have been withdrawn by its publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-Margolies_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Margolies-92">[92]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Farrar-Myers_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farrar-Myers-93">[93]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sachsman_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sachsman-94">[94]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Skocpol_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Skocpol-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deterrence">Deterrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Deterrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All parties involved in the dishonesty—not just the individual whose grade is increased by it—can be punished.</p>
<p>Historically the job of preventing cheating has been given to the teacher. It used to be that in college the professor acted <i><a href="/wiki/In_loco_parentis" title="In loco parentis">in loco parentis</a></i> and was able to regulate student behavior as a parent.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> Thus, professors who discovered cheating could assign essentially any punishment they deemed appropriate. Students often had no mechanism for appeal. Generally, proctors were hired to patrol exams. If a case was particularly serious, a dean or other top-level administrator might have been involved. Against this inconsistent and paternalistic system, students at some schools rebelled and demanded to be treated as adults.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honor_codes">Honor codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Honor codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Academic_honor_code" title="Academic honor code">Academic honor code</a></div>
<p>First at the <a href="/wiki/College_of_William_and_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="College of William and Mary">College of William and Mary</a> in 1779, and then followed by schools like the University of Virginia in the 1850s and <a href="/wiki/Wesleyan_University" title="Wesleyan University">Wesleyan University</a> in 1893, the students, with the agreement of faculty who declared themselves dedicated to ideals of democracy and human character, created honor codes.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> B. Melendez of Harvard University defined an honor code as a code of academic conduct that includes a written pledge of honesty that students sign, a student controlled <a href="/wiki/Judiciary" title="Judiciary">judiciary</a> that hears alleged violations, unproctored examinations, and an obligation for all students help enforce the code.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> This system relied on student self-enforcement, which was considered more becoming of young gentlemen than the policing by proctors and professors that existed previously. Of interest, the <a href="/wiki/Military_academies" class="mw-redirect" title="Military academies">military academies</a> of the US took the honor code one step further than civilian colleges, disallowing "tolerance", which means that if a cadet or midshipman is found to have failed to report or outright protected someone engaged in academic dishonesty (as well as other dishonesties or stealing), that individual is to be expelled along with the perpetrator.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed_judicial_boards">Mixed judicial boards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mixed judicial boards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>However, many people doubted the advisability of relying on an abstract notion of honor to prevent academic dishonesty. This doubt has perhaps led to the reality that no more than a quarter of American universities have adopted honor codes.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-99">[99]</a></sup> Moreover, many professors could not envisage a student run trial process that treated faculty accusers fairly. In response to these concerns, in the middle of the twentieth century, many schools devised mixed judicial panels composed of both students and faculty. This type of academic integrity system was similar to the traditional faculty control system in that it relied on professors to detect cheating, except in this system cheaters were brought before centralized boards of students and faculty for punishment. By the 1960s over a quarter of American universities had adopted this system of mixed judicial boards.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-99">[99]</a></sup> Still, though, over half of American universities continued to use faculty-centered control systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_due_process_rights">Student due process rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Student due process rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting in the 1960s, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> began chipping away at the <i>in loco parentis</i> doctrine, giving college students more <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> such as the right of <a href="/wiki/Due_process" title="Due process">due process</a> in disciplinary proceedings (<i><a href="/wiki/Dixon_v._Alabama_Board_of_Education" class="mw-redirect" title="Dixon v. Alabama Board of Education">Dixon v. Alabama Board of Education</a>,</i> 1961).<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> In <i>Cooper v. Blair</i> (1973), specifically academic misconduct was ruled to require due process, being a disciplinary matter and not an educational matter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Could not google anything about it at all. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The due process rights of students in academic misconduct cases is not to the same degree as in a court of law. For instance, the student has no right to representation and the burden of proof is not necessarily stringent. In the "General Order on Judicial Standards of Procedure and Substance in Review of Student Discipline in Tax Supported Institutions of Higher Education", (1968) student due process rights were laid out as follows:</p>
<blockquote>
<div>
<ol>
<li>The student should be given adequate notice in writing of the specific ground or grounds and the nature of the evidence on which the discipline proceedings are based.</li>
<li>The student should be given an opportunity for a hearing in which the disciplinary authority provides a fair opportunity for hearing of the student's position, explanations, or evidence.</li>
<li>No disciplinary action may be taken on grounds which are not supported by any substantial evidence.</li>
</ol>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>These new rules put an end to the old faculty-based system of policing academic dishonesty, now students were entitled to an impartial hearing. While schools using the old honor code method or the mixed judicial system were not affected by these decisions, schools using the faculty based system generally instituted systems that relied on a committee of faculty and administrators or a dean to run the academic misconduct hearings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modified_honor_codes">Modified honor codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Modified honor codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recently, Donald L. McCabe and Linda Klebe Trevino, two experts in the field of academic dishonesty, have proposed a new way of deterring cheating that has been implemented in schools such as the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park" title="University of Maryland, College Park">University of Maryland</a>. Modified honor codes put students in charge of the judicial hearing process, making it clear that it is the students' responsibility to stop cheating amongst themselves, but at the same time students still have proctored exams and are not allowed to take pledges of good conduct in place of professor oversight.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> The researchers who advocate this type of code seem to think that the normal honor code is something of a special case that is not applicable to many schools.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> According to supporters of this system, schools with a large student body, a weak college community, or no history of student self-governance will not be able to support a full honor code. However, while modified honor codes seem to be more effective than faculty or administration run integrity codes of conduct, research shows that schools with modified codes still have higher rates of cheating than schools with full honor codes.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_different_systems_of_enforcement">Comparison of different systems of enforcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Comparison of different systems of enforcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research has shown that there is a strong correlation between forms of academic integrity system and levels of cheating at a school. Several studies have found students who attend schools with honor codes are less likely to cheat than students at schools with traditional integrity codes.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Another study found that only 28% of schools with honor codes have high levels of cheating, whereas 81% of schools with mixed judicial boards have high rates of cheating.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-72">[72]</a></sup> Whereas faculty or administration run codes of conduct tend to rely on policing and punishment to deter students from cheating, honor codes tend to rely on and cultivate student senses of honor and group peer pressure to deter academic misconduct.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> As mentioned above in the section on causes of cheating, increased enforcement or punishment is rarely effective at discouraging cheating, whereas there is a high correlation between peer pressure and academic honesty. The modified honor code attempts to cultivate peer disapproval of cheating while maintaining the traditional proctor system, although critics argue that the proctor system undermines the creation of an atmosphere of student self-policing, reducing the effectiveness of the honor code, possibly explaining why modified honor codes have not been as effective as the original version.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Faculty_issues_in_deterring_academic_dishonesty">Faculty issues in deterring academic dishonesty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Faculty issues in deterring academic dishonesty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are limitations to relying on the faculty to police academic dishonesty. One study found that up to 21% of professors have ignored at least one clear cut case of cheating.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Another study revealed that 40% of professors "never" report cheating, 54% "seldom" report cheating, and that a mere 6% act on all cases of academic misconduct that confront them.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> A third survey of professors found that while 79% had observed cheating, only 9% had penalized the student.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> According to a manual for professors on cheating,</p>
<blockquote>
<div>
<p>the reasons for this lack of action include unwillingness to devote time and energy to the issue, reluctance to undergo an emotional confrontation, and fear of retaliation by the student, of losing students, of being accused of harassment or discrimination, and even of being sued for these offenses and/or defamation of character.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>There are other reasons as well. Some professors are reluctant to report violations to the appropriate authorities because they believe the punishment to be too harsh.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some professors may have little incentive to reduce cheating in their classes below a point that would otherwise be obvious to outside observers, as they are rated by how many research papers they publish<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> and research grants they win for the college, and not by how well they teach.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Others do not report academic misconduct because of <a href="/wiki/Postmodernist" class="mw-redirect" title="Postmodernist">postmodernist</a> views on cheating. Postmodernism calls into question the very concepts of "authorship" and "originality." From the perspective of cultural studies and historicism, authors themselves are simply constructs of their social surroundings, and thus they simply rewrite already written cultural stories. Moreover, in the field of composition studies, students are being encouraged more and more to do group work and participate in ongoing collective revision. The postmodernist view is that "the concept of intellectual malpractice is of limited <a href="/wiki/Epistemological" class="mw-redirect" title="Epistemological">epistemological</a> value. Under the ironic gaze of postmodernism, the distinctions between guilt and innocence, integrity and deceit permeating the scandal debates appear irrelevant."<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> However, there is an argument that postmodernism is just <a href="/wiki/Moral_relativism" title="Moral relativism">moral relativism</a>, therefore cheating is condoned as a valid academic method, even if it is morally and legally wrong. One professor wrote in an article in <i>The English Journal</i> that when he peeked in on an unproctored class taking a test and saw several students up and consulting with one another, he decided that they were not cheating, but were using non-traditional techniques and collaborative learning to surmount the obstacles teachers had put in their way.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> Issues of cultural relativism also affect professors' views on cheating; the standard objection being that "students from certain Middle Eastern, Asian, and African cultures are baffled by the notion that one can 'own' ideas, since their cultures regard words and ideas as the property of all rather than as individual property".<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another issue teachers may have with deterring cheating is that they may decide that it is not their job. The argument that "they're professors, not policemen" is often heard in academia.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> In economic terms, some professors believe they are being paid to provide learning, and if the student loses that learning through cheating, he is only cheating himself out of the money he paid.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_of_falsified_data">Exposure of falsified data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Academic_dishonesty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Exposure of falsified data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the advancement of the internet, there are now several tools available to aid in the detection of plagiarism and <a href="/wiki/Multiple_publication" class="mw-redirect" title="Multiple publication">multiple publication</a> within biomedical literature. One tool developed in 2006 by researchers in <a href="/wiki/Harold_Garner" title="Harold Garner">Harold Garner</a>'s laboratory at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_Southwestern_Medical_Center_at_Dallas" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center at Dallas">University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center at Dallas</a> is <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://spore.swmed.edu/dejavu/">Déjà Vu</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since October 2016">permanent dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup>, an open-access database containing several thousand instances of duplicate publication.</p>

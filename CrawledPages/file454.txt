<p><b>Kempten</b> is the largest <a href="/wiki/Town#Germany" title="Town">town</a> of <a href="/wiki/Allg%C3%A4u" title="Allgäu">Allgäu</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Swabia_(Bavaria)" title="Swabia (Bavaria)">Swabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The population was about 68,000 in 2016. The area was possibly settled originally by <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celts</a>, but was later taken over by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>, who called the town <i>Cambodunum</i>. Kempten is the oldest urban settlement (town) in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kempten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Roman">Pre-Roman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kempten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-Roman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Greek geographer <a href="/wiki/Strabon" class="mw-redirect" title="Strabon">Strabo</a> mentions in 50&#160;BC a town of the Celtic Estiones named <i>Kambodunon</i>. This is considered the oldest written reference of any German city. So far no archaeological evidence could be found that this Celtic settlement really existed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_era">Roman era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kempten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roman era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 15&#160;BC Roman troops led by <a href="/wiki/Nero_Claudius_Drusus" title="Nero Claudius Drusus">Nero Claudius Drusus</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a> conquered and destroyed an existing Celtic settlement. Later the settlement was named <b>Cambodunum</b>. In the following years the city was rebuilt on a classical Roman city plan with baths, forum and temples. Initially in wood, the city was later rebuilt in stone after a devastating fire that destroyed almost the entire city in the year 69&#160;AD. The city possibly served as provincial capital of <a href="/wiki/Raetia" title="Raetia">Raetia</a> during the first century before <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a> took over this role. Extensive archaeological excavations at the end of the 19th century and again during the 1950s at what were then the outskirts of Kempten unearthed the extensive structural foundations.</p>
<p>The city was again destroyed in 233&#160;AD by the <a href="/wiki/Alemanni" title="Alemanni">Alemanni</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">Suebic</a> tribe. The original site of Cambodunum was then abandoned and the settlement moved to a strategically safer location on the Burghalde hill overlooking the river <a href="/wiki/Iller" title="Iller">Iller</a>.</p>
<p>In the middle of the 5th century the last Roman troops had left the area and the city was entirely taken over by the Alemanni.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kempten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the Romans abandoned the settlement, it was moved from the hill down to the plains located next to the river Iller. In written sources, the town appears as <i>Cambidano</i>. Being still predominantly Alemannic, the town once more was destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> in 683 as a consequence of the city's support of an uprising against the Frankish kingdom.</p>
<p>Around 700 a monastery — <a href="/wiki/Kempten_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Kempten Abbey">Kempten Abbey</a> — was built, the first in the Allgäu region, founded by two Benedictine monks from the <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Saint_Gall" title="Abbey of Saint Gall">Abbey of Saint Gall</a>, Magnus von Füssen and Theodor.<sup id="cite_ref-HDS_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDS-3">[3]</a></sup> Audogar was the first abbot of the new Benedictine monastery. Through the financial and lobbyist support of <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>’s wife <a href="/wiki/Hildegard,_wife_of_Charlemagne" class="mw-redirect" title="Hildegard, wife of Charlemagne">Hildegard</a>, an Allemannic princess, the monastery managed to become one of the most privileged of the <a href="/wiki/Frankish_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankish Empire">Frankish Empire</a>.</p>
<p>After several ravages by the <a href="/wiki/Magyars" class="mw-redirect" title="Magyars">Magyars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_of_Augsburg" title="Ulrich of Augsburg">Ulrich of Augsburg</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Augsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric of Augsburg">Augsburg</a> and also abbot of <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Kempten" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbey of Kempten">Kempten</a>, began the rebuilding of the monastery and the city in 941.</p>
<p>In 1213, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II_of_Hohenstaufen" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick II of Hohenstaufen">Frederick II</a> declared the abbots members of the <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichstag (Holy Roman Empire)">Reichstag</a> and granted the abbot the right to bear the title of Duke. However in 1289, King <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_of_Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Rudolf of Habsburg">Rudolf I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Germany</a> also granted special privileges to the urban settlement in the river valley, making it the <a href="/wiki/Free_Imperial_City_of_Kempten" title="Free Imperial City of Kempten">Free Imperial City of Kempten</a>. In 1525 the last property rights of the abbots in the Imperial City were sold in the so-called "Great Purchase", marking the start of the co-existence of two independent cities bearing the same name next to each other. More conflict arose after the Imperial City converted to <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> in direct opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> monastery (and <i>Free</i> City) in 1527.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_and_Baroque_to_Modern_Age">Renaissance and Baroque to Modern Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kempten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Renaissance and Baroque to Modern Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the turmoil of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> (1632–33), both cities were destroyed by the imperial forces and the Swedish troops respectively.</p>
<p>In 1652<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Roman Giel of Gielsberg, the <a href="/wiki/Abbot_of_Kempten" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbot of Kempten">Abbot of Kempten</a>, commissioned the architects <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Beer_(architect)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michael Beer (architect) (page does not exist)">Michael Beer</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Serro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johann Serro (page does not exist)">Johann Serro</a> from <a href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" class="mw-redirect" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a> to build <a href="/wiki/St._Lorenz_Basilica" title="St. Lorenz Basilica">St. Lorenz Basilica</a> as a new church to serve the parish and monastery, including a representative residence for the Duke-Abbots. This is acknowledged as the first large church built in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> the Duke-Abbey and Imperial City came under Bavarian rule (1802–03). Finally, in 1819, the two rival cities were united into a single communal entity.</p>
<p>The city was the location of a subcamp of the <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">Dachau concentration camp</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Claude_(Claudius)_Honor%C3%A9_Desir%C3%A9_Dornier" class="mw-redirect" title="Claude (Claudius) Honoré Desiré Dornier">Claude (Claudius) Honoré Desiré Dornier</a> born in Kempten im Allgäu (May 14, 1884 - December 5, 1969) was a German aircraft engineer and founder of Dornier GmbH. His legacy remains in the few aircraft named after him, including the Dornier Do 18 and the 12-engine Dornier Do X flying boat, for decades the world's largest and most powerful airplane.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kempten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kempten is well connected with the region through the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_7" title="Bundesautobahn 7">A 7</a> <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">autobahn</a> (<a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>–<a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a>–<a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCssen" title="Füssen">Füssen</a>). <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe" title="Bundesstraße">Bundesstraßen</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_12&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bundesstraße 12 (page does not exist)">B 12</a> (partly as <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_980" title="Bundesautobahn 980">A 980</a> autobahn), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_19&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bundesstraße 19 (page does not exist)">B 19</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundersstra%C3%9Fe_309&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bundersstraße 309 (page does not exist)">B 309</a> also intersect in Kempten.</p>
<p>The city is on the <a href="/wiki/Allg%C3%A4u_Railway_(Bavaria)" title="Allgäu Railway (Bavaria)">Allgäu Railway</a>, opened as part of the <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_South-North_Railway" title="Ludwig South-North Railway">Ludwig South-North Railway</a> in 1852, and <a href="/wiki/Kempten_(Allg%C3%A4u)_Hauptbahnhof" title="Kempten (Allgäu) Hauptbahnhof">Kempten station</a> currently boasts good <a href="/wiki/Intercity_(Deutsche_Bahn)" title="Intercity (Deutsche Bahn)">InterCity</a> and <a href="/wiki/EuroCity_in_Germany" title="EuroCity in Germany">EuroCity</a> rail connections.</p>
<p>The city bus system is operated by Kemptener Verkehrsbetriebe,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which operates over 20 lines.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kempten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kempten_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Kempten University of Applied Sciences">Kempten University of Applied Sciences</a> started in the winter semester of 1978–79 with 89 students and since then expanded and now accommodates more than 2800 students in eight degree courses:</p>
<ul>
<li>Business Administration</li>
<li>Computer Science</li>
<li>Electrical Engineering</li>
<li>Industrial Engineering – Electronic and Information Technology</li>
<li>Industrial Engineering – Mechanical Engineering with Distribution Management or Information Technology</li>

<p>There are also three college preparatory schools, called Gymnasium, (Allgäu-Gymnasium, Hildegardis-Gymnasium, Carl-von-Linde-Gymnasium) offering secondary education to the entire region of the Allgäu.</p>

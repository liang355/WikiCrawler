<p><a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>'s <b>Smith Campus Center</b> (formerly <b>Holyoke Center</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Brutalist_architecture" title="Brutalist architecture">Brutalist</a> administrative and service building occupying the block bounded by Massachusetts Avenue, Dunster Street, Holyoke Street, and Mount Auburn Street in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, directly opposite the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadsworth_Gate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wadsworth Gate (page does not exist)">Wadsworth Gate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Yard" title="Harvard Yard">Harvard Yard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-docomomo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-docomomo-1">[1]</a></sup> It houses administrative offices, an infirmary of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Health_Services" title="Harvard University Health Services">University Health Services</a>, and a retail/restaurant arcade.<sup id="cite_ref-docomomo_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-docomomo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smith_Campus_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Primarily designed by <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Sert" class="mw-redirect" title="José Luis Sert">José Luis Sert</a> (then dean of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Design" title="Harvard Graduate School of Design">Harvard Graduate School of Design</a>) and completed in 1966, the Smith Campus Center is an H-shaped ten-story <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a> building. Low-rise portions, including an underground <a href="/wiki/Parking_garage" class="mw-redirect" title="Parking garage">parking garage</a>, have a larger footprint of 360,000 square feet (33,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>).</p>
<p>After the first phase of construction in 1963, the <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson" title="Harvard Crimson">Harvard Crimson</a></i> cited a local joke: "The one nice feature about Holyoke Center is that it's the one place in Cambridge from which you can't see Holyoke Center".<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-dean_sert_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dean_sert-2">[2]</a></sup> Within a few years the building's novel design and technical features began to present numerous difficulties, which a Harvard official likened to "a five-car accident at an intersection. You just can't tell what caused it."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-crumbles_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crumbles-3">[3]</a></sup> These included crumbling of exterior structural concrete and an inefficient <a href="/wiki/Hydronics#Piping_arrangements" title="Hydronics">three-pipe</a> heating and cooling system.<sup id="cite_ref-crumbles_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crumbles-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was Harvard's first highrise building, and has been called a "<a href="/wiki/White_elephant" title="White elephant">gray elephant</a>" for the color of its concrete facades.<sup id="cite_ref-dean_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dean-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cromie_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cromie-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artworks">Artworks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smith_Campus_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Artworks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1964 to 1979, the penthouse dining room was decorated with five large paintings installed by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rothko" title="Mark Rothko">Mark Rothko</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Abstract_Expressionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Abstract Expressionist">Abstract Expressionist</a> artist. Due to an unstable paint formulation and high levels of direct sunlight, the pigments faded severely, and the paintings were moved to protective storage. Since their removal, the artworks have been publicly displayed only five times, most recently from November 2014 to July 2015, at the newly-renovated <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Art_Museums" title="Harvard Art Museums">Harvard Art Museums</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sheets_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sheets-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HAM_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAM-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaming_and_planned_renovation">Renaming and planned renovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smith_Campus_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Renaming and planned renovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally known as Holyoke Center, in 2013 it was renamed the Richard A. and Susan F. Smith Campus Center. It is scheduled to undergo a "major transformation" starting in 2016, in order to provide "large, flexible indoor gathering spaces for students, faculty, and staff and include food-service, lounge, and study areas, as well as space for exhibitions, events, and performances."<span class="Unicode nowrap"> </span><sup id="cite_ref-new_social_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_social-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

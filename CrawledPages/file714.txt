			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Semitic Museum</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SemiticMuseumHarvard.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/SemiticMuseumHarvard.jpg/250px-SemiticMuseumHarvard.jpg" width="250" height="333" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/SemiticMuseumHarvard.jpg/375px-SemiticMuseumHarvard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/SemiticMuseumHarvard.jpg/500px-SemiticMuseumHarvard.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="2592" /></a>

The Harvard Semitic Museum</div>

<p>The Harvard Semitic Museum was founded in 1889, and moved into its present location at 6 Divinity Avenue in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a> in 1903.</p>
<p>From the beginning, it was the home of the Department of Near Eastern Languages and Civilizations, a departmental library, a repository for research collections, a public educational institute, and a center for archaeological exploration. Among the Museum's early achievements were the first scientific excavations in <a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Land" class="mw-redirect" title="The Holy Land">the Holy Land</a> (at <a href="/wiki/Samaria" title="Samaria">Samaria</a> in 1907-1912) and excavations at <a href="/wiki/Nuzi" title="Nuzi">Nuzi</a> and Tell el-Khaleifeh in the <a href="/wiki/Sinai" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinai">Sinai</a>, where the earliest alphabet was found.</p>
<p>The Museum's artifacts include <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_seal" title="Cylinder seal">cylinder seals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clay_tablet" title="Clay tablet">cuneiform tablets</a>, and Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummy</a> <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagi" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarcophagi">sarcophagi</a>. Many are from museum-sponsored excavations in <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>. The museum holds plaster casts of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Obelisk_of_Shalmaneser_III" title="Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III">Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Hammurabi" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws of Hammurabi">Laws of Hammurabi</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Victory_stele_of_Esarhaddon" title="Victory stele of Esarhaddon">Stele of Esarhaddon</a>, as well as a full-scale model of an <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Israelite" class="mw-redirect" title="Israelite">Israelite</a> house. The Museum is dedicated to the use of these collections for the teaching, research, and publication of Near Eastern archaeology, history, and culture.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semitic_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Architectural firm A. W. Longfellow broke ground on the present Harvard Semitic Museum site on 27 September 1900. Construction was completed in Spring of 1902, and the public portions of the Museum were opened on February 5, 1903.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Museum's facilities were repurposed during World War II, and it was closed to the public from August 1942 through April 1946. Twelve years later it was closed again to the public, lasting from 1958 through 1982.</p>
<p>The Museum reopened in April 1982, and then Harvard President Derek Bok spoke at the reopening ceremony.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In December 2012, Harvard announced a new consortium, the Harvard Museums of Science and Culture, whose members are the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Harvard Museum of Natural History">Harvard Museum of Natural History</a>, the Harvard Semitic Museum, the <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Museum_of_Archaeology_and_Ethnology" title="Peabody Museum of Archaeology and Ethnology">Peabody Museum of Archaeology and Ethnology</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Collection_of_Historical_Scientific_Instruments" class="mw-redirect" title="Collection of Historical Scientific Instruments">Collection of Historical Scientific Instruments</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semitic_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibition_and_Education">Exhibition and Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semitic_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exhibition and Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Semitic Museum, through the collaborative efforts of departmental faculty, curators, museum curatorial staff, and students; mounts exhibits, often in conjunction with university courses, which not only serve the University community, but also attract the general public. The Museum has an active public outreach program featuring tours for school groups and teacher training workshops. The Museum also sponsors, either alone or in conjunction with other institutions, a number of public lectures each year. Through these educational efforts, the Museum seeks to promote a wider understanding of the civilizations of the Near East and their great cultural legacies.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Teaching_with_the_Collection">Teaching with the Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semitic_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Teaching with the Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many opportunities for Harvard University students and faculty to make use of the museum collections and facilities. Objects can be used for coursework, viewing assignments, research papers, senior theses, dissertations, and teaching displays. The basement seminar room is available for sections scheduled to view the collection.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_Publication">Research and Publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Semitic_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Research and Publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Harvard Semitic Museum sponsors archaeological field research into the complex societies of the Near East, with special emphasis on those ancient cultures related to the world of the Bible. Each year more than 100 staff, students, and volunteers participate in the Ashkelon Excavations (The Leon Levy Expedition), led by Honorary Museum Director Lawrence E. Stager, Dr. Daniel M. Master, and Dr. Adam Aja. The Museum, through its Harvard Semitic Series and Harvard Semitic Monographs, publishes archaeological, historical, philological, and cultural studies of the Near East, many of which present the research of the department faculty and their students.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Salzburg</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈzaltsbʊɐ̯k]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Salzburg.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Salzburg.ogg" class="internal" title="Salzburg.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[note 1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_language" title="Bavarian language">Bavarian</a>: <span lang="bar"><i>Såizburg</i></span>; literally: "Salt Fortress") is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_and_towns_in_Austria#List_of_cities_and_towns_by_population_size" title="List of cities and towns in Austria">fourth-largest city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and the capital of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Austria" title="States of Austria">federal state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_(state)" title="Salzburg (state)">Salzburg</a>.</p>
<p>Salzburg's "Old Town" (<i>Altstadt</i>) is internationally renowned for its <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">baroque architecture</a> and is one of the best-preserved city centers north of the Alps. It was listed as a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="UNESCO World Heritage Site">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a> in 1997. The city has three universities and a large population of students. Tourists also visit Salzburg to tour the historic center and the scenic <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alpine</a> surroundings.</p>
<p>Salzburg was the birthplace of 18th-century composer <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a>. In the mid‑20th century, the city was the setting for the musical play and film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sound_of_Music_(film)" title="The Sound of Music (film)">The Sound of Music</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Salzburg" title="Timeline of Salzburg">Timeline of Salzburg</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity_to_the_High_Middle_Ages">Antiquity to the High Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Antiquity to the High Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traces of <a href="/wiki/Human_settlements" class="mw-redirect" title="Human settlements">human settlements</a> have been found in the area, dating to the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> Age. The first settlements in Salzburg continuous with the present were apparently by the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> around the 5th century BC.</p>
<p>Around 15&#160;BC the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> merged the settlements into one city. At this time, the city was called "Juvavum" and was awarded the status of a Roman <i><a href="/wiki/Municipium" title="Municipium">municipium</a></i> in 45 <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">AD</a>. Juvavum developed into an important town of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Noricum" title="Noricum">Noricum</a>. After the Norican frontiers collapse, Juvavum declined so sharply that by the late 7th century it nearly became a ruin.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Life of <a href="/wiki/Rupert_of_Salzburg" title="Rupert of Salzburg">Saint Rupert</a></i> credits the 8th-century saint with the city's rebirth. When <a href="/wiki/Theodo_of_Bavaria" title="Theodo of Bavaria">Theodo of Bavaria</a> asked Rupert to become <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> c.&#160;700, Rupert reconnoitered the river for the site of his <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a>. Rupert chose Juvavum, ordained priests, and annexed the manor Piding. Rupert named the city "Salzburg". He traveled to <a href="/wiki/Evangelise" class="mw-redirect" title="Evangelise">evangelise</a> among <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagans</a>.</p>
<p>The name Salzburg means "Salt Castle" (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>Salis Burgium</i>). The name derives from the barges carrying salt on the <a href="/wiki/Salzach" title="Salzach">Salzach</a> River, which were subject to a toll in the 8th century as was customary for many communities and cities on European rivers. The <a href="/wiki/Festung_Hohensalzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Festung Hohensalzburg">Festung Hohensalzburg</a>, the city's <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortress</a>, was built in 1077 by Archbishop Gebhard, who made it his residence.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> It was greatly expanded during the following centuries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independence">Independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Independence from <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bavaria" title="History of Bavaria">Bavaria</a> was secured in the late 14th century. Salzburg was the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Salzburg" title="Archbishopric of Salzburg">Archbishopric of Salzburg</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Prince-bishop" title="Prince-bishop">prince-bishopric</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. As the reformation movement gained steam, riots broke out among peasants in the areas in and around Salzburg. The city was occupied during the <a href="/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War" title="German Peasants' War">German Peasants' War</a>, and the archbishop had to flee to the safety of the fortress<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> It was besieged for three months in 1525.</p>
<p>Eventually, tensions were quelled, and the city's independence led to an increase in wealth and prosperity, culminating in the late 16th to 18th centuries under the Prince Archbishops <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Dietrich_von_Raitenau" class="mw-redirect" title="Wolf Dietrich von Raitenau">Wolf Dietrich von Raitenau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Sittich_von_Hohenems_(Prince-Archbishop_of_Salzburg)" title="Mark Sittich von Hohenems (Prince-Archbishop of Salzburg)">Markus Sittikus</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Lodron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Paris Lodron (page does not exist)">Paris Lodron</a>. It was in the 17th century that Italian architects (and Austrians who had studied the Baroque style) rebuilt the city center as it is today along with many palaces.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_conflict">Religious conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religious conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_Protestants" title="Salzburg Protestants">Salzburg Protestants</a></div>
<p>On 31 October 1731, the 214th anniversary of <a href="/wiki/The_Ninety-Five_Theses" class="mw-redirect" title="The Ninety-Five Theses">the 95 Theses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Salzburg" title="Archbishopric of Salzburg">Archbishop</a> Count <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Anton_von_Firmian" class="mw-redirect" title="Leopold Anton von Firmian">Leopold Anton von Firmian</a> signed an Edict of Expulsion, the <i>Emigrationspatent</i>, directing all <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> citizens to recant their non-Catholic beliefs. 21,475 citizens refused to recant their beliefs and were expelled from Salzburg. Most of them accepted an offer by King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_I_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William I of Prussia">Friedrich Wilhelm I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a>, traveling the length and breadth of Germany to their new homes in East Prussia.<sup id="cite_ref-Perry_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perry-9">[8]</a></sup> The rest settled in other Protestant states in Europe and the British colonies in America.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Illuminism">Illuminism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Illuminism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1772–1803, under archbishop <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Graf_von_Colloredo" class="mw-redirect" title="Hieronymus Graf von Colloredo">Hieronymus Graf von Colloredo</a>, Salzburg was a centre of late <a href="/wiki/Illuminism" class="mw-redirect" title="Illuminism">Illuminism</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electorate_of_Salzburg">Electorate of Salzburg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Electorate of Salzburg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1803, the archbishopric was secularised by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>; he transferred the territory to <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_III_of_Tuscany" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferdinand III of Tuscany">Ferdinando III of Tuscany</a>, former Grand Duke of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Salzburg" title="Electorate of Salzburg">Electorate of Salzburg</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austrian_annexation_of_Salzburg">Austrian annexation of Salzburg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Austrian annexation of Salzburg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1805, Salzburg was annexed to the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian Empire</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Berchtesgaden_Provostry" title="Berchtesgaden Provostry">Berchtesgaden Provostry</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salzburg_under_Bavarian_rule">Salzburg under Bavarian rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Salzburg under Bavarian rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1809, the territory of Salzburg was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Kingdom of Bavaria</a> after Austria's defeat at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Wagram" title="Battle of Wagram">Wagram</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_of_Salzburg_and_annexation_by_Austria_and_Bavaria">Division of Salzburg and annexation by Austria and Bavaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Division of Salzburg and annexation by Austria and Bavaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Munich_(1816)" title="Treaty of Munich (1816)">Treaty of Munich (1816)</a>, Salzburg was definitively returned to Austria, but without Rupertigau and Berchtesgaden, which remained with <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. Salzburg was integrated into the Salzach province and Salzburgerland was ruled from <a href="/wiki/Linz" title="Linz">Linz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1850, Salzburg's status was restored as the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Salzburg" title="Duchy of Salzburg">Duchy of Salzburg</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Crown_land#Austria.E2.80.93Hungary" title="Crown land">crownland</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian Empire</a>. The city became part of <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> in 1866 as the capital of a crownland into the Austrian Empire. The nostalgia of the <a href="/wiki/Romantic_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantic Era">Romantic Era</a> led to increased tourism. In 1892, a <a href="/wiki/Funicular" title="Funicular">funicular</a> was installed to facilitate tourism to the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Hohensalzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Hohensalzburg">Hohensalzburg</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_republic">First republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: First republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War&#160;I</a> and the dissolution of the Austro-Hungarian Empire; Salzburg, as the capital of one of the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Austro-Hungarian">Austro-Hungarian</a> territories, became part of the new <a href="/wiki/German_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="German Austria">German Austria</a>. In 1918, it represented the residual <a href="/wiki/German_speaking_world" class="mw-redirect" title="German speaking world">German-speaking</a> territories of the Austrian heartlands. This was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/First_Austrian_Republic" title="First Austrian Republic">First Austrian Republic</a> in 1919, after the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint-Germain-en-Laye_(1919)" title="Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye (1919)">Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye (1919)</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Annexation_by_the_Third_Reich">Annexation by the Third Reich</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Annexation by the Third Reich">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a> (the occupation and annexation of Austria, including Salzburg, into the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Third Reich</a>) took place the 12 March 1938, one day before a scheduled <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a> about Austria's independence. German troops moved into the city. Political opponents, <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Austria" title="History of the Jews in Austria">Jewish citizens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Racial_policy_of_Nazi_Germany#Germanization_between_1939_and_1945" title="Racial policy of Nazi Germany">other minorities</a> were subsequently arrested and <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">deported</a> to concentration camps. The <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> was destroyed. After Germany invaded the Soviet Union, several <a href="/wiki/Prisoner-of-war_camp" title="Prisoner-of-war camp">POW camps</a> for prisoners from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and other enemy nations were organized in the city.</p>
<p>During the Nazi occupation, a <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a> camp was built in Salzburg-Maxglan. It was an Arbeitserziehungslager (work 'education' camp), which provided slave labour to local industry. It also operated as a Zwischenlager (transit camp), holding Roma before their deportation to German extermination camps or ghettos in German-occupied territories in eastern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> bombing destroyed 7,600 houses and killed 550 inhabitants. Fifteen strikes destroyed 46 percent of the city's buildings, especially those around Salzburg train station. Although the town's bridges and the dome of the <a href="/wiki/Salzburger_Dom" class="mw-redirect" title="Salzburger Dom">cathedral</a> were destroyed, much of its Baroque architecture remained intact. As a result, it is one of the few remaining examples of a town of its style. <a href="/wiki/List_of_major_U.S._Commands_of_World_War_II" title="List of major U.S. Commands of World War II">American troops</a> entered Salzburg on 5 May 1945.</p>
<p>In the city of Salzburg, there were several <a href="/wiki/Displaced_persons_camp" title="Displaced persons camp">DP Camps</a> following World War&#160;II. Among these were Riedenburg, Camp Herzl (Franz-Josefs-Kaserne), Camp Mülln, Bet Bialik, Bet Trumpeldor, and New Palestine. Salzburg was the centre of the American-occupied area in Austria.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_day">Present day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Present day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After World War&#160;II, Salzburg became the capital city of the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Salzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="State of Salzburg">State of Salzburg</a> (<i>Land Salzburg</i>). On 27 January 2006, the 250th anniversary of the birth of <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a>, all 35 churches of Salzburg rang their bells after 8:00&#160;p.m. (local time) to celebrate the occasion. Major celebrations took place throughout the year.</p>
<p>As of 2017 Salzburg has a GDP per capita of €46,100, which is greater than Austria’s, and most European countries’, average.<sup id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9Cveconomist.E2.80.9D_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9Cveconomist.E2.80.9D-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Salzburg is on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Salzach" title="Salzach">Salzach</a> River, at the northern boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. The mountains to Salzburg's south contrast with the rolling plains to the north. The closest alpine peak, the 1,972‑metre-high <a href="/wiki/Untersberg" title="Untersberg">Untersberg</a>, is less than 16 kilometres (10 miles) from the city centre. The <i>Altstadt</i>, or "old town", is dominated by its <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> towers and churches and the massive <i><a href="/wiki/Festung_Hohensalzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Festung Hohensalzburg">Festung Hohensalzburg</a></i>. This area is surrounded by two smaller mountains, the <i><a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6nchsberg" title="Mönchsberg">Mönchsberg</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kapuzinerberg" title="Kapuzinerberg">Kapuzinerberg</a></i>, which offer green relief within the city. Salzburg is approximately 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi) east of <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, 281&#160;km (175&#160;mi) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, and 300&#160;km (186&#160;mi) west of <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Salzburg is part of the <a href="/wiki/Temperateness" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> zone. The <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> specifies the climate as either <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (Cfb) or <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental</a> (Dfb), depending on which isotherm for winter means are used. With the more regular −3&#160;°C (27&#160;°F) one for the coldest month, Salzburg would be a four-season oceanic climate with significant temperature differences between seasons. Due to the location at the northern rim of the Alps, the amount of precipitation is comparatively high, mainly in the summer months. The specific <a href="/wiki/Drizzle" title="Drizzle">drizzle</a> is called <i>Schnürlregen</i> in the local dialect. In winter and spring, pronounced <a href="/wiki/Foehn_wind" title="Foehn wind">foehn winds</a> regularly occur.</p>

<p>Salzburg's official population significantly increased in 1935 when the city absorbed adjacent <a href="/wiki/Municipalities" class="mw-redirect" title="Municipalities">municipalities</a>. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War&#160;II</a>, numerous <a href="/wiki/Refugees" class="mw-redirect" title="Refugees">refugees</a> found a new home in the city. New residential space was constructed for American soldiers of the postwar occupation, and could be used for <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugees</a> when they left. Around 1950, Salzburg passed the mark of 100,000 citizens, and in 2006, it reached the mark of 150,000 citizens.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romanesque_and_Gothic">Romanesque and Gothic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Romanesque and Gothic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monasteries</a> and the early carcass houses dominated the <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> city for a long time. The Cathedral of <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">Archbishop</a> <a href="/wiki/Conrad_of_Wittelsbach" title="Conrad of Wittelsbach">Conrad of Wittelsbach</a> was the largest <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. The choir of the <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Franziskanerkirche_(Salzburg)" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Franziskanerkirche (Salzburg)">Franciscan Church Hall</a>, construction was begun by <a href="/wiki/Hans_von_Burghausen" title="Hans von Burghausen">Hans von Burghausen</a> and completed by <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Stephan_Krumenauer" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Stephan Krumenauer">Stephan Krumenauer</a>, is one of the most prestigious religious gothic constructions of southern Germany. At the end of the Gothic era the Collegiate church "Nonnberg", Margaret Chapel in <a href="/wiki/St_Peter%27s_Archabbey,_Salzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="St Peter's Archabbey, Salzburg">St.&#160;Peter's Cemetery</a>, the St.&#160;George's Chapel and the stately halls of the "Hoher Stock" in the <a href="/wiki/Hohensalzburg_Castle" title="Hohensalzburg Castle">Hohensalzburg Castle</a> were constructed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_and_baroque">Renaissance and baroque</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Renaissance and baroque">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Inspired by <a href="/wiki/Vincenzo_Scamozzi" title="Vincenzo Scamozzi">Vincenzo Scamozzi</a>, Prince Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Dietrich_von_Raitenau" class="mw-redirect" title="Wolf Dietrich von Raitenau">Wolf Dietrich von Raitenau</a> began to transform the medieval town to the architectural ideals of the late <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>. Plans for a massive cathedral by Scamozzi failed to materialize upon the fall of the archbishop. A second cathedral planned by <a href="/wiki/Santino_Solari" title="Santino Solari">Santino Solari</a> rose as the first early <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> church in Salzburg. It served as an example for many other churches in Southern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Markus_Sittikus_von_Hohenems_(Salzburg)" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Markus Sittikus von Hohenems (Salzburg)">Markus Sittikus</a> and <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Paris_von_Lodron" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Paris von Lodron">Paris von Lodron</a> continued to rebuild the city with major projects such as <a href="/wiki/Hellbrunn_Palace" title="Hellbrunn Palace">Hellbrunn Palace</a>, the prince archbishop's residence, the university buildings, fortifications, and many other buildings. Giovanni Antonio Daria managed by order of Prince Archbishop Guido von Thun the construction of the residential well. Giovanni <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Gaspare_Zuccalli" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Gaspare Zuccalli">Gaspare Zuccalli</a>, by order of the same archbishop, created the Erhard and the Kajetan church in the south of the town. The city's redesign was completed with buildings designed by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bernhard_Fischer_von_Erlach" title="Johann Bernhard Fischer von Erlach">Johann Bernhard Fischer von Erlach</a>, donated by Prince Archbishop Johann Ernst von Thun.</p>
<p>After the era of Ernst von Thun, the city's expansion came to a halt, which is the reason why there are no churches built in the <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">rococo</a> style. <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_von_Schrattenbach" title="Sigismund von Schrattenbach">Sigismund von Schrattenbach</a> continued with the construction of "Sigmundstor" and the statue of holy Maria on the cathedral square. With the fall and division of the former "Fürsterzbistums Salzburg" (Archbishopric) to <a href="/wiki/Upper_Austria" title="Upper Austria">Upper Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> (Rupertigau) and <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tyrol (region)">Tyrol</a> (Zillertal Matrei) began a long period of urban stagnancy. This era didn't end before the period of promoterism (<i><a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%BCnderzeit" title="Gründerzeit">Gründerzeit</a></i>) brought new life into urban development. The builder dynasty <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Jakob_Ceconi" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Jakob Ceconi">Jakob Ceconi</a> and <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Carl_Freiherr_von_Schwarz" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Carl Freiherr von Schwarz">Carl Freiherr von Schwarz</a> filled major positions in shaping the city in this era.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Buildings of classical <a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture">modernism</a> and in particular the post-war modernism are frequently encountered in Salzburg. Examples are the Zahnwurzen house (a house in the Linzergasse&#160;22 in the right center of the old town), the "Lepi" (a public baths in <i>Leopoldskron</i>) (built 1964) and the original 1957 constructed congress center of Salzburg, which was replaced by a new building in 2001. An important and famous example of architecture of this era is the 1960 opening of the <a href="/wiki/Gro%C3%9Fes_Festspielhaus" title="Großes Festspielhaus">Großes Festspielhaus</a> by <a href="/wiki/Clemens_Holzmeister" title="Clemens Holzmeister">Clemens Holzmeister</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_architecture">Contemporary architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Contemporary architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adding <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_architecture" title="Contemporary architecture">contemporary architecture</a> to Salzburg's old town without risking its UNESCO World Heritage status is problematic. Yet some new structures have been added: the <a href="/wiki/Mozarteum" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozarteum">Mozarteum</a> at the baroque <a href="/wiki/Mirabell_Palace" title="Mirabell Palace">Mirabell garden</a> (Architecture Robert Rechenauer),<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> the 2001 Congress house (Architecture: Freemasons), the 2011 Unipark Nonntal (Architecture: Storch Ehlers partners), the 2001 "Makartsteg" bridge (Architecture: HALLE1), and the "Residential and studio house" of the architects <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Christine_Lechner" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Christine Lechner">Christine</a> and <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Horst_Lechner" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Horst Lechner">Horst Lechner</a> in the middle of Salzburg's old town (winner of the <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Architekturpreis_des_Landes_Salzburg" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Architekturpreis des Landes Salzburg">architecture award of Salzburg 2010</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Other examples of contemporary architecture lie outside the old town: the Faculty of Science building (Universität Salzburg – Architecture <a href="https://www.mediawiki.org/wiki/de:Wilhelm_Holzbauer" class="extiw" title="mw:de:Wilhelm Holzbauer">Willhelm Holzbauer</a>) built on the edge of free green space, the <a href="/wiki/Blobitecture" title="Blobitecture">blob architecture</a> of Red Bull <a href="/wiki/Hangar-7" title="Hangar-7">Hangar‑7</a> (Architecture: Volkmar Burgstaller<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup>) at Salzburg Airport, home to <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Mateschitz" title="Dietrich Mateschitz">Dietrich Mateschitz</a>'s Flying Bulls and the Europark shopping mall. (Architecture: <a href="/wiki/Massimiliano_Fuksas" title="Massimiliano Fuksas">Massimiliano Fuksas</a>)</p>

<p>Salzburg is a <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Austria" title="Tourism in Austria">tourist</a> favourite, with the number of tourists outnumbering locals by a large margin in peak times. In addition to Mozart's birthplace noted above, other notable places include:</p>
<p><b>Old Town</b></p>
<ul>
<li>Historic Centre of the City of Salzburg, declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in 1996</li>
<li>Baroque architecture, including many churches</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Salzburg_Cathedral" title="Salzburg Cathedral">Salzburg Cathedral</a> (<i>Salzburger Dom</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hohensalzburg_Castle" title="Hohensalzburg Castle">Hohensalzburg Castle</a> (<i>Festung Hohensalzburg</i>), overlooking the Old Town, is one of the largest castles in Europe</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Franciscan_Church,_Salzburg" title="Franciscan Church, Salzburg">Franciscan Church</a> (<i>Franziskanerkirche</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/St_Peter%27s_Abbey,_Salzburg" title="St Peter's Abbey, Salzburg">St Peter's Abbey</a> with the <i>Petersfriedhof</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nonnberg_Abbey" title="Nonnberg Abbey">Nonnberg Abbey</a>, a Benedictine monastery</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Salzburg_Residenz" title="Salzburg Residenz">Salzburg Residenz</a>, the magnificent former residence of the Prince-Archbishops</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Residenzgalerie" title="Residenzgalerie">Residenzgalerie</a>, an art museum in the Salzburg Residenz</li>
<li>Mozart's Birthplace</li>
<li>Mozart's Residence</li>
<li>University Church</li>
<li>Siegmundstor (or Neutor)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Getreidegasse" title="Getreidegasse">Getreidegasse</a></li>
<li>Sphaera, a sculpture of a man on a golden sphere (Stephan Balkenhol, 2007)</li>
</ul>
<p><b>Outside the Old Town</b></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Mirabell_Palace" title="Mirabell Palace">Mirabell Palace</a>, with its wide gardens full of flowers</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Hellbrunn" title="Hellbrunn">Hellbrunn</a> with its parks and castles</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_citizens">Notable citizens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Notable citizens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mozart_(5).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Mozart_%285%29.JPG/220px-Mozart_%285%29.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Mozart_%285%29.JPG/330px-Mozart_%285%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Mozart_%285%29.JPG/440px-Mozart_%285%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mozart_(5).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mozart's birthplace at Getreidegasse 9</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>The composer <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a>, born and raised in Salzburg, was employed as musician at the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">archbishopal</a> court from 1773 to 1781. His house of birth and residence are tourist attractions. His family is buried in a small church graveyard in the old town, and there are many monuments to "Wolferl" in the city.</li>
<li>The composer <a href="/wiki/Johann_Michael_Haydn" class="mw-redirect" title="Johann Michael Haydn">Johann Michael Haydn</a>, brother of the composer <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Haydn" title="Joseph Haydn">Joseph Haydn</a>. His works were admired by <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Schubert</a>. He was also the teacher of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Maria_von_Weber" title="Carl Maria von Weber">Carl Maria von Weber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anton_Diabelli" title="Anton Diabelli">Anton Diabelli</a> and is known for his sacred music.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Christian_Doppler" title="Christian Doppler">Christian Doppler</a>, expert on <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustic</a> theory, was born in Salzburg. He is most known for his discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler effect</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Josef_Mohr" class="mw-redirect" title="Josef Mohr">Josef Mohr</a>, born in Salzburg. Together with <a href="/wiki/Franz_Gruber_(musician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Franz Gruber (musician)">Franz Gruber</a>, he composed and wrote the text for "<a href="/wiki/Silent_Night_(song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Silent Night (song)">Silent Night</a>". As a priest in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Oberndorf_bei_Salzburg" title="Oberndorf bei Salzburg">Oberndorf</a> he performed the song for the first time on Christmas Eve 1818.</li>
<li>King <a href="/wiki/Otto_of_Greece" title="Otto of Greece">Otto</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Greece" title="Kingdom of Greece">Greece</a> was born Prince Otto Friedrich Ludwig of Bavaria at the Palace of Mirabell, a few days before the city reverted from Bavarian to Austrian rule.</li>
<li>Writer <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Zweig" title="Stefan Zweig">Stefan Zweig</a>, lived in Salzburg for about 15 years, until 1934.</li>
<li>Maria Von Trapp (later <a href="/wiki/Maria_Trapp" class="mw-redirect" title="Maria Trapp">Maria Trapp</a>) and her family lived in Salzburg until they fled to the United States following the Nazi takeover.</li>
<li>Salzburg is the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Makart" title="Hans Makart">Hans Makart</a>, a 19th-century Austrian painter-decorator and national celebrity. Makartplatz (<i>Makart Square</i>) is named in his honour.</li>
<li>Writer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bernhard" title="Thomas Bernhard">Thomas Bernhard</a>, raised in Salzburg and spent part of his life there.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Herbert_von_Karajan" title="Herbert von Karajan">Herbert von Karajan</a>, notable musician and conductor. He was born in Salzburg and died in 1989 in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Anif" title="Anif">Anif</a>.</li>
<li>Anthropologist Udo Ludwig was born here.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roland_Ratzenberger" title="Roland Ratzenberger">Roland Ratzenberger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> driver, was born in Salzburg. <p>The city is served by comprehensive rail connections, with frequent east-west trains serving <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>, including daily high-speed <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">ICE</a> services. The city acts as a hub for south-bound trains through the Alps into <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salzburg_Airport" title="Salzburg Airport">Salzburg Airport</a> has scheduled flights to European cities such as <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>. In addition to these, there are numerous charter flights.</p>
<p>In the main city, there is the <a href="/wiki/Trolleybuses_in_Salzburg" title="Trolleybuses in Salzburg">Salzburg trolleybus system</a> and bus system with a total of more than 20 lines, and service every 10 minutes. Salzburg has an <a href="/wiki/S-Bahn" class="mw-redirect" title="S-Bahn">S-Bahn</a> system with four Lines (S1, S2, S3, S11), trains depart from the main station every 30 minutes, and they are part of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%96BB" class="mw-redirect" title="ÖBB">ÖBB</a> network. Suburb line number S1 reaches the world-famous Silent Night chapel in <a href="/wiki/Oberndorf_bei_Salzburg" title="Oberndorf bei Salzburg">Oberndorf</a> in about 25 minutes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1960s, the movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sound_of_Music_(film)" title="The Sound of Music (film)">The Sound of Music</a></i> used some locations in and around Salzburg and <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_(state)" title="Salzburg (state)">the state of Salzburg</a>. The movie was based on the true story of <a href="/wiki/Maria_von_Trapp" title="Maria von Trapp">Maria von Trapp</a> who took up with an aristocratic family and fled the German <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a>. Although the film is not particularly popular nor well known among Austrians, the town draws many visitors who wish to visit the <a href="/wiki/Filming_location" title="Filming location">filming locations</a>, alone or on tours.</p>
<p>Salzburg is the setting for the Austrian crime series <a href="/wiki/Stockinger" title="Stockinger">Stockinger</a>.</p>
<p>In the 2010 film <i><a href="/wiki/Knight_%26_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Knight &amp; Day">Knight &amp; Day</a></i>, Salzburg serves as the backdrop for a large portion of the film.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Austrian_German" title="Austrian German">Austrian German</a> is widely written. <a href="/wiki/Austro-Bavarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Austro-Bavarian">Austro-Bavarian</a> is the German dialect of this territory and widely spoken.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The former SV Austria Salzburg reached the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="UEFA Cup">UEFA Cup</a> final in <a href="/wiki/1993%E2%80%9394_UEFA_Cup" title="1993–94 UEFA Cup">1994</a>. On 6 April 2005 <a href="/wiki/Red_Bull" title="Red Bull">Red Bull</a> bought the club and changed its name into <a href="/wiki/FC_Red_Bull_Salzburg" title="FC Red Bull Salzburg">FC Red Bull Salzburg</a>. The home stadium of Red Bull Salzburg is the <a href="/wiki/Wals_Siezenheim_Stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Wals Siezenheim Stadium">Wals Siezenheim Stadium</a> in a suburb in the agglomeration of Salzburg and was one of the venues for the <a href="/wiki/2008_European_Football_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="2008 European Football Championship">2008 European Football Championship</a>. The FC Red Bull Salzburg plays in the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Football_Bundesliga" title="Austrian Football Bundesliga">Austrian Bundesliga</a>.</p>
<p>After Red Bull had bought the SV Austria Salzburg and changed its name and team colors, some supporters of the club decided to leave and form a new club with the old name and old colors, wanting to preserve the traditions of their club. The reformed <a href="/wiki/SV_Austria_Salzburg" title="SV Austria Salzburg">SV Austria Salzburg</a> was founded in 2005 and currently plays in the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Football_First_League" title="Austrian Football First League">Erste Liga</a>, only one tier below the Bundesliga.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_hockey">Ice hockey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Ice hockey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Red Bull also sponsors the local <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> team, the <a href="/wiki/EC_Red_Bull_Salzburg" title="EC Red Bull Salzburg">EC Salzburg Red Bulls</a>. The team plays in the <a href="/wiki/Erste_Bank_Eishockey_Liga" class="mw-redirect" title="Erste Bank Eishockey Liga">Erste Bank Eishockey Liga</a>, an Austria-headquartered crossborder league featuring the best teams from Austria, Hungary, Slovenia and Italy, as well as one Czech team.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sports">Other sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Salzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Other sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Salzburg was a candidate city for the <a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics" title="2010 Winter Olympics">2010</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Olympics" title="2014 Winter Olympics">2014 Winter Olympics</a>, but lost to <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sochi" title="Sochi">Sochi</a> respectively.</p>

<p><i><b>U.S. News &amp; World Report</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Media_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Media company">media company</a> that publishes news, opinion, consumer advice, rankings, and analysis. Founded as a <a href="/wiki/Newsmagazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Newsmagazine">newsweekly magazine</a> in 1933, U.S. News transitioned to primarily web-based publishing in 2010. U.S. News is best known today for its influential Best Colleges and Best Hospitals rankings, but it has expanded its content and product offerings in education, health, money, careers, travel, and cars. The rankings are popular in North America but have drawn widespread criticism from colleges, administrations, and students for their dubious, disparate, and arbitrary nature. The ranking system by <i>U.S. News</i> is usually contrasted with the <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Monthly" title="Washington Monthly">Washington Monthly</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> rankings.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><b>United States News</b></i> was founded in 1933 by David Lawrence (1888–1973), who also started <i><b>World Report</b></i> in 1946. The two magazines covered national and international news separately, but Lawrence merged them into <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> in 1948<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and subsequently sold the magazine to his employees. Historically, the magazine tended to be slightly more <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">conservative</a> than its two primary competitors, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i>, and focused more on economic, health, and education stories. It also eschewed sports, entertainment, and celebrity news.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Important milestones in the early history of the magazine include the introduction of the "Washington Whispers" column in 1934 and the "News You Can Use" column in 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-about-usnews_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-usnews-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-usnews-timeline_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usnews-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1958, the weekly magazine's circulation passed one million and reached two million by 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-about-usnews_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-usnews-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1983, it has become known primarily for its influential ranking and annual reports of colleges and graduate schools, spanning across most fields and subjects. <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> is America's oldest and best-known ranker of academic institutions,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and covers the fields of business, law, medicine, engineering, education, social sciences and public affairs, in addition to many other areas.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Its print edition was consistently included in national <a href="/wiki/Bestseller" title="Bestseller">bestseller</a> lists, augmented by online subscriptions. Additional rankings published by <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> include hospitals, medical specialties and automobiles.</p>
<p>In October 1984, <a href="/wiki/Publisher" class="mw-redirect" title="Publisher">publisher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> developer <a href="/wiki/Mortimer_B._Zuckerman" class="mw-redirect" title="Mortimer B. Zuckerman">Mortimer B. Zuckerman</a> purchased U.S. News &amp; World Report.<sup id="cite_ref-usnews-timeline_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usnews-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> Zuckerman is also the owner of the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_News_(New_York)" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily News (New York)">New York Daily News</a></i>. In 1993, <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> entered the digital world by providing content to <a href="/wiki/CompuServe" title="CompuServe">CompuServe</a> and in 1995, the website usnews.com was launched. In 2001, the website won the <a href="/wiki/National_Magazine_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="National Magazine Award">National Magazine Award</a> for General Excellence Online.<sup id="cite_ref-infoplease_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infoplease-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2007, <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> published its first list of the nation's best high schools. Its ranking methodology includes state test scores and the success of poor and minority students on these exams, and schools' performance in <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Placement" title="Advanced Placement">Advanced Placement exams</a>.</p>
<p>Starting in June 2008, the magazine reduced its publication frequency in three steps. It switched in June 2008 from weekly to biweekly.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In November 2008 it decreased to monthly.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In November 2010, it was reported that <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> would be switched to an online-only format, effective after it published its December issue, with the exception of print publishing special issues on colleges, hospitals, and personal finance.<sup id="cite_ref-poynter_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poynter-10">[10]</a></sup> Moving forward, the publication expressed its plans to focus mainly on research and the provision of relevant information to students pursuing <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a>. Its rankings and university guide books are available in print at the majority of bookstores and magazine vendors in the United States, selling millions of copies on an annual basis.<sup id="cite_ref-circulation_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-circulation-11">[11]</a></sup> In June 2008, citing the decline overall magazine circulation and advertising, <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> announced that it would become a <a href="/wiki/Biweekly" title="Biweekly">biweekly</a> publication, starting January 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It hoped advertisers would be attracted to the schedule, which allowed ads to stay on newsstands a week longer. However, five months later the magazine changed its frequency again, becoming monthly.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In August 2008, <i>U.S. News</i> expanded and revamped its online opinion section.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The new version of the opinion page included daily new op-ed content as well as the new Thomas Jefferson Street blog.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> An internal memo was sent on November 5, 2010, to the staff of the magazine informing them that the "December issue will be our last print monthly sent to subscribers, whose remaining print and digital replica subscriptions will be filled by other publishers."<sup id="cite_ref-poynter_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poynter-10">[10]</a></sup> The memo went on to say that the publication would be moving to a primarily digital format but that it would continue to print special issues such as "the college and grad guides, as well as hospital and personal finance guides." Prior to going defunct, <i>U.S. News</i> was the lowest-ranking news magazine in the U.S., after <i>Time</i> and <i>Newsweek</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A weekly digital magazine, <i>U.S. News Weekly</i>, introduced in January 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> continued to offer subscription content until it ceased at the end of April 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company is owned by U.S. News &amp; World Report, L.P., a privately held company based in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">Daily News</a></i> building in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. The editorial staff is headquartered in Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-about-usnews_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-usnews-3">[3]</a></sup> The company's move to the Web made it possible for <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> to expand its <a href="/wiki/Service_journalism" title="Service journalism">service journalism</a> with the introduction of several consumer-facing rankings products. The company returned to profitability in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-19">[19]</a></sup> The editorial staff of U.S. News &amp; World Report is based in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Kelly_(editor)" title="Brian Kelly (editor)">Brian Kelly</a> has been the <a href="/wiki/Chief_content_officer" title="Chief content officer">chief content officer</a> since April 2007. The company is owned by media proprietor <a href="/wiki/Mortimer_Zuckerman" title="Mortimer Zuckerman">Mortimer Zuckerman</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rankings">Rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Who_Runs_America?"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Who_Runs_America.3F">Who Runs America?</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Who Runs America?">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first of the U.S. News &amp; World Report's famous rankings was its "Who Runs America?" surveys. These ran in the spring of each year from 1974 to 1986. The magazine would have a cover typically featuring persons selected by the USN &amp; WR as being the ten most powerful persons in the United States. Every single edition of the series listed the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> as the most powerful person, but the #2 position included such persons as <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a> (1974),<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chair_of_the_Federal_Reserve" title="Chair of the Federal Reserve">Federal Reserve Chairmen</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Volcker" title="Paul Volcker">Paul Volcker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Burns" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Burns">Arthur Burns</a> (each listed multiple years) and US Senator <a href="/wiki/Edward_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Kennedy">Edward Kennedy</a> (1979).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> While most of the top ten each year were officials in government, occasionally others were included, including TV anchormen <a href="/wiki/Walter_Cronkite" title="Walter Cronkite">Walter Cronkite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dan_Rather" title="Dan Rather">Dan Rather</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chase_Manhattan_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Chase Manhattan Bank">Chase Manhattan Bank</a> Chairman <a href="/wiki/David_Rockefeller" title="David Rockefeller">David Rockefeller</a>, <a href="/wiki/AFL-CIO" class="mw-redirect" title="AFL-CIO">AFL-CIO</a> leader <a href="/wiki/George_Meany" title="George Meany">George Meany</a>, and consumer advocate <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>. The only woman to make the top ten list was First Lady <a href="/wiki/Rosalynn_Carter" title="Rosalynn Carter">Rosalynn Carter</a> in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to these overall top ten persons, the publication also included top persons in each of several fields, including Education, Business, Finance, Journalism, and many other areas. The survey was discontinued after 1986.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Best_colleges">Best colleges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Best colleges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report_Best_Colleges_Ranking" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report Best Colleges Ranking">U.S. News &amp; World Report Best Colleges Ranking</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:US_News_College.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2e/US_News_College.jpg/220px-US_News_College.jpg" width="220" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2e/US_News_College.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="270" data-file-height="368" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:US_News_College.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
2016 Best Colleges cover</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1983, <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> published its first "America's Best Colleges" report. <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The rankings have been compiled and published annually since 1987. These rankings are based upon data that <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> collects from each educational institution from an annual survey sent to each school. The rankings are also based upon opinion surveys of university faculties and administrators who do not belong to the schools.</p>
<p>The popularity of U.S. News &amp; World Report's Best Colleges rankings is reflected in its 2014 release, which brought 2.6 million unique visitors and 18.9 million page views to usnews.com in one day.<sup id="cite_ref-minonline_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-minonline-24">[24]</a></sup> Traffic came from over 3,000 sites, including <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-minonline_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-minonline-24">[24]</a></sup> U.S. News &amp; World Report continues to publish comprehensive college guides in book form.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Robert Morse created the U.S. News Best Colleges rankings methodology, and continues to oversee its application as chief data strategist at U.S. News. In 2014, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> featured a profile of Morse, exploring his 30-year career with the publication.<sup id="cite_ref-morse-wapo-profile_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morse-wapo-profile-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_college_and_university_rankings_(North_America)" title="Criticism of college and university rankings (North America)">Criticism of college and university rankings (North America)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_college_and_university_rankings_(2007_United_States)" title="Criticism of college and university rankings (2007 United States)">Criticism of college and university rankings (2007 United States)</a></div>
<p>During the 1990s, several educational institutions in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> were involved in a movement to boycott the <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> college rankings survey. The first was <a href="/wiki/Reed_College" title="Reed College">Reed College</a>, which stopped submitting the survey in 1995. The survey was also criticized by <a href="/wiki/Alma_College" title="Alma College">Alma College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s_College_(Annapolis/Santa_Fe)" title="St. John's College (Annapolis/Santa Fe)">St. John's College</a> during the late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/SAT_scores" class="mw-redirect" title="SAT scores">SAT scores</a> play a role in The <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> college rankings even though <i>U.S. News</i> is not empowered with the ability to formally verify or recalculate the scores that are represented to them by schools. Since the mid-1990s there have been many instances documented by the popular press wherein schools lied about their SAT scores in order to obtain a higher ranking.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Expos%C3%A9_(journalism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Exposé (journalism)">exposé</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> stated that the elements in the methodology of <i>U.S News &amp; World Report's</i> rankings are redundant and can be reduced to one thing: money.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On June 19, 2007, during the annual meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Annapolis_Group" title="Annapolis Group">Annapolis Group</a>, members discussed <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_college_and_university_rankings_(2007_United_States)#Presidents_Letter" title="Criticism of college and university rankings (2007 United States)">the letter to college presidents</a> asking them not to participate in the "reputation survey" section of the <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> survey (this section comprises 25% of the ranking).</p>
<p>As a result, "a majority of the approximately 80 presidents at the meeting said that they did not intend to participate in the <i>U.S. News</i> reputational rankings in the future."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The statement also said that its members "have agreed to participate in the development of an alternative common format that presents information about their colleges for students and their families to use in the college search process."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This database will be web-based and developed in conjunction with higher-education organizations including the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Independent_Colleges_and_Universities" title="National Association of Independent Colleges and Universities">National Association of Independent Colleges and Universities</a> (NAICU) and the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Independent_Colleges" title="Council of Independent Colleges">Council of Independent Colleges</a>.</p>
<p>On June 22, 2007, <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> editor Robert Morse issued a response in which he argued, "in terms of the peer assessment survey, we at <i>U.S. News</i> firmly believe the survey has significant value because it allows us to measure the 'intangibles' of a college that we can't measure through statistical data. Plus, the reputation of a school can help get that all-important first job and plays a key part in which grad school someone will be able to get into. The peer survey is by nature subjective, but the technique of asking industry leaders to rate their competitors is a commonly accepted practice. The results from the peer survey also can act to level the playing field between private and public colleges."<sup id="cite_ref-aboutanna_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aboutanna-32">[32]</a></sup> In reference to the alternative database discussed by the Annapolis Group, Morse also argued, "It's important to point out that the Annapolis Group's stated goal of presenting college data in a common format has been tried before [...] <i>U.S. News</i> has been supplying this exact college information for many years already. And it appears that <a href="/wiki/NAICU" class="mw-redirect" title="NAICU">NAICU</a> will be doing it with significantly less comparability and functionality. <i>U.S. News</i> first collects all these data (using an agreed-upon set of definitions from the Common Data Set). Then we post the data on our website in easily accessible, comparable tables. In other words, the Annapolis Group and the others in the NAICU initiative actually are following the lead of <i>U.S. News</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-aboutanna_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aboutanna-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some higher education experts, such as Kevin Carey of Education Sector, have asserted that <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report'</i>s college rankings system is merely a list of criteria that mirrors the superficial characteristics of elite colleges and universities. According to Carey, the <i>U.S. News</i> ranking system is deeply flawed. Instead of focusing on the fundamental issues of how well colleges and universities educate their students and how well they prepare them to be successful after college, the magazine's rankings are almost entirely a function of three factors: fame, wealth, and exclusivity. He suggests that there are more important characteristics parents and students should research to select colleges, such as how well students are learning and how likely students are to earn a degree.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The question of college rankings and their impact on admissions gained greater attention in March 2007, when <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Lawrence_College#Outgoing_president" title="Sarah Lawrence College">Dr. Michele Tolela Myers</a> (the former President of <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Lawrence_College" title="Sarah Lawrence College">Sarah Lawrence College</a>) <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_college_and_university_rankings_(2007_United_States)#Sarah_Lawrence_College" title="Criticism of college and university rankings (2007 United States)">shared in an op-ed</a><sup id="cite_ref-Tolela_Myers_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tolela_Myers-34">[34]</a></sup> that the <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i>, when not given SAT scores for a university, chooses to simply rank the college with an invented SAT score of approximately one standard deviation (roughly 200 SAT points) behind those of peer colleges, with the reasoning being that <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts_colleges_in_the_United_States#SAT_optional_movement" title="Liberal arts colleges in the United States">SAT-optional</a> universities will, because of their test-optional nature, accept higher numbers of less academically capable students.</p>
<p>In a 2011 article regarding the Sarah Lawrence controversy, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sacks" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Sacks">Peter Sacks</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i> criticized the <i>U.S. News</i> rankings' centering on test scores and denounced the magazine's "best colleges" list as a scam:<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>In the <i>U.S. News</i> worldview of college quality, it matters not a bit what students actually learn on campus, or how a college actually contributes to the intellectual, ethical and personal growth of students while on campus, or how that institution contributes to the public good [...] and then, when you consider that student SAT scores are profoundly correlated [to] parental income and education levels – the social class that a child is born into and grows up with – you begin to understand what a corrupt emperor 'America's Best Colleges' really is. The ranking amounts to little more than a pseudo-scientific and yet popularly legitimate tool for perpetuating inequality between educational haves and have nots – the rich families from the poor ones, and the well-endowed schools from the poorly endowed ones.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Best_global_universities">Best global universities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Best global universities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report_Best_Global_University_Ranking" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report Best Global University Ranking">U.S. News &amp; World Report Best Global University Ranking</a></div>
<p>In October 2014, the <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> published its inaugural "Best Global Universities" rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Inside_Higher_Ed" title="Inside Higher Ed">Inside Higher Ed</a></i> noted that the <i>U.S. News</i> is entering into the international college and university rankings area that is already "dominated by three major global university rankings," namely the <i><a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education_World_University_Rankings" title="Times Higher Education World University Rankings">Times Higher Education World University Rankings</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Academic_Ranking_of_World_Universities" title="Academic Ranking of World Universities">Academic Ranking of World Universities</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/QS_World_University_Rankings" title="QS World University Rankings">QS World University Rankings</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HigherEd_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HigherEd-37">[37]</a></sup> Robert Morse stated that "it's natural for <i>U.S. News</i> to get into this space."<sup id="cite_ref-HigherEd_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HigherEd-37">[37]</a></sup> Morse also noted that the <i>U.S. News</i> "will also be the first American publisher to enter the global rankings space."<sup id="cite_ref-HigherEd_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HigherEd-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Best_hospitals">Best hospitals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Best hospitals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report_Best_Hospitals_Rankings" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report Best Hospitals Rankings">U.S. News &amp; World Report Best Hospitals Rankings</a></div>
<p>Since 1990, <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i> has compiled the Best Hospitals rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The Best Hospitals rankings are specifically based on a different methodology that looks at difficult (<a href="/w/index.php?title=High_acuity_medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="High acuity medicine (page does not exist)">high acuity</a>) cases within 16 specialties, including <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>; <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" class="mw-redirect" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endocrinology" title="Endocrinology">endocrinology</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ear,_nose_and_throat" class="mw-redirect" title="Ear, nose and throat">ear, nose and throat</a>; <a href="/wiki/Gastroenterology" title="Gastroenterology">gastroenterology</a>; <a href="/wiki/Geriatrics" title="Geriatrics">geriatrics</a>; <a href="/wiki/Gynecology" class="mw-redirect" title="Gynecology">gynecology</a>; <a href="/wiki/Cardiology" title="Cardiology">heart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_surgery" title="Cardiac surgery">heart surgery</a>; <a href="/wiki/Kidney_disorders" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidney disorders">kidney disorders</a>; <a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">neurology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neurosurgery" title="Neurosurgery">neurosurgery</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ophthalmology" title="Ophthalmology">ophthalmology</a>; <a href="/wiki/Orthopedics" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthopedics">orthopedics</a>; <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pulmonology" title="Pulmonology">pulmonology</a>; <a href="/wiki/Physical_medicine_and_rehabilitation" title="Physical medicine and rehabilitation">rehabilitation</a>; <a href="/wiki/Rheumatology" title="Rheumatology">rheumatology</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Urology" title="Urology">urology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Comarow_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Comarow-40">[40]</a></sup> In addition to rankings for each of these specialties, hospitals that excel in many <i>U.S. News</i> areas are ranked in the Honor Roll.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Best_cars">Best cars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Best cars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2007, U.S. News has developed an innovative rankings system for new and used <a href="/wiki/Automobiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobiles">automobiles</a>. The rankings span over 30 classes of cars, trucks, SUVs, minivans, wagons, and sports cars. Each automobile receives an overall score, as well as a performance, interior, and recommendation score to the nearest tenth on a 1-10 scale. Scores are based on the consensus opinion of America's trusted automotive experts, as well as reliability and safety data.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> U.S. News also produces annual "Best Cars for the Money" and "Best Cars for Families" awards across approximately 20 classes of cars, trucks, SUVs, and minivans. Money award winners are derived by combining vehicle price and five-year cost of ownership with the opinion of the automotive press,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> while family awards are tabulated by combining critics' opinions with the vehicle's availability of family-friendly features and interior space, as well as safety and reliability data. Money and family award winners are announced in February and March of each year, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Best_states">Best states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._News_%26_World_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Best states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, U.S. News published its first ranking of all 50 U.S. states, incorporating metrics in seven categories: health care, education, crime and corrections, infrastructure, opportunity, economy, and government. The weighting of the individual categories in determining overall rank was informed by surveys on what matters most to residents. <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> occupies the top rank of the 2017 list, with an overall #2 ranking in health care and #1 ranking in education.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

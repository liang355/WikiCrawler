<p><b>Jean Louis Rodolphe Agassiz</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small>English:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/iː/ or /ɪ/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>French:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[agasi]</a></span>; May 28, 1807&#160;– December 14, 1873) was a Swiss-American <a href="/wiki/Biologist" title="Biologist">biologist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologist</a> recognized as an innovative and prodigious scholar of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a>, with later American writings that have received criticism for their endorsement of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_racism" title="Scientific racism">scientific racism</a>. Agassiz grew up in Switzerland, and studied and received Doctor of Philosophy and medical degrees at Erlangen and Munich, respectively. After further studies with <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Cuvier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Humboldt</a> in Paris, Agassiz proceeded with research leading to his appointment as professor of <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a> at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Neuch%C3%A2tel" title="University of Neuchâtel">University of Neuchâtel</a>.</p>
<p>After visiting <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> mid-career, he emigrated to the United States in 1847 and became a professor of zoology and geology at Harvard, and to head its <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Scientific_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence Scientific School">Lawrence Scientific School</a> and found its <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology" title="Museum of Comparative Zoology">Museum of Comparative Zoology</a>. Agassiz made extensive contributions to <a href="/wiki/Ichthyology" title="Ichthyology">ichthyological</a> classification (including of extinct species) and to the study of <a href="/wiki/Geological_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Geological history">geological history</a> (including to the founding of <a href="/wiki/Glaciology" title="Glaciology">glaciology</a>), and has become broadly known through study of his thorough regimen of observational data gathering and analysis. He made vast institutional and scientific contributions to zoology, geology, and related areas—including many multi-volume research series running to thousands of pages.</p>
<p>In the 21st century, his resistance to <a href="/wiki/Darwinism" title="Darwinism">Darwinian</a> <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_racism" title="Scientific racism">scientific racism</a> evident in his writings on human <a href="/wiki/Polygenism" title="Polygenism">polygenism</a>, tarnished his reputation and led to controversies over his legacy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Agassiz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Agassiz_family" title="Agassiz family">Agassiz family</a></div>
<p>Louis Agassiz was born in Môtier (now part of <a href="/wiki/Haut-Vully" title="Haut-Vully">Haut-Vully</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Fribourg" title="Canton of Fribourg">canton of Fribourg</a>, Switzerland. Educated first at home, then spending four years of secondary school in <a href="/wiki/Bienne" class="mw-redirect" title="Bienne">Bienne</a>, he completed his elementary studies in <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>. Having adopted medicine as his profession, he studied successively at the universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Zurich" title="University of Zurich">Zürich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruprecht_Karl_University_of_Heidelberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruprecht Karl University of Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Maximilians_University_of_Munich" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwig Maximilians University of Munich">Munich</a>; while there he extended his knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a>, especially of <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>. In 1829 he received the degree of <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctor of philosophy">doctor of philosophy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Friedrich-Alexander-University,_Erlangen-Nuremberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Friedrich-Alexander-University, Erlangen-Nuremberg">Erlangen</a>, and in 1830 that of <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctor of medicine">doctor of medicine</a> at Munich.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-2">[2]</a></sup> Moving to Paris he came under the tutelage of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a> (and later his financial benevolence)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Humboldt and <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Georges Cuvier</a> launched him on his careers of geology and zoology respectively. Previously he had not paid special attention to the study of <a href="/wiki/Ichthyology" title="Ichthyology">ichthyology</a>, but it soon became the focus of his life's work.<sup id="cite_ref-AMB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMB-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1819–1820, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Baptist_von_Spix" title="Johann Baptist von Spix">Johann Baptist von Spix</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Philipp_von_Martius" title="Carl Friedrich Philipp von Martius">Carl Friedrich Philipp von Martius</a> were engaged in an expedition to <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, and on their return to Europe, amongst other collections of natural objects they brought home an important set of the <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_fish" title="Freshwater fish">fresh water fish</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, and especially of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_river#Fish" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon river">Amazon River</a>. Spix, who died in 1826, did not live long enough to work out the history of these fish, and Agassiz, having just completed his studies, was selected by Martius for this project. He at once threw himself into the work with an enthusiasm which characterized him to the end of his busy life. The task of describing the Brazilian fish was completed and published in 1829. This was followed by research into the history of the fish found in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Neuch%C3%A2tel" title="Lake Neuchâtel">Lake Neuchâtel</a>. Enlarging his plans, in 1830 he issued a prospectus of a <i>History of the Freshwater Fish of Central Europe</i>. It was only in 1839, however, that the first part of this publication appeared, and it was completed in 1842.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1832 he was appointed professor of natural history in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Neuch%C3%A2tel" title="University of Neuchâtel">University of Neuchâtel</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fish" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fish">fossil fish</a> there soon attracted his attention. The fossil-rich stones furnished by the <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slates</a> of <a href="/wiki/Glarus" title="Glarus">Glarus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestones</a> of Monte <a href="/wiki/Bolca" title="Bolca">Bolca</a> were known at the time, but very little had been accomplished in the way of scientific study of them. Agassiz, as early as 1829, planned the publication of the work which, more than any other, laid the foundation of his worldwide fame. Five volumes of his <i>Recherches sur les poissons fossiles</i> ("Research on Fossil Fish") appeared at intervals from 1833 to 1843. They were magnificently illustrated, chiefly by Joseph Dinkel.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In gathering materials for this work Agassiz visited the principal museums in Europe, and meeting Cuvier in Paris, he received much encouragement and assistance from him.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-2">[2]</a></sup> They had known him for seven years at the time.</p>

<p>Agassiz found that his palaeontological labors made necessary a new basis of ichthyological classification. The fossils rarely exhibited any traces of the soft tissues of fish. They consisted chiefly of the teeth, scales and fins, with the bones being perfectly preserved in comparatively few instances. He therefore adopted a classification which divided fish into four groups: Ganoids, Placoids, Cycloids and Ctenoids, based on the nature of the scales and other dermal appendages. While Agassiz did much to improve fish <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a>, his classification has been superseded by later work.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Agassiz's descriptive work proceeded, it became obvious that it would over-tax his resources unless financial assistance could be found. The <a href="/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="British Association for the Advancement of Science">British Association</a> came to his aid, and the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Ellesmere" title="Earl of Ellesmere">Earl of Ellesmere</a>—then <a href="/wiki/Francis_Egerton,_1st_Earl_of_Ellesmere" title="Francis Egerton, 1st Earl of Ellesmere">Lord Francis Egerton</a>—stepped in to help. The 1,290 original drawings made for the work were purchased by the Earl, and presented by him to the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Society_of_London" title="Geological Society of London">Geological Society of London</a>. In 1836 the <a href="/wiki/Wollaston_Medal" title="Wollaston Medal">Wollaston Medal</a> was awarded to Agassiz by the council of that society for his work on fossil ichthyology; and in 1838 he was elected a foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> animals engaged his attention. In 1837 he issued the "Prodrome" of a monograph on the recent and fossil <a href="/wiki/Echinodermata" class="mw-redirect" title="Echinodermata">Echinodermata</a>, the first part of which appeared in 1838; in 1839–40 he published two quarto volumes on the fossil Echinoderms of Switzerland; and in 1840–45 he issued his <i>Études critiques sur les mollusques fossiles</i> ("Critical Studies on Fossil <a href="/wiki/Mollusk" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusk">Mollusks</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before his first visit to England in 1834, the labours of <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Miller" title="Hugh Miller">Hugh Miller</a> and other geologists brought to light the remarkable fish of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a> of the northeast of Scotland. The strange forms of the <i><a href="/wiki/Pterichthys" class="mw-redirect" title="Pterichthys">Pterichthys</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Coccosteus" title="Coccosteus">Coccosteus</a></i> and other genera were then made known to geologists for the first time. They were of intense interest to Agassiz, and formed the subject of a special monograph by him published in 1844–45: <i>Monographie des poissons fossiles du Vieux Grès Rouge, ou Système Dévonien (Old Red Sandstone) des Îles Britanniques et de Russie</i> ("Monograph on Fossil Fish of the Old Red Sandstone, or Devonian System of the British Isles and of Russia").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-2">[2]</a></sup> In the early stages of his career in Neuchatel, Agassiz also made a name for himself as a man who could run a scientific department well. Under his care, the University of Neuchâtel soon became a leading institution for scientific inquiry.</p>

<p>He was the only person to name a species after <a href="/wiki/Mary_Anning" title="Mary Anning">Mary Anning</a> during her lifetime. She was a paleontologist who was known around the world for important finds, but because of her gender, usually omitted from formal recognition for her work. In the early 1840s he named two fossil fish species after her—<i><a href="/wiki/Acrodus_anningiae" class="mw-redirect" title="Acrodus anningiae">Acrodus anningiae</a></i>, and <i>Belenostomus anningiae</i>—and another after her friend, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Philpot" title="Elizabeth Philpot">Elizabeth Philpot</a>. Agassiz was grateful for the help the women had given him in examining fossil fish specimens during his visit to Lyme Regis in 1834.<sup id="cite_ref-Emling169_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emling169-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_age">Ice age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Agassiz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ice age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1837 Agassiz was the first to scientifically propose that the Earth had been subject to a past <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-evans_1887_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans_1887-7">[7]</a></sup> when he proposed to the <a href="/wiki/Helvetic_Society" title="Helvetic Society">Helvetic Society</a> that ancient glaciers had not only flowed outward from the Alps, but that even larger glaciers had simultaneously encroached southward on the plains and mountains of Europe, Asia and North America, smothering the entire <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a> in a prolonged Ice Age. In the same year, he was elected a foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences</a>. Prior to this proposal, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Goethe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horace-B%C3%A9n%C3%A9dict_de_Saussure" title="Horace-Bénédict de Saussure">de Saussure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_Venetz" title="Ignaz Venetz">Venetz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Charpentier" title="Jean de Charpentier">Jean de Charpentier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Schimper" title="Karl Friedrich Schimper">Karl Friedrich Schimper</a> and others had made the <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> of the Alps the subjects of special study, and Goethe,<sup id="cite_ref-goethe_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goethe-8">[8]</a></sup> Charpentier as well as Schimper<sup id="cite_ref-evans_1887_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans_1887-7">[7]</a></sup> had even arrived at the conclusion that the <a href="/wiki/Glacial_erratic" title="Glacial erratic">erratic</a> blocks of alpine rocks scattered over the slopes and summits of the <a href="/wiki/Jura_Mountains" title="Jura Mountains">Jura Mountains</a> had been moved there by glaciers. The question having attracted the attention of Agassiz, he not only discussed it with Charpentier and Schimper and made successive journeys to the alpine regions in company with them, but he had a hut constructed upon one of the <a href="/wiki/Aar_Glaciers" class="mw-redirect" title="Aar Glaciers">Aar Glaciers</a>, which for a time he made his home, in order to investigate the structure and movements of the ice.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>These labours resulted, in 1840, in the publication of his work in two volumes entitled <i>Études sur les glaciers</i> ("Studies on Glaciers").<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In it he discussed the movements of the glaciers, their <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a>, their influence in grooving and rounding the rocks over which they travelled, and in producing the striations and <i>roches moutonnees</i> seen in Alpine-style landscapes. He not only accepted Charpentier's and Schimper's idea that some of the alpine glaciers had extended across the wide plains and valleys drained by the <a href="/wiki/Aar" class="mw-redirect" title="Aar">Aar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Rhône">Rhône</a>, but he went still farther. He concluded that, in the relatively recent past, Switzerland had been another <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>; that instead of a few glaciers stretching across the areas referred to, one vast sheet of ice, originating in the higher Alps, had extended over the entire valley of northwestern Switzerland until it reached the southern slopes of the Jura, which, though they checked and deflected its further extension, did not prevent the ice from reaching in many places the summit of the range. The publication of this work gave a fresh impetus to the study of glacial phenomena in all parts of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-368_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911367-368-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thus familiarized with the phenomena associated with the movements of recent glaciers, Agassiz was prepared for a discovery which he made in 1840, in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/William_Buckland" title="William Buckland">William Buckland</a>. The two visited the mountains of Scotland together, and found in different locations clear evidence of ancient glacial action. The discovery was announced to the Geological Society of London in successive communications. The mountainous districts of England, Wales, and Ireland were also considered to constitute centres for the dispersion of glacial debris; and Agassiz remarked "that great sheets of ice, resembling those now existing in Greenland, once covered all the countries in which unstratified gravel (boulder drift) is found; that this gravel was in general produced by the <a href="/wiki/Trituration" title="Trituration">trituration</a> of the sheets of ice upon the subjacent surface, etc."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Agassiz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1842–1846 he issued his <i>Nomenclator Zoologicus</i>, a classified list, with references, of all names employed in zoology for genera and groups — a work of great labour and research. With the aid of a grant of money from the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Prussia">King of Prussia</a>, Agassiz crossed the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> in the autumn of 1846 with the twin purposes of investigating the natural history and geology of North America and delivering a course of 12 lectures on "The Plan of Creation as shown in the Animal Kingdom,"<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> by invitation from <a href="/wiki/John_Amory_Lowell" title="John Amory Lowell">J. A. Lowell</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Institute" title="Lowell Institute">Lowell Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. The financial offers presented to him in the United States induced him to settle there, where he remained to the end of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368-11">[11]</a></sup> He was elected a Foreign Honorary Member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1846.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-13">[13]</a></sup> Agassiz knew Harvard botanist <a href="/wiki/Asa_Gray" title="Asa Gray">Asa Gray</a> and they had a cordial relationship but disagreed on some scientific issues.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> For example, Agassiz was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_Lazzaroni" title="Scientific Lazzaroni">Scientific Lazzaroni</a>, a group of mostly physical scientists who wanted American academia to mimic the autocratic academic structures of European universities, whereas Gray was a staunch opponent of that group. Agassiz also felt each human race had different origins but Gray believed in the unity of all humans.<sup id="cite_ref-dupree_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dupree-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>His engagement for the Lowell Institute lectures precipitated the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Scientific_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence Scientific School">Lawrence Scientific School</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1847 with him as its head.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Harvard appointed him professor of zoology and geology, and he founded the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology" title="Museum of Comparative Zoology">Museum of Comparative Zoology</a> there in 1859 serving as the museum's first director until his death in 1873. During his tenure at Harvard, he was, among many other things, an early student of the effect of the last Ice Age on North America.</p>
<p>He continued his lectures for the Lowell Institute. In succeeding years, he gave series of lectures on "Ichthyology" (1847–48 season), "Comparative Embryology" (1848–49), "Functions of Life in Lower Animals" (1850–51), "Natural History" (1853–54), "Methods of Study in Natural History" (1861–62), "Glaciers and the Ice Period" (1864–65), "Brazil" (1866–67) and "Deep Sea <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology_dredge" title="Marine biology dredge">Dredging</a>" (1869–70).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1850 he married an American college teacher, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Cabot_Agassiz" title="Elizabeth Cabot Agassiz">Elizabeth Cabot Cary</a>, who later wrote introductory books about natural history and, after his death, a lengthy biography of her husband.</p>
<p>Agassiz served as a non-resident lecturer at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> while also being on faculty at Harvard.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1852 he accepted a medical professorship of <a href="/wiki/Comparative_anatomy" title="Comparative anatomy">comparative anatomy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlestown, Massachusetts">Charlestown, Massachusetts</a>, but he resigned in two years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368-11">[11]</a></sup> From this time his scientific studies dropped off, but he was a profound influence on the American branches of his two fields, teaching decades worth of future prominent scientists, including <a href="/wiki/Alpheus_Hyatt" title="Alpheus Hyatt">Alpheus Hyatt</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Starr_Jordan" title="David Starr Jordan">David Starr Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joel_Asaph_Allen" title="Joel Asaph Allen">Joel Asaph Allen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Le_Conte" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Le Conte">Joseph Le Conte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Ingersoll" title="Ernest Ingersoll">Ernest Ingersoll</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_James" title="William James">William James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Shaler" title="Nathaniel Shaler">Nathaniel Shaler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hubbard_Scudder" title="Samuel Hubbard Scudder">Samuel Hubbard Scudder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpheus_Packard" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpheus Packard">Alpheus Packard</a>, and his son <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Emanuel_Agassiz" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander Emanuel Agassiz">Alexander Emanuel Agassiz</a>, among others. He had a profound impact on paleontologist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Doolittle_Walcott" title="Charles Doolittle Walcott">Charles Doolittle Walcott</a> and natural scientist <a href="/wiki/Edward_S._Morse" title="Edward S. Morse">Edward S. Morse</a>. In return his name appears attached to several species, as well as here and there throughout the American landscape, notably <a href="/wiki/Lake_Agassiz" title="Lake Agassiz">Lake Agassiz</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> precursor to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipeg" title="Lake Winnipeg">Lake Winnipeg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Red_River_of_the_North" title="Red River of the North">Red River</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Agassiz_(California)" title="Mount Agassiz (California)">Mount Agassiz</a>, a bastion of the <a href="/wiki/Palisades_(California_Sierra)" title="Palisades (California Sierra)">Palisade Crest</a>, the largest glaciated region of California's <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>.</p>
<p>During this time he grew in fame even in the public consciousness, becoming one of the best-known scientists in the world. By 1857 he was so well-loved that his friend <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wadsworth_Longfellow" title="Henry Wadsworth Longfellow">Henry Wadsworth Longfellow</a> wrote "The fiftieth birthday of Agassiz" in his honor, and read it at a dinner given for Agassiz by the <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Club_(Boston,_Massachusetts)" title="Saturday Club (Boston, Massachusetts)">Saturday Club</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368-11">[11]</a></sup> His own writing continued with four (of a planned ten) volumes of <i>Natural History of the United States</i> which were published from 1857 to 1862. During this time he also published a catalog of papers in his field, <i>Bibliographia Zoologiae et Geologiae</i>, in four volumes between 1848 and 1854.</p>
<p>Stricken by ill health in the 1860s, he resolved to return to the field for relaxation and to resume his studies of Brazilian fish. In April 1865 he led a party to Brazil. Returning home in August 1866, an account of this expedition, entitled <i>A Journey in Brazil</i>, was published in 1868. In December 1871 he made a second eight-month excursion, known as the <a href="/wiki/Hassler_(vessel)" title="Hassler (vessel)"><i>Hassler</i></a> expedition under the command of Commander Philip Carrigan Johnson (brother of <a href="/wiki/Eastman_Johnson" title="Eastman Johnson">Eastman Johnson</a>), visiting South America on its southern Atlantic and Pacific seaboards. The ship explored the <a href="/wiki/Magellan_Strait" class="mw-redirect" title="Magellan Strait">Magellan Strait</a>, which drew the praise of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>.</p>
<p>Elizabeth Agassiz wrote, at the Strait: '.....the Hassler pursued her course, past a seemingly endless panorama of mountains and forests rising into the pale regions of snow and ice, where lay glaciers in which every rift and crevasse, as well as the many cascades flowing down to join the waters beneath, could be counted as she steamed by them.... These were weeks of exquisite delight to Agassiz. The vessel often skirted the shore so closely that its geology could be studied from the deck.'</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Agassiz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>From his first marriage to Cecilie Bruan, Agassiz had two daughters in addition to son Alexander.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1863, Agassiz's daughter Ida married <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lee_Higginson" title="Henry Lee Higginson">Henry Lee Higginson</a>, later to be founder of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Boston Symphony Orchestra">Boston Symphony Orchestra</a> and benefactor to <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and other schools. On November 30, 1860, Agassiz's daughter Pauline was married to <a href="/wiki/Quincy_Adams_Shaw" title="Quincy Adams Shaw">Quincy Adams Shaw</a> (1825–1908), a wealthy Boston merchant and later benefactor to the Boston <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts,_Boston" title="Museum of Fine Arts, Boston">Museum of Fine Arts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the last years of his life, Agassiz worked to establish a permanent school where zoological science could be pursued amid the living subjects of its study. In 1873, a private philanthropist (John Anderson) gave Agassiz the island of <a href="/wiki/Penikese" class="mw-redirect" title="Penikese">Penikese</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Buzzards_Bay_(bay)" class="mw-redirect" title="Buzzards Bay (bay)">Buzzards Bay</a>, Massachusetts (south of <a href="/wiki/New_Bedford,_Massachusetts" title="New Bedford, Massachusetts">New Bedford</a>), and presented him with $50,000 to permanently endow it as a practical school of natural science, especially devoted to the study of marine zoology.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368-11">[11]</a></sup> The John Anderson school collapsed soon after Agassiz's death; it is considered a precursor of the Woods Hole <a href="/wiki/Marine_Biological_Laboratory" title="Marine Biological Laboratory">Marine Biological Laboratory</a>, which is nearby.</p>
<p>Within his lifetime, Agassiz had developed a reputation for a particularly demanding teaching style. He would allegedly "lock a student up in a room full of turtle-shells, or lobster-shells, or oyster-shells, without a book or a word to help him, and not let him out till he had discovered all the truths which the objects contained."<sup id="cite_ref-James_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-21">[21]</a></sup> Two of Agassiz's most prominent students detailed their personal experiences under his tutelage, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hubbard_Scudder" title="Samuel Hubbard Scudder">Samuel Hubbard Scudder</a> in a short magazine article for <a href="/wiki/Every_Saturday" title="Every Saturday">Every Saturday</a><sup id="cite_ref-Scudder_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scudder-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Southgate_Shaler" class="mw-redirect" title="Nathaniel Southgate Shaler">Nathaniel Southgate Shaler</a> in his <i>Autobiography</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Shaler_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shaler-23">[23]</a></sup> These and other recollections were collected and published by Lane Cooper in 1917,<sup id="cite_ref-Cooper_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper-24">[24]</a></sup> which Ezra Pound was to draw on for his anecdote of <a href="/wiki/Parable_of_the_sunfish" class="mw-redirect" title="Parable of the sunfish">Agassiz and the sunfish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pound_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pound-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agassiz is remembered today for his theories on ice ages, and for his resistance to Charles Darwin's theories on <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, which he kept up his entire life. He died in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a> in 1873 and was buried at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Auburn_Cemetery" title="Mount Auburn Cemetery">Mount Auburn Cemetery</a>, joined later by his wife. His monument is a boulder selected from the moraine of the glacier of the Aar near the site of the old <a href="/wiki/File:Hotelneuchatelois2.jpg" title="File:Hotelneuchatelois2.jpg">Hôtel des Neuchâtelois</a>, not far from the spot where his hut once stood; and the pine-trees that shelter his grave were sent from his old home in Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodward1911368-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Cambridge elementary school north of Harvard University was named in his honor and the surrounding neighborhood became known as "<a href="/wiki/Agassiz,_Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Agassiz, Cambridge, Massachusetts">Agassiz</a>" as a result. The school's name was changed to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_L._Baldwin_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Maria L. Baldwin School (page does not exist)">Maria L. Baldwin School</a> on May&#160;21, 2002, due to concerns about Agassiz's racism, and to honor <a href="/wiki/Maria_Louise_Baldwin" title="Maria Louise Baldwin">Maria Louise Baldwin</a> the African-American principal of the school who served from 1889 until 1922.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The neighborhood, however, continues to be known as Agassiz.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>An ancient <a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake" title="Glacial lake">glacial lake</a> that formed in the Great Lakes region of North America, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Agassiz" title="Lake Agassiz">Lake Agassiz</a>, is named after him, as are <a href="/wiki/Mount_Agassiz_(California)" title="Mount Agassiz (California)">Mount Agassiz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Palisades_(California_Sierra)" title="Palisades (California Sierra)">California's Palisades</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Agassiz_(Utah)" title="Mount Agassiz (Utah)">Mount Agassiz</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Uinta_Mountains" title="Uinta Mountains">Uinta Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agassiz_Peak" title="Agassiz Peak">Agassiz Peak</a> in Arizona and in his native Switzerland, the <a href="/wiki/Agassizhorn" title="Agassizhorn">Agassizhorn</a> in the Bernese Alps. <a href="/wiki/Agassiz_Glacier" title="Agassiz Glacier">Agassiz Glacier</a> and Agassiz Creek in <a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(U.S.)" title="Glacier National Park (U.S.)">Glacier National Park</a> and Mount Agassiz in Bethlehem, New Hampshire in the White Mountains also bear his name. A <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">crater</a> on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Crater_Agassiz" class="mw-redirect" title="Crater Agassiz">Crater Agassiz</a></i> and a promontorium on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> are also named in his honour. A headland situated in <a href="/wiki/Palmer_Land" title="Palmer Land">Palmer Land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> is named in his honor, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Agassiz" title="Cape Agassiz">Cape Agassiz</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">main-belt</a> <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> named <a href="/wiki/2267_Agassiz" class="mw-redirect" title="2267 Agassiz">2267 Agassiz</a> is also named in association with Louis Agassiz.</p>
<p>Several animal species are named in honor of Louis Agassiz, including <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apistogramma_agassizi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Apistogramma agassizi (page does not exist)">Apistogramma agassizi</a></i> <small><a href="/wiki/Franz_Steindachner" title="Franz Steindachner">Steindachner</a>, 1875</small> (Agassiz's dwarf cichlid); <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isocapnia_agassizi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Isocapnia agassizi (page does not exist)">Isocapnia agassizi</a></i> <small>Ricker, 1943</small> (a <a href="/wiki/Stonefly" class="mw-redirect" title="Stonefly">stonefly</a>); <i><a href="/wiki/Publius_agassizi" title="Publius agassizi">Publius agassizi</a></i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Johann_Jakob_Kaup" title="Johann Jakob Kaup">Kaup</a>, 1871)</small> (a <a href="/wiki/Passalidae" title="Passalidae">passalid beetle</a>); <i><a href="/wiki/Xylocrius_agassizi" title="Xylocrius agassizi">Xylocrius agassizi</a></i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/John_Lawrence_LeConte" title="John Lawrence LeConte">LeConte</a>, 1861)</small> (a <a href="/wiki/Longhorn_beetle" title="Longhorn beetle">longhorn beetle</a>); <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exoprosopa_agassizi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Exoprosopa agassizi (page does not exist)">Exoprosopa agassizi</a></i> <small>Loew, 1869</small> (a <a href="/wiki/Bombyliidae" title="Bombyliidae">bee fly</a>); <i><a href="/wiki/Chelonia_agassizii" class="mw-redirect" title="Chelonia agassizii">Chelonia agassizii</a></i> <small><a href="/wiki/Marie_Firmin_Bocourt" title="Marie Firmin Bocourt">Bocourt</a>, 1868</small> (Galápagos green turtle);<sup id="cite_ref-EDR_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EDR-29">[29]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Philodryas" title="Philodryas">Philodryas agassizii</a></i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Jan" title="Giorgio Jan">Jan</a>, 1863)</small> (a South American snake);<sup id="cite_ref-EDR_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EDR-29">[29]</a></sup> and the most well-known, <i><a href="/wiki/Gopherus_agassizii" class="mw-redirect" title="Gopherus agassizii">Gopherus agassizii</a></i> <small>(<a href="/wiki/James_Graham_Cooper" title="James Graham Cooper">Cooper</a>, 1863)</small> (the desert tortoise).<sup id="cite_ref-EDR_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EDR-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>An elementary school called the Agassiz Elementary School in Minneapolis, Minnesota existed from 1922-1981.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005 the <a href="/wiki/European_Geosciences_Union" title="European Geosciences Union">EGU</a> Division on Cryospheric Sciences established the Louis Agassiz Medal, awarded to individuals in recognition of their outstanding scientific contribution to the study of the <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a> on Earth or elsewhere in the solar system.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>He took part in a monthly gathering called the <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Club_(Boston,_Massachusetts)" title="Saturday Club (Boston, Massachusetts)">Saturday Club</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Omni_Parker_House" title="Omni Parker House">Parker House</a>, a meeting of Boston writers and intellectuals. He was therefore mentioned in a stanza of the <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Wendell_Holmes_Sr." title="Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr.">Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr.</a> poem "At the Saturday Club":</p>
<blockquote>
<div class="poem">
<p>There, at the table's further end I see<br />
In his old place our Poet's vis-à-vis,<br />
The great PROFESSOR, strong, broad-shouldered, square,<br />
In life's rich noontide, joyous, debonair<br />
...<br />
<br />
How will her realm be darkened, losing thee,<br />
Her darling, whom we call our AGASSIZ!</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polygenism">Polygenism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Agassiz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polygenism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Agassiz came to the United States he wrote prolifically on <a href="/wiki/Polygenism" title="Polygenism">polygenism</a>, the idea that races were created separately, that they could be classified on the basis of specific climatic zones, and that they were endowed with unequal attributes,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> ideas now included under the rubric of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_racism" title="Scientific racism">scientific racism</a>.</p>
<p>Agassiz never supported <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a>, and claimed his views on polygenism had nothing to do with politics.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> He was influenced by philosophical <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">idealism</a> and the scientific work of <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Georges Cuvier</a>. According to Agassiz, genera and species were ideas in the mind of God; their existence in God's mind prior to their physical creation meant that God could create humans as one species yet in several distinct and geographically separate acts of creation. Per Church historian Paul M. Blowers, Agassiz believed there is one species of humans but many different creations of races.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agassiz was in modern terms a <a href="/wiki/Creationist" class="mw-redirect" title="Creationist">creationist</a> who believed nature had order because God created it directly. Agassiz viewed his career in science as a search for ideas in the mind of the creator expressed in creation. Agassiz denied that <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Migration" class="extiw" title="wikt:Migration">migration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptation</a> could account for the geographical age or any of the past. Adaptation takes time; in an example, Agassiz questioned how plants or animals could migrate through regions they were not equipped to handle.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup> According to Agassiz the conditions in which particular creatures live "are the conditions necessary to their maintenance, and what among organized beings is essential to their temporal existence must be at least one of the conditions under which they were created".<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

After the <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">1906 San Francisco earth­quake</a> toppled Agassiz's statue from the façade of <a href="/wiki/Stanford" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanford">Stanford</a>'s zoology building, Stanford President <a href="/wiki/David_Starr_Jordan" title="David Starr Jordan">David Starr Jordan</a> wrote that "Somebody‍—‌<a href="/wiki/Frank_Angell" title="Frank Angell">Dr. Angell</a>, perhaps‍—‌remarked that 'Agassiz was great in the abstract but not in the concrete.<span style="padding-right:.2em;">'</span>"<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></div>

<p>In his work he noted similarities of distribution of like species in different geological eras, a phenomenon clearly not the result of migration. Agassiz questioned how fish of the same species live in lakes well separated with no joining waterway, concluding they were created at both locations. He held that the intelligent adaptation of creatures to their environments testified to an intelligent plan. According to historian Paul Blowers, the conclusions of his studies led him to believe that whichever region each animal was found in, was created there-"animals are naturally autochthones wherever they are found". He later extended this idea to humans in his theory of polygenism.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup> This holds that animals, plants and humans were all created in "special provinces" with distinct populations of species created in and for each province. He claimed plants, animals and humans did not originate in pairs but were created in large numbers. According to Agassiz, the different races were created in different provinces, with each race <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indigenous" class="extiw" title="wikt:indigenous">indigenous</a> to the province it was created in, citing evidence from <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Egypt" title="Culture of Egypt">Egyptian</a> monuments to prove that racial types had been fixed for at least five millennia. He held that all species and human races are <a href="/wiki/Punctuated_equilibrium" title="Punctuated equilibrium">fixed</a>, and that species do not evolve into other species.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup> The provinces that the different races were created in included <i>Western American Temperate</i> (the indigenous peoples west of the Rockies), <i>Eastern American Temperate</i> (east of the Rockies), <i>Tropical Asiatic</i> (south of the Himalayas), <i>Temperate Asiatic</i> (east of the Urals and north of the Himalayas), <i>South American Temperate</i> (South America), <i>New Holland</i> (Australia), <i>Arctic</i> (Alaska and Arctic Canada). <i>Cape of Good Hope</i> (South Africa), and <i>American Tropical</i> (Central America and the West Indies).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Agassiz, like other polygenists, believed the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a> recounted the origin of the white race only and that the animals and plants in the Bible refer only to those species proximate and familiar to <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam and Eve</a>. Agassiz, <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Clark_Nott" class="mw-redirect" title="Josiah Clark Nott">Josiah Clark Nott</a>, and other polygenists such as <a href="/wiki/George_Gliddon" title="George Gliddon">George Gliddon</a>, believed that the original Hebrew form of the name Adam came from a Biblical Hebrew <a href="/wiki/Consonantal_root" class="mw-redirect" title="Consonantal root">consonantal root</a> referring to redness, so that the name can be interpreted to mean "to show red in the face" or "blusher". They also believed that since only light-skinned people can blush, then the biblical Adam must have been Caucasian.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup> Agassiz believed that the writers of the Bible only knew of local events, for example <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_flood" class="mw-redirect" title="Noah's flood">Noah's flood</a> was a local event only known to the regions that were populated by ancient Hebrews. Agassiz also believed that the writers of the Bible did not know about any events other than what was going on in their own region and their intermediate neighbors.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Per Blowers, Agassiz also opposed <a href="/wiki/Monogenism" title="Monogenism">monogenism</a> and evolution, believing that the theory of evolution reduced the wisdom of God to an impersonal materialism.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Shaler" title="Nathaniel Shaler">Nathaniel Shaler</a> studied under Agassiz at <a href="/wiki/Harvard" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard">Harvard</a>, and shared his views on polygenism.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a> asserted that Agassiz's observations sprang from racist bias, in particular from his revulsion on first encountering African-Americans in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, Blowers notes that despite favoring polygenism, Agassiz rejected racism and believed in a spiritualized human unity.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup> According to Blowers, Agassiz believed God made all men equal:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Those intellectual and moral qualities which are so eminently developed in civilized society, but which equally exist in the natural dispositions of all human races, constituting the higher unity among men, making them all equal before God.<sup id="cite_ref-Blowers2008_34-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blowers2008-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The accusations of racism have prompted the renaming of landmarks, schoolhouses, and other institutions which bear the name of Agassiz (which abound in Massachusetts).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Opinions on these events are often mixed, given his extensive scientific legacy in other areas.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In 2007 the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Council" title="Swiss Federal Council">Swiss government</a> acknowledged the "racist thinking" of Agassiz but declined to rename the Agassizhorn summit.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alpine_Club" title="Swiss Alpine Club">Swiss Alpine Club</a> declined to revoke Agassiz's status as a member of honor, which he received in 1856 for his scientific work, because the club considered this status to have lapsed on Agassiz's death.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <b>Harvard Art Museums</b> are part of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and comprise three museums: the Fogg Museum (established in 1895<sup id="cite_ref-History_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-1">[1]</a></sup>), the Busch-Reisinger Museum (established in 1903<sup id="cite_ref-History_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-1">[1]</a></sup>), and the Arthur M. Sackler Museum (established in 1985<sup id="cite_ref-History_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-1">[1]</a></sup>) and four research centers: the Archaeological Exploration of Sardis (founded in 1958<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>), the Center for the Technical Study of Modern Art (founded in 2002),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the Harvard Art Museums Archives, and the Straus Center for Conservation and Technical Studies (founded in 1928<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>). The three museums that constitute the Harvard Art Museums were initially integrated into a single institution under the name <b>Harvard University Art Museums</b> in 1983. The word "University" was dropped from the institutional name in 2008.</p>
<p>The collections include approximately 250,000 objects in all media, ranging in date from antiquity to the present and originating in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renovation_and_expansion">Renovation and expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Renovation and expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008, the Harvard Art Museums' historic building at 32 Quincy Street, Cambridge, was closed for a major renovation and expansion project. During the beginning phases of this project, the Arthur M. Sackler Museum at 485 Broadway, Cambridge, displayed selected works from the collections of the Fogg, Busch-Reisinger, and Sackler museums from September 13, 2008 through June 1, 2013.</p>
<p>The renovated building at 32 Quincy Street unites the three museums in a single state-of-the-art facility designed by architect <a href="/wiki/Renzo_Piano" title="Renzo Piano">Renzo Piano</a>, which increases gallery space by 40% and adds a glass, pyramidal roof.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In a view of the front facade, the glass roof and other expansions are mostly concealed, largely preserving the original appearance of the building.</p>
<p>The renovation adds six levels of galleries, classrooms, lecture halls, and new study areas providing access to parts of the 250,000-piece collection of the museums.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The new building was opened in November 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fogg_Museum">Fogg Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fogg Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The Fogg Museum, opened to the public in 1896, is the oldest and largest component of the Harvard Art Museums.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The museum was originally housed in an <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a>-style building designed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Morris_Hunt" title="Richard Morris Hunt">Richard Morris Hunt</a>. In 1925, the building was replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgian Revival">Georgian Revival</a>-style structure on Quincy Street, designed by <a href="/wiki/Shepley_Bulfinch" title="Shepley Bulfinch">Coolidge, Shepley, Bulfinch, and Abbott</a>. (The original Hunt Hall remained, underutilized until it was demolished in 1974 to make way for new freshman dormitories.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Fogg Museum is renowned for its holdings of Western paintings, sculpture, decorative arts, photographs, prints, and drawings from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> to the present. Particular strengths include <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a>, British <a href="/wiki/Pre-Raphaelite" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-Raphaelite">Pre-Raphaelite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/French_art_of_the_19th_century" class="mw-redirect" title="French art of the 19th century">French art of the 19th century</a>, as well as 19th- and 20th-century American paintings and drawings.</p>
<p>The museum's <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wertheim" title="Maurice Wertheim">Maurice Wertheim</a> Collection is a notable group of <a href="/wiki/Impressionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Impressionist">impressionist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Post-impressionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-impressionist">post-impressionist</a> works that contains many famous masterpieces, including paintings and sculptures by <a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul Cézanne">Paul Cézanne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Degas" title="Edgar Degas">Edgar Degas</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">Édouard Manet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Matisse" title="Henri Matisse">Henri Matisse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a>. Central to the Fogg's holdings is the Grenville L. Winthrop Collection, with more than 4,000 works of art. Bequeathed to Harvard in 1943, the collection continues to play a pivotal role in shaping the legacy of the Harvard Art Museums, serving as a foundation for teaching, research, and professional training programs. It includes important 19th-century paintings, sculpture, and drawings by <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burne-Jones" title="Edward Burne-Jones">Edward Burne-Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_David" title="Jacques-Louis David">Jacques-Louis David</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Daumier" title="Honoré Daumier">Honoré Daumier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winslow_Homer" title="Winslow Homer">Winslow Homer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Auguste_Dominique_Ingres" class="mw-redirect" title="Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres">Jean Auguste Dominique Ingres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Barye" title="Alfred Barye">Alfred Barye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Auguste_Renoir" title="Pierre-Auguste Renoir">Pierre-Auguste Renoir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Rodin" title="Auguste Rodin">Auguste Rodin</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Singer_Sargent" title="John Singer Sargent">John Singer Sargent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Toulouse-Lautrec" title="Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec">Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec</a>, and <a href="/wiki/James_Abbott_McNeill_Whistler" title="James Abbott McNeill Whistler">James Abbott McNeill Whistler</a>.</p>
<p>The art museum has Late Medieval Italian paintings by the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Offida" title="Master of Offida">Master of Offida</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Master of Camerino,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Daddi" title="Bernardo Daddi">Bernardo Daddi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simone_Martini" title="Simone Martini">Simone Martini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luca_di_Tomme" class="mw-redirect" title="Luca di Tomme">Luca di Tomme</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Lorenzetti" title="Pietro Lorenzetti">Pietro Lorenzetti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambrogio_Lorenzetti" title="Ambrogio Lorenzetti">Ambrogio Lorenzetti</a>, Master of Orcanesque Misercordia, Master of Saints Cosmas and Damiançand Bartolomeo Bulgarini.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dutch_and_Flemish_Renaissance_painting" title="Dutch and Flemish Renaissance painting">Flemish Renaissance paintings</a> — Master of Catholic Kings, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Provoost" title="Jan Provoost">Jan Provoost</a>, Master of Holy Blood, <a href="/wiki/Aelbert_Bouts" class="mw-redirect" title="Aelbert Bouts">Aelbert Bouts</a>, and Master of Saint Ursula.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_painting" title="Italian Renaissance painting">Italian Renaissance period paintings</a> — <a href="/wiki/Fra_Angelico" title="Fra Angelico">Fra Angelico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">Sandro Botticelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Ghirlandaio" title="Domenico Ghirlandaio">Domenico Ghirlandaio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gherardo_Starnina" title="Gherardo Starnina">Gherardo Starnina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cosme_Tura" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosme Tura">Cosme Tura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_di_Paolo" title="Giovanni di Paolo">Giovanni di Paolo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Lotto" title="Lorenzo Lotto">Lorenzo Lotto</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/17th-century_French_art" title="17th-century French art">French Baroque</a> <a href="/wiki/Baroque_painting" title="Baroque painting">period paintings</a> — <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Poussin" title="Nicolas Poussin">Nicolas Poussin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Stella" title="Jacques Stella">Jacques Stella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Regnier" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicolas Regnier">Nicolas Regnier</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Champaigne" title="Philippe de Champaigne">Philippe de Champaigne</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age_painting" title="Dutch Golden Age painting">Dutch Master paintings</a> — <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_de_Witte" title="Emanuel de Witte">Emanuel de Witte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Steen" title="Jan Steen">Jan Steen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willem_Van_de_Velde" class="mw-redirect" title="Willem Van de Velde">Willem Van de Velde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Van_Ruisdael" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacob Van Ruisdael">Jacob Van Ruisdael</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salomon_van_Ruysdael" title="Salomon van Ruysdael">Salomon van Ruysdael</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_der_Heyden" title="Jan van der Heyden">Jan van der Heyden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dirck_Hals" title="Dirck Hals">Dirck Hals</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/American_painting" class="mw-redirect" title="American painting">American paintings</a> — <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Stuart" title="Gilbert Stuart">Gilbert Stuart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Willson_Peale" title="Charles Willson Peale">Charles Willson Peale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Feke" title="Robert Feke">Robert Feke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanford_Gifford" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanford Gifford">Sanford Gifford</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_McNeil_Whistler" class="mw-redirect" title="James McNeil Whistler">James McNeil Whistler</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Singer_Sargent" title="John Singer Sargent">John Singer Sargent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Eakins" title="Thomas Eakins">Thomas Eakins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">Man Ray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Shahn" title="Ben Shahn">Ben Shahn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Lawrence" title="Jacob Lawrence">Jacob Lawrence</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lewis_Rubenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lewis Rubenstein (page does not exist)">Lewis Rubenstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Sloan" title="Robert Sloan">Robert Sloan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Guston" class="mw-redirect" title="Phillip Guston">Phillip Guston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Pollock" title="Jackson Pollock">Jackson Pollock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerry_James_Marshall" title="Kerry James Marshall">Kerry James Marshall</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clyfford_Still" title="Clyfford Still">Clyfford Still</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Busch-Reisinger_Museum">Busch-Reisinger Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Busch-Reisinger Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Founded in 1901 as the Germanic Museum, the Busch–Reisinger Museum is the only museum in North America dedicated to the study of art from the German-speaking countries of Central and Northern Europe in all media and in all periods.<sup id="cite_ref-HAMHistory_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAMHistory-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_James" title="William James">William James</a> spoke at its dedication.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Its holdings include significant works of Austrian <a href="/wiki/Secession_(art)" title="Secession (art)">Secession</a> art, <a href="/wiki/German_expressionism" class="mw-redirect" title="German expressionism">German expressionism</a>, 1920s abstraction, and material related to the <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> design school. Other strengths include late medieval sculpture and 18th-century art. The museum also holds noteworthy postwar and contemporary art from German-speaking Europe, including works by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Baselitz" title="Georg Baselitz">Georg Baselitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anselm_Kiefer" title="Anselm Kiefer">Anselm Kiefer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Richter" title="Gerhard Richter">Gerhard Richter</a>, and one of the world’s most comprehensive collections of works by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Beuys" title="Joseph Beuys">Joseph Beuys</a>.</p>
<p>The Busch-Reisinger Art Museum has oil paintings by artists <a href="/wiki/Lovis_Corinth" title="Lovis Corinth">Lovis Corinth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Liebermann" title="Max Liebermann">Max Liebermann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Klimt" title="Gustav Klimt">Gustav Klimt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Munch" title="Edvard Munch">Edvard Munch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paula_Modersohn-Becker" title="Paula Modersohn-Becker">Paula Modersohn-Becker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Ernst" title="Max Ernst">Max Ernst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner" title="Ernst Ludwig Kirchner">Ernst Ludwig Kirchner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franz_Marc" title="Franz Marc">Franz Marc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Schmidt-Rotluff" class="mw-redirect" title="Karl Schmidt-Rotluff">Karl Schmidt-Rotluff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emil_Nolde" title="Emil Nolde">Emil Nolde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erich_Heckel" title="Erich Heckel">Erich Heckel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Hoerle" title="Heinrich Hoerle">Heinrich Hoerle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Baselitz" title="Georg Baselitz">Georg Baselitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Moholy-Nagy" title="László Moholy-Nagy">László Moholy-Nagy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Max_Beckmann" title="Max Beckmann">Max Beckmann</a>. It has sculpture by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Barye" title="Alfred Barye">Alfred Barye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kathe_Kollwitz" class="mw-redirect" title="Kathe Kollwitz">Kathe Kollwitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Minne" title="George Minne">George Minne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Barlach" title="Ernst Barlach">Ernst Barlach</a>.</p>
<p>From 1921 to 1991, the Busch-Reisinger was located in <a href="/wiki/Adolphus_Busch_Hall" title="Adolphus Busch Hall">Adolphus Busch Hall</a> at 29 Kirkland Street. The Hall continues to house the Busch-Reisinger's founding collection of medieval <a href="/wiki/Plaster_casts" class="mw-redirect" title="Plaster casts">plaster casts</a> and an exhibition on the history of the Busch–Reisinger Museum; it also hosts concerts on its <a href="/wiki/Flentrop" title="Flentrop">Flentrop</a> pipe organ. In 1991, the Busch-Reisinger moved to the new Werner Otto Hall, designed by <a href="/wiki/Gwathmey_Siegel_%26_Associates" class="mw-redirect" title="Gwathmey Siegel &amp; Associates">Gwathmey Siegel &amp; Associates</a>, at 32 Quincy Street.<sup id="cite_ref-HAMHistory_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAMHistory-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthur_M._Sackler_Museum">Arthur M. Sackler Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Arthur M. Sackler Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sackler_Museum,_Harvard_University.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Sackler_Museum%2C_Harvard_University.jpg/220px-Sackler_Museum%2C_Harvard_University.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Sackler_Museum%2C_Harvard_University.jpg/330px-Sackler_Museum%2C_Harvard_University.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Sackler_Museum%2C_Harvard_University.jpg/440px-Sackler_Museum%2C_Harvard_University.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sackler_Museum,_Harvard_University.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Sackler Building is no longer used for public exhibition spaces</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Arthur M. Sackler Museum opened in 1985. The museum building, which was designed by British architect <a href="/wiki/James_Stirling_(architect)" title="James Stirling (architect)">James Stirling</a>, was named for the major donor, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_M._Sackler" title="Arthur M. Sackler">Arthur M. Sackler</a>, a psychiatrist, entrepreneur, and philanthropist.<sup id="cite_ref-glueck1985_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glueck1985-16">[16]</a></sup> The museum also housed offices for the History of Art and Architecture faculty, as well as the Digital Images and Slides Collection of the Fine Arts Library. As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the old Sackler Museum building houses the History of Art and Architecture Department and the Media Slide Library.<sup id="cite_ref-HAMHistory_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAMHistory-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection_2">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The museum collection holds important collections of Asian art, most notably, archaic Chinese jades (the widest collection outside of China) and Japanese surimono, as well as outstanding Chinese bronzes, ceremonial weapons, Buddhist cave-temple sculptures, ceramics from China and Korea, Japanese works on paper, and lacquer boxes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ancient Mediterranean and Byzantine collections comprise significant works in all media from Greece, Rome, Egypt, and the Near East. Strengths include Greek vases, small bronzes, and coins from throughout the ancient Mediterranean world.</p>
<p>The museum also holds works on paper from Islamic lands and India, including paintings, drawings, calligraphy, and manuscript illustrations, with particular strength in Rajput art, as well as important Islamic ceramics from the 8th through 19th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<blockquote>
<p>"James Stirling has dealt in a very high kind of order and organisation in the design of the [Sackler Museum]: this is a dense, tight plan on a small restricted site that brilliantly solves administrative and gallery needs. The building is remarkable for the creative virtuosity with which its functions are accommodated while suggesting a monumentality that belies actual dimensions. Stirling was lucky to have as a client the director of the Fogg [Museum], <a href="/wiki/Seymour_Slive" title="Seymour Slive">Seymour Slive</a>, who understood this achievement immediately. Professors John Coolidge and Neil Levine...complete a formidable triumvirate of sympathetic experts." <a href="/wiki/Ada_Louise_Huxtable" title="Ada Louise Huxtable">Ada Louise Huxtable</a>, "A Style Crystallised"<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Sackler Museum, originally designed as an extension to the Fogg, elicited worldwide attention from the time of Harvard's commission of Stirling to design the building, following a selection process that evaluated more than 70 architects.<sup id="cite_ref-stapen1985_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stapen1985-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> As a measure of the excitement generated by the project, the University mounted an exhibition of the architects' preliminary design drawings in 1981, <i>James Stirling's Design to Expand the Fogg Museum</i> and issued a portfolio of Stirling's drawings to the press.</p>
<p>After completion, the building's coverage was even greater,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> with general acknowledgment of the building's significance as a Stirling design and a Harvard undertaking. Aside from descriptions of the building's organization and exterior appearance, perhaps most noted was the way in which the inventive design accommodated its diverse program on a challenging site.<sup id="cite_ref-stapen1985_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stapen1985-19">[19]</a></sup> Harvard published a 50-page book on the Sackler, with extensive color photos by Timothy Hursley, an interview with Stirling by Michael Dennis, a tribute to Arthur M. Sackler, and essays by Slive, Coolidge and Rosenfield.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In spite of global critical acclaim, a few have been critical, Martin Peretz even proposing demolition, though his case was undermined by mis-attributing the building to another British architect, Norman Foster.<sup id="cite_ref-Peretz_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peretz-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the future use of the building was unclear,<sup id="cite_ref-MacGregor_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacGregor-23">[23]</a></sup> as its collection has been relocated to the Renzo Piano extension to the Fogg. Stirling's structure still stands as of December 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Art_Museums&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>The <b>Harvard School of Dental Medicine</b> (HSDM) is the <a href="/wiki/Dental_school" title="Dental school">dental school</a> of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. It is located in the <a href="/wiki/Longwood_Medical_and_Academic_Area" title="Longwood Medical and Academic Area">Longwood Medical Area</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, United States. In addition to the DMD degree, HSDM offers specialty training programs, advanced training programs, a Ph.D. program through the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Harvard Graduate School of Arts and Sciences">Harvard Graduate School of Arts and Sciences</a>, and Master of Medical Sciences &amp; Doctor of Medical Sciences degrees through <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a>. Being highly selective, admission into the pre-doctoral DMD program, as well as into the post-doctoral residency programs, is fiercely competitive. At its heart, the program considers dentistry a specialty of medicine. Therefore, all students at HSDM experience dual citizenship in both Harvard School of Dental Medicine and Harvard Medical School.</p>
<p>Today, HSDM is the smallest school at Harvard University. With a total student body of 280, including pre-doctoral and post-doctoral students spread over several disciplines, HSDM is an intimate community of students and faculty. Even when compared to other dental schools, HSDM is small, and its total living alumni number is approximately 2,500 worldwide and continues to have considerable influence on dental education and research within the broader oral health community. Many other HSDM alumni pursue careers as full-time faculty members, department heads, and leaders of organized dentistry.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_university-based_dental_school">First university-based dental school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First university-based dental school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 19th century a dentist was culturally understood as a <a href="/wiki/Tradesman" title="Tradesman">tradesman</a>, as opposed to a <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">professional</a> in the medical sense. Most dentists had either learned their trade through <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprenticeships</a> or simply offered their services to the public as self-proclaimed experts.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine,_Boston_MA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine%2C_Boston_MA.jpg/220px-Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine%2C_Boston_MA.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine%2C_Boston_MA.jpg/330px-Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine%2C_Boston_MA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine%2C_Boston_MA.jpg/440px-Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine%2C_Boston_MA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine,_Boston_MA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Harvard School of Dental Medicine</div>

<p>Even <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physicians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surgeon" title="Surgeon">surgeons</a> had once been <a href="/wiki/Tradesman" title="Tradesman">tradesmen</a>; for example, in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, many <a href="/wiki/Barber" title="Barber">barbers</a> were <a href="/wiki/Barber_surgeon" title="Barber surgeon">also surgeons by trade</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The advent of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> was a principal factor in the <a href="/wiki/Professionalization" title="Professionalization">professionalization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a>, because as scientific knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a> advanced, it became necessary to be educated academically in order to master the full body of knowledge in medicine; as medicine changed from an art to a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a> and art, it became something qualitatively different from the <a href="/wiki/Folk_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Folk medicine">folk medicine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">traditional medicine</a> that it had earlier been.</p>
<p>A similar evolution happened in dentistry, as dentists today are required to understand a large amount of <a href="/wiki/Oral_medicine" title="Oral medicine">oral medicine</a> to earn license to practice. The move toward more formal dental education and the <a href="/wiki/Professionalization" title="Professionalization">professionalization</a> of dentistry in the United States began when the state of Maryland chartered the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland_School_of_Dentistry" title="University of Maryland School of Dentistry">Baltimore College of Dental Surgery</a> in 1840. The establishment of this independent college, which may have occurred after the University of Maryland refused to add dental education to its curriculum, exemplified the nineteenth-century debate over whether dentistry should be part of scholarly education or should be taught in separate <a href="/wiki/Vocational_school" title="Vocational school">trade schools</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> As a result of this resistance, the various American dental schools that existed by 1866 were all freestanding (not university-affiliated). They included the aforementioned <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland_School_of_Dentistry" title="University of Maryland School of Dentistry">Baltimore College of Dental Surgery</a> (1840), the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_College_of_Dental_Surgery" title="Ohio College of Dental Surgery">Ohio College of Dental Surgery</a> (1845), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania_School_of_Dental_Medicine" title="University of Pennsylvania School of Dental Medicine">Philadelphia College of Dental Surgery</a> (1852), the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University_College_of_Dental_Medicine" title="Columbia University College of Dental Medicine">New York College of Dentistry</a> (1852), the <a href="/wiki/Maurice_H._Kornberg_School_of_Dentistry" title="Maurice H. Kornberg School of Dentistry">Philadelphia Dental College</a> (1863), and the <a href="/wiki/Washington_University_School_of_Dental_Medicine" title="Washington University School of Dental Medicine">Missouri Dental College</a> (1866).</p>
<p>The move towards university-based dental education institutions (as they exist today) began with the formation of Harvard Dental School in 1867. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Reidar_Fauske_Sognnaes" title="Reidar Fauske Sognnaes">Reidar Fauske Sognnaes</a>, noted oral pathologist and founding dean of the <a href="/wiki/UCLA_School_of_Dentistry" title="UCLA School of Dentistry">UCLA School of Dentistry</a>, commented on the significance of the school's formation in a 1977 <a href="/wiki/New_England_Journal_of_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="New England Journal of Medicine">New England Journal of Medicine</a> article:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>There was a time when the mouth, relatively speaking, was considered a scientific "no-man's land." That was when dental education fell between academic chairs--literally between the eyes, ears, nose and throat. In the United States dentistry was denied the academic status of other segments of higher education until 1867, when Harvard established the first dental school affiliated with a university-based faculty of medicine.</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_DMD_degree">Origins of DMD degree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins of DMD degree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harvard was the first dental school to award the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Dental_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctor of Dental Medicine">DMD</a> degree.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The establishment of the degree is detailed at <i><a href="/wiki/Dental_degree#DDS_vs_DMD_degree" title="Dental degree">Dental degree § DDS vs DMD degree</a></i>. There is no difference between the DMD and DDS degree; all dentists must meet the same national and regional certification standards.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaming_the_school">Renaming the school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Renaming the school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The school was established as Harvard Dental School in 1867, but renamed the Harvard School of Dental Medicine in 1940.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This symbolic change was made to emphasize the biological basis of oral medicine and the increasingly multidisciplinary focus of dental research.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_of_postdoctoral_educational_programs">Expansion of postdoctoral educational programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expansion of postdoctoral educational programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1957, Harvard School of Dental Medicine was awarded a training grant from the National Institute of Dental Research (now the National Institute of Dental and Craniofacial Research) to expand its postdoctoral training programs. These new programs included an <a href="/wiki/Oral_and_maxillofacial_surgery" title="Oral and maxillofacial surgery">oral and maxillofacial surgery</a>/<a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Medicine" title="Doctor of Medicine">MD</a>/<a href="/wiki/General_surgery" title="General surgery">general surgery</a> residency program; a Doctor of Medical Sciences (DMSc) degree; and 3- and 4-year joint-degree programs with an emphasis on combining clinical training with research into <a href="/wiki/Health_policy" title="Health policy">health policy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>, and/or biomedical sciences.</p>
<p>The school's current post-doctoral programs include both school-based and hospital based residencies. School-based programs award the Master of Medical Science (MMSc) degree, with an optional Doctor of Medical Sciences (DMSc) degree available for those spending extra time on research activities.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_changes_in_predoctoral_curriculum_1994">Major changes in predoctoral curriculum 1994</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Major changes in predoctoral curriculum 1994">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1994, a major change in the predoctoral curriculum included an increase of the predoctoral class size to 35, introduction of problem-based curriculum, and a switch from a 5-year program to a 4-year case based curriculum.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvard_Odontological_Society">Harvard Odontological Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Harvard Odontological Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Harvard Odontological Society was established in 1878 to promote education and good fellowship amongst graduates and faculty of the Harvard School of Dental Medicine. In continuous operation for over 130 years, the society meets four times a year in a Boston, Massachusetts venue to hold a regular business meeting and an educational presentation of interest to its members.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvard_Dental_Alumni_Association">Harvard Dental Alumni Association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_School_of_Dental_Medicine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Harvard Dental Alumni Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Harvard School of Dental Medicine has an active group of over 2500 alumni who continue to participate in the day-to-day events of the school and are major contributors to the school's vision and goals for the new millennium. In addition to publishing a quarterly bulletin, marking the current events of the school as well as the achievements of alumni, the Association also sponsors the annual Gold Medal and Silver Medal awards at graduation, honoring the <a href="/wiki/Valedictorian" title="Valedictorian">valedictorian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salutatorian" title="Salutatorian">salutatorian</a> of the graduating class, respectively.</p>

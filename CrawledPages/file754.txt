<p>The <b>Harvard Crimson men's ice hockey</b> team is a <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">National Collegiate Athletic Association</a> (NCAA) <a href="/wiki/Division_I_(NCAA)" class="mw-redirect" title="Division I (NCAA)">Division I</a> <a href="/wiki/College_ice_hockey" title="College ice hockey">college ice hockey</a> program that represents <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson" title="Harvard Crimson">Crimson</a> are a member of <a href="/wiki/ECAC_Hockey" title="ECAC Hockey">ECAC Hockey</a>. They play at the <a href="/wiki/Bright_Hockey_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Bright Hockey Center">Bright Hockey Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Crimson hockey team is one of the oldest college ice hockey teams in the United States, having played their first game on January 19, 1898, in a 0–6 loss to <a href="/wiki/Brown_Bears_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Brown Bears men's ice hockey">Brown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Crimson's archrival is the <a href="/wiki/Cornell_Big_Red_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Cornell Big Red men's ice hockey">Cornell Big Red</a>. The teams meet at least twice each season for installments of the historic <a href="/wiki/Cornell-Harvard_hockey_rivalry" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornell-Harvard hockey rivalry">Cornell-Harvard hockey rivalry</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson_men%27s_ice_hockey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Crimson hockey team was founded in 1898 making the team one of the oldest college ice hockey teams in the United States. The team played on a local pond and played their first recorded intercollegiate game against <a href="/wiki/Brown_Bears_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Brown Bears men's ice hockey">Brown</a> on January 19, 1898, at <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Park_(Boston)" title="Franklin Park (Boston)">Franklin Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> The rivalry is the oldest continuing college hockey series in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-ecacnotes1711_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ecacnotes1711-5">[5]</a></sup> The Crimson lost that game 0–6 but the Brown-Harvard rivalry continued and later become US college hockey's oldest rivalry.<sup id="cite_ref-ecacnotes1711_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ecacnotes1711-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two teams played again the following winter; Brown won that game by a score of 2–1. The Crimson would get their first recorded win in program history in 1900 with a 10–1 win over MIT.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard1900_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1900-6">[6]</a></sup> That same season Harvard beat Brown in back to back games. On February 26, 1900, Harvard played <a href="/wiki/Yale_Bulldogs_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Yale Bulldogs men's ice hockey">Yale</a> for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> The game was held at the <a href="/wiki/St._Nicholas_Rink" title="St. Nicholas Rink">St. Nicholas Rink</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="New York, New York">New York</a> and the 4–5 loss was the Crimson's only loss of the 1899–1900 season.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard1900_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1900-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1903 <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Winsor" title="Alfred Winsor">Alfred Winsor</a> became the team's first official head coach.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> The team previously used <a href="/wiki/Captain_(ice_hockey)" title="Captain (ice hockey)">captains</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Player-coach" title="Player-coach">player-coach</a> role, including Winsor who served as the Crimson's captain from 1901–1902.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> Under Winsor the team recorded a 22-game winning streak that spanned five and a half seasons and lasted from January 10, 1903, to Jan 19, 1907.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard1907_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1907-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>After beginning the program on a pond and playing on various outdoor rinks constructed on campus over the seasons, the university constructed two ice rinks inside <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Stadium" title="Harvard Stadium">Harvard Stadium</a> for the 1904 season and move some old bleachers to the side of the rink. The university also began charging admission for select important games for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> The Crimson later moved home games to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Arena" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Arena">Boston Arena</a>, the first indoor ice rink in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> and one of the oldest in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A few years after moving into the Boston Arena, on March 14, 1913, the Harvard University Athletic Committee voted to make ice hockey a major sport in the university's athletic department.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> Following the 1917 season Alfred Winsor stepped down as the head coach after compiling a 124–29 record in 15 seasons as Crimson head coach. The following season was suspended because of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> with the team resuming play in 1919.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="1919–1944"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1919.E2.80.931944">1919–1944</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson_men%27s_ice_hockey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1919–1944">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1919–1921 the team went back to playing at outdoor rinks after the Boston Arena caught fire in the fall of 1918. The Crimson returned to the arena after it was rebuilt in the fall of 1921.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> Following three seasons again with no official coach, <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Claflin,_Jr." title="William Henry Claflin, Jr.">William H. Claflin</a> became the second coach in program history in 1921<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> On March 7, 1923, William H. Claflin and captain George Owen '23 substituted entire forward lines instead of individuals, in the first recorded use of a <a href="/wiki/Line_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Line change">line change</a>. The idea proved successful as Harvard defeated Yale 2–1 in overtime.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 12, 1930, Harvard and Yale played the final game of a three-game series to end the 1930 season. The two teams split the first two games of the series.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> The game was called off at midnight due to <a href="/wiki/Blue_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue laws">blue laws</a> despite the teams being tied in the third overtime and a record crowd in excess of 14,000.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard2930_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard2930-11">[11]</a></sup> The resulting tie caused the two rivals to share the 1929–30 intercollegiate title.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Joseph Stubbs became the fourth head coach in program history starting with the 1927–28 season.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> He stepped down after the 1937–38 season with a record of 95–43–6 record in 11 seasons. Stubbs led the team to four seasons of double-digit wins, including two back-to-back 11-win seasons in 1930–31 and 1931–32, as well as a 12-win season in 1935–36,<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> and leading the Crimson to a 15–1 record in 1936–37.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Quadrangular League was created for the 1933–34 with Harvard, Yale, Princeton and Dartmouth, the league is considered the predecessor to the <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1936, the Council of Ivy Group Presidents agreed on the formal formation of the League, however the agreement did not go into effect until the 1955–56 season.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="1945–1960"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1945.E2.80.931960">1945–1960</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson_men%27s_ice_hockey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1945–1960">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The program was suspended for two years during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> but then returned to the ice for an abbreviated 1945–46 season.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1950 <a href="/wiki/Ralph_%22Cooney%22_Weiland" class="mw-redirect" title="Ralph &quot;Cooney&quot; Weiland">Ralph "Cooney" Weiland</a> became head coach, Weiland was a former <a href="/wiki/NHL" class="mw-redirect" title="NHL">NHL</a> scoring champion who won <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cup" title="Stanley Cup">Stanley Cups</a> as both a player and a coach with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">Boston Bruins</a>. With the hire, Weiland became the first non-alumnus to become head coach of the program.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Weiland guided Harvard to win the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Beanpot_(ice_hockey)" title="Beanpot (ice hockey)">Beanpot hockey tournament</a> on December 27, 1952, when the team defeated <a href="/wiki/Boston_University_Terriers" title="Boston University Terriers">Boston University</a> 7–4.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> A few seasons later Weiland's Crimson team competed in the <a href="/wiki/1955_NCAA_Division_I_Men%27s_Ice_Hockey_Tournament" title="1955 NCAA Division I Men's Ice Hockey Tournament">1955 NCAA Ice Hockey Tournament</a>. It was the first <a href="/wiki/Frozen_Four" class="mw-redirect" title="Frozen Four">Frozen Four</a> appearance by the university.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> The 1954–55 season was highlighted by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Cleary_(ice_hockey)" title="Bill Cleary (ice hockey)">William J. "Billy" Cleary</a> leading the nation in scoring with 89 points in 21 games, his point total still stands as the Harvard single-season record.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> Cleary and classmate Chuck Flynn become Harvard's first <a href="/wiki/All-American" class="mw-redirect" title="All-American">All-Americans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> The team returned to the tournament in <a href="/wiki/1957_NCAA_Division_I_Men%27s_Ice_Hockey_Tournament" title="1957 NCAA Division I Men's Ice Hockey Tournament">1957</a> and <a href="/wiki/1958_NCAA_Division_I_Men%27s_Ice_Hockey_Tournament" title="1958 NCAA Division I Men's Ice Hockey Tournament">1958</a>. All three tournament appearances under Weiland saw the Crimson exit in the semi-final round.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1956 Harvard moved into the 2,000-seat <a href="/wiki/Bright_Hockey_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Bright Hockey Center">Donald C. Watson Rink</a> bringing hockey back on campus from the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Arena" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Arena">Boston Arena</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-facility_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-facility-12">[12]</a></sup> The rink was located north of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Stadium" title="Harvard Stadium">Harvard Stadium</a>.</p>
<p>Harvard became a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/ECAC_Hockey_League" class="mw-redirect" title="ECAC Hockey League">ECAC Hockey League</a> in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> That same season, on January 4, 1962, Harvard defeated <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Huskies_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Northeastern Huskies men's ice hockey">Northeastern</a> 6–1 for the program's 500th win. In its second season in the ECAC, Harvard won both the league regular season championship and, with a 4–3 overtime win over Boston University, won the league championship.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard1963_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1963-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the conclusion of the 1970–71 season Weiland left the program after 21 years. He compiled a record of 316–172–17, six Ivy League championships, two ECAC championships, and five NCAA appearances.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> Weiland was named coach of the year by the <a href="/wiki/American_Hockey_Coaches_Association" title="American Hockey Coaches Association">American Hockey Coaches Association</a> in 1955 and 1971. In 1971 he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Hockey_Hall_of_Fame" title="Hockey Hall of Fame">Hockey Hall of Fame</a> and received the <a href="/wiki/Lester_Patrick_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Lester Patrick Award">Lester Patrick Award</a> for contribution to hockey in the United States in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="1971–1990"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1971.E2.80.931990">1971–1990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson_men%27s_ice_hockey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1971–1990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bill_Cleary_(ice_hockey)" title="Bill Cleary (ice hockey)">Bill Cleary</a>, former Crimson All-American, took over coaching duties for the 1971–72 season after serving as an assistant coach under Weiland.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> On March 7, 1975 – Harvard defeated <a href="/wiki/Cornell_Big_Red_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Cornell Big Red men's ice hockey">Cornell</a> 6–4 to win the 23rd game of the season,<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> the first 20-win season in program history.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> Unfortunately, the Crimson would lose in the finals the next day to Boston University and finish fourth in the NCAA Tournament with defeats to Minnesota and the same BU squad. The team was captained by All-American Randy Roth '75, who the previous year was named the Crimson's first ever ECAC Player of the Year.</p>
<p>By the mid-1970s the Donald C. Watson Rink was becoming outdated and the university made plans to construct a new hockey facility. A decision was eventually made to extensively upgrade the facility and in 1978 the walls were removed and the roof was extended before the new arena was installed at a cost exceeding $5-million.<sup id="cite_ref-facility_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-facility-12">[12]</a></sup> During the renovation the Harvard's men's and new <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson_women%27s_ice_hockey" title="Harvard Crimson women's ice hockey">women's</a> teams played out of various local rinks in 1978–79. Following the renovation the facility was named after former Harvard hockey player Alec Bright '19.<sup id="cite_ref-facility_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-facility-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bright_Hockey_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Bright Hockey Center">Bright Hockey Center</a> increased seating by more than 800 people The ice surface was extended by five feet to 204 feet by 87 feet under the direction of coach Cleary.<sup id="cite_ref-facility_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-facility-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cleary lead the Crimson to their third conference tournament championship by beating <a href="/wiki/Providence_Friars_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Providence Friars men's ice hockey">Providence</a> 4–1 in the ECAC Championship, sending the Crimson to the <a href="/wiki/1983_NCAA_Division_I_Men%27s_Ice_Hockey_Tournament" title="1983 NCAA Division I Men's Ice Hockey Tournament">1983 NCAA Tournament</a>. Harvard first defeated <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_Spartans_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Michigan State Spartans men's ice hockey">Michigan State</a> in the two-game quarter-final series by a combined score 9–8.<sup id="cite_ref-83ncaa_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83ncaa-16">[16]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-83ncaa_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83ncaa-16">[16]</a></sup> The Crimson advanced to the Frozen Four in <a href="/wiki/Grand_Forks,_North_Dakota" title="Grand Forks, North Dakota">Grand Forks, North Dakota</a> and defeated <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers men's ice hockey">Minnesota</a> 5–3 to send Harvard to the program's first NCAA Championship appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-83ncaa_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83ncaa-16">[16]</a></sup> The Crimson was defeated in the NCAA final 2–6 by <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Badgers_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Wisconsin Badgers men's ice hockey">Wisconsin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-83ncaa_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83ncaa-16">[16]</a></sup> Despite the loss in the final, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Fusco" title="Mark Fusco">Mark Fusco</a> became the school's first <a href="/wiki/Hobey_Baker_Award" title="Hobey Baker Award">Hobey Baker Award</a> winner.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> Three seasons later, his brother, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Fusco" title="Scott Fusco">Scott Fusco</a> became the second player and first ever pair of brothers to win the Award.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite a 2–3 overtime loss to <a href="/wiki/Vermont_Catamounts_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Vermont Catamounts men's ice hockey">Vermont</a> in the ECAC semifinals,<sup id="cite_ref-harvard1989_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1989-17">[17]</a></sup> the 1988–89 team received an at-large bid to the <a href="/wiki/1989_NCAA_Division_I_Men%27s_Ice_Hockey_Tournament" title="1989 NCAA Division I Men's Ice Hockey Tournament">1989 NCAA Tournament</a>, the team's fifth straight NCAA Tournament appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> Harvard swept <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior_State_Lakers_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Lake Superior State Lakers men's ice hockey">Lake Superior State</a> in the two game quarter-final round by a combined score of 9–4.<sup id="cite_ref-harvard1989_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1989-17">[17]</a></sup> The team advanced to the Frozen Four in <a href="/wiki/St._Paul,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul, Minnesota</a> and defeated Michigan State 6–3 before facing the hometown <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Golden_Gophers_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Minnesota Golden Gophers men's ice hockey">Minnesota Golden Gophers</a> in the championship.<sup id="cite_ref-89ncaa_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89ncaa-18">[18]</a></sup> Harvard won their first NCAA Championship on April 1, 1989 when senior forward Ed Krayer score in overtime to give the Crimson a 4–3 overtime victory.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvard1989_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvard1989-17">[17]</a></sup> Following the game, <a href="/wiki/Lane_MacDonald" title="Lane MacDonald">Lane MacDonald</a> became the third player in program history to earn the Hobey Baker Award.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson_men%27s_ice_hockey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Bill Cleary ended his tenure as head coach at the conclusion of the 1989–90 season to become the director of athletics.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> In his 19 seasons as head coach for the program he won 324 games and took the Crimson to the NCAA Tournament nine times, the Frozen Four on seven occasions, and the first National Championship for Harvard.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> The ECAC regular season championship, the <a href="/wiki/ECAC_Hockey#Men.27s_ECAC_championship_games" title="ECAC Hockey">Cleary Cup</a>, is named in honor of the former Harvard player, coach, and Olympian for his efforts to form the conference.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Longtime assistant Ronn Tomassoni was named head coach for the following season. In his first season as head coach in February 1, 1992, Tomassoni guided Harvard to the program's 1,000 win when it defeats Union, 7–3.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993 the team returned to the <a href="/wiki/1993_NCAA_Division_I_Men%27s_Ice_Hockey_Tournament" title="1993 NCAA Division I Men's Ice Hockey Tournament">NCAA Tournament</a> for the first time since their championship in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> The Crimson lost in double overtime to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Michigan_Wildcats_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="Northern Michigan Wildcats men's ice hockey">Northern Michigan</a> 2–3.<sup id="cite_ref-93ncaa_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93ncaa-20">[20]</a></sup> Harvard returned to the NCAA the following season in 1994 and advanced to the Frozen Four with a 7–1 win over <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_Wildcats_men%27s_ice_hockey" title="New Hampshire Wildcats men's ice hockey">New Hampshire</a>. In the NCAA semifinal the Crimson fell to Lake Superior State 3–2 in overtime.<sup id="cite_ref-94ncaa_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94ncaa-21">[21]</a></sup> finishing the year a record of 24–5–4.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup> The appearance in the 1994 Frozen Four was the last of the program to date.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mark Mazzoleni became the tenth coach in program history on July 16, 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_4-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> Under Mazzoleni the Crimson reached the NCAA Tournament three straight seasons in 2001–02, 2002–03, and 2003–04.<sup id="cite_ref-uschohist_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uschohist-9">[9]</a></sup> The streak was extended to five straight seasons, tying a program high, by <a href="/wiki/Ted_Donato" title="Ted Donato">Ted Donato</a> in 2004–05 and 2005–06.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coaches">Coaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Crimson_men%27s_ice_hockey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Coaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harvard's men's team has been in continual operation since 1897 with two notable exceptions. The university did not field a team for the 1917-18 season nor was there a team from 1943 through the spring of 1945. These three seasons were lost as a result of the two <a href="/wiki/World_war" title="World war">world wars</a> that occurred during the first half of the <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a>. For two periods early in the team's history the team was coached by their captains; those years have been included here for continuity.</p>

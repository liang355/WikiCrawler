<p><b>Bremerhaven</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˌbʁeːmɐˈhaːfn̩]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Bremerhaven.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/be/De-Bremerhaven.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Bremerhaven.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>, literally "<a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a>'s harbour", <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a>: <i>Bremerhoben</i>) is a city at the <a href="/wiki/Port_city" class="mw-redirect" title="Port city">seaport</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bremen_(state)" title="Bremen (state)">Free Hanseatic City of Bremen</a>, a <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a>.</p>
<p>It forms an <a href="/wiki/Enclave" class="mw-redirect" title="Enclave">enclave</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a> and is located at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Weser_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Weser River">River Weser</a> on its eastern bank, opposite the town of <a href="/wiki/Nordenham" title="Nordenham">Nordenham</a>. Though a relatively new city, it has a long history as a <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a> and today is one of the most important German ports, playing a crucial role in Germany's trade.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Denkmal_Johann_Smidt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Denkmal_Johann_Smidt.jpg/170px-Denkmal_Johann_Smidt.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Denkmal_Johann_Smidt.jpg/255px-Denkmal_Johann_Smidt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Denkmal_Johann_Smidt.jpg/340px-Denkmal_Johann_Smidt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1712" data-file-height="2288" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Denkmal_Johann_Smidt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
City founder <a href="/wiki/Johann_Smidt" title="Johann Smidt">Johann Smidt</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><span style="white-space: nowrap">The town was founded</span> in 1827, but settlements, such as Lehe, were in the vicinity as early as the 12th century, and Geestendorf, which was "mentioned in documents of the ninth century".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These tiny villages were built on small islands in the swampy estuary. In 1381, the city of <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a> established <i>de facto</i> rule over the lower <a href="/wiki/Weser" title="Weser">Weser</a> stream, including Lehe, later therefore called Bremerlehe. Early in 1653, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Bremen-Verden" title="Bremen-Verden">Bremen-Verden</a>'s troops captured Bremerlehe by force.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Ferdinand_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Ferdinand III">Emperor Ferdinand III</a> ordered his vassal <a href="/wiki/Christina_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Christina of Sweden">Christina of Sweden</a>, then Duchess regnant of Bremen-Verden, to restitute Bremerlehe to Bremen. However, Swedish Bremen-Verden soon enacted the <a href="/wiki/First_Bremian_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First Bremian War">First Bremian War</a> (March to July 1654) and in the following peace treaty (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Recess_of_Stade_(1654)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Recess of Stade (1654) (page does not exist)">First Stade Recess</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erster_Stader_Vergleich" class="extiw" title="de:Erster Stader Vergleich">de</a>)</span>; November 1654) Bremen had to cede Bremerlehe and its surroundings to Swedish Bremen-Verden. The latter developed plans to found a fortified town on the site, and much later this location became the present-day city of Bremerhaven. In 1672, under the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_XI_of_Sweden" title="Charles XI of Sweden">Charles XI of Sweden</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a> Duke of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Duchy_of_Bremen-Verden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Duchy of Bremen-Verden (page does not exist)">Bremen-Verden</a>—colonists tried unsuccessfully to erect a castle (named <a href="/wiki/Carlsburg,_Weser" title="Carlsburg, Weser">Carlsburg</a> after Charles XI) there; this fortified structure was meant to protect, as well as control shipping heading for Bremen.</p>
<p>Finally, in 1827, the city of Bremen under <a href="/wiki/Burgomaster" title="Burgomaster">Burgomaster</a> <a href="/wiki/Johann_Smidt" title="Johann Smidt">Johann Smidt</a> bought the territories at the mouth of the Weser from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Kingdom of Hanover</a>. Bremen sought this territory to retain its share of Germany's overseas trade, which was threatened by the silting up of the Weser around the old inland port of Bremen. Bremerhaven (literally in English: Bremener Haven/Harbour) was founded to be a haven for Bremen's merchant marine, becoming the second harbour for Bremen, despite being 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) downstream. Due to trade with, and emigration to North America, the port and the town grew quickly. In 1848, Bremerhaven became the home port of the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Reichsflotte" title="Reichsflotte">Navy</a> under <a href="/wiki/Karl_Rudolf_Brommy" title="Karl Rudolf Brommy">Karl Rudolf Brommy</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Kingdom of Hanover</a> founded a rival town next to Bremerhaven and called it <b>Geestemünde</b> (1845). Both towns grew and established the three economic pillars of trade, shipbuilding and fishing. Following inter-state negotiations at different times, Bremerhaven's boundary was several times extended at the expense of Hanoveran territory. In 1924, Geestemünde and the neighbouring municipality of Lehe were united to become the new city of <b>Wesermünde</b>, and in 1939 Bremerhaven (apart from the overseas port) was removed from the jurisdiction of Bremen and made a part of Wesermünde, then a part of the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hanover" title="Province of Hanover">Province of Hanover</a>.</p>
<p>Bremerhaven was one of the important harbours of emigration in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>As possibly the most critical North Sea base of the Nazi War Navy, the <a href="/wiki/Kriegsmarine" title="Kriegsmarine">Kriegsmarine</a>, most of the city was destroyed in the allied air <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Bremen_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Bremen in World War II">bombing of Bremen in World War II</a>; however, key parts of the port were deliberately spared<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> by the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a> to provide a usable harbour for supplying the Allies after the war. All of Wesermünde, including those parts which did not previously belong to Bremerhaven, was a postwar enclave run by the United States within the British zone of northern Germany. Most of the US military units and their personnel were assigned to the city's Carl Schurz Kaserne. One of the longest US units based at the Kaserne was a US military radio and TV station, an "Amerikanischer Soldatensender", <a href="/wiki/AFN_Bremerhaven" title="AFN Bremerhaven">AFN Bremerhaven</a>, which broadcast for 48 years. In 1993, the Kaserne was vacated by the US military and returned to the German government. In 1947 the city became part of the federal state <a href="/wiki/Bremen_(state)" title="Bremen (state)">Free Hanseatic City of Bremen</a> and was consequently renamed from Wesermünde to Bremerhaven. Today, Bremerhaven is therefore part of the <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a> of Bremen, being to all intents and purposes a state comprising two cities, while also a city in its own right. This is complicated somewhat by the fact that the city of Bremen has owned the "overseas port" within Bremerhaven since 1927. To further complicate matters, a treaty between the two cities (as mentioned in Section 8 of Bremerhaven's municipal constitution) makes Bremerhaven responsible for the municipal administration of those parts owned directly by Bremen (known as <i>stadtbremisch</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade">Trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The port of Bremerhaven is the sixteenth-largest <a href="/wiki/Containerization" title="Containerization">container</a> port in the world and the fourth-largest in Europe with 4.9 million&#160;<a href="/wiki/Twenty-foot_equivalent_unit" title="Twenty-foot equivalent unit">twenty-foot equivalent units</a>&#160;(TEU) of cargo handled in 2007<sup id="cite_ref-dcn_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dcn-5">[5]</a></sup> and 5,5 million in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The container terminal is situated on the bank of the river Weser opening to the north-sea. In the wet dock parts, accessible by two large locks, more than 2 million cars are imported or exported every year with 2,3 million in 2014. Bremerhaven imports and exports more cars than any other city in Europe. Another million tons of "High-and-Heavy" goods are handled with <a href="/wiki/Roll-on/roll-off" title="Roll-on/roll-off">ro-ro</a> ships. In 2011 a new <a href="/wiki/Panamax" title="Panamax">panamax</a>-sized lock has replaced the 1897 <i>Kaiserschleuse</i>, then the largest lock worldwide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bremerhaven has a temperate <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">maritime climate</a>; severe frost and heat waves with temperatures above 30&#160;°C (86&#160;°F) are rare. On average, the city receives about 742&#160;mm (29.2&#160;in) of precipitation distributed throughout the year, with a slight peak in the summer months between June and August. The hottest temperature ever recorded was 35.8&#160;°C (96.4&#160;°F) on 9 August 1992, and the coldest was −18.6&#160;°C (−1.5&#160;°F) on 25 February 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its unique geographic situation, Bremerhaven suffers from a few transportational difficulties. The city has been connected to the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">autobahn</a> network since the late 1970s. The <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_27" title="Bundesautobahn 27">A 27</a> runs north-south, east of the city, connecting Bremerhaven to Bremen and <a href="/wiki/Cuxhaven" title="Cuxhaven">Cuxhaven</a>. Road connections to <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, however, are poor. The <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_71" title="Bundesstraße 71">Bundesstraße 71</a> and secondary roads therefore carry most of the heavy <a href="/wiki/Lorry" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorry">lorry</a> traffic. A proposed solution is the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_22" title="Bundesautobahn 22">A 22</a>, the so-called <i>Küstenautobahn</i> (or "coastal motorway"), which would link Bremerhaven to Hamburg and <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmshaven" title="Wilhelmshaven">Wilhelmshaven</a>/<a href="/wiki/Oldenburg" title="Oldenburg">Oldenburg</a> (using the <a href="/wiki/Weser_tunnel" class="mw-redirect" title="Weser tunnel">Weser tunnel</a>). Roads leading to the overseas port are regularly overloaded with freight traffic, and solutions are presently being discussed, including a deep-cut road favoured by the city government and various interest groups.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Railway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bremerhaven has three active passenger rail stations, <a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bremerhaven Hauptbahnhof">Bremerhaven Hauptbahnhof</a> in the city centre, <a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven-Lehe_railway_station" title="Bremerhaven-Lehe railway station">Bremerhaven-Lehe</a> north of the centre and <a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven-Wulsdorf_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Bremerhaven-Wulsdorf railway station">Bremerhaven-Wulsdorf</a> in the southern part of the city. A fourth station, <a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven-Speckenb%C3%BCttel_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Bremerhaven-Speckenbüttel railway station">Bremerhaven-Speckenbüttel</a> near the border to <a href="/wiki/Langen,_Lower_Saxony" class="mw-redirect" title="Langen, Lower Saxony">Langen</a> has been out of service since 1988, though it might reopen when the <a href="/wiki/Bremen_S-Bahn" title="Bremen S-Bahn">Bremen S-Bahn</a> scheme becomes operational. Bremerhaven's central station lost its last long-distance train in 2001. Now only regional connections to <a href="/wiki/Bremen_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bremen (city)">Bremen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuxhaven" title="Cuxhaven">Cuxhaven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="Osnabrück">Osnabrück</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hannover" class="mw-redirect" title="Hannover">Hannover</a> are available. The railways in Bremerhaven, however, still carry a heavy load of freight traffic, mostly new <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">cars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Container_(cargo)" class="mw-redirect" title="Container (cargo)">containers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>.</p>

<p>Bremerhaven has only a few historical buildings, and the <a href="/wiki/High_street" class="mw-redirect" title="High street">high street</a> and city centre are almost entirely post-war. The main attractions for tourists are found at the <i>Havenwelten</i> and include a attraction about climate change, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Klimahaus_Bremerhaven_8%C2%B0_Ost&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Klimahaus Bremerhaven 8° Ost (page does not exist)">Klimahaus Bremerhaven 8° Ost</a>, the <a href="/wiki/German_Emigration_Center" title="German Emigration Center">German Emigration Center</a> (since August 8, 2005) and the <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Schiffahrtsmuseum" class="mw-redirect" title="Deutsches Schiffahrtsmuseum">German Maritime Museum</a> (<i>Deutsches Schiffahrtsmuseum</i>) from 1975, featuring the <a href="/wiki/Bremen_cog" title="Bremen cog">Hansekogge</a>, a vintage <a href="/wiki/Cog_(ship)" title="Cog (ship)">cog</a> dating from 1380, excavated in Bremen in 1962, and the historical harbour (<i>Museumshafen</i>) with a number of <a href="/wiki/Museum_ship" title="Museum ship">museum ships</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Type_XXI_U-boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Type XXI U-boat">Type XXI U-boat</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Bauer_(U-boat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelm Bauer (U-boat)">Wilhelm Bauer</a></i> (a museum of its own), the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seute_Deern&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Seute Deern (page does not exist)">Seute Deern</a></i> (a wooden three-masted sailing vessel), and the salvage tug <i>Seefalke</i> from 1924. The <i><a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven_Zoo" title="Bremerhaven Zoo">Bremerhaven Zoo</a></i> reopened on 27 March 2004, after a lengthy renovation. It features <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> wildlife, both terrestrial and marine. The latest addition is the <i>Klimahaus</i> from 2009, simulating travel adventure along the 8th line of <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> and dealing with climate issues. Two gazebos can be found on top of the <i>Atlantic Hotel Sail City</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven_Radar_Tower" title="Bremerhaven Radar Tower">Radar Tower</a>. Another tourist spot is the <i>Fischereihafen</i> (fishing port) in Geestemünde which also houses an aquarium (the Atlanticum). The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lloyd-Werft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lloyd-Werft (page does not exist)">Lloyd-Werft</a> <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipyard</a> is renowned for building and renovating large cruise liners, for example <a href="/wiki/SS_France_(1961)" title="SS France (1961)"><i>Norway</i></a>.</p>
<p>Every five years <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sail_Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sail Bremerhaven (page does not exist)">Sail Bremerhaven</a> is held, a large sailing convention that attracts <a href="/wiki/Tall_ship" title="Tall ship">tall ships</a> from all over the world. The last time it was held was in 2015 with over 270 vessels and 3,500 crew members.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2011 Bremerhaven set the record for the largest ever parade of boats, with 327 vessels in the parade. This record was broken in 2012 by the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Diamond_Jubilee_Pageant" title="Thames Diamond Jubilee Pageant">Thames Diamond Jubilee Pageant</a>, with 1,000 boats.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The passenger terminal <i>Columbuskaje</i>, built at the Weser bank in 1927 to avoid time-absorbing locking, has been transferred into a cruise terminal (Columbus Cruise Center Bremerhaven/CCCB). Also three <a href="/wiki/Marina" title="Marina">marinas</a> are available, the latest accessible through a new lock at <i>Neuer Hafen</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bremerhaven has a city council with 49 members.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fischtown_Pinguins" title="Fischtown Pinguins">Fischtown Pinguins</a>, also known as REV Bremerhaven, are a professional ice hockey team in the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Eishockey_Liga" title="Deutsche Eishockey Liga">DEL</a>, Germany's top ice hockey league.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eisb%C3%A4ren_Bremerhaven" title="Eisbären Bremerhaven">Eisbären Bremerhaven</a> (Polar Bears), founded 2001, is a basketball team playing in the 1st division of the <a href="/wiki/Basketball_Bundesliga" title="Basketball Bundesliga">Basketball Bundesliga</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_Education">Research and Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremerhaven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Research and Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bremerhaven is home to the <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener_Institute_for_Polar_and_Marine_Research" title="Alfred Wegener Institute for Polar and Marine Research">Alfred Wegener Institute</a>, a national research institute which is concerned with maritime sciences and climate and keeps a number of <a href="/wiki/Research_vessel" title="Research vessel">research vessels</a>, amongst them the heavy research <a href="/wiki/Icebreaker" title="Icebreaker">icebreaker</a> <a href="/wiki/RV_Polarstern" title="RV Polarstern">RV Polarstern</a>. It also runs the <a href="/wiki/Neumayer-Station_III" title="Neumayer-Station III">Neumayer-Station III</a> in the Antarctic.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a> <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraunhofer-Institut_f%C3%BCr_Windenergie_und_Energiesystemtechnik" class="extiw" title="de:Fraunhofer-Institut für Windenergie und Energiesystemtechnik">Institute for Wind Energy and Energy System Technology (German Wikipedia)</a> maintains research laboratories in Bremerhaven for development and testing of Wind Power components.<sup id="cite_ref-IWES_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWES-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/German_Maritime_Museum" title="German Maritime Museum">German Maritime Museum</a> is part of the German <a href="/wiki/Leibniz_Association" title="Leibniz Association">Leibniz Association</a>.</p>
<p>The <i>Bremerhaven University of Applied Sciences</i>/<i>Hochschule Bremerhaven</i> (The word <i>Hochschule</i> literally means a high school) was founded in 1975 and is expanding since with more than 3.000 students in 2009. The university is attended by a large number of overseas students from all over the world. Among the courses offered are Process Engineering and Information Technology.</p>

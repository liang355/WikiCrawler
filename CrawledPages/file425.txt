<p><b>Koblenz</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Koblenz</span> <small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ÀàkoÀêbl…õntÕ°s]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Koblenz.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/De-Koblenz.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Koblenz.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>), also spelled <b>Coblenz</b> (<a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and pre-1926 <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Spelling" title="Spelling">spelling</a>) or <b>Coblence</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Coblence</i></span>), is a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> city situated on both banks of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> at its confluence with the <a href="/wiki/Moselle_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Moselle (river)">Moselle</a>, where the <i><a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Eck" title="Deutsches Eck">Deutsches Eck</a></i> (German Corner) and its <a href="/wiki/Monument" title="Monument">monument</a> (<a href="/wiki/William_I,_German_Emperor" title="William I, German Emperor">Emperor William I</a> on horseback) are situated.</p>
<p>As Koblenz was one of the military posts established by <a href="/wiki/Nero_Claudius_Drusus" title="Nero Claudius Drusus">Drusus</a> about 8 BC, the city celebrated its 2000th anniversary in 1992.</p>
<p>The name Koblenz originates from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>(ad) confluentes</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> or "(at the) merging of <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>". Subsequently, it was Covelenz and Cobelenz. In the local dialect the name is Kowelenz.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen_am_Rhein" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwigshafen am Rhein">Ludwigshafen am Rhein</a>, it is the third largest city in <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>, with a population of c. 106,000 (2006). Koblenz lies in the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_era">Ancient era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Koblenz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Around 1000 BC, early fortifications were erected on the <a href="/wiki/Festung_Ehrenbreitstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Festung Ehrenbreitstein">Festung Ehrenbreitstein</a> hill on the opposite side of the Moselle. In 55 BC, Roman troops commanded by <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> reached the Rhine and built a <a href="/wiki/Caesar%27s_Rhine_bridges" title="Caesar's Rhine bridges">bridge</a> between Koblenz and <a href="/wiki/Andernach" title="Andernach">Andernach</a>. About 9 BC, the <i>"Castellum apud Confluentes"</i>, was one of the military posts established by <a href="/wiki/Nero_Claudius_Drusus" title="Nero Claudius Drusus">Drusus</a>.</p>
<p>Remains of a large bridge built in 49 AD by the Romans are still visible. The Romans built two castles as protection for the bridge, one in 9 AD and another in the 2nd century, the latter being destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> in 259. North of Koblenz was a temple of Mercury and <a href="/wiki/Rosmerta" title="Rosmerta">Rosmerta</a> (a Gallo-Roman deity), which remained in use up to the 5th century.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Koblenz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>, the city was conquered by the Franks and became a royal seat. After the division of <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>'s empire, it was included in the lands of his son <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">Louis the Pious</a> (814). In 837, it was assigned to <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a>, and a few years later it was here that Carolingian heirs discussed what was to become the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a> (843), by which the city became part of <a href="/wiki/Lotharingia" title="Lotharingia">Lotharingia</a> under <a href="/wiki/Lothair_I" title="Lothair I">Lothair I</a>. In 860 and 922, Koblenz was the scene of ecclesiastical synods. At the first synod, held in the Liebfrauenkirche, the reconciliation of <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_German" title="Louis the German">Louis the German</a> with his half-brother Charles the Bald took place. The city was sacked and destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norsemen</a> in 882. In 925, it became part of the eastern German Kingdom, later the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Koblenz_castle_and_building.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Koblenz_castle_and_building.jpg/220px-Koblenz_castle_and_building.jpg" width="220" height="326" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Koblenz_castle_and_building.jpg/330px-Koblenz_castle_and_building.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Koblenz_castle_and_building.jpg/440px-Koblenz_castle_and_building.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1228" data-file-height="1818" /></a>

<p>In 1018, the city was given by the emperor <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry II, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry II</a> to the archbishop-elector of Trier after receiving a charter. It remained in the possession of his successors until the end of the 18th century, having been their main residence since the 17th century. Emperor <a href="/wiki/Conrad_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Conrad II, Holy Roman Emperor">Conrad II</a> was elected here in 1138. In 1198, the battle between <a href="/wiki/Philip_of_Swabia" title="Philip of Swabia">Philip of Swabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Otto_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto IV</a> took place nearby. In 1216, prince-bishop <a href="/wiki/Theoderich_von_Wied" title="Theoderich von Wied">Theoderich von Wied</a> donated part of the lands of the basilica and the hospital to the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a>, which later became the <i>Deutsches Eck</i>.</p>
<p>In 1249‚Äì1254, Koblenz was given new walls by Archbishop <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arnold_II_of_Isenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arnold II of Isenburg (page does not exist)">Arnold II of Isenburg</a>; and it was partly to overawe the turbulent citizens that successive archbishops built and strengthened the fortress of Ehrenbreitstein that still dominates the city.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Koblenz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city was a member of the league of the Rhenish cities which rose in the 13th century. The <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick_of_Koblenz" class="mw-redirect" title="Bailiwick of Koblenz">Bailiwick of Koblenz</a> in or around 1231. Koblenz attained great prosperity and it continued to advance until the disaster of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> brought about a rapid decline. After <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Christoph_von_S%C3%B6tern" title="Philipp Christoph von S√∂tern">Philip Christopher, elector of Trier</a>, surrendered Ehrenbreitstein to the French, the city received an imperial garrison in 1632. However, this force was soon expelled by the Swedes, who in their turn handed the city over again to the French. Imperial forces finally succeeded in retaking it by storm in 1636.</p>
<p>In 1688, Koblenz was besieged by the French under <a href="/wiki/Louis_Fran%C3%A7ois_Boufflers" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis Fran√ßois Boufflers">Marshal de Boufflers</a>, but they only succeeded in bombing the Old City (<i>Altstadt</i>) into ruins, destroying among other buildings the Old Merchants' Hall (<i>Kaufhaus</i>), which was restored in its present form in 1725. The city was the residence of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Trier" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Trier">archbishop-electors of Trier</a> from 1690 to 1801.</p>

<p>In 1786, the last archbishop-elector of Trier, <a href="/wiki/Clemens_Wenceslaus_of_Saxony" title="Clemens Wenceslaus of Saxony">Clemens Wenceslaus of Saxony</a>, greatly assisted the extension and improvement of the city, turning the <a href="/wiki/Ehrenbreitstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Ehrenbreitstein">Ehrenbreitstein</a> into a magnificent baroque palace. After the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Storming_of_the_Bastille" title="Storming of the Bastille">Bastille</a> in 1789, the city became, through the invitation of the archbishop-elector's chief minister, Ferdinand Freiherr von Duminique, one of the principal rendezvous points for French <a href="/wiki/%C3%89migr%C3%A9s" class="mw-redirect" title="√âmigr√©s">√©migr√©s</a>. The archbishop-elector approved of this because he was the uncle of the persecuted king of France, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI_of_France" title="Louis XVI of France">Louis XVI</a>. Among the many royalist French refugees who flooded into the city were <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XVI">Louis XVI</a>'s two younger brothers, the <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XVIII">Comte de Provence</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Charles_X_of_France" title="Charles X of France">Comte d'Artois</a>. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XVI">Louis XVI</a>'s cousin, Prince <a href="/wiki/Louis_Joseph_de_Bourbon,_prince_de_Cond%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis Joseph de Bourbon, prince de Cond√©">Louis Joseph de Bourbon, prince de Cond√©</a>, arrived and formed an army of young aristocrats willing to fight the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> and restore the <a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien R√©gime">Ancien R√©gime</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Army_of_Cond%C3%A9" title="Army of Cond√©">Army of Cond√©</a></i> joined with an allied army of Prussian and Austrian soldiers led by Duke <a href="/wiki/Charles_William_Ferdinand,_Duke_of_Brunswick" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles William Ferdinand, Duke of Brunswick">Karl Wilhelm Ferdinand</a> of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brunswick" title="Duchy of Brunswick">Brunswick</a> in an unsuccessful invasion of France in 1792. This drew down the wrath of the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="First French Republic">First French Republic</a> on the archbishop-elector; in 1794, Coblenz was taken by the French Revolutionary army under <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_S%C3%A9verin_Marceau-Desgraviers" title="Fran√ßois S√©verin Marceau-Desgraviers">Marceau</a> (who was killed during the siege), and, after the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lun%C3%A9ville" title="Treaty of Lun√©ville">Treaty of Lun√©ville</a> (1801) it was made the capital of the new French <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9partment" class="mw-redirect" title="D√©partment">d√©partment</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhin-et-Moselle" title="Rhin-et-Moselle">Rhin-et-Moselle</a>. In 1814, it was occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russians</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> assigned the city to <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, and in 1822, it was made the seat of government for the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Province" title="Rhine Province">Rhine Province</a>.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, France <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Rhineland" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupation of the Rhineland">occupied</a> the area once again. In defiance of the French, the German populace of the city has insisted on using the more German spelling of <b>Koblenz</b> since 1926. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> it was the location of the command of German <a href="/wiki/Army_Group_B" title="Army Group B">Army Group B</a> and like many other German cities, it was heavily bombed and rebuilt afterwards. Between 1947 and 1950, it served as the <a href="/wiki/Seat_of_government#Seat_of_government" title="Seat of government">seat of government</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Gorge" title="Rhine Gorge">Rhine Gorge</a> was declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in 2002, with Koblenz marking the northern end.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Koblenz_Fortress" title="Koblenz Fortress">Koblenz Fortress</a></div>
<p>Its defensive works are extensive, and consist of strong forts crowning the hills encircling the city to the west, and the citadel of <a href="/wiki/Ehrenbreitstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Ehrenbreitstein">Ehrenbreitstein</a> on the opposite bank of the Rhine. The old city was triangular in shape, two sides being bounded by the Rhine and Mosel and the third by a line of fortifications. The latter were razed in 1890, and the city was permitted to expand in this direction. The <a href="/wiki/Koblenz_Hauptbahnhof" title="Koblenz Hauptbahnhof">Koblenz Hauptbahnhof</a> (central station) was built on a spacious site outside the former walls at the junction of the <a href="/wiki/Linke_Rheinstrecke" class="mw-redirect" title="Linke Rheinstrecke">Cologne-Mainz railway</a> and the strategic <a href="/wiki/Cannons_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Cannons Railway">Metz-Berlin line</a>. In April 2011 <a href="/wiki/Koblenz-Stadtmitte_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Koblenz-Stadtmitte station">Koblenz-Stadtmitte station</a> was opened in the inner city to coincide with the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Garden_Show_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Garden Show 2011">Federal Garden Show 2011</a>. The Rhine is crossed by the <a href="/wiki/Pfaffendorf_Bridge" title="Pfaffendorf Bridge">Pfaffendorf Bridge</a>, originally the location of a rail bridge, but now a road bridge and, a mile south of city, by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Horchheim_Railway_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Horchheim Railway Bridge (page does not exist)">Horchheim Railway Bridge</a>, consisting of two wide and lofty spans carrying the <a href="/wiki/Lahn_Valley_Railway" title="Lahn Valley Railway">Lahn Valley Railway</a>, part of the Berlin railway referred to above. The Moselle is spanned by a Gothic freestone bridge of 14 arches, erected in 1344, two modern road bridges and also by two railway bridges.</p>
<p>Since 1890, the city has consisted of the Altstadt (old city) and the Neustadt (new city) or Klemenstadt. Of these, the Altstadt is closely built and has only a few fine streets and squares, while the Neustadt possesses numerous broad streets and a handsome frontage along the Rhine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sights">Other sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Koblenz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the more ancient part of Koblenz stand several buildings which have a historical interest. Prominent among these, near the point of confluence of the rivers, is the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_St._Castor" title="Basilica of St. Castor">Basilica of St. Castor</a> or <i>Kastorkirche</i>, dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Castor_of_Karden" title="Castor of Karden">Castor of Karden</a>, with four towers. The church was founded in 836 by <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">Louis the Pious</a>, but the present <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> building was completed in 1208, the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> vaulted roof dating from 1498. In front of the church of Saint Castor stands a fountain, erected by the French in 1812, with an inscription to commemorate Napoleon's invasion of Russia. Not long after, Russian troops occupied Koblenz; and St. Priest, their commander, added in irony these words: "Vu et approuv√© par nous, Commandant russe de la Ville de Coblence: Janvier 1er, 1814."</p>
<p>In this quarter of the city, too, is the <i>Liebfrauenkirche</i>, a fine church (nave 1250, choir 1404‚Äì1431) with lofty late Romanesque towers; the castle of the electors of <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a>, erected in 1280, which now contains the municipal picture gallery; and the family house of the Metternichs, where <a href="/wiki/Klemens_Wenzel_von_Metternich" class="mw-redirect" title="Klemens Wenzel von Metternich">Prince Metternich</a>, the Austrian statesman, was born in 1773. Also notable is the church of St. Florian, with a two towers fa√ßade from c. 1110.</p>
<p>The former Jesuit College is a Baroque edifice by J.C. Sebastiani (1694‚Äì1698) serves as the current City Hall.</p>
<p>Near Koblenz is the <a href="/wiki/Lahneck_Castle" title="Lahneck Castle">Lahneck Castle</a> near Lahnstein, open to visitors from 1 April to 31 October.</p>
<p>The city is close to the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(archaeology)" title="Earthworks (archaeology)">earthworks</a> at <a href="/wiki/Goloring" title="Goloring">Goloring</a>, a possible <a href="/wiki/Urnfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Urnfield">Urnfield</a> calendar constructed some 3000 years ago.</p>
<h3><span id="Elector's_palace"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Elector.27s_palace">Elector's palace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Koblenz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Elector's palace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Palace_(Koblenz)" class="mw-redirect" title="Electoral Palace (Koblenz)">Electoral Palace (Koblenz)</a></div>
<p>In the modern part of the city lies the palace (<i>Residenzschloss</i>), with one front looking towards the Rhine, the other into the Neustadt. It was built in 1778‚Äì1786 by Clement Wenceslaus, the last elector of Trier, under design by the French architect P.M. d'Ixnard. In 1833, the palace was used as a barracks, and became the final depot for the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_semaphore_system" title="Prussian semaphore system">optical telecommunications system</a> that originated in Potsdam. Today, the elector's former palace is a museum; among other curiosities, it contains some fine <a href="/wiki/Gobelin" title="Gobelin">Gobelin</a> tapestries. From it some pretty gardens and promenades (Kaiserin Augusta Anlagen) stretch along the bank of the Rhine, and in them is a memorial to the poet <a href="/wiki/Max_von_Schenkendorf" title="Max von Schenkendorf">Max von Schenkendorf</a>. A fine statue to the empress Augusta, whose favourite residence was Coblenz, stands in the Luisenplatz. But of all public memorials the most striking is the colossal equestrian statue of the emperor <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of Germany">William I of Germany</a>, erected by the Rhine provinces in 1897, standing on a lofty and massive pedestal, at the point where the Rhine and Mosel meet.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_I_monument">William I monument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Koblenz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: William I monument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a> were given an area for their <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutschherrenhaus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deutschherrenhaus (page does not exist)">Deutschherrenhaus</a> <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick" title="Bailiwick">Bailiwick</a> right at the confluence of the Rhine and Mosel, which became known as <i>German Corner</i> (<a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Eck" title="Deutsches Eck">Deutsches Eck</a>).</p>
<p>In 1897, a monument to <a href="/wiki/German_Emperor" title="German Emperor">German Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of Germany">William I of Germany</a>, mounted on a 14-metre-high horse, was inaugurated there by his grandson <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="William II of Germany">William II</a>. The architect was <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Schmitz" title="Bruno Schmitz">Bruno Schmitz</a>, who was responsible for a number of nationalistic German monuments and memorials. The <i>German Corner</i> is since associated with this monument, the (re) foundation of the German Empire and the German refusal of any French claims to the area, as described in the song "<a href="/wiki/Die_Wacht_am_Rhein" title="Die Wacht am Rhein">Die Wacht am Rhein</a>" together with the "Wacht am Rhein" called "<a href="/wiki/Niederwalddenkmal" title="Niederwalddenkmal">Niederwalddenkmal</a>" some 30 kilometres (19 miles) upstream.</p>
<p>During World War II, the statue was destroyed by US artillery. The French occupation administration intended the complete destruction of the monument and wanted to replace it with a new one.</p>
<p>In 1953, Bundespr√§sident <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Heuss" title="Theodor Heuss">Theodor Heuss</a> re-dedicated the monument to <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German unity</a>, adding the signs of the remaining western federal states as well as the ones of the lost areas in the East. A <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Germany" title="Flag of Germany">Flag of Germany</a> has flown there since. The <a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a> was added four years later after the population had voted to join Germany.</p>
<p>In the 1980s, a movie of the monument was often shown on late night TV when the National Anthem was played to mark the end of the day, a practise which was discontinued when nonstop broadcasting became common. On 3 October 1990, the very day the former GDR states joined, their signs were added to the monument.</p>
<p>As German unity was considered complete and the areas under Polish administration were ceded to Poland, the monument lost its official active purpose, now only reminding of history. In 1993, the flag was replaced by a copy of the statue, donated by a local couple. The day chosen for the reinstatement of the statue, however, caused controversy as it coincided with <a href="/wiki/Sedantag" title="Sedantag">Sedantag</a> (Sedan Day) (2 September 1870) a day of celebration remembering Germany's victory over France in the Battle of Sedan.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The event was widely celebrated from the 1870s until the start of the 20th century.</p>

<p>Koblenz is a principal seat of the Mosel and Rhenish wine trade, and also does a large business in the export of mineral waters. Its manufactures include automotive parts (braking systems ‚Äì <a href="/wiki/TRW_Automotive" title="TRW Automotive">TRW Automotive</a>, gas springs and hydraulic vibration dampers ‚Äì <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stabilus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stabilus (page does not exist)">Stabilus</a>), aluminium coils (<a href="/wiki/Aleris_International,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Aleris International, Inc.">Aleris Aluminum</a>), pianos, paper, cardboard, machinery, boats, and barges. Since the 17th century, it has been home to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsbacher" class="mw-redirect" title="K√∂nigsbacher">K√∂nigsbacher</a> brewery (the Old Brewery in Koblenz's city centre, and now a plant in Koblenz-Stolzenfels). It is an important transit centre for the Rhine railways and for the Rhine navigation.</p>
<p>The headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_Forces_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="German Army Forces Command">German Army Forces Command</a> is located in the city.</p>
<p>Since September 19, 2012 an <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon</a> logistics centre is in service.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is located some 15 kilometres (9 miles) outside the city at the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahnkreuz</a> Koblenz.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Koblenz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Koblenz_Hauptbahnhof" title="Koblenz Hauptbahnhof">Koblenz Hbf</a></i> is an <a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">Intercity-Express</a> stop on the <a href="/wiki/West_Rhine_Railway" title="West Rhine Railway">West Rhine Railway</a> between <a href="/wiki/Bonn_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bonn Hauptbahnhof">Bonn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mainz_Hauptbahnhof" title="Mainz Hauptbahnhof">Mainz</a> and is also served by trains on the <a href="/wiki/East_Rhine_Railway" title="East Rhine Railway">East Rhine Railway</a> <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden_Hauptbahnhof" title="Wiesbaden Hauptbahnhof">Wiesbaden</a>‚Äì<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ln_Hauptbahnhof" title="K√∂ln Hauptbahnhof">Cologne</a>. Koblenz is the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Koblenz%E2%80%93Trier_railway" title="Koblenz‚ÄìTrier railway">Moselle line</a> to <a href="/wiki/Trier_Hauptbahnhof" title="Trier Hauptbahnhof">Trier</a> (and connecting to <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_railway_station" title="Luxembourg railway station">Luxemburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Hauptbahnhof" title="Saarbr√ºcken Hauptbahnhof">Saarbr√ºcken</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Lahn_Valley_Railway" title="Lahn Valley Railway">Lahn Valley Railway</a> to <a href="/wiki/Limburg_(Lahn)_station" title="Limburg (Lahn) station">Limburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gie%C3%9Fen_station" title="Gie√üen station">Gie√üen</a>. The other stations in Koblenz are <a href="/wiki/Koblenz-Ehrenbreitstein_station" title="Koblenz-Ehrenbreitstein station">Koblenz-Ehrenbreitstein</a>, Koblenz-G√ºls, <a href="/wiki/Koblenz-L%C3%BCtzel_station" title="Koblenz-L√ºtzel station">Koblenz-L√ºtzel</a>, Koblenz-Moselwei√ü and <a href="/wiki/Koblenz_Stadtmitte_station" title="Koblenz Stadtmitte station">Koblenz Stadtmitte</a>, which opened on 14&#160;April 2011.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Philip_Reeve" title="Philip Reeve">Philip Reeve</a>'s series <a href="/wiki/The_Mortal_Engines_Quartet" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mortal Engines Quartet">The Mortal Engines Quartet</a>, Koblenz, as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Traktionstadtsgesellschaft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Traktionstadtsgesellschaft (page does not exist)">Panzerstadt Koblenz</a>, is a member of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Traktionstadtsgesellschaft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Traktionstadtsgesellschaft (page does not exist)">Traktionstadtsgesellschaft</a>, a fictional league of German <a href="/wiki/Traction_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Traction City">traction cities</a> formed to combat the ruthless advance of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Traction_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-Traction League">Anti-tractionists</a>, thousands of years in the future.</p>

<p><b>Walter Chauncey Camp</b> (April 7, 1859 – March 14, 1925) was an <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> player, coach, and <a href="/wiki/Sports_journalism" title="Sports journalism">sports writer</a> known as the "Father of American Football". Among a long list of inventions, he created the sport's <a href="/wiki/Line_of_scrimmage" title="Line of scrimmage">line of scrimmage</a> and the system of <a href="/wiki/Down_(American_and_Canadian_football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Down (American and Canadian football)">downs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-yalenews_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yalenews-1">[1]</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/John_Heisman" title="John Heisman">John Heisman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amos_Alonzo_Stagg" title="Amos Alonzo Stagg">Amos Alonzo Stagg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Scobey_Warner" class="mw-redirect" title="Glenn Scobey Warner">Pop Warner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fielding_H._Yost" title="Fielding H. Yost">Fielding H. Yost</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Halas" title="George Halas">George Halas</a>, Camp was one of the most accomplished persons in the <a href="/wiki/Early_history_of_American_football" title="Early history of American football">early history of American football</a>. He attended <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale College</a>, where he played and coached <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">college football</a>. Camp's <a href="/wiki/Yale_Bulldogs_football" title="Yale Bulldogs football">Yale teams</a> of 1888, 1891, and 1892 have been recognized as <a href="/wiki/College_football_national_championships_in_NCAA_Division_I_FBS" title="College football national championships in NCAA Division I FBS">national champions</a>. Camp was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/College_Football_Hall_of_Fame" title="College Football Hall of Fame">College Football Hall of Fame</a> as a coach in 1951.</p>
<p>Camp wrote articles and books on the gridiron and sports in general, annually publishing an "<a href="/wiki/College_Football_All-America_Team" title="College Football All-America Team">All-American</a>" team. By the time of his death, he had written nearly 30 books and more than 250 magazine articles.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Camp was born in the city of <a href="/wiki/New_Britain,_Connecticut" title="New Britain, Connecticut">New Britain, Connecticut</a>, the son of Leverett Lee and Ellen Sophia (Cornwell) Camp. Walter Camp was of English descent. His first immigrant ancestor was the English colonist Nicholas Camp, who came from Essex, England and arrived in colonial New England in 1630, arriving first in Massachusetts and then moving to Connecticut that same year.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He attended <a href="/wiki/Hopkins_School" title="Hopkins School">Hopkins Grammar School</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven</a>, entered <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale College</a> in 1875, and graduated in 1880.<sup id="cite_ref-Yaleobit192425_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yaleobit192425-3">[3]</a></sup> At Yale he was a member of <a href="/wiki/Delta_Kappa_Epsilon" title="Delta Kappa Epsilon">Delta Kappa Epsilon</a> fraternity, the <a href="/wiki/Linonian_Society" title="Linonian Society">Linonian Society</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Skull_and_Bones" title="Skull and Bones">Skull and Bones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Yaleobit192425_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yaleobit192425-3">[3]</a></sup> He attended <a href="/wiki/Yale_Medical_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale Medical School">Yale Medical School</a> from 1880 to 1883,<sup id="cite_ref-yalenews_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yalenews-1">[1]</a></sup> where his studies were interrupted first by an outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Typhoid_fever" title="Typhoid fever">typhoid fever</a> and then by work for the Manhattan Watch Company.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_career">Playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1873 Camp attended a meeting where representatives from Columbia, Rutgers, Princeton, and Yale universities created the intercollegiate football association (IFA). The representatives created the rule that each team is only allowed 15 plays per drive. Camp played as a <a href="/wiki/Halfback_(American_football)" title="Halfback (American football)">halfback</a> at Yale from <a href="/wiki/1876_Yale_Bulldogs_football_team" title="1876 Yale Bulldogs football team">1876</a> to <a href="/wiki/1882_Yale_Bulldogs_football_team" title="1882 Yale Bulldogs football team">1882</a>. Harvard player <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Curtis" class="mw-redirect" title="Nathaniel Curtis">Nathaniel Curtis</a> took one look at Camp, then only 156 pounds, and told Yale captain <a href="/wiki/Eugene_V._Baker" title="Eugene V. Baker">Gene Baker</a> "You don't mean to let that child play, do you? . . . He will get hurt."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Camp worked for the New Haven Clock Company beginning in 1883, working his way up to chairman of the board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-Yaleobit192425_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yaleobit192425-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 30, 1888, Camp married Alice Graham Sumner, sister of sociologist <a href="/wiki/William_Graham_Sumner" title="William Graham Sumner">William Graham Sumner</a>. They had two children: Walter Camp, Jr. (born 1891), who attended Yale as well and was elected as a member of <a href="/wiki/Scroll_and_Key" title="Scroll and Key">Scroll and Key</a> in 1912, and Janet Camp Troxell (born 1897).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coaching_career">Coaching career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Coaching career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Camp served as the head football coach at Yale from <a href="/wiki/1888_Yale_Bulldogs_football_team" title="1888 Yale Bulldogs football team">1888</a> to <a href="/wiki/1892_Yale_Bulldogs_football_team" title="1892 Yale Bulldogs football team">1892</a> before moving to <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, where he coached in December 1892 and in <a href="/wiki/1894_Stanford_football_team" title="1894 Stanford football team">1894</a> and <a href="/wiki/1895_Stanford_football_team" title="1895 Stanford football team">1895</a>. On <a href="/wiki/1894_Chicago_vs._Stanford_football_game" title="1894 Chicago vs. Stanford football game">Christmas 1894</a>, he and <a href="/wiki/Amos_Alonzo_Stagg" title="Amos Alonzo Stagg">Amos Alonzo Stagg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Maroons_football" title="Chicago Maroons football">Chicago Maroons</a> organized an early intersectional contest.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Father_of_American_football">Father of American football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Father of American football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Camp was on the various collegiate football rules committees that developed the American game from his time as a player at Yale until his death. English Rugby rules at the time required a tackled player, when the ball was "fairly held," to put the ball down immediately for scrummage. Camp proposed at the U.S. <a href="/wiki/College_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="College Football">College Football</a> 1880 rules convention that the contested <a href="/wiki/Scrum_(rugby)" title="Scrum (rugby)">scrimmage</a> be replaced with a "<a href="/wiki/Line_of_scrimmage" title="Line of scrimmage">line of scrimmage</a>" where the team with the ball started with uncontested possession. This change effectively created the evolution of the modern game of <a href="/wiki/History_of_American_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="History of American Football">American football</a> from its <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby football</a> origins.</p>
<p>He is credited with innovations such as the <a href="/wiki/Snap_(gridiron_football)" title="Snap (gridiron football)">snap</a>-back from <a href="/wiki/Center_(American_football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Center (American football)">center</a>, the system of downs, and the points system as well as the introduction of the now-standard offensive arrangement of players—a seven-man <a href="/wiki/Lineman_(American_football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lineman (American football)">line</a> and a four-man <a href="/wiki/Backfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Backfield">backfield</a> consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Quarterback" title="Quarterback">quarterback</a>, two halfbacks, and a <a href="/wiki/Fullback_(American_football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fullback (American football)">fullback</a>. Camp was also responsible for introducing the "<a href="/wiki/Safety_(gridiron_football_score)" title="Safety (gridiron football score)">safety</a>," the awarding of two points to the defensive side for tackling a ball carrier in his own end zone followed by a free kick by the offense from its own 20-yard line to restart play. This is significant as rugby union has no point value award for this action, but instead awards a scrum to the attacking side five meters from the goal line.</p>

<p>In 2011, reviewing Camp's role in the founding of the sport and of the <a href="/wiki/NCAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NCAA">NCAA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Branch" title="Taylor Branch">Taylor Branch</a> also credited Camp with cutting the number of players on a football team from 15 to 11 and adding measuring lines to the field. However, Branch noted that the revelation in a contemporaneous <i><a href="/wiki/McClure%27s" title="McClure's">McClure's</a></i> magazine story of "Camp's $100,000 slush fund," along with concern about the violence of the growing sport, helped lead to President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>'s intervention in the sport. The NCAA emerged from the national talks, but worked to Yale's disadvantage relative to rival (and Roosevelt's <i><a href="/wiki/Alma_mater" title="Alma mater">alma mater</a></i>) <a href="/wiki/Harvard" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard">Harvard</a>, according to Branch.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite having a full-time job at the New Haven Clock Company, a Camp family business, and being an unpaid yet very involved adviser to the Yale football team, Camp wrote articles and books on the gridiron and sports in general. By the time of his death, he had written nearly 30 books and more than 250 magazine articles. His articles appeared in national periodicals such as <i>Harper's Weekly</i>, <i>Collier's</i>, <i>Outing</i>, <i>Outlook</i>, and <i>The Independent</i>, and in juvenile magazines such as <i>St.&#160;Nicholas</i>, <i>Youth's Companion</i>, and <i>Boys' Magazine</i>. His stories also appeared in major daily newspapers throughout the United States. He also selected an annual "<a href="/wiki/All-America" title="All-America">All-American</a>" team. According to Richard P. Borkowski, "Camp was instrumental, through his extensive writing and lectures in attaching an almost mythical atmosphere of manliness and heroism to the game not previously known in American team sport."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the age of 33, twelve years after graduating from Yale, Walter Camp had already become known as the "Father of Football." In a column in the popular magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Weekly" title="Harper's Weekly">Harper's Weekly</a></i>, sports columnist <a href="/wiki/Caspar_Whitney" title="Caspar Whitney">Caspar Whitney</a> had applied the nickname; the sobriquet was appropriate because, by 1892, Camp had almost single-handedly fashioned the game of modern American football.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_bias">Eastern bias</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eastern bias">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The dominance of Ivy League players on Camp's All-America teams led to criticism over the years that his selections were biased against players from the leading Western universities, including Chicago, Michigan, Minnesota, Wisconsin, and Notre Dame.<sup id="cite_ref-Mans_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mans-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Many selectors picked only Eastern players. For example, <a href="/wiki/Wilton_S._Farnsworth" title="Wilton S. Farnsworth">Wilton S. Farnsworth</a>'s 1910 All-American eleven for the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Evening_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Evening Journal">New York Evening Journal</a></i> was made up of five players from Harvard, two from West Point, and one each from Yale, Princeton, Penn, and Brown.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The selectors were typically Eastern writers and former players who attended only games in the East. In December 1910, <i><a href="/wiki/Mansfield_News_Journal" title="Mansfield News Journal">The Mansfield News</a></i>, an Ohio newspaper, ran an article headlined: "All-American Teams of East Are Jokes: Critics Who Never Saw Western Teams Play to Name Best in Country -- Forget About Michigan, Minnesota and Illinois."<sup id="cite_ref-Mans_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mans-9">[9]</a></sup> The article noted: "Eastern sporting editors must be devoid of all sense of humor, judging by the way in which they permit their football writers to pick 'All-American' elevens. What man in the lot that have picked 'All-American' elevens this fall, saw a single game outside the North Atlantic States? With a conceit all their own they fail to recognize that the United States reaches more than 200 miles in any direction from New York. ... Suppose an Ohio football writer picked 'All-American' teams. Ohio readers would not stand for it. But apparently the eastern readers will swallow anything."<sup id="cite_ref-Mans_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mans-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Daily_Dozen_exercise_regimen">The Daily Dozen exercise regimen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Daily Dozen exercise regimen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Camp was a proponent of exercise, and not just for the athletes he coached. While working as an adviser to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_armed_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="United States armed forces">United States military</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War&#160;I</a>, he devised a program to help servicemen become more physically fit.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Walter Camp has just developed for the Naval Commission on Training Camp Activities a "short hand" system of setting up exercises that seems to fill the bill; a system designed to give a man a running jump start for the serious work of the day. It is called the "daily dozen set-up", meaning thereby twelve very simple exercises.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Both the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">Army</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a> used Camp's methods.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The names of the exercises in the original Daily Dozen, as the whole set became known, were hands, grind, crawl, wave, hips, grate, curl, weave, head, grasp, crouch, and wing. As the name indicates, there were twelve exercises, and they could be completed in about eight minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A prolific writer, Camp wrote a book explaining the exercises and extolling their benefits. During the 1920s, a number of newspapers and magazines used the term "Daily Dozen" to refer to exercise in general.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Starting in 1921 with the <i>Musical Health Builder</i> <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Gramophone record">record</a> sets, Camp began offering morning setting-up exercises to a wider market.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1922, the initiative reached the new medium of <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>A <b>teaching assistant</b> or <b>teacher's aide</b> (<b>TA</b>) or <b>education assistant</b> (<b>EA</b>) is an individual who assists a <a href="/wiki/Teacher" title="Teacher">teacher</a> with instructional responsibilities. TAs include: graduate teaching assistants (GTAs), who are <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate</a> students; undergraduate teaching assistants (UTAs), who are <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a> students; secondary school TAs, who are either high school students or adults; and elementary school TAs, who are adults (also known as <a href="/wiki/Paraprofessional_educator" title="Paraprofessional educator">paraprofessional educators</a> or teacher's aides). By definition, TAs assist with classes, but many graduate students serve as the sole instructor for one or more classes each semester as a <a href="/wiki/Teaching_fellow" title="Teaching fellow">teaching fellow</a> or <a href="/wiki/Graduate_student_instructor" class="mw-redirect" title="Graduate student instructor">graduate student instructor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Graduate and adult TAs generally have a fixed salary determined by each contract period (usually a semester or an academic year); however, undergraduates and high school students are sometimes unpaid and, in the US and other countries with the credit system, receive course credits in return for their assistance. Teaching assistants often help the main teacher by managing students with learning disabilities, such as <a href="/wiki/Attention-deficit_hyperactivity_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder">ADHD</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">Autism</a>, or even physical disabilities, such as blindness or deafness.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Graduate">Graduate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teaching_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Graduate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Graduate teaching assistants (often referred to as GTAs or simply TAs) are <a href="/wiki/Graduate_student" class="mw-redirect" title="Graduate student">graduate students</a> employed on a temporary contract by a department at a <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a> or <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> in teaching-related responsibilities. In New Zealand, Australian, and some Canadian universities, graduate TAs are known as <a href="/wiki/Tutor" title="Tutor">tutors</a>. North American graduate TA positions provide funding for postgraduate research—although the main purpose is to provide teaching support—and it often serves as a first career step for aspiring academics.<sup id="cite_ref-Park_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park-3">[3]</a></sup> TA responsibilities vary greatly and may include: tutoring; holding office hours; invigilating tests or exams; and assisting a <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> with a large lecture class by teaching <a href="/wiki/Student" title="Student">students</a> in <a href="/wiki/Recitation" title="Recitation">recitation</a>, laboratory, or discussion sessions. Professors may also use their teaching assistants to help teach discussions during regular class. This gives the graduate student opportunity to use their teaching skills, as many are in pursuit of teaching careers. Some graduate students assist in <a href="/wiki/Distance_education" title="Distance education">distance education</a> courses by meeting with the students as professors are not able to. Graduate TAs should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Teaching_fellow" title="Teaching fellow">teaching fellows</a> (TFs) or <a href="/wiki/Graduate_student_instructor" class="mw-redirect" title="Graduate student instructor">graduate student instructors</a> (GSIs), who are graduate students who serve as the primary instructors for courses. However, at some universities the TF and TA titles are used interchangeably.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tutorials">Tutorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teaching_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tutorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> universities, a <i>tutor</i> is often, but not always, a <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Postgraduate">postgraduate</a> student or a <a href="/wiki/Lecturer" title="Lecturer">lecturer</a> assigned to conduct a <a href="/wiki/Seminar" title="Seminar">seminar</a> for <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a> students, often known as a <a href="/wiki/Tutorial" title="Tutorial">tutorial</a>. The equivalent of this kind of tutor in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the rest of Canada is known as a graduate teaching assistant or a graduate student instructor (GSI).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Undergraduate">Undergraduate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teaching_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Undergraduate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UTAs or JTAs (Junior Teacher Assistants) usually serve as true assistants to a class; they typically have taken the course with which they are assisting, often with the same professor, and have performed well in it. This case is less common for GTAs, since many would have been undergraduates at other institutions. Unlike professors and GTAs, UTAs generally do not have a fixed salary but instead are paid by the hour, earn credit hours, or volunteer their time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High_school">High school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teaching_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>teaching assistant</i> is used in the high school and middle school setting for students or adults that assist a teacher with one or more classes. The responsibilities, situations, and conditions of these individuals' involvement differ from those in higher education. A less formal position, a TA job in <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a> is generally determined by the supervising teacher. Common tasks include assisting students with their work, and taking attendance. Most of the responsibilities of Teaching Assistants do not require the academic expertise of the professor in charge. Some teaching assistants at this level may teach portions of the class lessons, or teach lessons to small groups of students who need extra instruction. Many TAs work "one-on-one" with special needs students; these TAs shadow their student and assist with classwork, organization, and behavior management. In some parts of the United States it is customary or even required that each classroom have one certified teacher and one or more co-teachers or teaching assistants.</p>
<p>Students attending high school and middle schools can take a course, usually an elective, and perform tasks such as grade and record scores on homework or tests. The work done by the assistant in the role is not tedious and the teacher in this setting reviews the grading to assign partial credit on tests and uses discretion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elementary_school">Elementary school</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teaching_assistant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Elementary school">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school" title="Elementary school">elementary school</a> teaching assistant is an adult who is hired to help a <a href="/wiki/Teacher" title="Teacher">teacher</a> with class-related duties, which are similar to those encountered in <a href="/wiki/Middle_school" title="Middle school">middle</a> and <a href="/wiki/High_school" class="mw-redirect" title="High school">high school</a> settings. They are sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Paraprofessional" title="Paraprofessional">paraprofessionals</a> ('paras' for short) or teacher's aides. Elementary school teaching assistants are generally hired on a contract that lasts the entire academic year. Teaching assistants aide with multiple duties within schools, and can be hired in special education as well.</p>

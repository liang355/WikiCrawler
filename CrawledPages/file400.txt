<p><b>Kiel</b> (<small>German:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ËkiËl]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Kiel.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/94/Kiel.ogg" class="internal" title="Kiel.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is the capital and most populous city in the northern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a>, with a population of 240,832 (June 2014).</p>
<p>Kiel lies approximately 90 kilometres (56&#160;mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>. Due to its geographic location in the north of Germany, the southeast of the <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a> peninsula and the southwestern shore of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, Kiel has become one of the major maritime centres of Germany. For instance, the city is known for a variety of international <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a> events, including the annual <a href="/wiki/Kiel_Week" title="Kiel Week">Kiel Week</a>, which is the biggest sailing event in the world. The Olympic sailing competitions of the <a href="/wiki/1936_Summer_Olympics" title="1936 Summer Olympics">1936</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1972_Summer_Olympics#Venues" title="1972 Summer Olympics">1972 Summer Olympics</a> were held in <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Kiel" title="Bay of Kiel">Bay of Kiel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kiel has also been one of the traditional homes of the <a href="/wiki/German_Navy" title="German Navy">German Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a> fleet, and continues to be a major high-tech <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding" title="Shipbuilding">shipbuilding</a> centre. Located in Kiel is the <a href="/wiki/GEOMAR_Helmholtz_Centre_for_Ocean_Research_Kiel" title="GEOMAR Helmholtz Centre for Ocean Research Kiel">GEOMAR - Helmholtz Centre for Ocean Research Kiel</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kiel" title="University of Kiel">University of Kiel</a>. Kiel is an important sea transport hub, thanks to its location on the <a href="/wiki/Kiel_Fjord" class="mw-redirect" title="Kiel Fjord">Kiel Fjord</a> (<i>Kieler FÃ¶rde</i>) and the busiest artificial waterway in the world, <a href="/wiki/Kiel_Canal" title="Kiel Canal">Kiel Canal</a> (<i>Nord-Ostsee-Kanal</i>). A number of passenger ferries to <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and other countries operate from here. Moreover, today Kiel Harbour is an important port of call for <a href="/wiki/Cruise_ship" title="Cruise ship">cruise ships</a> touring the Baltic Sea.</p>
<p>Kiel was one of the founding cities of original European Green Capital Award in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2005 Kiel's <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a> per capita was <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">â¬</a>35,618, which is well above <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>'s national average, and 159% of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>'s average.<sup id="cite_ref-GDP_per_person_2005_in_Euro_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GDP_per_person_2005_in_Euro-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within Germany and parts of Europe, the city is known for its leading <a href="/wiki/Team_handball" class="mw-redirect" title="Team handball">handball</a> team, <i><a href="/wiki/THW_Kiel" title="THW Kiel">THW Kiel</a></i>. The city is home to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kiel" title="University of Kiel">University of Kiel</a> (established in 1665).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kiel_Fjord" class="mw-redirect" title="Kiel Fjord">Kiel Fjord</a> was probably first settled by <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> who wanted to colonise the land which they had raided, and for many years they settled in German villages. This is evidenced by the geography and architecture of the fjord. The city of Kiel was founded in 1233 as <i>Holstenstadt tom Kyle</i> by Count <a href="/wiki/Adolf_IV_of_Holstein" title="Adolf IV of Holstein">Adolf IV</a> of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Holstein" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Holstein">Holstein</a>, and granted <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck_law" title="LÃ¼beck law">LÃ¼beck city rights</a> in 1242 by Adolf's eldest son, <a href="/wiki/John_I,_Count_of_Holstein-Kiel" title="John I, Count of Holstein-Kiel">John I</a> of Schauenburg. Being a part of Holstein, Kiel belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> and was situated only a few kilometres south of the <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> border.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Kiel, the capital of the county (later duchy) of <a href="/wiki/Holstein" title="Holstein">Holstein</a>, was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> from 1284 until it was expelled in 1518 for harbouring <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">pirates</a>. In 1431, the <i>Kieler Umschlag</i> (<a href="/wiki/Trade_fair" title="Trade fair">trade fair</a>) was first held, which became the central market for goods and money in Schleswig-Holstein, until it began to lose significance from 1850 on, being held for the last time in 1900, until recently, when it has been restarted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">Modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kiel" title="University of Kiel">University of Kiel</a> was founded on 29 September 1665, by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Albert,_Duke_of_Holstein-Gottorp" title="Christian Albert, Duke of Holstein-Gottorp">Christian Albert</a>, Duke of <a href="/wiki/Holstein-Gottorp" class="mw-redirect" title="Holstein-Gottorp">Holstein-Gottorp</a>. A number of important scholars, including <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Mommsen" title="Theodor Mommsen">Theodor Mommsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felix_Jacoby" title="Felix Jacoby">Felix Jacoby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Geiger" title="Hans Geiger">Hans Geiger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck" title="Max Planck">Max Planck</a>, studied or taught there.</p>

<p>From 1773 to 1864, the town belonged to the king of <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. However, because the king ruled Holstein as a fief of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> only through a <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a>, the town was not incorporated as part of Denmark proper. Thus Kiel belonged to Germany, but it was ruled by the Danish king. Even though the empire was abolished in 1806, the Danish king continued to rule Kiel only through his position as Duke of Holstein, which became a member of the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a> in 1815. When <a href="/wiki/Schleswig" class="mw-redirect" title="Schleswig">Schleswig</a> and Holstein rebelled against Denmark in 1848 (the <a href="/wiki/First_Schleswig_War" title="First Schleswig War">First Schleswig War</a>), Kiel became the capital of Schleswig-Holstein until the Danish victory in 1850.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_Schleswig_War" title="Second Schleswig War">Second Schleswig War</a> in 1864, Kiel and the rest of the duchies of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig" class="mw-redirect" title="Schleswig">Schleswig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holstein" title="Holstein">Holstein</a> were conquered by a <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a> alliance of the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian Empire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>. After the war, Kiel was briefly administered by both the Austrians and the Prussians, but the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> in 1866 led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Schleswig-Holstein" title="Province of Schleswig-Holstein">Province of Schleswig-Holstein</a> and the annexation of Kiel by Prussia in 1867. On 24 March 1865 King <a href="/wiki/William_I,_German_Emperor" title="William I, German Emperor">William I</a> based Prussia's Baltic Sea fleet in Kiel instead of <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="GdaÅsk">Danzig (GdaÅsk)</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kaiserliche_Werft_Kiel" title="Kaiserliche Werft Kiel">Imperial shipyard Kiel</a> was established in 1867 in the town.</p>
<p>When William I of Prussia became Emperor <a href="/wiki/William_I,_German_Emperor" title="William I, German Emperor">William I</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871, he designated Kiel and <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmshaven" title="Wilhelmshaven">Wilhelmshaven</a> as <i>ReichskriegshÃ¤fen</i> ("Imperial War Harbours"). The prestigious <a href="/wiki/Kieler_Yacht-Club" title="Kieler Yacht-Club">Kiel Yacht Club</a> was established in 1887 with <a href="/wiki/Prince_Henry_of_Prussia_(1862-1929)" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Henry of Prussia (1862-1929)">Prince Henry of Prussia</a> as its patron. Emperor Wilhelm II became its <a href="/wiki/Commodore_(rank)" title="Commodore (rank)">commodore</a> in 1891.</p>
<p>Because of its new role as Germany's main naval base, Kiel very quickly increased in size in the following years, from 18,770 in 1864 to about 200,000 in 1910. Much of the old town centre and other surroundings were levelled and redeveloped to provide for the growing city. The <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Kiel" title="Trams in Kiel">Kiel tramway network</a>, opened in 1881, had been enlarged to 10 lines, with a total route length of 40&#160;km (25&#160;mi), before the end of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>.</p>
<p>Kiel was the site of the <a href="/wiki/Kiel_mutiny" title="Kiel mutiny">sailors' mutiny</a> which sparked the German Revolution in late 1918. Just before the end of the First World War, the German fleet stationed at Kiel was ordered to be sent out on a last great battle with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. The sailors, who thought of this as a suicide mission which would have no effect on the outcome of the war, decided they had nothing to lose and refused to leave the safety of the port. The sailors' actions and the lack of response of the government to them, fuelled by an increasingly critical view of the Kaiser, sparked a <a href="/wiki/German_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="German Revolution">revolution</a> which caused the abolition of the monarchy and the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a>.</p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Kiel remained one of the major naval bases and shipbuilding centres of the German Reich. There was also a <a href="/wiki/Slave_labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave labour">slave</a> <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps#Types_of_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">labour camp</a> for the local industry.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Because of its status as a naval port and as production site for submarines, Kiel was heavily bombed by the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> during the Second World War. The bombing destroyed more than 80% of the remaining old town, 72% of the central residential areas, and 83% of the industrial areas.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> During the RAF bombing of 23/24 July 1944, Luftwaffe fighters tried to intercept the spoof (i.e. decoy) force instead of the main force attacking Kiel,<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-10">[10]</a></sup> and there was no water for three days; trains and buses did not run for eight days and there was no gas available for cooking for three weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-diary_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-11">[11]</a></sup> There were several bombing raids of the port area during the period 20 February â 20 April 1945 which successfully eliminated many <a href="/wiki/U-Boats" class="mw-redirect" title="U-Boats">U-Boats</a>, and the few large warships (cruisers <a href="/wiki/German_cruiser_Admiral_Hipper" title="German cruiser Admiral Hipper">Hipper</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_pocket_battleship_Admiral_Scheer" class="mw-redirect" title="German pocket battleship Admiral Scheer">Scheer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Koln" class="mw-redirect" title="Koln">Koln</a>) still afloat at that time. Although the town was beyond the stop-line set for the western Allies in the <a href="/wiki/German_surrender_at_L%C3%BCneburg_Heath" title="German surrender at LÃ¼neburg Heath">German surrender at LÃ¼neburg Heath</a>, it and its port, its scientists, and the canal were seized by a British <a href="/wiki/T-Force" title="T-Force">T-Force</a> led by Major <a href="/wiki/Tony_Hibbert_(British_Army_officer)" title="Tony Hibbert (British Army officer)">Tony Hibbert</a> on 5 May 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This forestalled capture of the town by the Soviets, whom the Allies expected to advance from Germany to <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> in violation of the <a href="/wiki/Yalta_Conference" title="Yalta Conference">Yalta agreement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EclipseHistoryLearning_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EclipseHistoryLearning-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Just like other heavily bombed German cities, the city was rebuilt after the war. In 1946, Kiel was named the seat of government for <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a>, and it officially became the state's capital in 1952.</p>
<p>Today, Kiel is once again an important maritime centre of Germany, with high-tech shipbuilding, <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> construction and one of the three leading institutions in the field of marine sciences in Europe, the IFM-GEOMAR. Regular ferries to Scandinavia and Lithuania, as well as the largest sailing event in the world called the <a href="/wiki/Kiel_Week" title="Kiel Week">Kiel Week</a> (Kieler Woche) in German and The Kiel Regatta in English. The <i>Kieler Umschlag</i> is another festival, which has been taking place again since 1975. Kiel is also home to a large service sector and a number of research institutions including the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kiel" title="University of Kiel">University of Kiel</a>, which is the oldest, largest, and most prestigious university in the state.</p>

<p>The oldest building in the city is the 13th century <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaikirche_(Kiel)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nikolaikirche (Kiel) (page does not exist)">Nikolaikirche (Kiel)</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikolaikirche_(Kiel)" class="extiw" title="de:Nikolaikirche (Kiel)">de</a>)</span></i> (<a href="/wiki/St_Nicholas" class="mw-redirect" title="St Nicholas">St Nicholas</a>' Church), which has a sculpture by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Barlach" title="Ernst Barlach">Ernst Barlach</a> in front of it called <i>GeistkÃ¤mpfer</i>.</p>
<p>Kiel is <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a>'s largest city, and therefore Kiel's shopping district is a major attraction, and will see further improvement and renovation efforts in the upcoming years. Kiel's <i>HolstenstraÃe</i> (Holsten Street) is one of the longest shopping streets in Germany. The <i><a href="/wiki/Seat_of_local_government" title="Seat of local government">Rathaus</a></i> (town hall), which was built in 1911, has an operating <a href="/wiki/Paternoster" title="Paternoster">paternoster</a> and the design of its tower was based on one in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. The square in front of it is bordered by a lake and the <a href="/wiki/Opernhaus_Kiel" title="Opernhaus Kiel">Opera House</a>. There are also a number of lakes and parks in the city centre, e.g. Schrevenpark (Schreven Park). There are two <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical gardens</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Old_Botanical_Garden,_Kiel" title="Old Botanical Garden, Kiel">Old Botanical Garden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_der_Christian-Albrechts-Universit%C3%A4t_zu_Kiel" title="Botanischer Garten der Christian-Albrechts-UniversitÃ¤t zu Kiel">New Botanical Garden</a>.</p>
<p>As Kiel is situated near the sea, the beaches to the north of Kiel such as <a href="/wiki/Strande" title="Strande">Strande</a>, Kiel-Schilksee, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ltenort" class="mw-redirect" title="MÃ¶ltenort">MÃ¶ltenort</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laboe" title="Laboe">Laboe</a> are also popular places to visit in spring and summer.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kiel_Week" title="Kiel Week">Kiel Week</a>, more properly known in English as Kiel Regatta, is the largest sailing event in the world and takes place every year in the last full week in June. Many thousands of boats and ships of all kinds and eras take part in the parade. Kiel Week is also a festival, <i><a href="/wiki/Volksfest" title="Volksfest">Volksfest</a></i> and fair as well as a maritime event. There are a number of yachting and sailing clubs in picturesque settings.</p>
<p>Kiel also features a number of museums, including zoological, geological, historical, fine art, industrial and military museums. Notable is the Stadt- und Schifffahrtsmuseum Warleberger Hof (City and Maritime Museum), which belongs to the association <i>museen am meer</i>. In addition to preserving architecture from the 16th century and historic rooms with painted stucco ceilings, it displays urban and cultural exhibits of the 19th and 20th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtmuseum_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtmuseum-15">[15]</a></sup> Particularly intriguing is the history of the carnival in Kiel.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtmuseum_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtmuseum-15">[15]</a></sup> Laboe is home to the <a href="/wiki/Laboe_Naval_Memorial" title="Laboe Naval Memorial">Laboe Naval Memorial</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> submarine <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-995" title="German submarine U-995"><i>U-995</i></a>, which are popular tourist sites.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Holsten_strasse_Kiel.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Holsten_strasse_Kiel.JPG/220px-Holsten_strasse_Kiel.JPG" width="220" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Holsten_strasse_Kiel.JPG/330px-Holsten_strasse_Kiel.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Holsten_strasse_Kiel.JPG/440px-Holsten_strasse_Kiel.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2357" data-file-height="1970" /></a>

The <i>HolstenstraÃe</i> is one of the longest shopping streets in Germany â Kiel is the largest city in the state of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a>.</div>

<p>Kiel's economy is dominated by the service sector, transport and maritime industries. Kiel is also one of the major ports of the <a href="/wiki/German_Navy" title="German Navy">German Navy</a>, and a leading centre of German high-tech military and civil shipbuilding. Kiel is the home of <a href="/wiki/Howaldtswerke-Deutsche_Werft" title="Howaldtswerke-Deutsche Werft">Howaldtswerke-Deutsche Werft</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipyard</a> founded in 1838 famed for its construction of <a href="/wiki/Submarines" class="mw-redirect" title="Submarines">submarines</a>. HDW built the first German submarine <i><a href="/wiki/Brandtaucher" title="Brandtaucher">Brandtaucher</a></i> in 1850, and is today a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/ThyssenKrupp_Marine_Systems" title="ThyssenKrupp Marine Systems">ThyssenKrupp Marine Systems</a>, the leading German group of shipyards.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_scientific_research">Education and scientific research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Education and scientific research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kiel" title="University of Kiel">University of Kiel</a> (German: Christian-Albrechts-UniversitÃ¤t), which was founded by Duke <a href="/wiki/Christian_Albert,_Duke_of_Holstein-Gottorp" title="Christian Albert, Duke of Holstein-Gottorp">Christian Albrecht</a> in 1665, is with round about 25.000 students the only full university of Schleswig-Holstein. Independent, but partly linked to the University Kiel are other research facilities such as the <a href="/wiki/German_National_Library_of_Economics" title="German National Library of Economics">German National Library of Economics</a> â Leibniz Informationcenter for Economy, the <a href="/wiki/Kiel_Institute_for_the_World_Economy" title="Kiel Institute for the World Economy">Kiel Institute for the World Economy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/GEOMAR_Helmholtz_Centre_for_Ocean_Research_Kiel" title="GEOMAR Helmholtz Centre for Ocean Research Kiel">GEOMAR Helmholtz Centre for Ocean Research Kiel</a> and the research institute of the <a href="/wiki/Bundeswehr" title="Bundeswehr">Bundeswehr</a> for water sound and geophysics. Besides these there are other educational institutions such as the <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule_Kiel" title="Fachhochschule Kiel">Fachhochschule Kiel</a> (founded in 1969) and the Muthesius School of Arts (founded in 1907). The projects Murmann School of Global Management and Economics and Multimedia Campus Kiel werenât successful at last. The Wirtschaftsakademie Schleswig-Holstein offers besides advanced training at the Berfusakademie dual study courses for economists, business information specialists and industrial engineers.</p>
<p>Noteworthy as departmental research institute is the federal institute for dairy research which was merged into the Max-Rubner-Institut together with other institutions in 2004. The state capital Kiel is a corporative sponsoring member of the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Society" title="Max Planck Society">Max Planck Society</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/ARGE-SH" title="ARGE-SH">ARGE-SH</a> as eldest building research institution of the republic of Germany has its headquarters in Kiel.</p>
<p>There are twelve <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">Gymnasium</a>s in Kiel, of which the Kieler Gelehrtenschule, founded in 1320 as a humanistic gymnasium, is the oldest. Other secondary schools are amongst others the Gymnasium Elmschenhagen and the Max-Planck-Schule with a focus on natural sciences and the Ricarda-Huch-Schule with a focus on languages. Furthermore there are many comprehensive schools â partially with secondary schools â all over the city area and private schools, too.</p>

<p>The city districts of DÃ¼sternbrook, Schreventeich, Ravensberg and BlÃ¼cherplatz are popular places to live with many 19th century buildings, villas and tree-lined streets. The government offices, ministries and parliament of the state of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a> are also mainly based in these neighbourhoods, particularly DÃ¼sternbrook. In contrast to the heavy bomb damage inflicted on the central parts of the city during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, most of the residential areas were not severely damaged. Hence, Kiel's more modern-style inner city and Kiel's more historic/elaborate residential areas stand in architectural contrast to one another.</p>
<p>There are plans for large-scale improvement and building efforts for the inner city, providing better pavements, better access to and view of the waterfront, and a generally more attractive feel to the place. These plans, most notably the "Kleiner Kiel Kanal", a restoration of a historic canal that was filled in to make place for road infrastructure, are to be implemented in the next few years.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>Kiel is situated near an important pan-European motorway, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_7" title="Bundesautobahn 7">A7</a>, which connects northern Europe with central and southern Europe.</p>
<p>Kiel has a <a href="/wiki/Kiel_Hauptbahnhof" title="Kiel Hauptbahnhof">central railway station</a> with hourly trains to <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="LÃ¼beck">LÃ¼beck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flensburg" title="Flensburg">Flensburg</a>, and to <a href="/wiki/Husum" title="Husum">Husum</a>. Moreover, the <i><a href="/wiki/Intercity_Express" class="mw-redirect" title="Intercity Express">Intercity Express</a></i> (ICE) connects Kiel with <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>.</p>
<p>Kiel has public transport through the local company KVG, providing bus service within the city, Autokraft and Verkehrsbetriebe Kreis PlÃ¶n providing regional bus service, and the Schlepp- und FÃ¤hrgesellschaft Kiel, providing public transport on the fjord with ferries.</p>
<p>Kiel also has 8 regional railway stations within the city proper,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> which are connected with each other, the main railway station Kiel Hbf and other stations by regional trains, which can be used within the boundaries of the city with a normal bus ticket.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kiel is a significant port for passenger and cargo shipping from Germany to <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltic States">Baltic States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. Passenger ferries operate to and from <a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> (<a href="/wiki/Stena_Line" title="Stena Line">Stena Line</a>, 13Â½ hours, daily), <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> (<a href="/wiki/Color_Line_(ferry_operator)" title="Color Line (ferry operator)">Color Line</a>, 19Â½ hours, daily), and <a href="/wiki/Klaip%C4%97da" title="KlaipÄda">KlaipÄda</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> (<a href="/wiki/DFDS_Lisco" title="DFDS Lisco">DFDS Lisco</a>, 21 hours, 6 times per week). Cargo ferries operate from and to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (<a href="/wiki/DFDS_Lisco" title="DFDS Lisco">DFDS Lisco</a>, twice a week), and <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad" title="Kaliningrad">Kaliningrad</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (NSA, once a week).</p>
<p>The nearest international airport is <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_Airport" title="Hamburg Airport">Hamburg Airport</a>, which is situated approximately 90 kilometres (56&#160;mi) to the south of Kiel. There is a shuttle bus service (<i>KIELIUS</i>) operating between Hamburg Airport and Kiel central railway station. There is also an airport at LÃ¼beck.</p>

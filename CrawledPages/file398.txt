<p><b>Halle (Saale)</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Halle (Saale)</span>, <small>pronounced</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈhalə ˈzaːlə]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Halle_(Saale).ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/48/De-Halle_%28Saale%29.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Halle (Saale).ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in the southern part of the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a>.</p>
<p>Halle is an economic and educational center in central-eastern Germany. The <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_University_of_Halle-Wittenberg" title="Martin Luther University of Halle-Wittenberg">University of Halle-Wittenberg</a> is the largest university in Saxony-Anhalt and one of the oldest universities in Germany, and a nurturing ground for the local <a href="/wiki/Startup_ecosystem" title="Startup ecosystem">startup ecosystem</a>. Together with <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, Halle is at the heart of the <a href="/wiki/Central_German_Metropolitan_Region" title="Central German Metropolitan Region">Central German Metropolitan Region</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Halle (Saale) is in the southern part of <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a>, along the river <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> which drains the surrounding plains and the greater part of the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Thuringia" class="mw-redirect" title="Free State of Thuringia">Free State of Thuringia</a> just to its south, and the Thuringian basin, northwards from the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Forest" title="Thuringian Forest">Thuringian Forest</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, one of eastern Germany's other major cities, is only 35 kilometres (22&#160;mi) away.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Halle's early history is connected with harvesting of salt. The name <i>Halle</i> reflects early Celtic settlement given that <i>halen</i> is the Brythonic (Welsh/Breton) word for salt (cf. <i>salann</i> in Irish). The name of the river <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> also contains the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic language">Germanic</a> root for salt, and salt-harvesting has taken place in Halle at least since the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> (2300–600 BC).</p>
<p>The Latin name <i>Hala Saxonum</i> was also used.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_until_industrialisation">Middle Ages until industrialisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle Ages until industrialisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The earliest documented mention of Halle dates from AD 806. It became a part of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Magdeburg" title="Archbishopric of Magdeburg">Archbishopric of Magdeburg</a> in the 10th century and remained so until 1680, when <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Prussia" title="Brandenburg-Prussia">Brandenburg-Prussia</a> annexed it together with <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Magdeburg" title="Duchy of Magdeburg">Duchy of Magdeburg</a>, while it was an important location for <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a> in the 16th century. Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Albert_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Albert of Mainz">Albert of Mainz</a> (<a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Magdeburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop of Magdeburg">Archbishop of Magdeburg</a> from 1513 to 1545) also impacted on the town in this period. According to historic documents, the city of Halle became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> at least as early as 1281.</p>
<p>Halle became a center for <a href="/wiki/Pietism" title="Pietism">Pietism</a>, a movement encouraged by King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_I_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William I of Prussia">Frederick William I of Prussia</a> (reigned 1713-1740) because it caused the area's large <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> population to be more inclined to Fredrick William I's religion (<a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a>), as well as more loyal to the Prussian king instead of to the decentralized feudal system. By the 1740s Halle had established many orphanages as well as schools for the wealthy in the sober style Pietism encouraged. This Halle education was the first time the "modern education" system was established.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Halle Pietists also combated poverty.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Fourth_Coalition" title="War of the Fourth Coalition">War of the Fourth Coalition</a>, French and Prussian forces clashed in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Halle" title="Battle of Halle">Battle of Halle</a> on 17 October 1806. The fighting moved from the covered bridges on the city's west side, through the streets and market place, to the eastern suburbs.</p>
<p>In 1815 Halle became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussian</a> <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Saxony" title="Province of Saxony">Province of Saxony</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="World_War_II_(1939–1945)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II_.281939.E2.80.931945.29">World War II (1939–1945)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World War II (1939–1945)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halle_concentration_camp" title="Halle concentration camp">KZ-Außenlager Birkhahn</a>, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_subcamps_of_Buchenwald" title="List of subcamps of Buchenwald">subcamp</a> of <a href="/wiki/Buchenwald_concentration_camp" title="Buchenwald concentration camp">Buchenwald</a> was in Halle, where prisoners from Poland, Czechoslovakia, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, France, Netherlands and other nations<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> were <a href="/wiki/Forced_labour_under_German_rule_during_World_War_II" title="Forced labour under German rule during World War II">forced to work</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Siebel" title="Siebel">Siebel</a> aircraft plants, making combat aircraft. The plant was later dismantled. In <a href="/wiki/Ammendorf/Beesen" title="Ammendorf/Beesen">Ammendorf</a>, a large factory owned by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orgacid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Orgacid (page does not exist)">Orgacid</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orgacid" class="extiw" title="de:Orgacid">de</a>)</span> produced <a href="/wiki/Mustard_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Mustard gas">mustard gas</a>.</p>
<p>Near the end of World War II, there were two bombing raids carried out against the town: the first on 31 March 1945, the second a few days later. The first attack took place between the railway station and the city's centre, and the second bombing was in the southern district. It killed over 1,000 inhabitants and destroyed 3,600 buildings. Among them, the <a href="/wiki/Marktkirche_Unser_Lieben_Frauen" title="Marktkirche Unser Lieben Frauen">Market Church</a>, St. George Church, the Old Town Hall, the City Theatre, historic buildings on Bruederstrasse and on Grosse Steinstrasse, and the city cemetery.</p>
<p>On 17 April 1945, American soldiers occupied Halle, and the red tower was set on fire by artillery and destroyed. The Market Church and the Church of St. George received more hits. However, the city was spared further damage because an aerial bombardment was canceled, after former naval officer <a href="/wiki/Felix_von_Luckner" title="Felix von Luckner">Felix von Luckner</a> negotiated the city's surrender to the American army. In July, the Americans withdrew and the city was occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="German_Democratic_Republic_(1949–1990)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="German_Democratic_Republic_.281949.E2.80.931990.29">German Democratic Republic (1949–1990)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: German Democratic Republic (1949–1990)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After World War II, Halle served as the capital of the short-lived administrative region of Saxony-Anhalt until 1952, when the <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East German</a> government abolished its "Länder" (states). As a part of <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> (until 1990), it functioned as the capital of the administrative district (<i>Bezirk</i>) of Halle.</p>
<h3><span id="Since_German_Unity_(after_1990)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_German_Unity_.28after_1990.29">Since German Unity (after 1990)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Since German Unity (after 1990)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a> was re-established as a <i><a href="/wiki/Land_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Land (Germany)">Bundesland</a></i> in 1990, <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a>, not Halle, became the capital.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_heritage">Industrial heritage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Industrial heritage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SalineMuseum11.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/SalineMuseum11.jpg/220px-SalineMuseum11.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/SalineMuseum11.jpg/330px-SalineMuseum11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/SalineMuseum11.jpg/440px-SalineMuseum11.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1890" data-file-height="1296" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SalineMuseum11.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hallors_and_Saline_Museum" title="Hallors and Saline Museum">Hallors and Saline Museum</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Salt, also known as <i>white gold</i>, was extracted from four "Borns" (well-like structures). The four Borns/brine named Gutjahrbrunnen, Meteritzbrunnen, Deutscher Born and Hackeborn, were located around the Hallmarket (or "Under Market"), now a market square with a fountain, just across from the TV station, <a href="/wiki/Mitteldeutscher_Rundfunk" title="Mitteldeutscher Rundfunk">MDR</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> was highly concentrated and boiled in <i>Koten</i>, simple structured houses made from reed and clay. Salters, who wore a unique uniform with eighteen silver buttons, were known as <i>Halloren</i>, and this name was later used for the chocolates in the shape of these buttons.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Halloren_Chocolate_Factory" title="Halloren Chocolate Factory">Halloren-Werke</a>, the oldest chocolate factory in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, was founded in 1804. Old documents are on display and a chocolate room can be visited.</p>
<p>Within <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, Halle's chemical industry, now mainly shut down, was of great importance. The two main companies in the region were <a href="/wiki/Buna_Werke_Schkopau" title="Buna Werke Schkopau">Buna-Werke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leuna" title="Leuna">Leuna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Halle-Neustadt" title="Halle-Neustadt">Halle-Neustadt</a> was built in the 1960s to accommodate the employees of these two factories.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_culture">Science and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Science and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Halleuniplatz.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Halleuniplatz.JPG/220px-Halleuniplatz.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Halleuniplatz.JPG/330px-Halleuniplatz.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Halleuniplatz.JPG/440px-Halleuniplatz.JPG 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>

<p><a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> composer <a href="/wiki/Georg_Friedrich_H%C3%A4ndel" class="mw-redirect" title="Georg Friedrich Händel">Georg Friedrich Händel</a> (later <i>George Frideric Handel</i>) was born in Halle in 1685 and spent the first 17 years of his life in the city. The house where he lived is now a museum about his life. To celebrate his music, Halle has staged a <a href="/wiki/Handel_Festival,_Halle" title="Handel Festival, Halle">Handel Festival</a> since 1922, annually in June since 1952. The <a href="/wiki/August_Hermann_Francke" title="August Hermann Francke"><i>Franckesche Stiftungen</i></a> (Francke Foundations) are home to the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stadtsingechor_zu_Halle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stadtsingechor zu Halle (page does not exist)">Stadtsingechor zu Halle</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadtsingechor_zu_Halle" class="extiw" title="de:Stadtsingechor zu Halle">de</a>)</span></i>, which was founded before the year 1116 and is one of the oldest boys' choirs in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Halle" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Halle">University of Halle</a> was founded here in 1694. It is now combined with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wittenberg" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Wittenberg">University of Wittenberg</a> and called the <i><a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_University_of_Halle-Wittenberg" title="Martin Luther University of Halle-Wittenberg">Martin Luther University of Halle-Wittenberg</a></i>. The university's medical school was established by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hoffmann" title="Friedrich Hoffmann">Friedrich Hoffmann</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Botanische_Garten_der_Martin-Luther-Universit%C3%A4t_Halle-Wittenberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanische Garten der Martin-Luther-Universität Halle-Wittenberg">Botanische Garten der Martin-Luther-Universität Halle-Wittenberg</a>, dates back to 1698. Halle's <a href="/wiki/German_Academy_of_Sciences_Leopoldina" class="mw-redirect" title="German Academy of Sciences Leopoldina">German Academy of Sciences Leopoldina</a> is the oldest and one of the most respected scientific societies in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Halle is also home to Germany's oldest Evangelical Bible college, known as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marienbibliothek&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marienbibliothek (page does not exist)">Marienbibliothek</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marienbibliothek" class="extiw" title="de:Marienbibliothek">de</a>)</span>, with 27,000 titles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The seat of the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Social_Anthropology" title="Max Planck Institute for Social Anthropology">Max Planck Institute for Social Anthropology</a>, one of the world's largest social anthropological research institutions and a part of the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Society" title="Max Planck Society">Max Planck Society</a>, is in Halle.</p>
<p>Halle was a centre of German <a href="/wiki/Pietism" title="Pietism">Pietism</a> and played an important role in establishing the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> church in North America, when <a href="/wiki/Henry_Muhlenberg" title="Henry Muhlenberg">Henry Muhlenberg</a> and others were sent as missionaries to <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> in the mid-18th century. Muhlenberg is now called the first Patriarch of the Lutheran Church in America. He and his son, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Muhlenberg" title="Frederick Muhlenberg">Frederick Muhlenberg</a>, who was the first <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speaker of the United States House of Representatives</a>, were graduates of Halle University.</p>
<p>The Silver Treasure of the <i>Halloren</i> is displayed occasionally at the Technical Museum Saline. It is a unique collection of silver and gold goblets dating back to 1266. The ancient craft of "Schausieden" (boiling of the brine) can be observed there too. The State Museum of Prehistory houses the <a href="/wiki/Nebra_sky_disk" title="Nebra sky disk">Nebra sky disk</a>, a significant (though unproven) Bronze-Age find with astrological significance.</p>
<p>Halle Zoo contributes to the <a href="/wiki/EAZA" class="mw-redirect" title="EAZA">EAZA</a> breeding programme, in particular for the Angolan lion and the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_tiger" class="mw-redirect" title="Malaysian tiger">Malaysian tiger</a>. Halle is also known for its thriving <a href="/wiki/Coypu" title="Coypu">coypu</a> (or nutria) population, which is native to <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.</p>
<p>With writers such as <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Heine" title="Heinrich Heine">Heine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Freiherr_von_Eichendorff" title="Joseph Freiherr von Eichendorff">Eichendorff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schleiermacher" title="Friedrich Schleiermacher">Schleiermacher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Tieck" title="Ludwig Tieck">Tieck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Novalis" title="Novalis">Novalis</a> the town was a vibrant scene of the <a href="/wiki/German_Romanticism" title="German Romanticism">German Romanticism</a>. Also <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</a> was a regular guest at the house of his close friend <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Reichardt" title="Johann Friedrich Reichardt">Johann Friedrich Reichardt</a>.</p>
<p>German-American <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionist</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Lyonel_Feininger" title="Lyonel Feininger">Lyonel Feininger</a> worked in Halle on an invitation by the city from 1929 to 1931. As one of eleven views of the city termed <i>Halle Cycle</i>, he painted in 1931 <i>Die Türme über der Stadt</i><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> (The towers above the city), which is now in the <a href="/wiki/Museum_Ludwig" title="Museum Ludwig">Museum Ludwig</a> in Cologne.<sup id="cite_ref-feininger_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feininger-10">[10]</a></sup> This painting appeared on a 55 <a href="/wiki/Euro_coins" title="Euro coins">eurocent</a> stamp on 5 December 2002 as a part of the series “Deutsche Malerei des 20. Jahrhunderts” (German painting of the 20th century).<sup id="cite_ref-feininger2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feininger2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_history">Transport history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transport history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wucherer" title="Ludwig Wucherer">Ludwig Wucherer</a> made Halle an important rail hub in central Germany. In 1840 he opened the <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg-Leipzig_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Magdeburg-Leipzig Railway">Magdeburg-Halle-Leipzig line</a>, completing a connection between <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>. In 1841–1860, other lines to <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Thuringian Railway">Erfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halle-Kassel_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Halle-Kassel Railway">Kassel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anhalt_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Anhalt Railway">Berlin</a> followed.</p>
<p>The centrepiece of Halle's urban public transport system is the <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Halle_(Saale)" title="Trams in Halle (Saale)">Halle (Saale) tramway network</a>. It includes the world's first major electric-powered inner-city tram line, which was opened in 1891.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Halle_(Saale)_Hauptbahnhof" title="Halle (Saale) Hauptbahnhof">Halle (Saale) Hauptbahnhof</a> is the main railway station.</p>
<p>Halle's prominence as a railway centre is set to continue growing with the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Erfurt-Leipzig/Halle_high-speed_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Erfurt-Leipzig/Halle high-speed railway">Erfurt-Leipzig/Halle high-speed railway</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Leipzig will also be connected to this railway, but since it is a terminus station (though the <a href="/wiki/Leipzig_City_Tunnel" title="Leipzig City Tunnel">Leipzig City Tunnel</a> is currently under construction, the route will be shared with S-Bahn trains, making it unlikely that it will be used as a through station for Berlin-Munich trains), Halle is more likely to be used as an intermediate stop for Berlin-Munich trains. The completion of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Erfurt_high-speed_railway" title="Nuremberg–Erfurt high-speed railway">Nuremberg–Erfurt high-speed railway</a> will also provide a further impetus to use the route.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halle_(Saale)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The football team <i>Hallescher FC Wacker 1900</i> had some regional importance before World War II. In the German Championship <i>Wacker</i> reached the semi-finals in 1921, and the quarter-finals in 1928. The successor team became East German champions in 1949 and 1952 under the names of <i>ZSG Union</i> and <i>BSG Turbine Halle</i>. From these evolved today's <i><a href="/wiki/Turbine_Halle" title="Turbine Halle">Turbine Halle</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hallescher_FC" title="Hallescher FC">Hallescher FC</a></i>. In the era of the German Democratic Republic, the latter club (as <i>Chemie Halle</i> ) was a mainstay in the first division and won the Cup tournament in 1956 and 1962. The most prominent player was 72-times international <a href="/wiki/Bernd_Bransch" title="Bernd Bransch">Bernd Bransch</a>, who was with <i>Chemie</i> in the 1960s and 1970s. These days, <i>Hallescher FC</i> usually plays in the third division.</p>
<p>The general sports club <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=SV_Halle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="SV Halle (page does not exist)">SV Halle</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/SV_Halle" class="extiw" title="de:SV Halle">de</a>)</span></i>, originating from <i>Chemie Halle</i>, created a notable number of Olympic gold medallists and world champions, mainly in nautical and watersports, e.g., swimmer <a href="/wiki/Kornelia_Ender" title="Kornelia Ender">Kornelia Ender</a> won four Olympic gold medals in 1976 and <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Hajek" title="Andreas Hajek">Andreas Hajek</a> won four rowing world championships between 1998 and 2001. The basketball team of the club - these days known as <i>Lions</i> and focusing on the woman's team which plays in the national first division - won five men's and 10 women's championships of the German Democratic Republic. The Hallesher FC's location is extremely close to a tram station.</p>

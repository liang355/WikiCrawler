<p>A <b>manuscript</b> (abbreviated <b>MS</b> for singular and <b>MSS</b> for plural) is any document written by hand or <a href="/wiki/Typewritten" class="mw-redirect" title="Typewritten">typewritten</a>, as opposed to being mechanically printed or reproduced in some indirect or automated way.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> More recently, it is understood to be an author's written, typed, or word-processed copy of a work, as distinguished from the print of the same.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Before the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a>, all documents and books were manuscripts. Manuscripts are not defined by their contents, which may combine writing with mathematical calculations, maps, explanatory figures or illustrations. Manuscripts may be in book form, <a href="/wiki/Scroll_(parchment)" class="mw-redirect" title="Scroll (parchment)">scrolls</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Codex" title="Codex">codex</a> format. <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">Illuminated manuscripts</a> are enriched with pictures, border decorations, elaborately embossed initial letters or full-page illustrations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_background">Cultural background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuscript&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cultural background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sargis_Pitsak.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sargis_Pitsak.jpg/220px-Sargis_Pitsak.jpg" width="220" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sargis_Pitsak.jpg/330px-Sargis_Pitsak.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sargis_Pitsak.jpg/440px-Sargis_Pitsak.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1808" data-file-height="2530" /></a>

<p>The traditional abbreviations are <b>MS</b> for manuscript and <b>MSS</b> for manuscripts,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> while the forms <b>MS.</b>, <b>ms</b> or <b>ms.</b> for singular, and <b>MSS.</b>, <b>mss</b> or <b>mss.</b> for plural (with or without the full stop, all uppercase or all lowercase) are also accepted.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The second <i>s</i> is not simply the plural; by an old convention, it doubles the last letter of the abbreviation to express the plural, just as <i>pp.</i> means "pages".</p>
<p>Before the invention of <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">woodblock printing</a> in China or by <a href="/wiki/Moveable_type" class="mw-redirect" title="Moveable type">moveable type</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> in Europe, all written documents had to be both produced and reproduced by hand. Historically, manuscripts were produced in form of scrolls (<i>volume</i> in Latin) or books (<i>codex</i>, plural <i>codices</i>). Manuscripts were produced on <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a> and other parchment, on <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, and on paper. In Russia <a href="/wiki/Birch_bark_document" class="mw-redirect" title="Birch bark document">birch bark documents</a> as old as from the 11th century have survived. In India, the <a href="/wiki/Palm_leaf_manuscript" class="mw-redirect" title="Palm leaf manuscript">palm leaf manuscript</a>, with a distinctive long rectangular shape, was used from ancient times until the 19th century. <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">Paper</a> spread from China via the Islamic world to Europe by the 14th century, and by the late 15th century had largely replaced parchment for many purposes.</p>
<p>When Greek or Latin works were published, numerous professional copies were made simultaneously by scribes in a <a href="/wiki/Scriptorium" title="Scriptorium">scriptorium</a>, each making a single copy from an original that was declaimed aloud.</p>
<p>The oldest written manuscripts have been preserved by the perfect dryness of their Middle Eastern resting places, whether placed within <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagi</a> in Egyptian tombs, or reused as <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummy</a>-wrappings, discarded in the <a href="/wiki/Midden" title="Midden">middens</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxyrhynchus" title="Oxyrhynchus">Oxyrhynchus</a> or secreted for safe-keeping in jars and buried (<a href="/wiki/Nag_Hammadi_library" title="Nag Hammadi library">Nag Hammadi library</a>) or stored in dry caves (<a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_scrolls" class="mw-redirect" title="Dead Sea scrolls">Dead Sea scrolls</a>). Manuscripts in <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">Tocharian languages</a>, written on palm leaves, survived in desert burials in the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a> of Central Asia. Volcanic ash preserved some of the Roman library of the <a href="/wiki/Villa_of_the_Papyri" title="Villa of the Papyri">Villa of the Papyri</a> in <a href="/wiki/Herculaneum" title="Herculaneum">Herculaneum</a>.</p>

<p>Ironically, the manuscripts that were being most carefully preserved in the libraries of <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">antiquity</a> are virtually all lost. Papyrus has a life of at most a century or two in relatively moist Italian or Greek conditions; only those works copied onto parchment, usually after the general conversion to Christianity, have survived, and by no means all of those.</p>
<p>Originally, all books were in manuscript form. In China, and later other parts of East Asia, <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing" title="Woodblock printing">woodblock printing</a> was used for books from about the 7th century. The earliest dated example is the <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Sutra" title="Diamond Sutra">Diamond Sutra</a> of 868. In the Islamic world and the West, all books were in manuscript until the introduction of movable type printing in about 1450. Manuscript copying of books continued for a least a century, as printing remained expensive. Private or government documents remained hand-written until the invention of the typewriter in the late 19th century. Because of the likelihood of errors being introduced each time a manuscript was copied, the <a href="/wiki/Textual_criticism#Stemmatics" title="Textual criticism">filiation</a> of different versions of the same text is a fundamental part of the study and criticism of all texts that have been transmitted in manuscript.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, in the first millennium, documents of sufficiently great importance were inscribed on soft metallic sheets such as <a href="/wiki/Intaglio_printing" class="mw-redirect" title="Intaglio printing">copperplate</a>, softened by refiner's fire and inscribed with a metal stylus. In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, for example, as early as 900AD, specimen documents were not inscribed by stylus, but were punched much like the style of today's <a href="/wiki/Dot-matrix_printer" class="mw-redirect" title="Dot-matrix printer">dot-matrix printers</a>. This type of document was rare compared to the usual leaves and bamboo staves that were inscribed. However, neither the leaves nor paper were as durable as the metal document in the hot, humid climate. In <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a>, the kammavaca, Buddhist manuscripts, were inscribed on brass, copper or ivory sheets, and even on discarded monk robes folded and lacquered. In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> some important <a href="/wiki/Etruria" title="Etruria">Etruscan</a> texts were similarly inscribed on thin gold plates: similar sheets have been discovered in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>. Technically, these are all inscriptions rather than manuscripts.</p>
<p>The study of the writing, or "hand" in surviving manuscripts is termed <a href="/wiki/Palaeography" title="Palaeography">palaeography</a>. In the Western world, from the <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical period</a> through the early centuries of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian era">Christian era</a>, manuscripts were written without spaces between the words (<a href="/wiki/Scriptio_continua" title="Scriptio continua">scriptio continua</a>), which makes them especially hard for the untrained to read. Extant copies of these early manuscripts written in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and usually dating from the 4th century to the 8th century, are classified according to their use of either all <a href="/wiki/Majuscule" class="mw-redirect" title="Majuscule">upper case</a> or all <a href="/wiki/Lower_case" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower case">lower case letters</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> manuscripts, such as the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_scrolls" class="mw-redirect" title="Dead Sea scrolls">Dead Sea scrolls</a> make no such differentiation. Manuscripts using all upper case letters are called <a href="/wiki/Majuscule" class="mw-redirect" title="Majuscule">majuscule</a>, those using all lower case are called <a href="/wiki/Lower_case" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower case">minuscule</a>. Usually, the majuscule scripts such as <a href="/wiki/Uncial" class="mw-redirect" title="Uncial">uncial</a> are written with much more care. The scribe lifted his pen between each stroke, producing an unmistakable effect of regularity and formality. On the other hand, while minuscule scripts can be written with pen-lift, they may also be <a href="/wiki/Cursive" title="Cursive">cursive</a>, that is, use little or no pen-lift.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_variations">Modern variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuscript&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the context of <a href="/wiki/Library_science" title="Library science">library science</a>, a manuscript is defined as any hand-written item in the collections of a library or an archive. For example, a library's collection of hand-written letters or diaries is considered a manuscript collection. Such manuscript collections are described in finding aids, similar to an index or table of contents to the collection, in accordance with national and international content standards such as <a href="/wiki/Describing_Archives:_A_Content_Standard" title="Describing Archives: A Content Standard">DACS</a> and <a href="/wiki/ISAD(G)" title="ISAD(G)">ISAD(G)</a>.</p>
<p>In other contexts, however, the use of the term "manuscript" no longer necessarily means something that is hand-written. By analogy a <i>typescript</i> has been produced on a typewriter.<sup id="cite_ref-merriam-webster.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merriam-webster.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In book, magazine, and music publishing, a manuscript is an original copy of a work written by an author or composer, which generally follows standardized typographic and formatting rules. (The staff paper commonly used for handwritten music is, for this reason, often called "manuscript paper"). In film and theatre, a manuscript, or <i>script</i> for short, is an author's or dramatist's text, used by a theatre company or film crew during the production of the work's performance or filming. More specifically, a motion picture manuscript is called a screenplay; a television manuscript, a teleplay; a manuscript for the theatre, a stage play; and a manuscript for audio-only performance is often called a radio play, even when the recorded performance is disseminated via non-radio means.</p>
<p>In insurance, a manuscript policy is one that is negotiated between the insurer and the policyholder, as opposed to an off-the-shelf form supplied by the insurer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_manuscript_history">European manuscript history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuscript&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European manuscript history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">Illuminated manuscript</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">

After plummeting in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>, the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">high</a> and <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">late medieval period</a> witnessed a sharp increase of manuscript production.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most surviving pre-modern manuscripts use the codex format (as in a modern book), which had replaced the scroll by <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a>. <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">Parchment</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a>, as the best type of parchment is known, had also replaced <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, which was not nearly so long lived and has survived to the present only in the extremely dry conditions of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, although it was widely used across the Roman world. Parchment is made of animal skin, normally calf, sheep, and/or goat, but also other animals. With all skins, the quality of the finished product is based on how much preparation and skill was put into turning the skin into parchment. Parchment made from calf or sheep was the most common in Northern Europe, while civilizations in Southern Europe preferred goatskin.<sup id="cite_ref-Intro_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intro-11">[11]</a></sup> Often, if the parchment is white or cream in color and veins from the animal can still be seen, it is calfskin. If it is yellow, greasy or in some cases shiny, then it was made from sheepskin.<sup id="cite_ref-Intro_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intro-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vellum comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word vitulinum which means “of calf”/ “made from calf”. For modern parchment makers and calligraphers, and apparently often in the past, the terms parchment and vellum are used based on the different degrees of quality, preparation and thickness, and not according to which animal the skin came from, and because of this, the more neutral term "membrane" is often used by modern academics, especially where the animal has not been established by testing.<sup id="cite_ref-Intro_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intro-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because they are books, pre-modern manuscripts are best described using bibliographic rather than archival standards. The standard endorsed by the American Library Association is known as AMREMM.<sup id="cite_ref-AMREMM_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMREMM-12">[12]</a></sup> A growing digital catalog of pre-modern manuscripts is <a href="/wiki/Digital_Scriptorium" title="Digital Scriptorium">Digital Scriptorium</a>, hosted by the University of California at Berkeley.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_sample_of_common_genres_of_manuscripts">A sample of common genres of manuscripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuscript&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: A sample of common genres of manuscripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From ancient texts to medieval maps, anything written down for study would have been done with manuscripts. Some of the most common genres were bibles, religious commentaries, philosophy, law and government texts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bibles">Bibles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuscript&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bibles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>“<a href="/wiki/The_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="The Bible">The Bible</a> was the most studied book of the Middle Ages.”<sup id="cite_ref-beryl_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beryl-13">[13]</a></sup> The Bible was the center of medieval religious life. Along with the Bible came scores of commentaries. Commentaries were written in volumes, with some focusing on just single pages of scripture. Across Europe, there were universities that prided themselves on their biblical knowledge. Along with universities, certain cities also had their own celebrities of biblical knowledge during the medieval period.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_of_hours">Book of hours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuscript&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Book of hours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Book_of_hours" title="Book of hours">book of hours</a> is a type of devotional text which was widely popular during the Middle Ages. They are the most common type of surviving medieval <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscripts" class="mw-redirect" title="Illuminated manuscripts">illuminated manuscripts</a>. Each book of hours contain a similar collection of texts, <a href="/wiki/Christian_prayer" title="Christian prayer">prayers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psalms#The_Psalms_in_Christian_worship" title="Psalms">psalms</a> but decoration can vary between each and each example. Many have minimal illumination, often restricted to ornamented <a href="/wiki/Initials" class="mw-redirect" title="Initials">initials</a>, but books of hours made for wealthier patrons can be extremely extravagant with full-page <a href="/wiki/Miniature_(illuminated_manuscript)" title="Miniature (illuminated manuscript)">miniatures</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liturgical_books_and_calendars">Liturgical books and calendars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuscript&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Liturgical books and calendars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with Bibles, large numbers of manuscripts made in the Middle Ages were revieved in Church<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="received? revived? reviewed? (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Due to the complex church system of rituals and worship these books were the most elegantly written and finely decorated of all medieval manuscripts. Liturgical books usually came in two varieties. Those used during mass and those for divine office.<sup id="cite_ref-Intro_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intro-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Almost all medieval calendars give each day's date according to the Roman method of reckoning time. In the Roman system, each month had three fixed points known as Kalends (Kal), the Nons and the Ides. The Nones fell on the fifth of the month in January, February, April, June, August, September, November and December, but on the seventh of the month in March, May, July and October. The Ides fell on the thirteenth in those months in which the Nones fell on the fifth, and the fifteenth in the other four months. All other days were dated by the number of days by which they preceded one of those fixed points.<sup id="cite_ref-Intro_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intro-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scripts">Scripts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manuscript&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scripts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Codex_Manesse_193r_-_Albrecht_von_Rapperswil.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Codex_Manesse_193r_-_Albrecht_von_Rapperswil.jpg/220px-Codex_Manesse_193r_-_Albrecht_von_Rapperswil.jpg" width="220" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Codex_Manesse_193r_-_Albrecht_von_Rapperswil.jpg/330px-Codex_Manesse_193r_-_Albrecht_von_Rapperswil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Codex_Manesse_193r_-_Albrecht_von_Rapperswil.jpg/440px-Codex_Manesse_193r_-_Albrecht_von_Rapperswil.jpg 2x" data-file-width="636" data-file-height="849" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Codex_Manesse_193r_-_Albrecht_von_Rapperswil.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Merovingian_script" title="Merovingian script">Merovingian script</a>, or "Luxeuil minuscule", is named after an abbey in Western France, the <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Luxeuil" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbey of Luxeuil">Luxeuil Abbey</a>, founded by the Irish missionary St <a href="/wiki/Columba" title="Columba">Columba</a> ca. 590.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Caroline_minuscule" class="mw-redirect" title="Caroline minuscule">Caroline minuscule</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">calligraphic script</a> developed as a writing standard in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> so that the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> could be easily recognized by the literate class from different regions. It was used in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> between approximately 800 and 1200. Codices, classical and Christian texts, and educational material were written in Carolingian minuscule throughout the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian Renaissance</a>. The script developed into blackletter and became obsolete, though its revival in the Italian renaissance forms the basis of more recent scripts.<sup id="cite_ref-Intro_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intro-11">[11]</a></sup> In <i>Introduction to Manuscript Studies</i>, Clemens and Graham associate the beginning of this text coming from the Abby of Saint-Martin at <a href="/wiki/Tours" title="Tours">Tours</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Intro_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Intro-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Caroline Minuscule arrived in England in the second half of the 10th century. Its adoption there, replacing Insular script, was encouraged by the importation of continental European manuscripts by Saints Dunstan, Aethelwold, and Oswald. This script spread quite rapidly, being employed in many English centres for copying Latin texts. English scribes adapted the Carolingian script, giving it proportion and legibility. This new revision of the Caroline Minuscule was called English Protogothic Bookhand. Another script that is derived from the Caroline Minuscule was the German Protogothic Bookhand. It originated in southern Germany during the second half of the 12th century.<sup id="cite_ref-.E2.80.9Cscript_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-.E2.80.9Cscript-17">[17]</a></sup> All the individual letters are Caroline; but just as with English Protogothic Bookhand it evolved. This can be seen most notably in the arm of the letter h. It has a hairline that tapers out by curving to the left. When first read the German Protogothic h looks like the German Protogothic b.<sup id="cite_ref-manu_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manu-18">[18]</a></sup> Many more scripts sprang out of the German Protogothic Bookhand. After those came Bastard Anglicana, which is best described as: lipi = pandu lipi pura lipi vignayani</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The coexistence in the Gothic period of formal hands employed for the copying of books and cursive scripts used for documentary purposes eventually resulted in cross-fertilization between these two fundamentally different writing styles. Notably, scribes began to upgrade some of the cursive scripts. A script that has been thus formalized is known as a <i>bastard</i> script (whereas a bookhand that has had cursive elements fused onto it is known as a hybrid script). The advantage of such a script was that it could be written more quickly than a pure bookhand; it thus recommended itself to scribes in a period when demand for books was increasing and authors were tending to write longer texts. In England during the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, many books were written in the script known as Bastard Anglicana.</p>
</blockquote>

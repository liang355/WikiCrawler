			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Harvard Board of Overseers</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Harvard Board of Overseers</b> (more formally <b>The Honorable and Reverend the Board of Overseers</b>) is one of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>'s two governing boards. Although its function is more consultative and less hands-on than the <a href="/wiki/President_and_Fellows_of_Harvard_College" title="President and Fellows of Harvard College">President and Fellows of Harvard College</a>, the Board of Overseers is sometimes referred to as the "senior" governing board because its formation predates the fellows' 1650 incorporation.</p>
<p>Today there are 30 overseers, all directly elected by <a href="/wiki/Alumni" class="mw-redirect" title="Alumni">alumni</a>; at one point the board was <a href="/wiki/Self-perpetuating" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-perpetuating">self-perpetuating</a>. Originally the overseers included, <i><a href="/wiki/Ex_officio_member" title="Ex officio member">ex officio</a></i>, the public officials and <a href="/wiki/Puritans" title="Puritans">Puritan</a> clergy of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a> and the neighboring towns (hence the "honorable and reverend" of the title). Today, the President and the Treasurer of Harvard are <i>ex officio</i> members of the Board.</p>
<p>Each year, Harvard alumni elect five new overseers to serve six-year terms. Overseer candidates are nominated by the Harvard Alumni Association (HAA), and those not nominated by the HAA (petition candidates) must gather signatures from Harvard alumni to appear on the ballot.</p>
<p>Ambassador <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph P. Kennedy, Sr.">Joseph P. Kennedy, Sr.</a> quipped famously of the election of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, his son, to the Board in 1957: "Now I know his religion won't keep him out of the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>. If an <a href="/wiki/Irish_Catholic" title="Irish Catholic">Irish Catholic</a> can get elected as an Overseer at Harvard, he can get elected to anything."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Board_of_Overseers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the Harvard website, the Board of Overseers complements the work of the President and Fellows (also known as the Corporation):<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Drawing on the wide-ranging experience and expertise of its members, the Board exerts broad influence over the University’s strategic directions, provides counsel to the University leadership on priorities and plans, and has the power of consent to certain actions of the Corporation. The Board’s chief functions include superintendence of the visitation process, the principal mechanism for periodic external review of the quality and direction of the University’s schools, departments, and selected other programs and activities. The Board carries out this responsibility largely through the operation of more than fifty visiting committees, whose work is overseen by and reported to the Board.</p>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Petition_candidates">Petition candidates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard_Board_of_Overseers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Petition candidates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the late-1980s, a group calling for a withdrawal of Harvard's investments in Apartheid South Africa helped nominate petition candidates for overseer elections. Known as the Harvard-Radcliffe Alumni Against Apartheid (HRAAA), this group supported the first petition candidate to win an overseer's seat.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The HRAAA also backed South African Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Desmond_Tutu" title="Desmond Tutu">Desmond Tutu</a> in his successful bid to join the board in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Since Tutu's election in 1989 (and the altering of election rules<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>), no petition candidate has been successful, including <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>, who lost in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

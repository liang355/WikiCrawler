<p><b>Aschaffenburg</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[aˈʃafn̩bʊɐ̯k]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Aschaffenburg.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/18/Aschaffenburg.ogg" class="internal" title="Aschaffenburg.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a town in northwest <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The town of Aschaffenburg is not considered part of the <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg_(district)" title="Aschaffenburg (district)">district of Aschaffenburg</a>, but is the administrative seat.</p>
<p>Aschaffenburg belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Mainz">Archbishopric of Mainz</a> for more than 800 years. The town is located at the westernmost border of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Franconia" title="Lower Franconia">Lower Franconia</a> and separated from the central and eastern part of the <i><a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">Regierungsbezirk</a></i> (administrative region) by the <a href="/wiki/Spessart" title="Spessart">Spessart</a> hills, whereas it opens towards the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt/Rhine-Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt/Rhine-Main">Rhine-Main plain</a> in the west and north-west. Therefore, the inhabitants speak neither Bavarian nor East Franconian but rather a local version of <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Franconian" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine Franconian">Rhine Franconian</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town is located on both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> in the southwest part of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, 41 kilometers (25&#160;mi) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>. In the western part of the municipal territory, the smaller <a href="/wiki/Aschaff" title="Aschaff">Aschaff</a> flows into the Main. The region is also known as <i>Bayerischer Untermain</i> ("Bavarian Lower Main"). Aschaffenburg lies in the far northwest of the state of Bavaria, close to the border to the state of <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The climate is continental, typically with warm, dry summers and cold, damp winters. Aschaffenburg usually receives less snowfall during the winter than the nearby <a href="/wiki/Spessart" title="Spessart">Spessart</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighbouring_communities">Neighbouring communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neighbouring communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following municipalities border Aschaffenburg (clockwise, from the north): <a href="/wiki/Johannesberg,_Bavaria" title="Johannesberg, Bavaria">Johannesberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glattbach" title="Glattbach">Glattbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goldbach,_Bavaria" title="Goldbach, Bavaria">Goldbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haibach,_Lower_Franconia" title="Haibach, Lower Franconia">Haibach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bessenbach" title="Bessenbach">Bessenbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulzbach_am_Main" title="Sulzbach am Main">Sulzbach am Main</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niedernberg" title="Niedernberg">Niedernberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gro%C3%9Fostheim" title="Großostheim">Großostheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockstadt_am_Main" title="Stockstadt am Main">Stockstadt am Main</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mainaschaff" title="Mainaschaff">Mainaschaff</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name <i>Aschaffenburg</i> (<i>Ascaffaburc, Ascapha</i> or <i>Ascaphaburg</i> in the Middle Ages) originally meant "castle at the ash tree river" deriving from the river <i>Aschaff</i> that runs through parts of the town.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-history_to_Middle_Ages">Pre-history to Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pre-history to Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aschaffenburg,_die_Sankt_Peter_und_Alexander_Kirche_DmD-6-61-000-293_poging2_foto10_2016-08-08_20.18.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Aschaffenburg%2C_die_Sankt_Peter_und_Alexander_Kirche_DmD-6-61-000-293_poging2_foto10_2016-08-08_20.18.jpg/220px-Aschaffenburg%2C_die_Sankt_Peter_und_Alexander_Kirche_DmD-6-61-000-293_poging2_foto10_2016-08-08_20.18.jpg" width="220" height="306" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Aschaffenburg%2C_die_Sankt_Peter_und_Alexander_Kirche_DmD-6-61-000-293_poging2_foto10_2016-08-08_20.18.jpg/330px-Aschaffenburg%2C_die_Sankt_Peter_und_Alexander_Kirche_DmD-6-61-000-293_poging2_foto10_2016-08-08_20.18.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Aschaffenburg%2C_die_Sankt_Peter_und_Alexander_Kirche_DmD-6-61-000-293_poging2_foto10_2016-08-08_20.18.jpg/440px-Aschaffenburg%2C_die_Sankt_Peter_und_Alexander_Kirche_DmD-6-61-000-293_poging2_foto10_2016-08-08_20.18.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2762" data-file-height="3840" /></a>

<p>The earliest remains of settlements in the area of Aschaffenburg date from the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a>.</p>
<p>Aschaffenburg was originally a settlement of the <a href="/wiki/Alamanni" class="mw-redirect" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a>. <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Roman legions</a> were stationed here. In c. 700 AD, the <i><a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Cosmography" title="Ravenna Cosmography">Ravenna Cosmography</a></i> names two settlements in region: <i>Uburzis</i> (<a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>) and <i>Ascapha</i> (Aschaffenburg).<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:69</sup></p>
<p>Around 550, the area had been conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, and their <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_the_palace" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayor of the palace"><i>Hausmeier</i></a> built a castle here. In the 8th century, a <a href="/wiki/Benedictine" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> monastery was founded, dedicated to St. Michael, reportedly by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface" title="Saint Boniface">Saint Boniface</a>. This became the <i><a href="/wiki/St._Peter_und_Alexander_(Aschaffenburg)" title="St. Peter und Alexander (Aschaffenburg)">Kollegiatstift St. Peter und Alexander</a></i> in the second half of the 10th century (957). In 869, King <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Younger" title="Louis the Younger">Louis the Younger</a> married <a href="/wiki/Liutgard_of_Saxony_(died_885)" title="Liutgard of Saxony (died 885)">Liutgard of Saxony</a> at Aschaffenburg. She also died here in 885 and was later laid to rest with her daughter Hildegard in the <i>Stiftskirche</i>. <i>Ascaffinburg</i> is mentioned first in 974 in a gift document by <a href="/wiki/Otto_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto II, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto II</a>, in which he gave several villages including <a href="/wiki/Wertheim_am_Main" title="Wertheim am Main">Wertheim am Main</a> and a stretch of forest in the Spessart to the collegiate church.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:69</sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zeitreise_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeitreise-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:56</sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> the town was known as <i>Ascaffaburc</i>, <i>Ascapha</i> or <i>Ascaphaburg</i>. A stone bridge over the Main was reportedly built by Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Willigis" title="Willigis">Willigis</a> in 989, who also made the town his second residence. The town (referred to in 975 as a <i>civitas</i>) was part of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric of Mainz">Archbishopric of Mainz</a> from 982, when <a href="/wiki/Otto_I,_Duke_of_Swabia_and_Bavaria" title="Otto I, Duke of Swabia and Bavaria">Duke Otto</a> died. A <i>Vizedom</i> is mentioned for the first time in 1122 as the top local representative of the Archbishop. In 1292 a synod was held here, and in 1447 an <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichstag (Holy Roman Empire)">imperial diet</a>, preliminary to that of <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, approved a concordat (sometimes called the <i>Aschaffenburg Concordat</i>). In the <a href="/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War" title="German Peasants' War">German Peasants' War</a> (1525), the town backed the losing side.<sup id="cite_ref-Brit_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brit-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zeitreise_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeitreise-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:56–7</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times_through_19th_century">Modern times through 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modern times through 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1552, the late-<a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> castle of Johannisburg was destroyed. It was replaced in 1605-14 by the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance</a> <i>Schloss Johannisburg</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeitreise_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeitreise-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:57</sup> The town suffered greatly during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, being held in turn by the various belligerents. During the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dettingen" title="Battle of Dettingen">Battle of Dettingen</a> (1743), which took place to the north, the town was occupied by French troops. It formed part of the electorate of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop of Mainz">Archbishop of Mainz</a>, and in 1803 was made over to Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Karl_Theodor_Anton_Maria_von_Dalberg" title="Karl Theodor Anton Maria von Dalberg">Karl Theodor von Dalberg</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Aschaffenburg" title="Principality of Aschaffenburg">Principality of Aschaffenburg</a>.</p>
<p>Aschaffenburg was the site of the "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Forstliche_Hochschule_Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Forstliche Hochschule Aschaffenburg (page does not exist)">Forstliche Hochschule Aschaffenburg</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Bavarian_Central_Forest_Academy" class="extiw" title="de:Royal Bavarian Central Forest Academy">de</a>)</span>" ( <i>Königlich Bayerische Centralforstlehranstalt</i> ), established in 1807, "made famous by the researches of Professor Dr <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Ebermayer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ernst Ebermayer (page does not exist)">Ernst Ebermayer</a>." The Academy was "dissolved in 1832, but re-organized under the Ministry of Finance in 1874"; and, as "of 30th March, 1874, united to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Munich" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Munich">University of Munich</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1810, the Principality of Aschaffenburg was merged into the new <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Frankfurt" title="Grand Duchy of Frankfurt">Grand Duchy of Frankfurt</a>, although Dalberg retained Aschaffenburg as his residence. In 1814 the town was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Kingdom of Bavaria</a> by an Austrian-Bavarian treaty. In 1817 it was included within Bavarian <a href="/wiki/Lower_Franconia" title="Lower Franconia">Lower Franconia</a>. From 1840–1848, King <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig I of Bavaria">Ludwig I of Bavaria</a> had a Roman villa built to the west of town. It was named <i><a href="/wiki/Pompejanum" title="Pompejanum">Pompejanum</a></i> after its model, the house of <a href="/wiki/Castor_and_Polydeuces" class="mw-redirect" title="Castor and Polydeuces">Castor and Pollux</a> at <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Flyer_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flyer-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WS-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1866, the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Army" title="Prussian Army">Prussian Army</a> inflicted a severe defeat on the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrians</a> in the vicinity during the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Aschaffenburg was heavily damaged by Allied <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">area bombing</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Johannisburg" title="Schloss Johannisburg">Schloss Johannisburg</a> which was completely restored several years later. The Germans chose to defend Aschaffenburg with particular steadfastness, which resulted in the "Battle of Aschaffenburg" fought 28 March - 3 April 1945. The U.S. <a href="/wiki/45th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="45th Infantry Division (United States)">45th Infantry Division</a> was forced to take the fortified town against stiff German resistance in a series of frontal assaults that involved house-to-house fighting and vicious close combat. The resulting widespread urban destruction was quite severe, as cannon fire was used point-blank to blast through structures.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aschaffenburg_displaced_persons_camps">Aschaffenburg displaced persons camps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Aschaffenburg displaced persons camps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of World War II the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> occupied military facilities formerly used and controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a>. These were converted for use by U.S. military personnel as processing centres for <a href="/wiki/Displaced_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Displaced person">displaced persons</a> at the end of the war.<sup id="cite_ref-usarmygermany_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usarmygermany-9">[9]</a></sup> From 1945 7,000 <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainians</a> were accommodated in four <a href="/wiki/Displaced_persons_camp" title="Displaced persons camp">displaced persons camps</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Artillerie Kaserne</i>&#160;— approx. 2,000 people (1945–1949)<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Bois Brulé Kaserne</i>&#160;— 1,500 people (1946–1949)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>LaGarde Kaserne</i>&#160;— 1,700 people (1945–1949)<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Pionier Kaserne</i>&#160;— 2,000 people (1946–1949)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Two other camps, <i>Alte (old) Kaserne</i> and <i>Jäger Kaserne</i> housed mainly <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithuanians" title="Lithuanians">Lithuanians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (November 2013)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_development">Post-war development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Post-war development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the decades following the war, Aschaffenburg and the surrounding region experienced robust economic prosperity, partially due to its close proximity to <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>.</p>

<p>According to an online 2002 survey in <i><a href="/wiki/Stern_(magazine)" title="Stern (magazine)">Stern</a></i> magazine, [Stern 14/2002], 82 percent of residents living in the <i>Bayerischer Untermain</i> region where Aschaffenburg is located were satisfied with the place where they lived. This was the highest level recorded in the survey, making the region the #1 place to live in Germany, based on several factors including employment opportunities in the region, educational facilities, public services, transportation, recreational options, shopping, cultural facilities/events, climate, etc.</p>
<p>Another survey taken in 2006 by McKinsey, <i>Stern</i> magazine, <a href="/wiki/ZDF" title="ZDF">ZDF</a>, and web.de again showed that Aschaffenburg has one of the highest ratings for quality of life in Germany.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span id="U.S._military_presence_(1945–2007)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._military_presence_.281945.E2.80.932007.29">U.S. military presence (1945–2007)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: U.S. military presence (1945–2007)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aschaffenburg was the location of several United States Army installations throughout the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. After initially taking over the administration of the ex-Wehrmacht installations, which were then used as displaced persons camps, the American presence in the Aschaffenburg military community began after general renovations in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-usarmygermany_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usarmygermany-9">[9]</a></sup> The installation sites were known as <i>Ready Kaserne</i> (previously <i>Artillerie Kaserne</i>), <i>Smith Kaserne</i> (previously <i>LaGarde Kaserne</i>), <i>Graves Kaserne</i> (previously <i>Bois Brulé Kaserne</i>), <i>Fiori Kaserne</i> (previously <i>Pionier Kaserne</i>), and <i>Jaeger Kaserne</i> (previously <i>Jäger Kaserne</i>). These housed armour, infantry, engineer, maintenance and artillery elements of the U.S. Army 3rd Brigade, 3rd Infantry Division and various VII Corps elements including the 9th Engineer Battalion, the 3rd Bn 21st Field Artillery (Honest John), and the 1st Bn 80th Field Artillery (LANCE). Much of the U.S. Army presence in Aschaffenburg ended in 1992 with the ending of the Cold War. The last buildings, which were primarily used for housing, were handed back to the local government in 2007.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013 (latest data available) the <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a> per inhabitant was €68,460. This places the <i>kreisfreie Stadt</i> 7th out of 96 districts (rural and urban) in Bavaria (overall average: €36,691).<sup id="cite_ref-Stat_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Well-known companies in Aschaffenburg are (e.g.): Linde Material Handling, <a href="/wiki/Linde_Hydraulics" title="Linde Hydraulics">Linde Hydraulics</a>, Takata, DPD and redcoon.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shopping">Shopping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Shopping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>City Galerie</i>, opened in 1974 and located in the town center (Stadtmitte), is the largest shopping mall in northern Bavaria. It was one of the first indoor shopping malls to open in Germany. Aschaffenburg also has a pedestrian shopping zone (<i>Fußgängerzone</i> in German) closed to motor vehicles, except for deliveries. It is located in the town center.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">Sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main sights of Aschaffenburg are the <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Johannisburg" title="Schloss Johannisburg">Schloss Johannisburg</a>, built 1605–1614 by Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Johann_Schweikhard_von_Kronberg" title="Johann Schweikhard von Kronberg">Johann Schweikhard von Kronberg</a>, which contains a library with a number of <a href="/wiki/Incunabulum" class="mw-redirect" title="Incunabulum">incunabula</a>, a collection of engravings and paintings; the <a href="/wiki/Pompejanum" title="Pompejanum">Pompejanum</a>, a replica of a Roman town house discovered in <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a> commissioned by King Ludwig I. and opened in 1850; the <a href="/wiki/St._Peter_und_Alexander_(Aschaffenburg)" title="St. Peter und Alexander (Aschaffenburg)">St. Peter und Alexander</a> <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_church" title="Collegiate church">collegiate church</a>, founded in the second half of the 10th century, but dating in the main from the early 12th century on, in which are preserved various monuments by the <a href="/wiki/Vischer_Family_of_Nuremberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Vischer Family of Nuremberg">Vischers</a>, a sarcophagus with the relics of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_the_Virgin" title="Margaret the Virgin">Saint Margaret</a>, and a painting by <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Gr%C3%BCnewald" title="Matthias Grünewald">Matthias Grünewald</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Friars_Minor_Capuchin" title="Order of Friars Minor Capuchin">Capuchin</a> hospital; a theatre, which was formerly a house of the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Order</a>; several mansions of the nobility; and the historical old town. Across the river are the Park and Schloss <a href="/wiki/Sch%C3%B6nbusch_(Aschaffenburg)" title="Schönbusch (Aschaffenburg)">Schönbusch</a>.</p>
<p>The graves of <a href="/wiki/Clemens_Brentano" title="Clemens Brentano">Clemens Brentano</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Christian_Brentano" title="Christian Brentano">Christian Brentano</a> (died 1851) and that of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Heinse" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelm Heinse">Wilhelm Heinse</a> can be found in the <i>Altstadtfriedhof</i> (Old Town Cemetery).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Viktoria_Aschaffenburg" title="Viktoria Aschaffenburg">Viktoria Aschaffenburg</a> is the primary football club. The club was formed on 24 June 1904 out of the merger of FC Aschaffenburg (6 August 1901) and FC Viktoria Aschaffenburg (12 April 1902). It was renamed Sportverein Viktoria 01 Aschaffenburg on 3 June 1906. Their homefield is Stadion am Schönbusch (Schönbusch stadium), a modern stadium located in Nilkheim .</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_football">American football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: American football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1991, the Aschaffenburg Stallions began playing <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> at Schönbusch stadium. A <a href="/wiki/Cheerleader" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheerleader">cheerleader</a> squad also exists.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball_and_softball">Baseball and softball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Baseball and softball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aschaffenburg is also home to the Aschaffenburg Mohawks Baseball and Softball team. The softball team won the 2010 German Championship. The adult baseball team won the 2011 Landesliga South Championship and will play in the highest league of the state Hessen in 2012.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Aschaffenburg is located on <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_3" title="Bundesautobahn 3">Bundesautobahn 3</a> between <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>. The southern terminus of <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_45" title="Bundesautobahn 45">Bundesautobahn 45</a> is located just west of the town. Bundesstrasse B 26 passes through the town. Bundestrasse B 8 used to pass through Aschaffenburg, but has now been rerouted along the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_3" title="Bundesautobahn 3">Bundesautobahn 3</a>. Three road bridges cross the river <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> at Aschaffenburg: <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Ebert" title="Friedrich Ebert">Friedrich Ebert</a> Bridge (a new span opened in 2008), Willigis Bridge and <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a> Bridge. In the 1980s, a road tunnel was constructed under the Schlossplatz to improve traffic flow through the <i>Stadtmitte</i> (town center).</p>
<p>After 43 years of planning and construction, the limited-access <i>Innenstadtring</i> or "inner-town-ring" road was completed in July 2013. It allows motorists to bypass the <i>Stadtmitte</i> (town center) and reduces traffic congestion in Aschaffenburg, which has been a problem in recent years. Another road project called <i>Bahnparallele</i> is currently under construction, with a small section already opened. It is located in the <i>Stadtteil</i> of Damm and runs parallel to the railway tracks.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parking">Parking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Parking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are approximately 7,500 parking spaces in the ten <i>Stadtteile</i> of Aschaffenburg and eight public parking garages.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transport">Public transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Public transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Aschaffenburg has a comprehensive bus network serving the town and surrounding region. The 15 main bus lines which serve the <i>Stadtteile</i> of Aschaffenburg are run by <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stadtwerke_Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stadtwerke Aschaffenburg (page does not exist)">Stadtwerke Aschaffenburg</a></i>. There are several other bus lines which link Aschaffenburg with the surrounding region. Those lines are run by other companies, including the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a>. A new regional bus terminal opened in 2008, adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg_Hauptbahnhof" title="Aschaffenburg Hauptbahnhof">Aschaffenburg Hauptbahnhof</a> (main railway station). The <i>Regionale Omnibusbahnhof Aschaffenburg (ROB)</i> was built to consolidate all of the scattered bus stops in the area around the main railway station into one central location and reduce traffic congestion in the area.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway_stations_and_stops">Railway stations and stops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Railway stations and stops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg_Hauptbahnhof" title="Aschaffenburg Hauptbahnhof">Aschaffenburg Hauptbahnhof</a>/Aschaffenburg Central Station</i> - The station has long-distance <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a> and <a href="/wiki/InterCity" title="InterCity">InterCity</a> services as well as regional connections to neighboring towns and cities. A new, larger station building opened in 2011, replacing a smaller structure built in the 1950s.</li>
<li><i>Aschaffenburg-Hochschule/University of Applied Sciences</i> - The stop, located near Würzburger Straße, opened in 2007 to accommodate students attending nearby schools. It is positioned between Aschaffenburg Hauptbahnhof and Aschaffenburg-Süd/South. There is only a single track platform.</li>
<li><i>Aschaffenburg-Süd/South</i></li>
<li><i>Obernau</i></li>
<li><i>Schönbusch-Nilkheim</i> - The station closed when passenger service on the "Bachgaubahn" railway line ended in 1974.</li>
<li><i>Leider</i> - Freight railway terminal close to Aschaffenburg's port on the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a>. There is no passenger service.</li>
</ul>
<p>All passenger train service is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Harbor">Harbor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Harbor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Aschaffenburg has an active port along the Main in the <i>Stadtteil</i> of Leider. There is railway access to the port. In 2005, 2.8 million tons of cargo passed through the port.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Airports">Airports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Airports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A small <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> airport (<i>Flugplatz Aschaffenburg</i>, ICAO-Code: EDFC) is located in nearby <a href="/wiki/Gro%C3%9Fostheim" title="Großostheim">Großostheim</a>. <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Airport" title="Frankfurt Airport">Frankfurt Airport</a> is located 46 kilometers (29&#160;mi) from Aschaffenburg and offers flights to destinations all over the world. The trip to and from the airport takes about 30 minutes by car or approximately 45 minutes by <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a> train.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">Medical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aschaffenburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Medical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The three primary medical centers in Aschaffenburg are:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Klinikum Aschaffenburg</i>, the main hospital</li>
<li><i>Hofgartenklinik</i></li>
<li><i>Frauenklinik am Ziegelberg</i></li>
</ul>
<p>There are approximately 789 hospital beds, 159 doctors, 68 dentists and 38 pharmacies in the <i>Stadtteile</i> of Aschaffenburg.</p>

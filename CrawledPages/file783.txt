<p><b>Elena Kagan</b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born April 28, 1960)<sup id="cite_ref-whoswho_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-3">[3]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/Associate_Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States">Associate Justice</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a>. Kagan is the Court's fourth female justice.</p>
<p>Kagan was born and raised in New York City. After attending <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a>, she completed federal Court of Appeals and Supreme Court clerkships. She began her career as a professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Law_School" title="University of Chicago Law School">University of Chicago Law School</a>, leaving to serve as Associate <a href="/wiki/White_House_Counsel" title="White House Counsel">White House Counsel</a>, and later as policy adviser, under <a href="/wiki/US_President" class="mw-redirect" title="US President">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Clinton</a>. After a nomination to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_D.C._Circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit</a>, which expired without action, she became a professor at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a> and was later named its first female <a href="/wiki/Dean_(education)" title="Dean (education)">dean</a>.</p>
<p>In 2009, Kagan became the first female <a href="/wiki/Solicitor_General_of_the_United_States" title="Solicitor General of the United States">Solicitor General</a>. On May 10, 2010, President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> <a href="/wiki/Elena_Kagan_Supreme_Court_nomination" title="Elena Kagan Supreme Court nomination">nominated</a> her to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> to fill the vacancy arising from the impending retirement of Justice <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Stevens" title="John Paul Stevens">John Paul Stevens</a>, and she resigned her position as Solicitor General in August 2010 upon her confirmation to the Supreme Court.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After confirmation, Kagan was sworn in on August 7, 2010, by <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/John_G._Roberts" class="mw-redirect" title="John G. Roberts">John G. Roberts</a> in a private ceremony. Kagan's formal investiture ceremony before a special sitting of the United States Supreme Court took place on October 1, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Kagan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kagan was born in New York City, the middle of three children, on the city's <a href="/wiki/Upper_West_Side" title="Upper West Side">Upper West Side</a>. Her father, Robert Kagan, was an attorney, and her mother, Gloria (Gittelman) Kagan, taught at <a href="/wiki/Hunter_College_Elementary_School" title="Hunter College Elementary School">Hunter College Elementary School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Kagan's two brothers are public school teachers.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kagan and her family lived in a third-floor apartment at <a href="/wiki/West_End_Avenue" class="mw-redirect" title="West End Avenue">West End Avenue</a> and 75th Street<sup id="cite_ref-confidence-and-canniness_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-confidence-and-canniness-9">[9]</a></sup> and attended <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Square_Synagogue" title="Lincoln Square Synagogue">Lincoln Square Synagogue</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kagan-tested-faith_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kagan-tested-faith-2">[2]</a></sup> Kagan was independent and strong-willed in her youth and, according to a former law partner, clashed with her <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox</a> <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbi</a> over aspects of her <a href="/wiki/Bat_mitzvah" class="mw-redirect" title="Bat mitzvah">bat mitzvah</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-confidence-and-canniness_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-confidence-and-canniness-9">[9]</a></sup> "She had strong opinions about what a bat mitzvah should be like, which didn't parallel the wishes of the rabbi," said her former colleague. "But they finally worked it out. She negotiated with the rabbi and came to a conclusion that satisfied everybody." Kagan's rabbi, <a href="/wiki/Shlomo_Riskin" title="Shlomo Riskin">Shlomo Riskin</a>, had never performed a ritual bat mitzvah before.<sup id="cite_ref-kagan-tested-faith_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kagan-tested-faith-2">[2]</a></sup> "Elena Kagan felt very strongly that there should be ritual bat mitzvah in the synagogue, no less important than the ritual bar mitzvah. This was really the first formal bat mitzvah we had," said Riskin. Kagan asked to read from the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Weekly_Torah_portion" title="Weekly Torah portion">Saturday morning</a> but ultimately read on a <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Friday night</a>, May 18, 1973, from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ruth" title="Book of Ruth">Book of Ruth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kagan-tested-faith_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kagan-tested-faith-2">[2]</a></sup> Today, she identifies with <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Judaism" title="Conservative Judaism">Conservative Judaism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kagan-tested-faith_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kagan-tested-faith-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Childhood friend Margaret Raymond recalled that Kagan was a teenage smoker but not a partier. On Saturday nights, she and Kagan "were more apt to sit on the steps of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> and talk."<sup id="cite_ref-confidence-and-canniness_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-confidence-and-canniness-9">[9]</a></sup> Kagan also loved literature and re-read <a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pride_and_Prejudice" title="Pride and Prejudice">Pride and Prejudice</a></i> every year.<sup id="cite_ref-confidence-and-canniness_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-confidence-and-canniness-9">[9]</a></sup> In her <a href="/wiki/Hunter_College_High_School" title="Hunter College High School">Hunter College High School</a> yearbook of 1977, Kagan was pictured in a judge's robe and holding a <a href="/wiki/Gavel" title="Gavel">gavel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Next to her photo was a quote from former <a href="/wiki/Associate_Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/Felix_Frankfurter" title="Felix Frankfurter">Felix Frankfurter</a>: "Government is itself an art, one of the subtlest of arts."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Kagan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After graduating from high school, Kagan attended <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, where she earned an <a href="/wiki/Bachelors_of_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Bachelors of Arts">A.B.</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Summa_cum_laude" class="mw-redirect" title="Summa cum laude">summa cum laude</a></i> in history in 1981. Among the subjects she studied was the socialist movement in New York City in the early 20th century. She wrote a senior thesis under historian <a href="/wiki/Sean_Wilentz" title="Sean Wilentz">Sean Wilentz</a> titled "To the Final Conflict: Socialism in New York City, 1900–1933". In it she wrote, "Through its own internal feuding, then, the SP [Socialist Party] exhausted itself forever. The story is a sad but also a chastening one for those who, more than half a century after socialism's decline, still wish to change America."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Wilentz insists that she did not mean to defend socialism, noting that she "Was interested in it. To study something is not to endorse it."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Wilentz called Kagan "one of the foremost legal minds in the country, she is still the witty, engaging, down-to-earth person I proudly remember from her undergraduate days."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an undergraduate, Kagan also served as editorial chair of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Princetonian" title="The Daily Princetonian">The Daily Princetonian</a></i>. Along with eight other students (including <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Spitzer" title="Eliot Spitzer">Eliot Spitzer</a>, who was student body president at the time), Kagan penned the Declaration of the Campaign for a Democratic University, which called for "a fundamental restructuring of university governance" and condemned Princeton's administration for making decisions "behind closed doors".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kaganharvardlaw.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Kaganharvardlaw.png/220px-Kaganharvardlaw.png" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Kaganharvardlaw.png/330px-Kaganharvardlaw.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Kaganharvardlaw.png/440px-Kaganharvardlaw.png 2x" data-file-width="672" data-file-height="456" /></a>

Kagan graduates from Harvard Law School in 1986.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1980, Kagan received Princeton's Daniel M. Sachs Class of 1960 Graduating Scholarship,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> one of the highest general awards conferred by the university, which enabled her to study at <a href="/wiki/Worcester_College,_Oxford" title="Worcester College, Oxford">Worcester College, Oxford</a>. She earned a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Philosophy" title="Master of Philosophy">Master of Philosophy</a> in Politics at Oxford in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> She received a <a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">Juris Doctor</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Magna_cum_laude" class="mw-redirect" title="Magna cum laude">magna cum laude</a></i>, at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a> in 1986, where she was supervisory editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_Review" title="Harvard Law Review">Harvard Law Review</a></i>. Friend <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Toobin" title="Jeffrey Toobin">Jeffrey Toobin</a> recalled that Kagan "stood out from the start as one with a formidable mind. She's good with people. At the time, the law school was a politically charged and divided place. She navigated the factions with ease, and won the respect of everyone."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Kagan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kagan was a <a href="/wiki/Law_clerk" title="Law clerk">law clerk</a> for Judge <a href="/wiki/Abner_J._Mikva" title="Abner J. Mikva">Abner J. Mikva</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_District_of_Columbia_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit</a> in 1987 and for Justice <a href="/wiki/Thurgood_Marshall" title="Thurgood Marshall">Thurgood Marshall</a> of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> in 1988. Marshall nicknamed the 5 foot 3 inch Kagan "Shorty".<sup id="cite_ref-confidence-and-canniness_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-confidence-and-canniness-9">[9]</a></sup> She later entered private practice as an associate at the Washington, D.C., <a href="/wiki/Law_firm" title="Law firm">law firm</a> of <a href="/wiki/Williams_%26_Connolly" title="Williams &amp; Connolly">Williams &amp; Connolly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-whoswho_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kagan joined the faculty of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Law_School" title="University of Chicago Law School">University of Chicago Law School</a> as an assistant professor in 1991 and became a tenured professor of law in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> While at the University of Chicago, she published a law review article on the regulation of <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First Amendment</a> <a href="/wiki/Hate_speech" title="Hate speech">hate speech</a> in the wake of the Supreme Court's ruling in <i><a href="/wiki/R.A.V._v._City_of_St._Paul" title="R.A.V. v. City of St. Paul">R.A.V. v. City of St. Paul</a></i>;<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> an article discussing the significance of governmental motive in regulating speech;<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and a review of a book by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_L._Carter" title="Stephen L. Carter">Stephen L. Carter</a> discussing the judicial confirmation process.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In the first article, which became highly influential, Kagan argued that the Supreme Court should examine governmental motives when deciding First Amendment cases,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and analyzed historic <a href="/wiki/Draft-card_burning" title="Draft-card burning">draft-card burning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flag_desecration" title="Flag desecration">flag burning</a> cases in light of free speech arguments.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to her colleagues, Kagan's students complimented and admired her from the beginning, and she was granted tenure "despite the reservations of some colleagues who thought she had not published enough."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="White_House_and_judicial_nomination">White House and judicial nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Kagan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: White House and judicial nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kagan served as Associate White House Counsel for <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> from 1995–1996, beginning when her mentor Judge Mikva served as White House Counsel. From 1997–1999 she worked as Deputy Assistant to the President for Domestic Policy and Deputy Director of the Domestic Policy Council. Kagan worked on topics like budget appropriations, campaign finance reform, and social welfare issues. Her work is cataloged in the <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Presidential_Center" title="Clinton Presidential Center">Clinton Library</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Kagan co-authored a 1997 memo urging Clinton to support a ban on <a href="/wiki/Late-term_abortion" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-term abortion">late-term abortions</a>: "We recommend that you endorse the <a href="/wiki/Tom_Daschle" title="Tom Daschle">Daschle</a> amendment in order to sustain your credibility on HR 1122 and prevent Congress from overriding your veto."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 17, 1999, Clinton nominated Kagan to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_District_of_Columbia_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit">U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit</a>, to replace <a href="/wiki/James_L._Buckley" title="James L. Buckley">James L. Buckley</a>, who had taken <a href="/wiki/Senior_status" title="Senior status">senior status</a> in 1996. The <a href="/wiki/Senate_Judiciary_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate Judiciary Committee">Senate Judiciary Committee</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Republican_party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Republican party (United States)">Republican</a> Chairman <a href="/wiki/Orrin_Hatch" title="Orrin Hatch">Orrin Hatch</a> scheduled no hearing, effectively ending her nomination. When Clinton's term ended, her nomination to the D.C. Circuit Court lapsed, as did the nomination of fellow Clinton nominee <a href="/wiki/Allen_Snyder_(lawyer)" title="Allen Snyder (lawyer)">Allen Snyder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_academia">Return to academia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Kagan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Return to academia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After her service in the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> and her lapsed judicial nomination, Kagan returned to academia in 1999. She initially sought to return to the University of Chicago Law School. However, she had given up her tenured position during her extended stint in the <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinton Administration">Clinton Administration</a>. Thus, she needed to be rehired and the school chose not to do so; reportedly due to doubts about her commitment to academia.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Kagan quickly found a position as a visiting professor at Harvard Law School. While at Harvard, she authored a law review article on <a href="/wiki/United_States_administrative_law" title="United States administrative law">United States administrative law</a>, including the role of aiding the President of the United States in formulating and influencing federal administrative and regulatory law, which was honored as the year's top scholarly article by the <a href="/wiki/American_Bar_Association" title="American Bar Association">American Bar Association</a>'s Section on Administrative Law and Regulatory Practice, and is being developed into a book to be published by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Press" title="Harvard University Press">Harvard University Press</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elena_Kagan_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Elena_Kagan_2.jpg/220px-Elena_Kagan_2.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Elena_Kagan_2.jpg/330px-Elena_Kagan_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Elena_Kagan_2.jpg/440px-Elena_Kagan_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3504" data-file-height="2336" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Elena_Kagan_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kagan as Dean of Harvard Law School</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2001, she was named a full professor and in 2003 was named Dean of the Law School by Harvard University President <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">Lawrence Summers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Berman2008_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berman2008-31">[31]</a></sup> She succeeded <a href="/wiki/Robert_C._Clark" title="Robert C. Clark">Robert C. Clark</a>, who had served as dean for over a decade. The focus of her tenure was on improving student satisfaction. Efforts included constructing new facilities and reforming the first-year curriculum as well as aesthetic changes and creature comforts, such as free morning coffee. She has been credited for employing a consensus-building leadership style, which surmounted the school's previous ideological discord.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boston1_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston1-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-washburn_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washburn-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dean_Kagan.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/73/Dean_Kagan.jpg/220px-Dean_Kagan.jpg" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/73/Dean_Kagan.jpg/330px-Dean_Kagan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/73/Dean_Kagan.jpg 2x" data-file-width="425" data-file-height="427" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dean_Kagan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Kagan's official portrait as Dean of Harvard Law School</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In her capacity as dean, Kagan inherited a <span class="nowrap">$400 million</span> capital campaign, "Setting the Standard", in 2003. It ended in 2008 with a record breaking <span class="nowrap">$476 million</span> raised, 19% more than the original goal.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Kagan made a number of prominent new hires, increasing the size of the faculty considerably. Her coups included hiring legal scholar <a href="/wiki/Cass_Sunstein" title="Cass Sunstein">Cass Sunstein</a> away from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Lessig" title="Lawrence Lessig">Lawrence Lessig</a> away from <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Law_School" title="Stanford Law School">Stanford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> She also broke a logjam on conservative hires by bringing in scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Jack_Goldsmith" title="Jack Goldsmith">Jack Goldsmith</a>, who had been serving in the <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="George W. Bush administration">Bush administration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-boston1_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston1-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Kevin_K._Washburn" title="Kevin K. Washburn">Kevin Washburn</a>, then-dean of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Mexico_School_of_Law" title="University of New Mexico School of Law">University of New Mexico School of Law</a>, Kagan transformed Harvard Law School from a harsh environment for students to one that was much more student-centric.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>During her deanship, Kagan upheld a decades-old policy barring military recruiters from the Office of Career Services because she felt that the military's <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_ask,_don%27t_tell" title="Don't ask, don't tell">"Don't Ask, Don't Tell"</a> policy discriminated against gays and lesbians. According to <a href="/wiki/Campus_Progress" class="mw-redirect" title="Campus Progress">Campus Progress</a>,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>As dean, Kagan supported a lawsuit intended to overturn the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Amendment" title="Solomon Amendment">Solomon Amendment</a> so military recruiters might be banned from the grounds of schools like Harvard. When a federal appeals court ruled <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">The Pentagon</a> could not withhold funds, she banned the military from Harvard's campus once again. The case was challenged in the Supreme Court, which ruled the military could indeed require schools to allow recruiters if they wanted to receive federal money. Kagan, though she allowed the military back, simultaneously urged students to demonstrate against Don't Ask, Don't Tell.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In October 2003, Kagan transmitted an e-mail to students and faculty deploring that military recruiters had shown up on campus in violation of the school's anti-discrimination policy. It read, "This action causes me deep distress. I abhor the military's discriminatory recruitment policy." She also wrote that it was "a profound wrong—a moral injustice of the first order."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 2005 through 2008, Kagan was a member of the Research Advisory Council of the <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> Global Markets Institute and received a $10,000 <a href="/wiki/Stipend" title="Stipend">stipend</a> for her service in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>By early 2007, Kagan was a finalist for the presidency of Harvard University as a whole after <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">Lawrence Summers</a>' resignation the previous year, but lost out to <a href="/wiki/Drew_Gilpin_Faust" title="Drew Gilpin Faust">Drew Gilpin Faust</a>. She was reportedly disappointed not to be chosen, and supportive law school students threw her a party to express their appreciation for her leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solicitor_General">Solicitor General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Kagan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solicitor General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 5, 2009, <a href="/wiki/President-elect_of_the_United_States" title="President-elect of the United States">President-elect</a> <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> announced he would nominate Kagan to be <a href="/wiki/Solicitor_General_of_the_United_States" title="Solicitor General of the United States">Solicitor General</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Before this appointment she had never argued a case before any court.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> At least two previous solicitors general, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bork" title="Robert Bork">Robert Bork</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Starr" class="mw-redirect" title="Kenneth Starr">Kenneth Starr</a>, also had no previous Supreme Court appearances, though Starr was a judge on the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_District_of_Columbia_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit</a> before becoming Solicitor General.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kagan was confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> on March 19, 2009, by a vote of 61 to 31,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> becoming the first woman to hold the position. She made her first appearance before the Supreme Court on September 9, 2009, in <i><a href="/wiki/Citizens_United_v._Federal_Election_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission">Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_Center" title="First Amendment Center">First Amendment Center</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cato_Institute" title="Cato Institute">Cato Institute</a> later expressed concern over arguments Kagan advanced as a part of her role as Solicitor General. For example, during her time as Solicitor General, Kagan prepared a brief defending a law later ruled unconstitutional that criminalized depictions of <a href="/wiki/Animal_cruelty" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal cruelty">animal cruelty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> During her confirmation hearing, she said that "there is no federal constitutional right to <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>." Also during her confirmation hearing, she was asked about the <a href="/wiki/Defense_of_Marriage_Act" title="Defense of Marriage Act">Defense of Marriage Act</a>, pursuant to which states were not required to recognize same-sex marriages originating in other states. Kagan indicated that she would defend the act "if there is any reasonable basis to do so".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supreme_Court">Supreme Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Kagan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Supreme Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomination">Nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elena_Kagan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Elena_Kagan_Supreme_Court_nomination" title="Elena Kagan Supreme Court nomination">Elena Kagan Supreme Court nomination</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the Senate's roll call vote on confirmation, see <a href="/wiki/Elena_Kagan_Supreme_Court_nomination#Full_Senate" title="Elena Kagan Supreme Court nomination">Elena Kagan Supreme Court nomination §&#160;Full Senate</a>.</div>
<p>Prior to the election of President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>, Kagan was the subject of media speculation regarding her potential to be nominated to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a> if a <a href="/wiki/U.S._Democratic_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Democratic Party">Democratic</a> president were elected in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> This speculation increased after the retirement announcement of Associate Justice <a href="/wiki/David_H._Souter" class="mw-redirect" title="David H. Souter">David H. Souter</a>, effective at the start of the Court's summer 2009 recess.<sup id="cite_ref-Souter_Resignation_letter-2009-05-01_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Souter_Resignation_letter-2009-05-01-58">[58]</a></sup> Upon the death of Justice <a href="/wiki/Antonin_Scalia" title="Antonin Scalia">Antonin Scalia</a> in 2016, CNN political commentator and former senior advisor to Obama <a href="/wiki/David_Axelrod" title="David Axelrod">David Axelrod</a> reported that Scalia had personally recommended Kagan as an adequate replacement for Souter.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was speculated that her position as Solicitor General would increase Kagan's chances for nomination, since Solicitors General have been considered potential nominees to the Supreme Court in the past. On May 13, 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> reported that Obama was considering Kagan, among others, for possible appointment to the United States Supreme Court.<sup id="cite_ref-AP_source:_Obama_has_more_than_6_people_for_court_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP_source:_Obama_has_more_than_6_people_for_court-60">[60]</a></sup> On May 26, 2009, however, Obama announced that he was nominating <a href="/wiki/Sonia_Sotomayor" title="Sonia Sotomayor">Sonia Sotomayor</a> to the post.<sup id="cite_ref-NPRSouter_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPRSouter-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<p>On April 9, 2010, Justice <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Stevens" title="John Paul Stevens">John Paul Stevens</a> announced that he would retire at the start of the Court's summer 2010 recess, triggering new speculation about Kagan's potential nomination to the bench.<sup id="cite_ref-NPRStevens_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPRStevens-62">[62]</a></sup> In a <i>Fresh Dialogues</i> interview, <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Toobin" title="Jeffrey Toobin">Jeffrey Toobin</a>, a Supreme Court analyst and Kagan's friend and law school classmate,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> speculated that Kagan would likely be President Obama's nominee, describing her as "very much an Obama type person, a moderate Democrat, a consensus builder."<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> This possibility alarmed many liberals and progressives, who worried that "replacing Stevens with Kagan risks moving the Court to the right, perhaps substantially to the right."<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Kagan's name was mentioned as a possible replacement for Justice Stevens, the <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">New York Times</a></i> noted that she "has supported assertions of <a href="/wiki/Executive_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive power">executive power</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> This view of vast executive power has caused some commentators to fear that she would reverse the majority in favor of protecting <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> on the Supreme Court were she to replace Stevens.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 10, 2010, Obama nominated Kagan to the Supreme Court to fill the vacancy left by Justice Stevens.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The deans of over one-third of the country's law schools, sixty-nine people in total, endorsed Kagan's nomination in an <a href="/wiki/Open_letter" title="Open letter">open letter</a> in early June. It lauded what it considered her coalition-building skills and "understanding of both doctrine and policy" as well as her written record of legal analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-open_letter_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-open_letter-69">[69]</a></sup></p>

<p>The confirmation hearings began June 28. Kagan's testimony and her answers to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_the_Judiciary" title="United States Senate Committee on the Judiciary">Senate Judiciary Committee's</a> questions on July 20 were uneventful, containing no new revelations about her character or background. <a href="/wiki/Arlen_Specter" title="Arlen Specter">Arlen Specter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> cited an article Kagan had published in the <i><a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Law_Review" title="University of Chicago Law Review">Chicago Law Review</a></i> in 1995, criticizing the evasiveness of Supreme Court nominees in their hearings.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Kagan, noted Specter, was now practicing that very evasiveness.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> On July 20, 2010, the Senate Judiciary Committee voted 13–6 to recommend Kagan's confirmation to the full Senate. On August 5 the full Senate confirmed her nomination by a vote of 63–37.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The voting was largely on party lines, with five Republicans (Richard Lugar, Judd Gregg, Lindsey Graham, Susan Collins, and Olympia Snowe) supporting her and one Democrat (Ben Nelson) opposing. The Senate's two independents voted in favor of confirmation. She was sworn in by <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Roberts" title="John Roberts">John Roberts</a> on Saturday August 7, in a private ceremony.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kagan is the first justice appointed without any prior experience as a judge since <a href="/wiki/William_Rehnquist" title="William Rehnquist">William Rehnquist</a> in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> She is the fourth female justice in the Court's history (and, for the first time, part of a Court with three female justices) and the eighth <a href="/wiki/List_of_Jewish_United_States_Supreme_Court_justices" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Jewish United States Supreme Court justices">Jewish justice</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-isjewish_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isjewish-77">[77]</a></sup> making three of the nine current justices Jewish.</p>

<p>Kagan's first opinion, <i><a href="/wiki/Ransom_v._FIA_Card_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Ransom v. FIA Card Services">Ransom v. FIA Card Services</a></i>, was filed on January 11, 2011. In an 8–1 decision, Kagan found that an individual declaring bankruptcy could not count expenses for a car he had paid off in his "applicable monthly expenses".<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Schweinfurt</b> (in German literally '<a href="/wiki/Swine" class="mw-redirect" title="Swine">swine</a> <a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">ford</a>') is a city in the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Franconia" title="Lower Franconia">Lower Franconia</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> in Germany on the right bank of the navigable <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> River, which is spanned by several bridges here, 27&#160;km northeast of <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schweinfurt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city was first documented in the year 790,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-2">[2]</a></sup> although as early as 740 a settlement called <i>Villa Suinfurde</i> was mentioned. In the 10th century Schweinfurt was the seat of a <a href="/wiki/Margrave" title="Margrave">margraviate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-2">[2]</a></sup> After the defeat of count <a href="/wiki/Henry_of_Schweinfurt" title="Henry of Schweinfurt">Henry of Schweinfurt</a> in 1002/1003, in the feud against King <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry II of Germany">Henry II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Germany</a>, his family lost its leading position in the town. In the first half of the 13th century Schweinfurt expanded to become a proper city with city wall, towers and city gates. At that time the Nikolaus hospital was founded, a mint was established and construction work on the Saint Johannis church began.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Around 1250 Schweinfurt was totally destroyed during a feud between the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Count_of_Henneberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Count of Henneberg (page does not exist)">Count of Henneberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishop">Prince-Bishop</a> of <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishopric of Würzburg">Würzburg</a>. In the following years it was reconstructed. A document from 1282 signed by <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_I_of_Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Rudolf I of Habsburg">Rudolf I of Habsburg</a> states that Schweinfurt was a <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">free city</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. At least since then the coat of arms of Schweinfurt has been an imperial <a href="/wiki/Eagle_(heraldry)" title="Eagle (heraldry)">white eagle</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1309 the city was given to the Count of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Henneberg" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Henneberg">Henneberg</a>, but in the 1360s the city regained its independence and joined the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_League" title="Swabian League">Swabian–Franconian Confederation</a>. In 1397 <a href="/wiki/Wenzel_IV_of_Bohemia" class="mw-redirect" title="Wenzel IV of Bohemia">King Wenzel</a> entitled the town to utilize the River Main, and in 1436–1437 Schweinfurt acquired the village of Oberndorf, as well as the Teutonic Order Fort on the Peterstirn (the site of a former castle, derived from the monastery name <i>stella Petri</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) and a small piece of land – including the villages of Zell and Weipoltshausen. Some years later there was the first uprising of Schweinfurt's citizens against the town council, followed by a second in 1513–1514. This time the issuing of a constitution was allowed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The city joined <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> in 1542. Schweinfurt was again destroyed in the course of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Margrave_War" title="Second Margrave War">Second Margrave War</a>, in 1554. The years up to 1615 were spent by the citizens for its reconstruction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Schweinfurt joined the Protestant Union in 1609. In the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> it was occupied by <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus" class="mw-redirect" title="Gustavus Adolphus">Gustavus Adolphus</a>, who erected fortifications, the remains of which are still extant.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1652 the four doctors Johann Laurentius Bausch, Johann Michael Fehr, Georg Balthasar Wolfahrt and Balthasar Metzger founded the <i>Academia Curiosorum</i> in Schweinfurt, which is known today as the German Academy of Life Scientists, "<a href="/wiki/German_Academy_of_Sciences_Leopoldina" class="mw-redirect" title="German Academy of Sciences Leopoldina">Leopoldina</a>".</p>
<p>At some point Schweinfurt became a predominantly Roman Catholic city owing to migration from the surrounding Catholic territories, only again to receive a large section of Lutheran refugees/<a href="/wiki/Expellees" class="mw-redirect" title="Expellees">expellees</a> after 1945 from Germany east of the <a href="/wiki/Oder-Neisse_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Oder-Neisse line">Oder-Neisse line</a>. The latest addition to the Lutheran churches in Schweinfurt arrived during the last years of the Soviet Union.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1777, Johann Martin Schmidt began to produce <a href="/wiki/White_lead" title="White lead">white lead</a> (ceruse). Schweinfurt suffered from heavy casualties during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> of 1796–1801.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schweinfurt remained a free imperial city until 1802, when it passed to the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Electorate of Bavaria</a>. Assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Grand Duchy of Würzburg">grand duke of Würzburg</a> in 1810, it was granted to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Kingdom of Bavaria</a> four years later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-2">[2]</a></sup> The first railway junction was opened in 1852. In the following years Schweinfurt became a world leading centre for the production of <a href="/wiki/Ball_bearing" title="Ball bearing">ball bearings</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This was to lead to grievous consequences for the city during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schweinfurt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WWII_Schweinfurt_Raid.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/42/WWII_Schweinfurt_Raid.jpg" width="210" height="400" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="210" data-file-height="400" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WWII_Schweinfurt_Raid.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">USAAF</a> raid on ball-bearing works in Schweinfurt in 1943</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1939 Schweinfurt produced most of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ball_bearings" class="mw-redirect" title="Ball bearings">ball bearings</a>, and factories such as the <b>Schweinfurter Kugellagerwerke</b> became a target of <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">strategic bombing during World War II</a> to cripple <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tank</a> and aircraft production. Schweinfurt was bombed 22 times during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Pointblank" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Pointblank">Operation Pointblank</a> by a total of 2285 aircraft.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Schweinfurt-Regensburg_mission" class="mw-redirect" title="Schweinfurt-Regensburg mission">Schweinfurt-Regensburg mission</a> caused an immediate 34% loss of production<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and all plants but the largest were devastated by fire. Efforts to disperse the surviving machinery began immediately and the Luftwaffe deployed large numbers of interceptors along the corridor to Schweinfurt.<sup id="cite_ref-Walden_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walden-6">[6]</a></sup> Bombing also included the <a href="/wiki/Second_Raid_on_Schweinfurt" title="Second Raid on Schweinfurt">Second Raid on Schweinfurt</a> on 14 October 1943 ("called Black Thursday because of the enormous loss of aircraft (60) and lives (600+)")<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2008)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Big_Week" title="Big Week">Big Week</a> in February 1944.</p>
<p>Although losses of production bearings and machinery were high and much of the industrial and residential areas of the city were destroyed, killing more than a thousand civilians, the factories were restored to production and the industry dispersed. Although German planners initially thought it essential to purchase the entire output of the Swedish ball-bearing industry, losses in the production of bearings were actually made up from surpluses found within Germany in the aftermath of the first raid. The decentralized industry was able to restore output to 85% of its pre-bombing level.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Hitler</a> made restoration of ball bearing production a high priority and massive efforts were undertaken to repair and rebuild the factories,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (May 2008)">specify</span></a></i>]</sup> partly in bomb-proof underground facilities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="42nd Infantry Division (United States)">42nd Infantry Division (United States)</a> entered Schweinfurt on 11 April 1945 and conducted <a href="/wiki/Urban_warfare" title="Urban warfare">house-to-house</a> fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-Walden_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walden-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:2</sup> On 12 April an internment camp at Goethe-Schule<sup id="cite_ref-Walden_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walden-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:2</sup> held male civilians aged 16–60.<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_years">Recent years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schweinfurt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recent years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After the war Schweinfurt became a stronghold of the US military and their dependants. Thus Schweinfurt recovered relatively quickly from its third period of destruction. Schweinfurt formerly hosted the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Garrison_Schweinfurt" title="U.S. Army Garrison Schweinfurt">U.S. Army Garrison Schweinfurt</a>, which the U.S. Army closed on 19 September 2014 due to an ongoing effort to concentrate the U.S. military's footprint in Germany to fewer communities.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in post-war years, the new suburbs of Bergl, Hochfeld and Steinberg were developed to settle a growing population. In 1954 the city laid the foundation stone for the new town hall and commemorated the 700th and 500th anniversaries of the two earlier periods of destruction as well as the ongoing reconstruction following World War II. In 1998 German and American veterans and survivors of the bombing raids came together to erect a <a href="/wiki/War_memorial" title="War memorial">war memorial</a> to the fallen.</p>

<p>Schweinfurt's main landmarks include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Town_Hall,_Schweinfurt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Town Hall, Schweinfurt (page does not exist)">Town Hall</a> (1570–1572)</li>
<li>The church of <a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Johannes,_Schweinfurt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="St. Johannes, Schweinfurt (page does not exist)">St. Johannes</a> (1554–1562)</li>
<li>The Old Grammar School, seat of the local museum</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Museum_Georg_Sch%C3%A4fer" title="Museum Georg Schäfer">Museum Georg Schäfer</a> specializes in 19th-century paintings by artists from German-speaking countries.</p>
<p>The Schweinfurter Rathaus (town hall) square has a large <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_R%C3%BCckert" title="Friedrich Rückert">Friedrich Rückert</a> monument in the centre, around which weekly markets and many city festivals are held. A large number of people, including immigrants from many other countries, add to the crowded inner-city traffic-free <i>Markthalle</i> shopping area.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Motherwell" title="Motherwell">Motherwell</a> Park connects the surrounding medieval buildings to the inner-city market square. To avoid car-filled streets, walking through the park with part of the original city walls offers paths and shortcuts, bringing one on foot from one end of town to another, reminiscent of medieval town life.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schweinfurt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Schweinfurt is known for its metal industry, especially ball bearing plants and bicycle manufacturing; <i>see also <a href="/wiki/FAG_Kugelfischer_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="FAG Kugelfischer AG">FAG Kugelfischer AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/ZF_Sachs_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="ZF Sachs AG">ZF Sachs AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosch_Rexroth" title="Bosch Rexroth">Bosch Rexroth</a> and <a href="/wiki/SKF" title="SKF">SKF</a></i>. The pigment <a href="/wiki/Paris_Green" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris Green">Schweinfurt Green</a> is manufactured here. <a href="/wiki/SRAM_Corporation" title="SRAM Corporation">SRAM Corporation</a> hosts the world's largest development center of the bicycle industry in Schweinfurt.</p>
<p>With GDP per capita of €78,382, Schweinfurt ranks third richest city in Germany after <a href="/wiki/Wolfsburg" title="Wolfsburg">Wolfsburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a recent study by the Swiss Institute of Economic Research forecasters Schweinfurt is one of the fastest growing cities in Germany. The study confirmed the city, among other things, the highest concentration of jobs in Germany with particularly high Beschäftigungsanteil (employment share) in the German high-tech sector. The Contor-2010 study, which was commissioned by the Manager magazine, ranked Schweinfurt as one of the most dynamic cities in Europe in terms of development opportunities. From rank 63 in 2007, the city significantly improved to rank 16 in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

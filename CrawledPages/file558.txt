<p>The <b>colonial colleges</b> are nine institutions of <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a> chartered in the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> before the United States of America became a sovereign nation after the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These nine have long been considered together, notably in the survey of their origins in the 1907 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cambridge_History_of_English_and_American_Literature" title="The Cambridge History of English and American Literature">The Cambridge History of English and American Literature</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Seven of the nine colonial colleges are part of the <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> athletic conference: <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth</a>. (The eighth member of the <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, was founded in 1865.)</p>
<p>The two colonial colleges not in the <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> are now both <a href="/wiki/Public_universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Public universities">public universities</a>—the <a href="/wiki/College_of_William_%26_Mary" title="College of William &amp; Mary">College of William &amp; Mary</a> in Virginia and <a href="/wiki/Rutgers_University" title="Rutgers University">Rutgers University</a> in New Jersey. William &amp; Mary was a royal (state) institution from 1693 until the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. Between the Revolution and the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, William &amp; Mary was a private institution. It suffered significant damage during the Civil War and began to receive public support in the 1880s. William &amp; Mary officially became a public college in 1906, and Rutgers became "The State University of New Jersey" after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_nine_colonial_colleges">The nine colonial colleges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonial_colleges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The nine colonial colleges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seven of the nine colonial colleges began their histories as institutions of higher learning <i>de novo</i> (i.e., with no predecessor parent organization). Dartmouth College began operating in 1768 as the collegiate department of <a href="/wiki/Moor%27s_Charity_School" title="Moor's Charity School">Moor's Charity School</a>, a secondary school started in 1754 by Dartmouth founder <a href="/wiki/Eleazar_Wheelock" title="Eleazar Wheelock">Eleazar Wheelock</a>. Dartmouth considers its founding date to be 1769, when it was granted a collegiate charter. The University of Pennsylvania began operating in 1751 as a secondary school, the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy of Philadelphia">Academy of Philadelphia</a>, and added an institution of higher education in 1755 with the granting of a charter to the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect" title="College of Philadelphia">College of Philadelphia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonial_colleges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 33em; -webkit-column-width: 33em; column-width: 33em; list-style-type: decimal;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The institution was founded in 1636 by a vote of the legislature of the colony to provide money for "a school or college" at Newtowne (the present <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>.) Nothing further was done about actually creating a school until 1638, when in his will John Harvard bequeathed money and books to the yet-uncreated college. Construction began shortly thereafter on a school that was given the name of its first benefactor.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-7">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="/wiki/The_College_of_William_%26_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="The College of William &amp; Mary">The College of William &amp; Mary</a> sometimes asserts a connection with an attempt to found a "University of Henrico" at Henricopolis (also known as <a href="/wiki/Henricus" title="Henricus">Henricus</a>) in the Colony of Virginia, which received a charter in 1618; but only a small school for <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> had begun operation by 1622, when the town was destroyed in a Native American raid. A page on their website says "The College of William &amp; Mary [...] was the first college planned for the United States. Its roots go back to the College proposed at Henrico in 1619." However, it immediately proceeds to note that "The College is second only to Harvard University in actual operation."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Since William &amp; Mary describes itself as "America's second-oldest college" and gives its year of founding as 1693, it does not seem to be suggesting institutional continuity with the University of Henrico, rather, W&amp;M is providing historical perspective.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (April 2016)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> However, this depends upon the orientation and competitiveness of the administration at any given time, for instance, when a Harvard grad is President, Wm &amp; M is presented as "second college", but when Va grad is president, it is "the first college in its roots"..<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (April 2016)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> (This original college has been revived, in 1992, as "Henricus Colledge (1619), America's 1st College.".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (April 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup>) William &amp; Mary has a published list of its early graduates by its <a href="/wiki/Swem_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Swem Library">Swem Library</a>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In the wake of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the College ceased to enroll students in 1882 due to attendant financial pressures. Students returned in 1888 after the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth of Virginia">Commonwealth of Virginia</a> authorized $10,000 for it to become a "State normal" school for men. In 1906 it became a public, non-sectarian school with the college's royal charter still in effect, except where superseded by state or federal laws.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-21"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-21">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There is some disagreement about Penn's date of founding as the university has never used its legal charter date for this purpose and, in addition, took the unusual step of changing its official founding date approximately 150 years after the fact. The first meeting of the founding trustees of the secondary school which eventually became the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> took place in November 1749. Secondary instruction for boys at the <i><a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy of Philadelphia">Academy of Philadelphia</a></i> began in August 1751. Undergraduate education for men began after a collegiate charter for the <i><a href="/wiki/College_of_Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect" title="College of Philadelphia">College of Philadelphia</a></i> was granted in 1755. Penn initially designated 1750 as its founding date. Sometime later in its early history, Penn began to refer to 1749 instead. The school considered 1749 to be its founding date for more than a century until, in 1895, elite universities in the United States agreed that formal <a href="/wiki/Academic_procession" title="Academic procession">academic processions</a> would place visiting dignitaries and other officials in the order of their institution's founding dates. Four years later in 1899, Penn's board of trustees voted to retroactively revise the university's founding date from 1749 to 1740 in order to become older than Princeton, which had been chartered in 1746. The premise for this revised founding date was the fact that the Academy of Philadelphia purchased the building and assumed the educational mandate of an inactive trust which had originally hoped to open a charity school for indigent children. This was part of a 1740 project that had been planned to comprise both a church and school though, due to insufficient funding, only the church was built and even it was never put into use. The dormant church building was conveyed to the Academy of Philadelphia in 1750.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[16]</a></sup> To further complicate the comparison of founding dates, Princeton University has historical ties to an older college. Five of the twelve members of Princeton's first board of trustees were very closely associated with a "<a href="/wiki/Log_College" title="Log College">Log College</a>" operated by Presbyterian minister <a href="/wiki/William_Tennent" title="William Tennent">William Tennent</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Tennent" title="Gilbert Tennent">Gilbert</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bucks_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Bucks County, Pennsylvania">Bucks County, Pennsylvania</a> from 1726 until 1746.<sup id="cite_ref-princeton1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-princeton1-20">[17]</a></sup> Because the College of New Jersey and the Log College shared the same religious affiliation (a moderate element within the "<a href="/wiki/The_Old_Side-New_Side_Controversy" class="mw-redirect" title="The Old Side-New Side Controversy">New Side</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Old_and_New_Light" title="Old and New Light">New Light</a>" wing of the <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterian Church</a>) and there was a considerable overlap in their boards of trustees, some historians suggest that there is sufficient connection between this school and the College of New Jersey which would enable Princeton to claim a founding date of 1726. However, Princeton does not officially do so and a university historian says that the "facts do not warrant" such a claim.<sup id="cite_ref-princeton1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-princeton1-20">[17]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-28"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-28">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Penn's website, like other sources, makes an important point of Penn's heritage being nonsectarian, associated with <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> and the Academy of Philadelphia's nonsectarian board of trustees: "The goal of Franklin's nonsectarian, practical plan would be the education of a business and governing class rather than of clergymen.".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[20]</a></sup> Jencks and Riesman (2001) write: "The <a href="/wiki/Anglicans" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicans">Anglicans</a> who founded the University of Pennsylvania, however, were evidently anxious not to alienate Philadelphia's <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a>, and they made their new college officially nonsectarian." Franklin himself was a self-described "thorough <a href="/wiki/Deist" class="mw-redirect" title="Deist">Deist</a>." Starting in 1751, the same trustees also operated a Charity School for Boys, whose curriculum combined "general principles of Christianity" with practical instruction leading toward careers in business and the "mechanical arts",<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[21]</a></sup> and thus might be described as "non-denominational Christian." The charity school was originally planned and a trust was organized on paper in 1740 by followers of traveling evangelist <a href="/wiki/George_Whitefield" title="George Whitefield">George Whitefield</a>. The school was to have operated inside a church supported by the same group of adherents. But the organizers ran short of financing and, although the frame of the building was raised, the interior was left unfinished. The founders of the Academy of Philadelphia purchased the unused building in 1750 for their new venture and, in the process, assumed the original trust. Since 1899, Penn has claimed a founding date of 1740, based on the organizational date of the charity school and the premise that it had institutional identity with the Academy of Philadelphia. Whitefield was a firebrand <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> associated with the <a href="/wiki/Great_Awakening" title="Great Awakening">Great Awakening</a>; since the Methodists did not formally break from the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> until 1784, Whitefield in 1740 would be labelled <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Episcopalian</a>, and in fact <i>Brown</i> University, emphasizing its own pioneering nonsectarianism, refers to Penn's origin as "Episcopalian" <sup id="cite_ref-brown1_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown1-26">[22]</a></sup>). Penn is sometimes assumed to have Quaker ties (its athletic teams are called "Quakers," and the cross-registration alliance between Penn, <a href="/wiki/Haverford_College" title="Haverford College">Haverford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swarthmore_College" title="Swarthmore College">Swarthmore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Mawr_College" title="Bryn Mawr College">Bryn Mawr</a> is known as the "Quaker Consortium.") But Penn's website does not assert any formal affiliation with Quakerism, historic or otherwise, and Haverford College implicitly asserts a non-Quaker origin for Penn when it states that "Founded in 1833, Haverford is the oldest institution of higher learning with Quaker roots in North America."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[23]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-33"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-33">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Brown's website characterizes it as "the <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> answer to <a href="/wiki/Congregationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Congregationalist">Congregationalist</a> Yale and Harvard; <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> Princeton; and <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopalian</a> Penn and Columbia," but adds that at the time it was "the only one that welcomed students of all religious persuasions."<sup id="cite_ref-brown1_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brown1-26">[22]</a></sup> Brown's charter stated that "into this liberal and catholic institution shall never be admitted any religious tests, but on the contrary, all the members hereof shall forever enjoy full, free, absolute, and uninterrupted liberty of conscience." The charter further required that its president and twenty-two of the thirty-six trustees be Baptists, and that the remainder consist of "five <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Friends</a>, four Congregationalists, and five Episcopalians"<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[27]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-36"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-36">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Dartmouth College began operating during 1768 as the collegiate department of Moor's School (1754) in <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Connecticut" title="Columbia, Connecticut">Columbia, Connecticut</a>. The collegiate department was being described in writing as "Dartmouth College" by January of 1769, when the Township of <a href="/wiki/Hanover,_New_Hampshire" title="Hanover, New Hampshire">Hanover, New Hampshire</a> voted to offer it a grant of land. The institution received a royal charter on December 13, 1769 and its students moved from Columbia to Hanover during October 1770. The first degrees were awarded in August 1771. Queen's College, although granted a charter earlier, began operation during 1771, after Dartmouth College began awarding degrees.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-41"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-41">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Although most early records of the university were destroyed in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_Pittsburgh" title="Great Fire of Pittsburgh">Great Fire of 1845</a> as well as a subsequent fire in 1849, it is known that the school began its life as a preparatory academy, possibly as early as 1770,<sup id="cite_ref-Pitt1770_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pitt1770-37">[30]</a></sup> or at some point in the 1780s.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[32]</a></sup> Presumably starting its life in a <a href="/wiki/Log_Cabin_(University_of_Pittsburgh)" title="Log Cabin (University of Pittsburgh)">log cabin</a><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[33]</a></sup> on what was then the nation's frontier, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Henry_Brackenridge" title="Hugh Henry Brackenridge">Hugh Henry Brackenridge</a> sought and obtained a charter for the school from the state legislature of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Commonwealth of Pennsylvania</a> that was passed by the assembly on February 28, 1787. The school's charter was altered in 1819 to grant it university status and conferring on it the name of the Western University of Pennsylvania. The university received its current name, the University of Pittsburgh, with a subsequent alteration to its charter in 1908.</span></li>

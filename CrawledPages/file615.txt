			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">William Ellery Channing</h1>

<p><b>William Ellery Channing</b> (April 7, 1780 – October 2, 1842) was the foremost <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarian</a> preacher in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in the early nineteenth century and, along with <a href="/wiki/Andrews_Norton" title="Andrews Norton">Andrews Norton</a> (1786–1853), one of Unitarianism's leading theologians. Channing was known for his articulate and impassioned sermons and public speeches, and as a prominent thinker in the liberal theology of the day. His religion and thought were among the chief influences on the <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Transcendentalist">Transcendentalists</a> although he never countenanced their views, which he saw as extreme. He espoused, especially in his "Baltimore Sermon"<sup id="cite_ref-American_Unitarian_Conference_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American_Unitarian_Conference-1">[1]</a></sup> of May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>5, 1819, given at the ordination of the theologian and educator <a href="/wiki/Jared_Sparks" title="Jared Sparks">Jared Sparks</a> (1789–1866) as the first minister of the newly organized <a href="/wiki/First_Unitarian_Church_(Baltimore,_Maryland)" title="First Unitarian Church (Baltimore, Maryland)">First Independent Church of Baltimore</a>, the principles and tenets of the developing philosophy and theology of <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a>, leading to the organization in 1825 of the first Unitarian denomination in America (<a href="/wiki/American_Unitarian_Association" title="American Unitarian Association">American Unitarian Association</a>) and the later developments and mergers between Unitarians and Universalists, resulting finally in the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association of America</a> in 1961.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">Life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Ellery_Channing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Ellery_Channing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Channing, the son of William Channing and Lucy Ellery, was born April<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>7, 1780, in <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>. He was a grandson of <a href="/wiki/William_Ellery" title="William Ellery">William Ellery</a> (1727–1820), a signer of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">United States Declaration of Independence</a>, Deputy Governor of Rhode Island, Chief Justice, and influential citizen. He became a <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a>, rejecting the <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinist</a> doctrines of <a href="/wiki/Total_depravity" title="Total depravity">total depravity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Divine_election" class="mw-redirect" title="Divine election">divine election</a>.</p>
<p>Channing enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> at a troubled time, particularly because of the recent <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. He later wrote of these years:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>College was never in a worse state than when I entered it. Society was passing through a most critical stage. The French Revolution had diseased the imagination and unsettled the understanding of men everywhere. The old foundations of social order, loyalty, tradition, habit, reverence for antiquity, were everywhere shaken, if not subverted. The authority of the past was gone.<sup id="cite_ref-Broaddus22_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broaddus22-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Graduating first in his class in 1798, he was elected commencement speaker though he was prohibited by the Harvard College faculty from mentioning the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">Revolution</a> and other political subjects in his address.<sup id="cite_ref-Broaddus22_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broaddus22-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_theologian">As theologian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Ellery_Channing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As theologian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Ellery_Channing.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/William_Ellery_Channing.JPG/220px-William_Ellery_Channing.JPG" width="220" height="373" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/William_Ellery_Channing.JPG/330px-William_Ellery_Channing.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/William_Ellery_Channing.JPG/440px-William_Ellery_Channing.JPG 2x" data-file-width="815" data-file-height="1383" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Ellery_Channing.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of William Ellery Channing standing on the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Public_Garden_(Boston)" title="Public Garden (Boston)">Boston Public Garden</a>, in Boston, Massachusetts</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In opposition to traditional American Calvinist orthodoxy, Channing preferred a gentle, loving relationship with God. He opposed <a href="/wiki/Portal:Calvinism" title="Portal:Calvinism">Calvinism</a> for</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>… proclaiming a God who is to be dreaded. We are told to love and imitate God, but also that God does things we would consider most cruel in any human parent, "were he to bring his children into life totally depraved and then to pursue them with endless punishment" (Channing 1957: 56).<sup id="cite_ref-Channing39_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Channing39-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Channing's inner struggle continued through two years during which he lived in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond, Virginia</a>, working as a tutor for <a href="/wiki/David_Meade_Randolph" class="mw-redirect" title="David Meade Randolph">David Meade Randolph</a>. He came to his definitive faith only through much spiritual turmoil and difficulty. Channing was called as pastor of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Street_Church_(Boston)" title="Federal Street Church (Boston)">Federal Street Church</a> in Boston in 1803, where he remained for the rest of his life. He lived through the increasing tension between religious liberals and conservatives and took a moderate position, rejecting the extremes of both groups. In 1809 he was elected a <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">Fellow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1815 Channing engaged in a noted controversy on the principles of <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a> with <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Worcester_(theologian)" title="Samuel Worcester (theologian)">Samuel Worcester</a>, (1770–1821).<sup id="cite_ref-acab_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acab-5">[5]</a></sup> A review of a pamphlet on American Unitarianism (<i>American Unitarianism; or a Brief History of the Progress and Present State of the Unitarian Churches of America</i>), attributed to <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Evarts" title="Jeremiah Evarts">Jeremiah Evarts</a>, was published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Panoplist" title="The Panoplist">The Panoplist</a></i> in June 1815. Channing objected to the way Unitarians in the United States were portrayed in the review. Worcester replied to this objection, and an exchange of pamphlets followed.<sup id="cite_ref-dab_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dab-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notwithstanding his moderate position, Channing later became the primary spokesman and interpreter of Unitarianism, after sixteen years at Boston's Federal Street Church. He was invited to come south again to <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> to preach the ordination sermon of the future noted educator and theologian <a href="/wiki/Jared_Sparks" title="Jared Sparks">Jared Sparks</a> (1789–1866), the first minister (1819–1823) called to the newly organized congregation (1817) in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/First_Unitarian_Church_(Baltimore,_Maryland)" title="First Unitarian Church (Baltimore, Maryland)">First Independent Church of Baltimore</a> (located at West Franklin and North Charles Streets, in a landmark two-year-old structure designed by noted French émigré architect <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_Godefroy" title="Maximilian Godefroy">J. Maximilian M. Godefroy</a>), later known, after a merger with Second Universalist Church in 1935, as the <a href="/wiki/First_Unitarian_Church_(Baltimore,_Maryland)" title="First Unitarian Church (Baltimore, Maryland)">First Unitarian Church of Baltimore (Unitarian and Universalist)</a>, which was forever after known as "The Baltimore Sermon".<sup id="cite_ref-American_Unitarian_Conference_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American_Unitarian_Conference-1">[1]</a></sup> The sermon, or address, was given on Wednesday, May<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>5, 1819, and was entitled "Unitarian Christianity". In it, he explicated the distinctive tenets of the developing Unitarian movement, one of which was the rejection of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>. Other important tenets were the belief in human goodness and the subjection of theological ideas to the light of <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>. The anniversary of the address is celebrated and observed annually by the <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> churches of the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_the_Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Districts of the Unitarian Universalist Association">Joseph Priestley District</a> as "Union Sunday", with occasional <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecumenical">ecumenical</a> guests from other Christian bodies).</p>
<p>In 1828 he gave another famous ordination sermon, entitled "Likeness to God". The idea of the human potential to be like God, which Channing advocated as grounded firmly in scripture, was seen as heretical by the <a href="/wiki/Portal:Calvinism" title="Portal:Calvinism">Calvinist</a> religious establishment of his day. It is in this address that Channing first advocated the possibility for revelation through reason rather than solely from Scripture. <i>American Philosophy: An Encyclopedia</i> classes him as one of several figures who "took a more <a href="/wiki/Pantheism" title="Pantheism">pantheist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pandeism" title="Pandeism">pandeist</a> approach by rejecting views of God as separate from the world."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even at the end of his life he adhered to the non-<a href="/wiki/Socinianism" title="Socinianism">Socinian</a> belief in the <a href="/wiki/Preexistence_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Preexistence of Christ">preexistence of Christ</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I have always inclined to the doctrine of the preexistence of Christ, though am not insensible to the weight of your objections" (Boston, March&#160;31, 1832)<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Ellery_Channing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In later years Channing addressed the topic of <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a> although he was never an ardent <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">abolitionist</a>. Channing wrote a book in 1835 entitled <i>Slavery.</i><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Channing has, however, been described as a <a href="/wiki/Romantic_racism" title="Romantic racism">romantic racist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He held a common American belief about the inferiority of African people and slaves and held a belief that once freed, Africans would need overseers. The overseers (largely former slave masters) were necessary because the slaves would lapse into laziness. Furthermore, he did not join the abolitionist movement because he did not agree with their way of conducting themselves, and he felt that voluntary associations limited a person's autonomy. Therefore, he often chose to remain separate from organizations and reform movements. This middle position characterized his attitude about most questions although his eloquence and strong influence on the religious world incurred the enmity of many extremists. Channing had an enormous influence over the religious (and social) life of New England, and America, in the nineteenth century.</p>
<p>Toward the end of his life Channing embraced immediate abolitionism. His evolving view of abolitionism was fostered by the success of British <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Abolitionism in the United Kingdom">abolition</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_West_Indies" title="British West Indies">British West Indies</a> in 1834 and the absence of the expected social and economic upheaval in the post-emancipated <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean</a>.</p>
<p>Channing wrote extensively about the emerging new national literature of the United States, saying that national literature is "the expression of a nation's mind in writing", and "the concentration of intellect for the purpose of spreading itself abroad and multiplying its energy".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Ellery_Channing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WilliamEChanningGrave.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/WilliamEChanningGrave.jpg/220px-WilliamEChanningGrave.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/WilliamEChanningGrave.jpg/330px-WilliamEChanningGrave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/WilliamEChanningGrave.jpg/440px-WilliamEChanningGrave.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WilliamEChanningGrave.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Grave of William Ellery Channing at Mount Auburn Cemetery in Cambridge, Massachusetts</div>

</div>
<p>Channing died in <a href="/wiki/Old_Bennington,_Vermont" title="Old Bennington, Vermont">Old Bennington, Vermont</a>, where a <a href="/wiki/Cenotaph" title="Cenotaph">cenotaph</a> is placed in his memory. He is buried in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Auburn_Cemetery" title="Mount Auburn Cemetery">Mount Auburn Cemetery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Ellery_Channing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Ellery_Channing_statue_and_Channing_Memorial_Church,_Touro_Park,_Newport,_Rhode_Island.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/William_Ellery_Channing_statue_and_Channing_Memorial_Church%2C_Touro_Park%2C_Newport%2C_Rhode_Island.jpg/220px-William_Ellery_Channing_statue_and_Channing_Memorial_Church%2C_Touro_Park%2C_Newport%2C_Rhode_Island.jpg" width="220" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/William_Ellery_Channing_statue_and_Channing_Memorial_Church%2C_Touro_Park%2C_Newport%2C_Rhode_Island.jpg/330px-William_Ellery_Channing_statue_and_Channing_Memorial_Church%2C_Touro_Park%2C_Newport%2C_Rhode_Island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/William_Ellery_Channing_statue_and_Channing_Memorial_Church%2C_Touro_Park%2C_Newport%2C_Rhode_Island.jpg/440px-William_Ellery_Channing_statue_and_Channing_Memorial_Church%2C_Touro_Park%2C_Newport%2C_Rhode_Island.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2319" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Ellery_Channing_statue_and_Channing_Memorial_Church,_Touro_Park,_Newport,_Rhode_Island.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Channing Memorial Church and statue in Newport</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>In 1880, a young Unitarian minister in Newport, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Timothy_Brooks" title="Charles Timothy Brooks">Charles Timothy Brooks</a>, published a biography, <i>William Ellery Channing, A Centennial Memory</i>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>The Channing Memorial Church<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> was built in <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a> in 1880 to commemorate the 100th anniversary of his birth.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>In 1885 the <a href="/wiki/Channing_School_For_Girls" class="mw-redirect" title="Channing School For Girls">Channing School For Girls</a> opened in London, primarily for the daughters of <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland">British</a> Unitarian ministers.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>A bronze statue of Channing by <a href="/wiki/William_Clark_Noble" title="William Clark Noble">William Clark Noble</a> was erected in 1892 in Newport's <a href="/w/index.php?title=Touro_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Touro Park (page does not exist)">Touro Park</a> across from the Channing Memorial Church.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>A bronze statue of Channing by <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Adams_(sculptor)" title="Herbert Adams (sculptor)">Herbert Adams</a> was erected in 1903 on the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Public_Garden" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Public Garden">Boston Public Garden</a>, at Arlington St. and <a href="/wiki/Boylston_Street" title="Boylston Street">Boylston St.</a> It stands across the street from the <a href="/wiki/Arlington_Street_Church" title="Arlington Street Church">Arlington Street Church</a> that he served (and from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Street_Church_(Boston)" title="Federal Street Church (Boston)">Federal Street Church</a>).</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>A portrait of him also hangs in the foyer of the <a href="/wiki/First_Unitarian_Church_(Baltimore,_Maryland)" title="First Unitarian Church (Baltimore, Maryland)">First Unitarian Church of Baltimore (Unitarian and Universalist)</a> at North Charles and West Franklin Streets in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore, Maryland</a>, along with the aforementioned "Union Sunday" annual commemoration services in May.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Channing had a profound impact on the <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">Transcendentalism</a> movement though he never officially subscribed to its views. However, two of Channing's nephews, <a href="/wiki/William_Ellery_Channing_(poet)" title="William Ellery Channing (poet)">Ellery Channing</a> (1818–1901) and <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Channing" title="William Henry Channing">William Henry Channing</a> (1810–1884), became prominent members of the movement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>

<p>The <b>Charles River</b> (sometimes called the <b>River Charles</b> or simply the <b>Charles</b>) is an 80&#160;mi (129&#160;km) long river in eastern <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. From its source in <a href="/wiki/Hopkinton,_Massachusetts" title="Hopkinton, Massachusetts">Hopkinton</a> the river flows in a northeasterly direction (after first coursing due south through <a href="/wiki/Milford,_Massachusetts" title="Milford, Massachusetts">Milford</a>), traveling through 23 cities and towns before reaching the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> at <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrography">Hydrography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hydrography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_rivers_in_Massachusetts#Massachusetts_Bay" class="mw-redirect" title="List of rivers in Massachusetts">List of rivers in Massachusetts §&#160;Massachusetts_Bay</a></div>
<p>The Charles River is fed by approximately 80 <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a> and several major <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> as it flows 80 miles (129&#160;km),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> starting at Teresa Road just north<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> of Echo Lake (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Charles_River&amp;params=42.215_N_71.514444_W_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">42°12′54″N</span> <span class="longitude">71°30′52″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">42.215°N 71.514444°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">42.215; -71.514444</span></span></span></a></span>) in Hopkinton, passing through 23 cities and towns in eastern Massachusetts before emptying into <a href="/wiki/Boston_Harbor" title="Boston Harbor">Boston Harbor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> Thirty-three lakes and ponds and 35 municipalities are entirely or partially part of the Charles River <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a>. Despite the river's length and relatively large drainage area (308 square miles; 798&#160;km²), its source is only 26 miles (42&#160;km) from its mouth, and the river drops only 350 feet (107 m) from source to sea. The Charles River watershed contains more than 8,000 acres of protected wetlands, referred to as Natural Valley Storage. These areas are important in preventing downstream flooding and providing natural habitats to native species.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brandeis_University" title="Brandeis University">Brandeis University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> are located along the Charles River. Near its mouth, it forms the border between downtown Boston and <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlestown, Massachusetts">Charlestown</a>. The river opens into a broad basin and is lined by the parks of the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River_Reservation" title="Charles River Reservation">Charles River Reservation</a>. On the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River_Esplanade" title="Charles River Esplanade">Charles River Esplanade</a> stands the <a href="/wiki/Hatch_Shell" class="mw-redirect" title="Hatch Shell">Hatch Shell</a>, where concerts are given in summer evenings. The basin is especially known for its <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">Independence Day</a> celebration. The middle section of the river between the <a href="/wiki/Watertown_Dam" title="Watertown Dam">Watertown Dam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wellesley,_Massachusetts" title="Wellesley, Massachusetts">Wellesley</a> is partially protected by the properties of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Charles_River_Reservation" title="Upper Charles River Reservation">Upper Charles River Reservation</a> and other state parks, including the <a href="/wiki/Hemlock_Gorge_Reservation" title="Hemlock Gorge Reservation">Hemlock Gorge Reservation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cutler_Park" title="Cutler Park">Cutler Park</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Elm_Bank_Reservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Elm Bank Reservation">Elm Bank Reservation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The river is well known for its <a href="/wiki/Watercraft_rowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Watercraft rowing">rowing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sculling" title="Sculling">sculling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canoeing" title="Canoeing">canoeing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kayaking" title="Kayaking">kayaking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paddleboarding" title="Paddleboarding">paddleboarding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragonboating" class="mw-redirect" title="Dragonboating">dragonboating</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a>, both recreational and competitive. The river may also be <a href="/wiki/Kayak" title="Kayak">kayaked</a>; depending on the season, however, kayakers can only navigate the Charles by getting out and dragging their kayaks for significant stretches. The "Lower Basin" between the <a href="/wiki/Longfellow_Bridge" title="Longfellow Bridge">Longfellow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Bridge" title="Harvard Bridge">Harvard</a> bridges is home to <a href="/wiki/Community_Boating" class="mw-redirect" title="Community Boating">Community Boating</a>, the Harvard University Sailing Center, and the MIT Sailing Pavilion. The <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_Charles_Regatta" title="Head of the Charles Regatta">Head of the Charles Regatta</a> is held here every October. In early June, the annual Hong Kong Boston Dragon boat Festival is held in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>, near the <a href="/wiki/John_W._Weeks_Bridge" title="John W. Weeks Bridge">Weeks Footbridge</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Charles_River_Bike_Path" title="Charles River Bike Path">Charles River Bike Path</a> runs 23 miles (37&#160;km) along the banks of the Charles, starting at the Museum of Science and passing the campuses of MIT, Harvard and Boston University. The path is popular with runners and bikers. Many runners gauge their distance and speed by keeping track of the mileage between the bridges along the route.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>For several years, the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River_Speedway" title="Charles River Speedway">Charles River Speedway</a> operated along part of the river.</p>
<p>On July 13, 2013, <a href="/wiki/Human_swimming" title="Human swimming">swimming</a> for the general public was permitted for the first time in more than 50 years.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The river was used by Native Americans for local transportation and fishing and as part of the passage from southeastern Massachusetts to northern New England.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Smith_of_Jamestown" class="mw-redirect" title="John Smith of Jamestown">Captain John Smith</a> explored and mapped the coast of New England, naming many features, originally naming the Charles River the Massachusetts River, which was derived from the <a href="/wiki/Massachusett_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusett people">tribe living in the region</a>. When Smith presented his map to <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> he suggested that the king should feel free to change any of the "barbarous names" for "English" ones. The king made many such changes, but only four survive today, one of which is the Charles River which Charles named for himself.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In portions of its length, the Charles drops slowly in elevation and has relatively little current. Despite this, early settlers in <a href="/wiki/Dedham,_Massachusetts" title="Dedham, Massachusetts">Dedham, Massachusetts</a>, found a way to use the Charles to power mills. In 1639, the town dug a canal from the Charles to a nearby brook that drained to the <a href="/wiki/Neponset_River" title="Neponset River">Neponset River</a>. By this action, a portion of the Charles's flow was diverted, providing enough current for several mills. The new canal and the brook together are now called <a href="/wiki/Mother_Brook" title="Mother Brook">Mother Brook</a>. The canal is regarded as the first industrial canal in North America. It remains in use for <a href="/wiki/Flood_control" title="Flood control">flood control</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Waltham,_Massachusetts" title="Waltham, Massachusetts">Waltham</a> was the site of the first fully integrated textile factory in America, built by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Cabot_Lowell_(businessman)" title="Francis Cabot Lowell (businessman)">Francis Cabot Lowell</a> in 1814,<sup id="cite_ref-FrancisCabotLowellPBS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FrancisCabotLowellPBS-7">[7]</a></sup> and by the 19th century the Charles River was one of the most industrialized areas in the United States. Its <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> soon fueled many mills and factories. By the century's end, 20 dams had been built across the river, mostly to generate power for industry. An 1875 government report listed 43 mills along the 9.5-mile (15&#160;km) tidal estuary from <a href="/wiki/Watertown_Dam" title="Watertown Dam">Watertown Dam</a> to Boston Harbor.</p>
<p>From 1816 to 1968, the U.S. Army operated a gun and ammunition storage and later production facility known as the <a href="/wiki/Watertown_Arsenal" title="Watertown Arsenal">Watertown Arsenal</a>. While it was key to many of the nation's war efforts over its several decades in operation, not the least of which being the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, its location in <a href="/wiki/Watertown,_Massachusetts" title="Watertown, Massachusetts">Watertown</a> so near the Charles did great environmental harm. The arsenal was declared a <a href="/wiki/Super_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="Super Fund">Super Fund</a> site, and after its closure by the government it had to be cleaned at significant expense before it could be safely used again for other purposes. Likewise, the many factories and mills along the banks of the Charles supported a buoyant economy in their time but left a legacy of massive pollution.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_modern_Boston-Cambridge_basin">Creation of modern Boston-Cambridge basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Creation of modern Boston-Cambridge basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Charles_River_Reservation" title="Charles River Reservation">Charles River Reservation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_River_Esplanade,_Boston,_Massachusetts.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Charles_River_Esplanade%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/220px-Charles_River_Esplanade%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Charles_River_Esplanade%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/330px-Charles_River_Esplanade%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Charles_River_Esplanade%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/440px-Charles_River_Esplanade%2C_Boston%2C_Massachusetts.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_River_Esplanade,_Boston,_Massachusetts.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A sunny day on the Charles River Esplanade</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Today's Charles River basin between Boston and Cambridge is almost entirely a work of human design. <a href="/wiki/Owen_A._Galvin" title="Owen A. Galvin">Owen A. Galvin</a> was appointed head of the Charles River Improvement Commission by Governor <a href="/wiki/William_Russell_(governor)" title="William Russell (governor)">William E. Russell</a> in 1891. Their work led to the design initiatives of noted <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architect" title="Landscape architect">landscape architects</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Eliot_(landscape_architect)" title="Charles Eliot (landscape architect)">Charles Eliot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Shurcliff" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Shurcliff">Arthur Shurcliff</a>, both of whom had apprenticed with <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted" title="Frederick Law Olmsted">Frederick Law Olmsted</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guy_Lowell" title="Guy Lowell">Guy Lowell</a>. This designed landscape includes over 20 parks and natural areas along 19 miles (31&#160;km) of shoreline, from the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River_Reservation#Charles_River_Dam" title="Charles River Reservation">New Dam</a> at the Charlestown Bridge to the dam near Watertown Square.</p>
<p>Eliot first envisioned today's river design in the 1890s, an important model being the layout of the <a href="/wiki/Alster" title="Alster">Alster</a> basin in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but major construction began only after Eliot's death with the damming of the river's mouth at today's <a href="/wiki/Boston_Museum_of_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Museum of Science">Boston Museum of Science</a>, an effort led by <a href="/wiki/James_Jackson_Storrow" class="mw-redirect" title="James Jackson Storrow">James Jackson Storrow</a>. The new dam, completed in 1910, stabilized the water level from Boston to Watertown, eliminating the existing mud flats, and a narrow embankment was built between Leverett Circle and Charlesgate. After Storrow's death, his widow Mrs. James Jackson Storrow donated $1 million toward the creation of a more generously landscaped park along the Esplanade; it was dedicated in 1936 as the Storrow Memorial Embankment. This also enabled the construction of many public docks in the Charles River Basin. In the 1950s a highway (<a href="/wiki/Storrow_Drive" title="Storrow Drive">Storrow Drive</a>) was built along the edge of the Esplanade to connect Charles Circle with Soldiers Field Road, and the Esplanade was enlarged on the water side of the new highway.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Interstate_695_(Massachusetts)" title="Interstate 695 (Massachusetts)">Inner Belt</a> highway was proposed to cross the Charles River at the <a href="/wiki/Boston_University_Bridge" title="Boston University Bridge">Boston University Bridge</a>, but its construction was canceled in the 1970s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_pollution_and_remediation_efforts">History of pollution and remediation efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History of pollution and remediation efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater_treatment" title="Industrial wastewater treatment">industrial wastewater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">urban runoff</a> flowed freely into the river from the surrounding city, the Charles River became well known for its high level of <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">pollutants</a>, gaining such notoriety that by 1955, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_DeVoto" title="Bernard DeVoto">Bernard DeVoto</a> wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Magazine" title="Harper's Magazine">Harper's Magazine</a></i> that the Charles was "foul and noisome, polluted by offal and industrious wastes, scummy with oil, unlikely to be mistaken for water."<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-9">[9]</a></sup> It was not an uncommon sight to see <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxins</a> coloring parts of the river pink and orange, fish kills and submerged cars.<sup id="cite_ref-BostonLawsuit_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BostonLawsuit-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once popular with swimmers, awareness of the river's high pollution levels forced the state to shut down several popular swimming areas, including Cambridge's Magazine Beach and Gerry Landing public beaches.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BSC_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSC-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BunkerHillBoats.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/BunkerHillBoats.jpg/220px-BunkerHillBoats.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/BunkerHillBoats.jpg/330px-BunkerHillBoats.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/BunkerHillBoats.jpg/440px-BunkerHillBoats.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BunkerHillBoats.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sailboat" title="Sailboat">Sailboats</a> <a href="/wiki/Mooring_(watercraft)" title="Mooring (watercraft)">moored</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlestown, Massachusetts">Charlestown</a> side of the Charles River with <a href="/wiki/Bunker_Hill_Monument" title="Bunker Hill Monument">Bunker Hill Monument</a> in the distance</div>
</div>

<p>Efforts to clean up the river and restore it to a state where swimming and fishing would be acceptable began as early as the 1960s, and the program to clean up the Charles for good took shape in 1965 with the creation of the Charles River Watershed Association.<sup id="cite_ref-CRWA_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRWA-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1978, a new <a href="/wiki/Charles_River_Dam" title="Charles River Dam">Charles River Dam</a> was constructed downstream from the Science Museum site to keep <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">salt water</a> out of the basin.</p>
<p>In 1995, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> declared a goal of making the river swimmable by 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1996, Governor <a href="/wiki/William_Weld" class="mw-redirect" title="William Weld">William Weld</a> plunged, fully clothed, into the river to prove his commitment to cleaning up the river.<sup id="cite_ref-welddive_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-welddive-13">[13]</a></sup> On November 12, 2004, Christopher Swain became the first person to swim the Charles River's entire length, in an effort to raise public awareness of the river's environmental health.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In July 2007, the river hosted the Charles River Masters Swim Race, the first sanctioned race in the Charles in over five decades.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A combination of public and private initiatives helped drastically lower levels of pollutants by focusing on eliminating <a href="/wiki/Combined_sewer_overflow" class="mw-redirect" title="Combined sewer overflow">combined sewer overflows</a> and storm water <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a>. Since Weld's stunt, the river's condition has improved dramatically, although it was not deemed entirely swimmable by 2005.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Conservation_Law_Foundation" title="Conservation Law Foundation">Conservation Law Foundation</a> opposes the permit given to <a href="/wiki/Mirant" class="mw-redirect" title="Mirant">Mirant</a> for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Veolia_Energy_North_America&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Veolia Energy North America (page does not exist)">Veolia Energy North America</a> <a href="/wiki/Kendall_Cogeneration_Station" title="Kendall Cogeneration Station">Kendall Cogeneration Station</a>, an electricity plant near <a href="/wiki/Kendall_Square" title="Kendall Square">Kendall Square</a>, charging that the water it releases causes blooms of hazardous <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> because of its warm temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The water quality of the Charles River is often at its worst after a large rainfall because of pollutants carried by runoff, and sewage overflows. For 2011, the EPA reported that the Charles met state bacterial standards for boating and swimming 96% and 89% of the time on dry days, and 74% and 35% of the time on wet days, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-epa_report_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-epa_report-19">[19]</a></sup> Overall boatability and swimability of 82% and 54% in 2011 compare with 39% and 19% in 1995.</p>
<p>A study<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> published in the <i>Journal of the American Water Resources Association</i> in April 2008 and completed by researchers at Northeastern University, found high concentrations of <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> in the Charles River after a long period of no rain. Using a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical model</a>, the researchers then determined that two major tributaries, the <a href="/wiki/Stony_Brook_(Boston)" title="Stony Brook (Boston)">Stony Brook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muddy_River,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Muddy River, Massachusetts">Muddy River</a>, are the predominant sources of <i>E. coli</i> in the lower Charles River.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Oysters have been used to filter and clean Charles River water.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 2013, boating is allowed on the Charles but swimming without a permit is punishable by a fine up to $250.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Starting in 2007,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the Charles River Swimming Club has organized an annual race for its members, but obtains a special permit and must monitor water quality and rainfall in the days leading up to the race.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The "first public swim" in the Charles since the 1950s was conducted on July 13, 2013, by the Charles River Conservancy<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[Note 1]</a></sup>, Charles River Watershed Association (CRWA), Esplanade Association, and DCR.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> Both the annual race<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> and the Conservancy event have been held in deep water with swimmers jumping in off a dock, to avoid the toxic sediments on the bottom of the river that still make beach swimming dangerous.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Hildesheim</b> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈhɪldəsˌhaɪ̯m]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Hildesheim.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0a/De-Hildesheim.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Hildesheim.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small> (<a href="/wiki/Eastphalian_dialect" title="Eastphalian dialect">Eastphalian</a>: <i>Hilmessen</i>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> with 103,804 inhabitants.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is located in the district of <a href="/wiki/Hildesheim_(district)" title="Hildesheim (district)">Hildesheim</a>, about 30&#160;km southeast of <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a> on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Innerste" title="Innerste">Innerste</a> River, a small tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Leine" title="Leine">Leine</a> River.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hildesheim">Hildesheim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hildesheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hildesheim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hildesheim_night.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Hildesheim_night.jpg/220px-Hildesheim_night.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Hildesheim_night.jpg/330px-Hildesheim_night.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Hildesheim_night.jpg/440px-Hildesheim_night.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hildesheim_night.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The historic market square at night</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hildesheim, one of the oldest cities in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Germany" title="Northern Germany">Northern Germany</a>, became the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Hildesheim" title="Bishopric of Hildesheim">Bishopric of Hildesheim</a> in 815 and may have been founded when the bishop moved from <a href="/wiki/Elze" title="Elze">Elze</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Innerste" title="Innerste">Innerste</a> <a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">ford</a>, which was an important market on the <a href="/wiki/Hellweg" title="Hellweg">Hellweg</a> trade route. The settlement around the cathedral very quickly developed into a town and was awarded <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market rights</a> by <a href="/wiki/Otto_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto III, Holy Roman Emperor">King Otto III</a> in 983.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Originally the market was held in a street called Old Market (<i>Alter Markt</i>) which still exists today. The first market place was laid out around the church <a href="/wiki/St._Andreas,_Hildesheim" title="St. Andreas, Hildesheim">St. Andreas</a>. When the city grew further, a bigger market place became necessary. The present market place of Hildesheim was laid out at the beginning of the 13th century when the city had about 5,000 inhabitants. When Hildesheim obtained <a href="/wiki/Town_privileges" title="Town privileges">city status</a> in 1249, it was one of the biggest cities in Northern Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For four centuries the clergy ruled Hildesheim, before a city hall was built and the citizens gained some influence and independence. Construction of the present City Hall started in 1268.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1367 Hildesheim became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>. A war between the citizens and their bishop cost dearly in 1519-1523 when they engaged in a feud. Hildesheim became <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> in 1542, and only the cathedral and a few other buildings remained in imperial (Catholic) hands. Several villages around the city remained Catholic as well. In 1813, after the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, the town became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Kingdom of Hanover</a>, which was annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hanover" title="Province of Hanover">province</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> in 1866. In 1868 a highly valuable trove of about 70 Roman silver vessels for eating and drinking, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Hildesheim_Treasure" title="Hildesheim Treasure">Hildesheim Treasure</a>, was unearthed by Prussian soldiers.</p>
<p>The city was heavily damaged by <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">air raids</a> in 1945, especially on 22 March. Although it had little military significance, two months before the end of the war in Europe the historic city was <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Hildesheim_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Hildesheim in World War II">bombed</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Area_Bombing_Directive" class="mw-redirect" title="Area Bombing Directive">Area Bombing Directive</a> in order to undermine the morale of the German people. 28.5% of the houses were completely destroyed and 44.7% damaged. 26.8% of the houses remained undamaged. The centre, which had retained its medieval character until then, was almost levelled. As in many cities, priority was given to rapid building of badly needed housing, and concrete structures took the place of the destroyed buildings. Fortunately, most of the major churches, two of them now UNESCO World Heritage Sites, were rebuilt in the original style soon after the war. During the war, valuable world heritage materials had been hidden in the basement of the city wall. In 1978, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hildesheim" title="University of Hildesheim">University of Hildesheim</a> was founded. In the 1980s a reconstruction of the historic centre began. Some of the unattractive concrete buildings around the market place were torn down and replaced by replicas of the original buildings. In the fall of 2007, a decision was made to reconstruct the <i>Umgestülpter Zuckerhut</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Upended_Sugarloaf,_Hildesheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Upended Sugarloaf, Hildesheim">Upended Sugarloaf</a>"), an iconic half-timbered house famous for its unusual shape. In 2015 the town celebrates 1200 years from 815 to 2015 with the <a href="/wiki/Day_of_Lower_Saxony" title="Day of Lower Saxony">Day of Lower Saxony</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religions">Religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hildesheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1542, most of the inhabitants became Lutherans. Today, 28.5% of the inhabitants self-identify as Roman Catholics (<a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Hildesheim" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Hildesheim">Hildesheim Diocese</a>) and 38.3% as Protestants (<a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_State_Church_of_Hanover" class="mw-redirect" title="Evangelical Lutheran State Church of Hanover">Lutheran State Church of Hanover</a>). 33.0% of the inhabitants are adherents of other religions or do not have a religion at all. The Serbian Orthodox bishop of <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Eparchy_of_Frankfurt_and_all_of_Germany" title="Serbian Orthodox Eparchy of Frankfurt and all of Germany">Frankfurt and all of Germany</a> has his seat in Himmelsthür (a locality of Hildesheim).</p>

<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Historic_Market_Place,_Hildesheim" title="Historic Market Place, Hildesheim">historic market place</a> (<i>Marktplatz</i>) was once considered one of the most beautiful market places in the world. It was reconstructed in 1984–1990 in its former splendour, after its destruction in the March 1945 air raid. The more noteworthy buildings in the square are:
<ul>
<li>The <i>Knochenhaueramtshaus</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Butchers%27_Guild_Hall,_Hildesheim" title="Butchers' Guild Hall, Hildesheim">Butchers' Guild Hall</a>"), known as a beautiful and fine specimen of half-timbered building. Originally built in 1529 and destroyed in 1945, it was reconstructed from 1987 to 1989 according to original plans. The façade is sumptuously decorated with colourful paintings and German proverbs. Today the building houses a restaurant and the City Museum.</li>
<li>The <i>Bäckeramtshaus</i> (Bakers' Guild Hall) is a half-timbered house which was originally built in 1825. It was destroyed in 1945 and rebuilt 1987-89. Today, it houses a café.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Historic_Market_Place,_Hildesheim" title="Historic Market Place, Hildesheim">Town Hall</a>, erected in the 13th century in <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> style. Partly destroyed in 1945, it was rebuilt and inaugurated in 1954.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Historic_Market_Place,_Hildesheim" title="Historic Market Place, Hildesheim">Tempelhaus</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Late-Gothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-Gothic">late-Gothic</a> 14th-century <a href="/wiki/Patricianship" class="mw-redirect" title="Patricianship">patrician</a> house, which today houses the tourist information office. It suffered some damage during the Second World War but was restored and inaugurated in 1950.</li>
<li>The <i>Wedekindhaus</i>, a 16th-century patrician house, is characterised by its high, ornately carved storeys including their ledges with depictions of allegorical figures.</li>
<li>The adjoining <i>Lüntzelhaus</i> was built in 1755 in <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> style.</li>
<li>The <i>Rolandhaus</i> was originally built in the 14th century in Gothic style. In 1730, the house was remodelled, and an impressive baroque portal and a large bay window were added.</li>
<li>The <i>Stadtschänke</i> (Old City Tavern) is a large half-timbered house which was originally built in 1666. The smaller adjoining <i>Rococcohaus</i> was built in 1730 in <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">rococo</a> style.</li>
<li>The <i>Wollenwebergildehaus</i> (Weavers' Guild Hall) was approximately built in 1600.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> <a href="/wiki/Hildesheim_Cathedral" title="Hildesheim Cathedral">Hildesheim Cathedral</a>, with its ancient bronze doors (<a href="/wiki/Bernward_Doors" title="Bernward Doors">Bernward Doors</a>) (c. 1015) and other treasures. The church was built in the 9th century, but almost completely destroyed in 1945; it was reconstructed soon after the war. It is listed as a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> since 1985. The "<a href="/wiki/Thousand-year_Rose" title="Thousand-year Rose">Thousand-year Rose</a>" is a reputedly 1,000‑year‑old <a href="/wiki/Dog_rose" class="mw-redirect" title="Dog rose">dog rose</a> bush, allegedly the world's oldest living <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">rose</a>. It continues to flourish on the wall of the cathedral apse.
<ul>
<li>Museum of the Cathedral: <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_Treasure" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral Treasure">Cathedral Treasure</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/St._Michael%27s_Church,_Hildesheim" title="St. Michael's Church, Hildesheim">St. Michael's Church</a> (UNESCO World Heritage Site) – a noteworthy early <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> church in Germany and a unique example of <a href="/wiki/Ottonian" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottonian">Ottonian</a> architecture. It was built from 1010 to 1022.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/St._Andreas,_Hildesheim" title="St. Andreas, Hildesheim">St. Andreas</a></i>, a 12th-century church with the <a href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_churches" class="mw-redirect" title="List of tallest churches">highest church steeple</a> (114,5&#160;m) in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a>. From the top you get an interesting view of Hildesheim and its surroundings. Opposite the church, the <a href="/wiki/Upended_Sugarloaf" title="Upended Sugarloaf">Upended Sugarloaf</a>, a famous half-timbered house dating from 1509, is worth a visit.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Roemer-und-Pelizaeus-Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Roemer-und-Pelizaeus-Museum">Roemer-und-Pelizaeus-Museum</a></i>, with significant collections from ancient <a href="/wiki/History_of_Egypt" title="History of Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Peru" title="History of Peru">Peru</a> and spectacular special exhibitions organized every year.</li>
<li>The <i>Kreuzkirche</i> (Church of the Holy Cross) was originally a part of the medieval fortifications. It was converted into a church around 1079, severely damaged in 1945 and rebuilt after the war.</li>
<li>The Romanesque <a href="/wiki/Minor_basilica" title="Minor basilica">Basilica minor</a> <a href="/wiki/St._Godehard,_Hildesheim" title="St. Godehard, Hildesheim">St. Godehard</a>, built 1133–1172, which is scheduled to become a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> World Heritage Site in the near future. Furthermore, there are some more sightworthy buildings opposite the church: <a href="/wiki/St._Nicolai%27s_Chapel,_Hildesheim" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Nicolai's Chapel, Hildesheim">St. Nicolai's Chapel</a> is a former parish church which was built in a Romanesque style in the 12th century and transformed into a residential building after 1803, and the <a href="/wiki/Hospital_of_the_Five_Wounds,_Hildesheim" title="Hospital of the Five Wounds, Hildesheim">Hospital of the Five Wounds</a> which is a large half-timbered house dating from 1770 with a <a href="/wiki/Hip_roof" title="Hip roof">half-hip roof</a>.</li>
<li>The church <a href="/wiki/St._Lamberti,_Hildesheim" title="St. Lamberti, Hildesheim">St. Lamberti</a> is a late Gothic building, venue of a weekly concert series at market time.</li>
<li>The <i>Kehrwiederturm</i> (Kehrwieder Tower), built around 1300, is the only remaining tower of the <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> fortifications.</li>
<li><i>Half-timbered houses</i> which were not destroyed during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> can be seen around <a href="/wiki/St._Godehard,_Hildesheim" title="St. Godehard, Hildesheim">St. Godehard</a> and the <i>Kehrwieder Tower</i>, in the streets Keßlerstraße, Knollenstraße, <a href="/wiki/Gelber_Stern_(Hildesheim)" title="Gelber Stern (Hildesheim)">Gelber Stern</a>, Am Kehrwieder, <a href="/wiki/Lappenberg_(Hildesheim)" title="Lappenberg (Hildesheim)">Lappenberg</a>, Brühl, Hinterer Brühl, and Godehardsplatz. Some of them have beautiful wood carvings in their façades, e.g. the <i>Wernersches Haus</i> (1606) in Godehardsplatz and the <i>Waffenschmiedehaus</i> (weapon smith house, 1548) at Gelber Stern.</li>
<li>The church <i>St. Jacobi</i> is a 500 years old pilgrim church and was one of the many St. James chapels at the <a href="/wiki/Camino_de_Santiago" title="Camino de Santiago">St. James's Path</a>. Today the Church is a Culture church with emphasis on literature.</li>
<li>The Monument of the Synagogue (consecrated in 1849 and destroyed in 1938 during the <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>) was erected in 1988 in the old Jewish quarter on <i><a href="/wiki/Lappenberg_(Hildesheim)" title="Lappenberg (Hildesheim)">Lappenberg</a></i> Street, one of the most beautiful streets in Hildesheim. The foundations were reconstructed and give you an idea of how big the synagogue was. The reddish brick building (built around 1840) opposite was the Jewish school.</li>
<li><i>Mauritiuskirche</i> (St. Maurice's Church), a Romanesque church (11th century) on a hill in the west of the city in the quarter of <a href="/wiki/Moritzberg_(Hildesheim)" title="Moritzberg (Hildesheim)">Moritzberg</a> with a beautiful cloister. The interior of the church is baroque and the tower was added in 1765. From the forest behind the church you get a beautiful view of Hildesheim with many different churches.</li>
<li><i>Kaiserhaus</i> (Emperor's House): <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> building (1586) in <i>Alter Markt</i>, the oldest street of Hildesheim, rebuilt after the war. The façade is decorated with Roman statues and medallions. Opposite, there is a noteworthy sandstone bay window dating from 1568. Originally, it belonged to a private house which was torn down at the end of the 19th century. The bay window was dismantled before and added to another house in the Eastern part of Hildesheim which remained undamaged during World War II. From there, the bay window was removed when the house was remodelled and added to the school at the present site in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>Close to the <i>Kaiserhaus</i> (Emperor's house) the <i>Alte Kemenate</i>, a noteworthy medieval store house reaching a height of 5.5&#160;meters, can be seen behind the school in the street Alter Markt. It has a rectangular basis measuring 6,5&#160;meters x 5,0&#160;meters and a cellar with a vaulted construction.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The store house, one of the oldest profane buildings in Hildesheim, was built of <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Gothic_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic (architecture)">gothic</a> style the 15th century. According to other sources it might be even older, i.e. built between the end of the 11th and the middle of the 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The building has two floors. As it did not consist of wood it did not burn down in 1945, but was only damaged and immediately repaired after the war. From 1945 - 1951 it was used for residential purposes. The <i>Alte Kemenate</i> is not open to the public, but you get a good impression from the school yard or from the small side street Schenkenstraße.</li>
<li>St. Magdalena's Church (<i>Magdalenenkirche</i>) is a small church with large <a href="/wiki/Lancet_windows" class="mw-redirect" title="Lancet windows">lancet windows</a> in the historic street Old Market (<i>Alter Markt</i>) which was consecrated in 1224. It was originally built in a Romanesque style, but enlarged and remodelled in a gothic style in 1456. It houses a wooden altar (about 1520) with carvings and other works of art. In the small street Süsternstraße, a well-preserved part of the medieval city wall with a round tower is worth a visit behind the church. Opposite the church, a tall half-timbered house which was rebuilt in 1981 on the medieval city wall can be seen in the small side street Mühlenstraße. The façade is decorated with wood carvings.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magdalenengarten" title="Magdalenengarten">Magdalenengarten</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> park which was laid out in 1720 - 1725, is near St. Magdalena's Church. There are many different kinds of rose bushes, a rose museum, pavilions, baroque statues, a well-preserved part of the medieval city wall in it and even a vineyard yielding 100 - 200 bottles of wine per year. In spring, a rare species of wild yellow tulips (tulipa sylvestris) blossoms in the western part of the park.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/St._Bernward%27s_Church,_Hildesheim" title="St. Bernward's Church, Hildesheim">St. Bernward's Church</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_Revival_architecture" title="Romanesque Revival architecture">neo-Romanesque</a> church built 1905-07, destroyed in 1945 and rebuilt from 1948–49, houses a gothic wooden altar <a href="/wiki/Retable" title="Retable">retable</a> dating from the beginning of the 15th century.</li>
<li>A part of the medieval defence system consisting of a wall with moats and ramparts which was built around the city in the 13th century is well-preserved. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kalenberger_Graben&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kalenberger Graben (page does not exist)">Kalenberger Graben</a>, an artificial lake, is a part of it. It is surrounded by a park with footpaths offering a scenic view of the city center and of many sights. In April a rare species of wilde yellow tulips (tulipa sylvestris) blossoms in the park.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Steuerwald_Castle,_Hildesheim" title="Steuerwald Castle, Hildesheim">Steuerwald Castle</a> (<i>Burg Steuerwald</i>) in the north of the city, about 3&#160;km from the Market Place, was built 1310–1313. Its tower (25&#160;m) was added in 1325. The chapel, dedicated to St Magdalena, was originally built in the Romanic style and transformed into a Gothic chapel in 1507. Today it is used for weddings and concerts.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marienburg_Castle,_Hildesheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Marienburg Castle, Hildesheim">Marienburg Castle</a> (<i>Burg Marienburg</i>) is in the southeast of Hildesheim, about 6&#160;km from the Market Place. It was built 1346-1349.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sorsum" title="Sorsum">Sorsum</a> is a former village in the West of Hildesheim which became a part of the city in 1974. There is a sightworthy former domain, founded in the Middle Ages, which was transformed into a residential area after 2000. The pigeon tower, built in 1733, the large barn (1786) and the impressive manor house dating from 1734 are interesting sights in this rural part of Hildesheim.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marienrode_Priory" title="Marienrode Priory">Marienrode Priory</a> (<i>Kloster Marienrode</i>) is in the southwest of Hildesheim, about 6&#160;km from the Market Place. It was founded in 1125. The foundation stone of the present church was laid in 1412. The church was built in gothic style with three naves and completed in 1462. The <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> ridge turret was added in the 18th century. In the church, there are two noteworthy baroque altars dating from 1750 approximately and a gothic sandstone sculpture of Saint Mary which was made in 1460. The organ dates from the middle of the 18th century. A small chapel of the priory, Saint Cosmas and Damian, which was built in 1792, was converted into a small Protestant church in 1830. The priory was dissolved in 1806, but returned to the Catholic Church in 1986. Since 1988, it has again been operated by nuns. Near the monastery there is a large fishpond with a scenic view and a tall windmill built in 1839. The area is especially beautiful at cherry blossom time.</li>
</ul>
<p>Other places of interest include the Theatre, offering opera, operetta and musicals, drama, ballet and concerts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hildesheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hildesheim is home to notable multinational corporations – besides many strong medium-sized companies – including <a href="/wiki/Blaupunkt" title="Blaupunkt">Blaupunkt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bosch_GmbH" title="Robert Bosch GmbH">Bosch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Krupp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thyssen_AG" title="Thyssen AG">Thyssen</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fairchild_Fasteners&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fairchild Fasteners (page does not exist)">Fairchild</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hildesheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hildesheim has an efficient traffic infrastructure: it is a regional hub for interstate roads and railway (<a href="/wiki/Hildesheim_Hauptbahnhof" title="Hildesheim Hauptbahnhof">Hildesheim Hauptbahnhof</a> is served by <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a> services), is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Motorway" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorway">motorway</a> (<a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a>), has a harbour at the artificial waterway <a href="/wiki/Mittellandkanal" class="mw-redirect" title="Mittellandkanal">Mittellandkanal</a> and Hildesheim Betriebsgesellschaf Airfield.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hildesheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The community has the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Eparchy_of_Frankfurt_and_all_of_Germany" title="Serbian Orthodox Eparchy of Frankfurt and all of Germany">Serbian Orthodox Eparchy of Frankfurt and all of Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

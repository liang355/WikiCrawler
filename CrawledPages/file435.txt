<p><b>Pforzheim</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ËpfÉÊtshaÉªÌ¯m]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Pforzheim.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6a/Pforzheim.ogg" class="internal" title="Pforzheim.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city of nearly 120,000 inhabitants in the federal state of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-WÃ¼rttemberg">Baden-WÃ¼rttemberg</a>, in the southwest of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is known for its jewelry and watch-making industry, and as such has gained the nickname "Goldstadt" ("Golden City"). It has an area of 97.8&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (38&#160;sq&#160;mi) and is situated between the cities of <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a> at the confluence of three rivers (<a href="/wiki/Enz" title="Enz">Enz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nagold_(river)" title="Nagold (river)">Nagold</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrm_(Nagold)" title="WÃ¼rm (Nagold)">WÃ¼rm</a>) and marks the frontier between <a href="/wiki/Baden" title="Baden">Baden</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="WÃ¼rttemberg">WÃ¼rttemberg</a>, being located on Baden territory. From 1535 to 1565 it was the home to the <a href="/wiki/Margraves_of_Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Margraves of Baden">Margraves of Baden</a>-Pforzheim.</p>
<p>The City of Pforzheim does not belong to any administrative district (<i>Kreis</i>), although it hosts the administrative offices of the <a href="/wiki/Enz_(district)" class="mw-redirect" title="Enz (district)">Enz</a> district that surrounds the town.</p>
<p>During World War II, Pforzheim was bombed a number of times. The largest raid, and one of the most devastating <a href="/wiki/Area_bombardment" title="Area bombardment">area bombardments</a> of World War II, was carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> (RAF) on the evening of 23 February 1945. Nearly a third of the town's population, 17,600 people, were killed in the air raid, and about 83% of the town's buildings were destroyed. The town was thought by the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> to be producing precision instruments for use in the German war effort and to be a transport centre for the movement of German troops. The story of the bombardment is dramatically recounted in the 2011 history book by <a href="/wiki/Giles_Milton" title="Giles Milton">Giles Milton</a>, entitled <a href="/wiki/Wolfram:_The_Boy_Who_Went_To_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Wolfram: The Boy Who Went To War">Wolfram: The Boy Who Went To War</a>. In the twenty years following the end of the war, Pforzheim was gradually rebuilt, giving the town a quite modern look and making it home to some landmark buildings of the 1950s.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pforzheim is located at the northern rim of the eastern part of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a> (Schwarzwald) and the rim of the hilly country of the <a href="/wiki/Kraichgau" title="Kraichgau">Kraichgau</a>, in an open valley at the confluences of the rivers <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrm" title="WÃ¼rm">WÃ¼rm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nagold" title="Nagold">Nagold</a> and the rivers Nagold and <a href="/wiki/Enz" title="Enz">Enz</a>. Due to its location, this city is also called the "three-valleys town" (<i>Drei-TÃ¤ler Stadt</i>) or the "Gateway to the Black Forest" (<i>Pforte zum Schwarzwald / Porta Hercynia</i>). The early settlement (in fact much earlier than the current centers <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>) by the Romans, who constructed a <a href="/wiki/Ford_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford (river)">ford</a> through the river, shortly past the confluence of the three rivers, for their military highway, is also due to this extraordinary geography. Due to this location, Pforzheim later on became a center for the <a href="/wiki/Timber_rafting" title="Timber rafting">timber-rafting</a> trade which transported timber from the Black Forest via the rivers Wuerm, Nagold, Enz and then the <a href="/wiki/Neckar" title="Neckar">Neckar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> to, among other destinations, the Netherlands for use in shipbuilding and the construction of <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> on poles in a swamp.</p>
<p>Pforzheim and its surrounding area belongs to the "Densely Populated Area Karlsruhe/Pforzheim". Pforzheim has the functions of a regional center (Mittelzentrum) for the towns and municipalities <a href="/wiki/Birkenfeld_(Enz)" title="Birkenfeld (Enz)">Birkenfeld (Enz)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eisingen" title="Eisingen">Eisingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engelsbrand" title="Engelsbrand">Engelsbrand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friolzheim" title="Friolzheim">Friolzheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heimsheim" title="Heimsheim">Heimsheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ispringen" title="Ispringen">Ispringen</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A4mpfelbach" title="KÃ¤mpfelbach">KÃ¤mpfelbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keltern" title="Keltern">Keltern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kieselbronn" title="Kieselbronn">Kieselbronn</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsbach-Stein" title="KÃ¶nigsbach-Stein">KÃ¶nigsbach-Stein</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6nsheim" title="MÃ¶nsheim">MÃ¶nsheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuenb%C3%BCrg" title="NeuenbÃ¼rg">NeuenbÃ¼rg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuhausen_(Enz)" title="Neuhausen (Enz)">Neuhausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neulingen" title="Neulingen">Neulingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niefern-%C3%96schelbronn" title="Niefern-Ãschelbronn">Niefern-Ãschelbronn</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%96lbronn-D%C3%BCrrn" title="Ãlbronn-DÃ¼rrn">Ãlbronn-DÃ¼rrn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remchingen" title="Remchingen">Remchingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Straubenhardt" title="Straubenhardt">Straubenhardt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiefenbronn" title="Tiefenbronn">Tiefenbronn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiernsheim" title="Wiernsheim">Wiernsheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wimsheim" title="Wimsheim">Wimsheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wurmberg" title="Wurmberg">Wurmberg</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_wikipedia"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_wikipedia">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Since 90:</b> A settlement was established by Roman citizens at the Enz river near the modern <i>AltstÃ¤dter BrÃ¼cke</i> (old town bridge). Archeological surveys have unearthed several items from that period which are kept and displayed in the Kappelhof Museum. The settlement was located where the Roman military road connecting the military camp <a href="/wiki/Argentoratum" title="Argentoratum">Argentoratum</a> (nowadays <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> in France) and the military camp at <a href="/wiki/Cannstatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Cannstatt">Cannstatt</a> (now a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>) at the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Germanic_Limes" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Germanic Limes">Upper Germanic Limes</a> border line of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> crossed the <a href="/wiki/Enz" title="Enz">Enz</a> river. This place was known as Portus (river crossing, harbor), which is believed to be the origin of the first part of the city's name "Pforzheim". A Roman milestone (the so-called 'Leugenstein') from the year 245 and later excavated at nowadays <a href="/wiki/Friolzheim" title="Friolzheim">Friolzheim</a> shows the exact distance to 'Portus'; it is the first document about the settlement.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist6"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>259/260</b>: The Roman settlement 'Portus' was destroyed completely, as the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alemanni" title="Alemanni">Alemanni</a> tribes overrun the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Germanic_Limes" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Germanic Limes">Upper Germanic Limes</a> border line of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> and conquered the Roman administrated area west of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> river. From then on, over an extended period of time historical records about the settlement are not available.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>6th/7th century</b>: Graves from this period indicate that the settlement had been continued.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist2"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist2">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1067:</b> The settlement of Pforzheim was mentioned for the first time in a document by <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry IV, Holy Roman Emperor</a> as "Phorzheim". Visits to Pforzheim by Heinrich IV in 1067 and 1074 are documented.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist2"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist2">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Before 1080:</b> The "old town" of Pforzheim was awarded <a href="/wiki/Market_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Market rights">market rights</a> (Marktrecht). At that time Pforzheim belonged to the estate of <a href="/wiki/Hirsau" title="Hirsau">Hirsau</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">Monastery</a>, according to monastery documents.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>From 1150:</b> Establishment of the "new town" west of the "old town" at the foot of the Schlossberg (palais hill) under Margrave <a href="/wiki/Hermann_V,_Margrave_of_Baden-Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Hermann V, Margrave of Baden-Baden">Hermann V</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1200:</b> The town charter of the "new town" was mentioned for the first time in a document. The "old town" continued to exist as a legally independent entity.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1220</b>: The <a href="/wiki/Rulers_of_Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Rulers of Baden">Margraves of Baden</a> selected Pforzheim as their residence. The "new town" became prominent.</p>
<p><b>1240:</b> A mayor of Pforzheim was mentioned in a document for the first time.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>13th/14th century:</b> Pforzheim enjoyed its first period of flourishment. A group of influential <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(post-Roman_Europe)" title="Patrician (post-Roman Europe)">patricians</a> emerged. They developed extensive activities on the financial markets of those days. The town drew its income from the wood trade, <a href="/wiki/Timber_rafting" title="Timber rafting">timber rafting</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tanning_(leather)" title="Tanning (leather)">tannery</a> trade, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> manufacturing and other crafts. Documents mention <i>mayor, judge, council and citizens</i>. The town walls surrounding the new town were completed at about 1290. During this era three <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Holy_Orders" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Orders">orders</a> established their <a href="/wiki/Convents" class="mw-redirect" title="Convents">convents</a> in town (the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> order established their domicile within the town wall at nowadays Barfuesserkirche (the choir of which remains), the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominican order">Dominican</a> <a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">nun</a> order established their domicile outside of the walls of the old town near Auer bridge, and the Prediger cloister was located east of the Schlossberg, probably inside the town walls). Outside of the town wall across the Enz river, the suburb <i>FlÃ¶sser Quarters</i> (the home of the timber floating trade) was established. Next to the western town wall, the suburb of <i>BrÃ¶tzingen</i> gradually developed. The Margraves of Baden considered Pforzheim as their most important power base up to the first half of the 14th century. Under Margrave <a href="/wiki/Bernard_I,_Margrave_of_Baden-Baden" title="Bernard I, Margrave of Baden-Baden">Bernard I</a> (Bernhard I) Pforzheim became one of the administrative centers of the margraviate.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist3"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist3">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist14"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist14">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist13"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist13">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1322:</b> Holy Ghost Hospital was founded at TrÃ¤nk Street (nowadays Deimling Street).<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist4"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist4">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>15th century:</b> Various <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">fraternities</a> among people working in the same trade were established: The fraternity of <a href="/wiki/Tailor" title="Tailor">tailors</a> in 1410, the fraternity of <a href="/wiki/Baker" title="Baker">bakers</a> on 14 May 1422, the fraternity of the <a href="/wiki/Weaver_(occupation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Weaver (occupation)">weavers</a> in 1469, the fraternity of the <a href="/wiki/Wine-grower" class="mw-redirect" title="Wine-grower">wine-growers</a> in 1491, the fraternity of the <a href="/wiki/Skipper_(boating)" class="mw-redirect" title="Skipper (boating)">skippers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timber_raftsmen" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber raftsmen">timber raftsmen</a> in 1501, and the fraternity of the <a href="/wiki/Carting_business" class="mw-redirect" title="Carting business">carters</a> in 1512. Members of the same fraternity assisted each other in various ways, for example with <a href="/wiki/Funerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Funerals">funerals</a> and in cases of sickness. In a sense, the fraternities were early forms of health and <a href="/wiki/Life_insurance" title="Life insurance">life insurance</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist4"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist4">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>8â9 August 1418:</b> <a href="/wiki/Sigismund,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor">Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor</a> visits <a href="/wiki/Margrave" title="Margrave">Margrave</a> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_I,_Margrave_of_Baden-Baden" title="Bernard I, Margrave of Baden-Baden">Bernard I</a> (Bernhard I) in Pforzheim. On this occasion the <a href="/wiki/Mint_(coin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mint (coin)">mint</a> of the Margraves of Baden in Pforzheim was mentioned. Mint master was Jakob Broeglin between 1414â1431. The emperor appointed the master of the Pforzheim mint, Jakob BrÃ¶glin, and Bois von der Winterbach for five years as Royal Mint Masters of the mints of <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> and <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B6rdlingen" title="NÃ¶rdlingen">NÃ¶rdlingen</a>. The Margrave was appointed as their patron.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist5"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist5">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist13"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist13">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1447:</b> The wedding of <a href="/wiki/Margrave" title="Margrave">Margrave</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_I,_Margrave_of_Baden-Baden" title="Charles I, Margrave of Baden-Baden">Charles I</a> (Karl I) of Baden with <a href="/wiki/Katharina" title="Katharina">Katharina</a> of Austria, the sister of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick III, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick III, Holy Roman Emperor</a> (Friedrich III), was celebrated in Pforzheim with great pomp (including <a href="/wiki/Tournaments" class="mw-redirect" title="Tournaments">tournaments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dances" class="mw-redirect" title="Dances">dances</a>).<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist7"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist7">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist12"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist12">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist13"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist13">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>1455:</b> <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Reuchlin" class="mw-redirect" title="Johannes Reuchlin">Johannes Reuchlin</a>, the great German <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a>, was born in Pforzheim on 29 January (he died in Stuttgart on 30 June 1522). He attended the <a href="/wiki/Latin_school" title="Latin school">Latin School</a> section of the monastery school run by the Dominican order of Pforzheim in the late 1460s. Later, partly due to Reuchlin's efforts, the <a href="/wiki/Latin_school" title="Latin school">Latin School</a> of Pforzheim developed into one of the most prominent schools in southwestern Germany, named <i><a href="/wiki/Reuchlin-Gymnasium" title="Reuchlin-Gymnasium">Reuchlin-Gymnasium</a></i>. The school's teachers and pupils played an outstanding role in the dissemination of the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant reformation">protestant reformation</a> movement. The most famous pupils included Reuchlin himself, Reuchlin's nephew <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Melanchthon" class="mw-redirect" title="Philipp Melanchthon">Philipp Melanchthon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Simon_Grynaeus" title="Simon Grynaeus">Simon Grynaeus</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist8"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist8">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist12"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist12">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1460:</b> Margrave Charles I established a kind of monastery (Kollegialstift) at the site of Schlosskirche St. Michael, turning the church into a <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_church" title="Collegiate church">collegiate church</a>. There were also plans to establish a university in Pforzheim, but this plan had to be abandoned because Margrave Charles I lost the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Seckenheim" title="Battle of Seckenheim">Battle of Seckenheim</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist9"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist9">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist10"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist10">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist12"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist12">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1463:</b> Margrave Charles I was forced to transfer the palace and the town of Pforzheim as a <a href="/wiki/Fiefdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiefdom">fiefdom</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Elector_Palatine" class="mw-redirect" title="Elector Palatine">Elector Palatine</a> after losing the Battle of Seckenheim. He then began to build a new palace in modern <a href="/wiki/Baden-Baden" title="Baden-Baden">Baden-Baden</a>. <a href="/wiki/Christoph_I,_Margrave_of_Baden-Baden" title="Christoph I, Margrave of Baden-Baden">Christoph I, Margrave of Baden-Baden</a> finally moved the residence of the margraves to Baden-Baden. This gradually ended the first period of Pforzheim's flourishment. The rich merchants gradually left the town, which declined to the status of a country town of mostly small traders. <sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist10"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist10">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist3"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist3">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist12"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist12">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1486:</b> The <i>Weavers Ordinance</i> (<i>Wollweberordnung</i>) for the towns Pforzheim und <a href="/wiki/Ettlingen" title="Ettlingen">Ettlingen</a> was approved by <a href="/wiki/Christoph_I,_Margrave_of_Baden-Baden" title="Christoph I, Margrave of Baden-Baden">Christoph I, Margrave of Baden-Baden</a>. This was a contract concerning the town privileges of Pforzheim. This regulation of the weaving trade did not allow the formation of a regular <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guild</a> (Zunft).<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist11"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist11">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist3"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist3">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1491:</b> A contract between <a href="/wiki/Christoph_I,_Margrave_of_Baden-Baden" title="Christoph I, Margrave of Baden-Baden">Christoph I, Margrave of Baden-Baden</a> and the citizens of Pforzheim was concluded, granting the town of Pforzheim several privileges concerning taxes and business.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist11"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist11">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist12"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist12">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1496:</b> Foundation of the first printer's shop by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Anshelm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thomas Anshelm (page does not exist)">Thomas Anshelm</a>. During the first half of the 16th century Pforzheim's printers contributed significantly to the establishment of this (in those days) new medium.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1501</b>: <a href="/wiki/Christoph_I,_Margrave_of_Baden-Baden" title="Christoph I, Margrave of Baden-Baden">Christoph I, Margrave of Baden-Baden</a> enacted the "<a href="/wiki/Local_ordinance" title="Local ordinance">Ordinance</a> on the timber rafting profession in Pforzheim". The single timber logs that were floated from the deeper Black Forest areas down the Enz, Nagold and Wuerm rivers were bound together in the Au area to form larger timber rafts. Those rafts were then floated down the lower Enz, Neckar and Rhine rivers. The timber rafting stations of Weissenstein, Dillstein and Pforzheim were well known in the profession.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist12"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist12">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>1501 was also the year for which an outbreak of the plague (probably the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a>) is recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabian</a> <a href="/wiki/Chronicle" title="Chronicle">chronicle</a> <i>Annalium Suevicorum</i> by <a href="/wiki/Eberhard_Karls_University_of_T%C3%BCbingen" class="mw-redirect" title="Eberhard Karls University of TÃ¼bingen">Eberhard Karls University of TÃ¼bingen</a> professor Martin Grusius, published 1596. It is not known how many of Pforzheim's citizens died in that year, but there are reports of 500 deceased in the close-by city of <a href="/wiki/Calw" title="Calw">Calw</a> and about 4000 in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, which accounted for approximately one quarter to one half of the populations of those towns. Outbreaks of the disease were reported for many places in southwestern Germany, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a> region in nowadays France, Switzerland, and Italy. <a href="/wiki/Mass_grave" title="Mass grave">Common graves</a> with massive numbers of human bones at the cemetery of St. Michael Church and the cemetery on the estate of the Dominican order near nowadays Waisenhausplatz found during the last century may indicate that hundreds of citizens became the victims of the plague. There are indications that a <a href="/wiki/Fraternal_and_service_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraternal and service organizations">fraternity</a> for taking care of the sick and removing the bodies of the deceased from houses was formed in 1501, whose members later on stayed together and became known as the <a href="/wiki/Choral_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Choral society">choral society</a> <i>Singergesellschaft</i>, which is still active today as the <i>Loebliche Singergesellschaft of 1501</i>. (They are probably one of the oldest clubs in Europe).<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist4"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist4">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1520s:</b> The ideas of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Religious" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious">religious</a> movement advanced by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> spread rapidly in Pforzheim. Its most prominent promoters were Johannes Schwebel, a preacher at Holy Ghost church (Heiliggeistkirche), and Johannes Unger, the principal of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominican order">Dominican</a> <a href="/wiki/Latin_school" title="Latin school">Latin school</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1535â1565:</b> Due to the <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">heritage</a> division of the clan of the Margraves of Baden, <a href="/wiki/Margrave" title="Margrave">Margrave</a> <a href="/wiki/Ernest,_Margrave_of_Baden-Durlach" title="Ernest, Margrave of Baden-Durlach">Ernst of Baden</a> made Pforzheim the residential town of his family line. He decided to use the Schlosskirche St. Michael as the entombment site for his family line.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1549:</b> A large fire caused severe damage to the town.</p>
<p><b>1556:</b> After the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Augsburg" title="Peace of Augsburg">Peace of Augsburg</a> in 1555, <a href="/wiki/Margrave" title="Margrave">Margrave</a> <a href="/wiki/Karl_II,_Margrave_of_Baden-Durlach" class="mw-redirect" title="Karl II, Margrave of Baden-Durlach">Karl II</a> introduced <i>Lutherism</i> (<a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">protestantism</a>) as the state religion in the district <a href="/wiki/Baden-Durlach" class="mw-redirect" title="Baden-Durlach">Baden-Durlach</a>, which included Pforzheim. The (Catholic) monasteries were gradually shut down.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1565:</b> Margrave Karl II chose Durlach as the new residential town. Pforzheim stayed one of the administrative centers of Baden.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1618</b>: At the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, the number of inhabitants of Pforzheim is estimated to have been between 2500 and 3000. This was the largest town among all towns in Baden, even though at that time it had already declined somewhat.</p>

<p><b>1645</b>: Toward the end of the Thirty Years' War the "old town" was burned down by <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> (i.e. Catholic) troops. It was rebuilt, but without the former <a href="/wiki/Fortifications" class="mw-redirect" title="Fortifications">fortifications</a>, which gave it the status of a village-like settlement. It soon vanished from historical records. The "new town" had survived.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1688â1697:</b> The "<a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Palatinian_Succession" class="mw-redirect" title="War of the Palatinian Succession">War of the Palatinian Succession</a>" (also called the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Nine Years War">Nine Years War</a>) caused tremendous destruction in Southwestern Germany. The French "sun king" <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>'s efforts to expand the territory of France up to the Upper Rhine river and to put the <a href="/wiki/Elector_Palatine" class="mw-redirect" title="Elector Palatine">Elector Palatine</a> under pressure to severe its ties with the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Augsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="League of Augsburg">League of Augsburg</a> included the <i><a href="/wiki/Br%C3%BBlez_le_Palatinat!" class="mw-redirect" title="BrÃ»lez le Palatinat!">BrÃ»lez le Palatinat!</a></i> tactics of destroying major towns on both sides of the Rhine river. These tactics seem to have been mainly the idea of the French war minister, FranÃ§ois Michel le Tellier, <a href="/wiki/Marquis_de_Louvois" class="mw-redirect" title="Marquis de Louvois">Marquis de Louvois</a>.</p>
<p>Pforzheim was occupied by French troops on 10 October 1688. Commanding officer is said to have been <a href="/wiki/Joseph_de_Montclar" title="Joseph de Montclar">Joseph de Montclar</a>. The town was forced to accommodate a large number of soldiers and had to pay a large amount of "contributions" to the French. When the army unit was about to depart early in the morning of 21 January 1689 (obviously because an army of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> had been approaching), they set many major buildings on fire, including the palais, the city hall, and vicarages. About 70 houses (i.e. one quarter of all houses) and part of the town's fortifications were reportedly destroyed.</p>
<p>Between 2 and 4 August, the French army under the general command of <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshal of France">Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Henri_de_Durfort_de_Duras" title="Jacques Henri de Durfort de Duras">Jacques Henri de Durfort de Duras</a> again crossed the Rhine river and began the destruction of major towns in Baden. On 10 August 1689, a French army unit under the command of General <a href="/wiki/Ez%C3%A9chiel_du_Mas,_Comte_de_M%C3%A9lac" title="EzÃ©chiel du Mas, Comte de MÃ©lac">EzÃ©chiel du Mas, Comte de MÃ©lac</a> <i>appeared in front of Pforzheims town gates, but this time the town refused to surrender. In response, the French army began shelling the town with cannons from the Rod hill located southwest of the town, and the several hundred soldiers of the German imperial command, who were defending the town, were forced to surrender. After a short period of looting, the French troops set the inner town area on fire on 15 August, which made that area uninhabitable for several weeks. Then the French moved on.</i></p>
<p>During the following two years, French troops stayed away from Pforzheim, but the economic situation of the town was miserable. In addition to this, the reconstruction of the town and the repairs of the fortifications under the supervision of Johann Matthaeus Faulhaber, the chief construction officer of the Margraviate Baden, required a lot of efforts. The accommodation of an imperial garrison under the command of (then) colonel Count <a href="/wiki/Palffy" class="mw-redirect" title="Palffy">Palffy</a> also was a heavy burden.</p>
<p>In 1691, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Michel_le_Tellier,_Marquis_de_Louvois" title="FranÃ§ois-Michel le Tellier, Marquis de Louvois">Louvois</a> instructed his marshals to destroy those towns which were to serve as winter quarters for imperial troops, explicitly including Pforzheim, and then continue to Wuerttemberg for further destructions. After the French troops had crossed the Rhine river under the command of Marshal <a href="/wiki/Guy_Aldonce_de_Durfort_de_Lorges" title="Guy Aldonce de Durfort de Lorges">Guy Aldonce de Durfort de Lorges</a> at <a href="/wiki/Philippsburg" title="Philippsburg">Philippsburg</a> on 3 August 1691, they assaulted the Margraves' residential town of <a href="/wiki/Durlach" title="Durlach">Durlach</a> and 1,200 <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> men, 300 <a href="/wiki/Dragoons" class="mw-redirect" title="Dragoons">dragoons</a> and 1,200 <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> men advanced toward Pforzheim where they arrived in the morning on 9 August and surrounded the town. When the approximately 200 imperial soldiers under the command of Captain Zickwolf and other men in the town refused to surrender, the siege began. After shelling the town during the day and the following night, the resistance of the town broke down and on 10 August in the morning the French forced the town gates open, occupied and looted it (although with little success, as there was not much left to be taken away). On 12 August, the French moved on, this time refraining from setting houses on fire. The fortification had again been damaged, though (the White Tower, the Auer Bridge Gate, the Upper Mill and the Nonnen Mill were burnt down). The French also stole all church bells, except for one minor one.</p>
<p>On 20 September 1692, again crossed the Rhine river under the general command of <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshal of France">Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/Guy_Aldonce_de_Durfort_de_Lorges" title="Guy Aldonce de Durfort de Lorges">Guy Aldonce de Durfort de Lorges</a>, and advanced toward <a href="/wiki/Durlach" title="Durlach">Durlach</a> and Pforzheim. On 24 September, 2,000 cavalry soldiers and 1,200 infantry and artillery troops under the command of <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshal of France">Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Bouton_de_Chamilly" title="NoÃ«l Bouton de Chamilly">NoÃ«l Bouton de Chamilly</a>, moved to Pforzheim, where the town and 600 soldiers of the imperial German army in town surrendered without any military engagements. The rest of the French army arrived on 27 September under the command of Marshal de Lorges. On the same day, the French army moved on to Oetisheim near <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BChlacker" title="MÃ¼hlacker">MÃ¼hlacker</a> and attacked an imperial army unit of 4,000 cavalry men under the command of Duke <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Charles_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg-Winnental" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick Charles of WÃ¼rttemberg-Winnental">Frederick Charles of WÃ¼rttemberg-Winnental</a> in their camp. As they were taken by surprise, they withdrew hastily and lost several hundred men, either killed or captured by the French. (The Duke himself was among the French prisoners.) On 28 September, the French army returned to Pforzheim and established a camp. It was reported that the entire <a href="/wiki/Enz" title="Enz">Enz</a> valley between the village of Eutingen east of Pforzheim and the village of Birkenfeld west of Pforzheim was occupied by the 30,000 French soldiers' camps. From their base in Pforzheim, French army units obviously under the leadership of Marshal de Chamilly advanced along the river valleys of <a href="/wiki/Nagold" title="Nagold">Nagold</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrm_(Nagold)" title="WÃ¼rm (Nagold)">WÃ¼rm</a> and looted and destroyed the villages and towns of Huchenfeld, <a href="/wiki/Calw" title="Calw">Calw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hirsau" title="Hirsau">Hirsau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liebenzell" class="mw-redirect" title="Liebenzell">Liebenzell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zavelstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Zavelstein">Zavelstein</a>. They also destroyed <a href="/w/index.php?title=Liebeneck_castle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Liebeneck castle (page does not exist)">Liebeneck castle</a> about 10&#160;kilometres from Pforzheim towering above the WÃ¼rm valley, where part of the Pforzheim town archives were hidden. The archive was burned. Another part of the town archive as well as documents of Baden administrative office had been brought to Calw, where they went up in flames, too.</p>
<p>When the French troops left after about one week of occupation, they again looted Pforzheim and put it on fire. This time, all houses which had survived the two previous fires, were destroyed. In the Au suburb, only three houses survived. The Au bridge was heavily damaged. Only four houses survived in the Broetzingen suburb. The town church of St. Stephen and a large part of the Dominican monastery complex were also destroyed. The Castle Church (Schlosskirche) of St. Michael was heavily damaged, and the family tombs of the Margraves of Baden in the church were desecrated by the soldiers. The last remaining church bell and the churches' clockworks were stolen as well. The town wall was damaged again, including the town gates. After the week-long presence of 30,000 soldiers in a town of only a few thousand citizens, all food was gone, including the seeds saved for next spring's sowing season. Every tree and <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grapevine</a> on the valley slopes had been used up as firewood. The French army reached their camp in <a href="/wiki/Philippsburg" title="Philippsburg">Philippsburg</a> on 5 October 1692.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist15"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist15">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1700s">1700s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1700s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>1718:</b> Inauguration of the "institution for orphans, the mad, the sick, for discipline and work" in a building of the former <a href="/wiki/Dominican_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominican order">Dominican order</a> <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">Convent</a> by the Enz river. Fifty years later this institution was to become the incubator of Pforzheim's jewellery and watchmaking industries.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1715â1730:</b> During this period, there was a prolonged dispute between Pforzheim's citizens and the Margrave of Baden concerning the privileges granted to the town in 1491, which the Margrave considered obsolete and therefore demanded significantly higher tax payments from Pforzheim citizens. The issue was taken all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Court_of_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Court of Justice">Imperial Court of Justice</a>, where the town's motion was defeated.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist15"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist15">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1767:</b> Establishment of a watch and jewellery factory in the orphanage. This led to Pforzheim's jewellery industries. Watchmaking was given up later on.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1800s">1800s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1800s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>1805â06</b>: Typhus epidemic in Pforzheim.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1809</b>: The Administrative District Pforzheim of Baden was split into a <i>Municipal District Administration Pforzheim</i> and two Rural Districts.</p>
<p><b>1813</b>: The two Rural Districts were combined to form the <i>Rural District Administration Pforzheim</i>.</p>
<p><b>1819</b>: <i>Municipal District Pforzheim</i> and <i>Rural District Pforzheim</i> are merged to form the <i>Higher District Administration Pforzheim</i>.</p>
<p><b>1836</b>: <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_%C3%96chsle" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferdinand Ãchsle">Ferdinand Ãchsle</a> in Pforzheim invented a device for measuring the sugar content in freshly <a href="/wiki/Pressed_(wine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pressed (wine)">pressed</a> grape juice for assessing the future quality of <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> (Mostwaage). It is still in use in the winery business.</p>
<p><b>1861â62:</b> Pforzheim was connected to the <a href="/wiki/German_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="German railway">German railway</a> network with the completion of a section of the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe%E2%80%93M%C3%BChlacker_railway" title="KarlsruheâMÃ¼hlacker railway">KarlsruheâMÃ¼hlacker line</a> between <a href="/wiki/Wilferdingen" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilferdingen">Wilferdingen</a> and Pforzheim.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1863</b>: The railway section between Pforzheim and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BChlacker" title="MÃ¼hlacker">MÃ¼hlacker</a> was completed, thus establishing railway traffic between the capital of <a href="/wiki/Baden" title="Baden">Baden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>, and the capital of <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="WÃ¼rttemberg">WÃ¼rttemberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1864</b>: The <i>Higher District Administration Pforzheim</i> was made the <i>Regional Administration Pforzheim</i>.</p>
<p><b>1868</b>: The <a href="/wiki/Enz_Valley_Railway" title="Enz Valley Railway">Enz Valley Railway</a> between Pforzheim and <a href="/wiki/Wildbad" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildbad">Wildbad</a> was completed.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1869:</b> Establishment of the first workers' union in Pforzheim, the "Pforzheim Gold(-metal) Craftsmen's Union".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1874</b>: The section of the <a href="/wiki/Nagold_Valley_Railway" title="Nagold Valley Railway">Nagold Valley Railway</a> between Pforzheim and <a href="/wiki/Calw" title="Calw">Calw</a> was completed.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1877:</b> Inauguration of the Arts and Crafts School (<a href="/wiki/Kunstgewerbeschule" title="Kunstgewerbeschule">Kunstgewerbeschule</a>; now incorporated into Hochschule (University) Pforzheim).<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1888:</b> <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Benz" title="Bertha Benz">Bertha Benz</a> and her two sons arrived in Pforzheim on the first "long-distance" drive in the history of the automobile in a car manufactured by her husband <a href="/wiki/Carl_Benz" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl Benz">Carl Benz</a> in order to visit relatives. She had started her drive in <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>, which is located about 106 kilometres (66 miles) from Pforzheim. The very first <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobile</a> with an <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> of the inventor had hit the roads only two years earlier after a <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> for this new technology had been granted to Karl Benz on 29 January 1886. She bought the gasoline necessary for her trip back home in a "<a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacy</a>" in Pforzheim. During the trip, Bertha Benz had to make repairs with a <a href="/wiki/Hairpin_(fashion)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hairpin (fashion)">hairpin</a> to open a blocked fuel line, and after returning home, suggested to her husband that another <a href="/wiki/Gear" title="Gear">gear</a> be provided in his automobile for climbing hills.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[61]</a></sup> To commemorate this first long-distance journey by automobile, the <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Benz_Memorial_Route" title="Bertha Benz Memorial Route">Bertha Benz Memorial Route</a> was officially approved as a route of industrial heritage of mankind in 2008. Now everybody can follow the 194&#160;km (121 miles) of signposted route from <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a> via <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> to Pforzheim and back.</p>
<p><b>1893:</b> Inauguration of the Pforzheim Synagogue.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>The company <a href="/wiki/Wellendorff" title="Wellendorff">Wellendorff</a>, a family-owned jewellery producing until now, is founded by Ernst Alexander Wellendorff. The enterprise sells many kinds of jewelry at the highest level worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>From <b>1933:</b> Along with the installation of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> government in Germany the local subsidiaries of all political parties, groups and organizations other than the <a href="/wiki/NSDAP" class="mw-redirect" title="NSDAP">NSDAP</a> were gradually disbanded in town. Persecution of Jewish fellow citizens occurred in Pforzheim, too, with boycotts of Jewish shops and companies.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1938:</b> Establishment of the municipal Jewellery Museum.</p>
<p><b>1938:</b> On 9 November, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>, the Pforzheim <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">Synagogue</a> (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.edwardvictor.com/pforzheim_main.htm">see WWW-site</a>) of the Jewish community was so badly damaged by <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazi</a> activists that it had to be demolished later on.</p>
<p><b>1939:</b> <i>Regional Administration Pforzheim</i> (Bezirksamt) was converted to the <i>Rural District Pforzheim</i> (Landkreis) with Pforzheim city as its administrative site. However, the town itself became a district-less administrative body.</p>
<p><b>1940:</b> <a href="/wiki/Deportation" title="Deportation">Deportation</a> of Jewish citizens of Pforzheim to the <a href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Concentration camp">concentration camp</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gurs" title="Gurs">Gurs</a> (France). Only 55 of the 195 deported persons escaped from the <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">holocaust</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl>
<dd>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Pforzheim_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Pforzheim in World War II">Bombing of Pforzheim in World War II</a></div>
</dd>
</dl>
<p><b>1944:</b> Many factories were converted to produce weaponry such as anti-aircraft <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)" title="Shell (projectile)">shells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuse_(explosives)" title="Fuse (explosives)">fuzes</a> for bombs, and allegedly even parts for the <a href="/wiki/V-1_flying_bomb" title="V-1 flying bomb">V1</a> and <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V2</a> rockets.</p>
<p><b>1945:</b> On 23 February, Pforzheim was bombed in one of the most devastating <a href="/wiki/Area_bombardment" title="Area bombardment">area bombardments</a> of World War II. It was carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> (RAF) on the evening of 23 February 1945.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[69]</a></sup> About one quarter of the town's population, over 17,000 people, was killed in the air raid,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist18"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist18">[70]</a></sup> and about 83% of the town's buildings were destroyed.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist19"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist19">[71]</a></sup> The mission order to bomb Pforzheim issued by <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">RAF Bomber Command</a> states as the intention of the raid on Pforzheim "to destroy built up area and associated industries and rail facilities".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist18"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist18">[72]</a></sup> The bombardment was carried out as part of the British <a href="/wiki/Carpet_bombing" title="Carpet bombing">carpet bombing</a> campaign. The town was put on the target list for bombardments in November 1944 because it was thought by the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> to be producing precision instruments for use in the German war effort and as transport centre<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_station"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_station">[73]</a></sup> for the movement of German troops.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist27"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist27">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were also several minor raids in 1944 and 1945.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist26"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist26">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the main attack, about 30,000 people had to be fed by makeshift public kitchens because their housing had been destroyed. Almost 90% of the buildings in the core city area had been destroyed. Many Pforzheim citizens were buried in mass graves at Pforzheim's main cemetery because they could not be identified. There are also many graves of complete families. Among the dead were several hundred foreigners who had been in Pforzheim as <a href="/wiki/Forced_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced labor">forced labor</a> workers. The inner-city districts were severely depopulated. According to the State Statistics Bureau (Statistisches Landesamt), in the Market Square area (Marktplatzviertel) in 1939 there were 4,112 registered inhabitants, in 1945 none (0). In the Old Town area (Altstadtviertel) in 1939 there were 5,109 inhabitants, in 1945 only 2 persons were still living there. In the Leopold Square area, in 1939 there were 4,416 inhabitants, in 1945 only 13.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist20"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist20">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist21"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist21">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist22"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist22">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>The German Army Report of 24 February 1945 devoted only two lines to reporting the bombardment: "In the early evening hours of February 23, a forceful British attack was directed at Pforzheim." <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">RAF Bomber Command</a> later assessed the bombing raid as the one with "probably the greatest proportion (of destroyed built-up area) (of any target) in one raid during the war".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist23"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist23">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>In early April as the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a> and notably the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French Army</a> advanced toward Pforzheim, the local German military commander gave orders to destroy the electric power generating plant and those gas and water supply lines that were still working, but citizens succeeded in persuading the <a href="/wiki/Staff_sergeant" title="Staff sergeant">staff sergeant</a> in charge of the operation to refrain from this absurd endeavor in the face of the imminent and inevitable surrender of the German military. Likewise, orders were issued for the destruction of those bridges that had remained unscathed (some of the bridges had been destroyed by air strikes even before and after 23 February), and this could not be prevented. Only the Iron (Railway) Bridge in WeiÃenstein ward was saved by stout-hearted citizens who, during an unguarded moment, pulled off the <a href="/wiki/Fuse_(explosives)" title="Fuse (explosives)">fuze</a> wiring from the explosive devices, which had already been installed, and dropped it into Nagold river. Soon after that on 8 April, French troops (an <a href="/wiki/Armored_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Armored vehicle">armored vehicle</a> unit) moved into Pforzheim from the northwest and were able to occupy the area north of Enz river, but the area south of the Enz river was defended by a German <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> unit using <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a>. Fighting was especially fierce in Broetzingen. The French army units (including an <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algerian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Moroccan</a> unit) suffered heavy losses; among the dead was the commander of the army unit, Capitaine <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dorance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dorance (page does not exist)">Dorance</a>. The advance of the French army came to a halt temporarily, but with the support of fighterbomber aircraft and due to the bad condition of the defenders (which included many old men and young boys who had been drafted into the <a href="/wiki/Volkssturm" title="Volkssturm">Volkssturm</a>) the French troops finally succeeded and on 18 April took possession of the vast rubble field which once was the proud residential town of the Baden Margraves.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist24"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist24">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist25"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist25">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>The three months of French <a href="/wiki/Military_occupation" title="Military occupation">occupation</a> were reportedly marked by hostile attitudes on both the French army side and the Pforzheim population side; incidents of rape and looting, mainly by Moroccan soldiers, were also reported. Au Bridge (Auerbruecke) and Wuerm Bridge received makeshift repairs by the French military. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">US Army</a>, which replaced the French troops on 8 July 1945, helped repair Goethe Bridge, Benckiser Bridge, Old Town Bridge (AltstÃ¤dterbrÃ¼cke) and Horse Bridge (RoÃbrÃ¼cke) in 1945 and the following year. The relationship between the population and the US military was reportedly more relaxed than had been the case with the French army.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist25"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist25">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-World_War_II">Post-World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Post-World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><b>1945â1965:</b> Pforzheim was gradually rebuilt, giving Pforzheim a quite modern look. In September 1951 the Northern Town Bridge (NordstadtbrÃ¼cke) was inaugurated (the ceremony was attended by then Federal President Prof. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Heuss" title="Theodor Heuss">Theodor Heuss</a>). Jahn Bridge followed in December 1951, Werder Bridge in May 1952, the rebuilt Goethe Bridge in October 1952, and the rebuilt Old Town Bridge was inaugurated in 1954.</p>
<p><b>1955:</b> On the occasion of the 500th birthday anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Reuchlin" class="mw-redirect" title="Johannes Reuchlin">Johannes Reuchlin</a>, the city of Pforzheim established the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reuchlin_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Reuchlin Prize (page does not exist)">Reuchlin Prize</a> and awarded it for the first time in the presence of then President of the Federal Republic of Germany (West-Germany), Prof. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Heuss" title="Theodor Heuss">Theodor Heuss</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1961:</b> Inauguration of the culture center "Reuchlinhaus", which from then on housed the Jewellery Museum, the Arts and Crafts Association, the City Library, the Homeland Museum (Heimatmuseum), and the City Archives.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1968:</b> On 10 July shortly before 22:00, Pforzheim and its surrounding areas were hit by a rare <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>. It had strength F4 on the <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">Fujita scale</a>. Two persons died and more than 200 were injured, and 1750 buildings were damaged. Across the town between Buechenbronn ward and the village of Wurmberg the storm caused severe damage to forest areas (i.e. most trees fell to the ground). During the first night and the following days the soldiers of the French 3rd Husar Regiment and the US Army Unit, which were still stationed at the Buckenberg Barracks, helped clear the streets of a lot of fallen trees (especially in the Buckenberg/Haidach area). It took about four weeks to carry out the most necessary repairs on buildings. The overhead electric contact wires for the electric <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">trolley</a> buses then still operating in town and the <a href="/wiki/Streetcar" class="mw-redirect" title="Streetcar">streetcar</a> transport system to the village of Ittersbach were never repaired; those transport systems were retired.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1971â1975:</b> The townships of WÃ¼rm, Hohenwart, Buechenbronn, Huchenfeld and Eutingen were incorporated into the city administration.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1973:</b> Inauguration of the new Pforzheim City Hall.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1973</b> As part of the reform of administrative districts, the rural district of Pforzheim was incorporated into the newly established <a href="/wiki/Enz_(district)" class="mw-redirect" title="Enz (district)">Enz</a> rural district, which has its administration in Pforzheim. But the city of Pforzheim itself remains a district-less city. In addition, Pforzheim became the administrative center of the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Northern_Black_Forest_Region" title="Northern Black Forest Region">Northern Black Forest Region</a>.</p>
<p><b>1975</b> On 1 January, the population exceeded 100.000 and Pforzheim gained the status of a "large city" (Grossstadt).</p>
<p><b>1979:</b> Inauguration of the Pforzheim City Museum.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[90]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1983:</b> Inauguration of the "Technical Museum of the Jewellery and Watchmaking Industry" and the "Citizens Museum".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1987:</b> Inauguration of the City Convention Center.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1987/1990:</b> Inauguration of the City Theater at the Waisenhausplatz.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1989:</b> Sister City agreement with the City of <a href="/wiki/Gernika" class="mw-redirect" title="Gernika">Gernika</a>, Spain.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1990:</b> Sister City agreement with the City of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Maur-des-Foss%C3%A9s" title="Saint-Maur-des-FossÃ©s">Saint-Maur</a>-des-Fosses, France.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1991:</b> Sister City agreement with the City of <a href="/wiki/Vicenza" title="Vicenza">Vicenza</a>, Italy.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[96]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1992:</b> State Gardening Expo in Pforzheim. Enzauenpark was created and part of the Enz river was re-naturalized.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1994:</b> Inauguration of the cultural institution "Kulturhaus Osterfeld".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[98]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1994:</b> Merger of the Pforzheim Business School and the Pforzheim School of Design to form the Pforzheim University of Applied Sciences in Design, Technology and Business.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[99]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1995:</b> Inauguration of the Archeological Site Kappelhof.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>2000:</b> Inauguration of the Pforzheim Gallery.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>2002:</b> In November, during excavation works for a new shopping center right in the center of the city, a power shovel hit a 250&#160;kg (551&#160;lb) bomb which had not gone off during the bombardment of 1945. On a Sunday, about 5000 citizens had to temporarily leave their homes as a precautionary measure while specialists were defusing and disposing of the (so far) last of a large number of unexploded explosive devices found in Pforzheim's grounds since 1945.</p>
<p><b>2006;</b> The <a href="/wiki/Timex_Group" title="Timex Group">Timex Group</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/TX_Watch_Company" title="TX Watch Company">a line of high-end watches</a> engineered in Pforzheim over a five-year period, to <a href="/wiki/Six_Sigma" title="Six Sigma">six sigma</a> standards.<sup id="cite_ref-txlaunch_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-txlaunch-3">[3]</a></sup> The technology used miniaturization with digital sensors and microprocessors driving independent motors and dial hands â to enable a range of specialized <a href="/wiki/Complication_(horology)" title="Complication (horology)">complications</a> atypical to non-digital, analog watches<sup id="cite_ref-txstart_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-txstart-4">[4]</a></sup> â an array of functions that would either be impossible or highly impractical in a <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_watch" title="Mechanical watch">mechanical</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-tximpossible_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tximpossible-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The population growth diagrams show that the largest growth rates were recorded between about 1830 and 1925, which was the period following the political reorganisation of Europe agreed upon at the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="Vienna Congress">Vienna Congress</a> of 1815 after the violent period that was so much dominated by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> of France. This high population growth period coincided with the period of intensive <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialisation</a> of Germany. Population growth weakened due to the effects of World War I and World War II. The population declined sharply due to the destruction on 23 February 1945, and increased sharply in the post-World War II era due to high economic growth levels in West-Germany and the rapid rebuilding efforts in Pforzheim. Earlier setbacks were recorded during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> period in the 17th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religions">Religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After margrave <a href="/wiki/Karl_II,_Margrave_of_Baden-Durlach" class="mw-redirect" title="Karl II, Margrave of Baden-Durlach">Karl II of Baden</a> in 1556 installed the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Baden" title="Margraviate of Baden">Margraviate of Baden</a>, of which Pforzheim was the capital in those days, Pforzheim continued to be a Protestant town for several centuries. The congregations in Pforzheim were affiliated with the deanery (Dekanat) of Pforzheim of the Protestant <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Church_of_Baden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="National Church of Baden (page does not exist)">National Church of Baden</a>, unless they were members of one of the independent churches (<a href="/wiki/Freikirche" class="mw-redirect" title="Freikirche">Freikirche</a>).</p>
<p>Since the 19th century at the latest <a href="/wiki/Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholics">Catholics</a> settled in Pforzheim again. They are affiliated with the deanery of Pforzheim which belongs to <a href="/wiki/Archdiocese_of_Freiburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Archdiocese of Freiburg">Archdiocese of Freiburg</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_council">City council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: City council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city council of Pforzheim consists of the Lord Mayor as its president and 40 elected (part-time) councillors. It is democratically elected by the citizens for a period of five years. The last election was on 25 May 2014. The city council is the main representative body of the city and determines the goals and frameworks for all local political activities. It makes decisions about all important issues regarding the public life and administration of the city and directs and monitors the work of the city administration. It forms expert committees in order to deal with specialized issues.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_council"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_council">[104]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_administration">City administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: City administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city administration is led by the Lord Mayor (presently Gert Hager) and three Mayors (presently Alexander Uhlig, Roger Heidt and Monika Mueller).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The administration consists of four departments (Dezernat) which are in charge of the following areas:</p>
<p><i>Department I</i>: Personnel, finances, business development, general administration. (Managed by Gerd Hager.)</p>
<p><i>Department II</i>: Construction and planning, environment. (Managed by Alexander Uhlig.)</p>
<p><i>Department III</i>: Education, culture, social affairs, sports. (Managed by Monika Mueller.)</p>
<p><i>Department IV</i>: Security and public order, health, energy and water supply, local transportation and traffic. (Managed by Roger Heidt.)<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_admin"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_admin">[105]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_coat_of_arms">The coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: The coat of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> of Pforzheim city shows in the left-hand half of a shield an inclined bar in red color on a golden background, and the right-hand half is divided into four fields in the colors red, silver, blue and gold. The city flag is white-blue.</p>
<p>The inclined bar can be traced back to the 13th century as the symbol of the lords (owners) of Pforzheim, which later on also became the National Coat of Arms of Baden, but its meaning is unknown. Since 1489 the coat of arms in its entire form can be verified, but its meaning is not known, either. Current coloring has been used only since 1853; in earlier times the coloring was different.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_wikipedia"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_wikipedia">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_coat_of_arms"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_coat_of_arms">[108]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_infrastructure">Economy and infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Economy and infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pforzheim is one of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Regional center (page does not exist)">regional centers</a> (Oberzentrum) in <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-WÃ¼rttemberg">Baden-WÃ¼rttemberg</a> and has one of the highest densities of industrial activity in the state.</p>
<p>Pforzheim is historically an important jewelry and watch-making centre in Germany. Due to this reason, Pforzheim is nicknamed as Golden City. Jewelry and watch-making industry is first set up by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Fran%C3%A7ois_Autran&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jean FranÃ§ois Autran (page does not exist)">Jean FranÃ§ois Autran</a> after receiving an edict from then overlord <a href="/wiki/Margrave_Karl_Friedrich_von_Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Margrave Karl Friedrich von Baden">Margrave Karl Friedrich von Baden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-invest1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-invest1-7">[7]</a></sup> This enterprise is later joined by other commercial enterprises and helped Pforzheim to become an important manufacturing city. Pforzheim accounts for just under 70 percent of the total sales of the German jewelry and silverware industry and around 80 percent of all the pieces of jewelry exported by Germany come from Pforzheim.</p>
<p>However, a smaller fraction of the economy nowadays is dedicated to producing the traditional products of <a href="/wiki/Watches" class="mw-redirect" title="Watches">watches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a>. Only 11,000 people are employed in the jewelry and watch-making industries.<sup id="cite_ref-invest1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-invest1-7">[7]</a></sup> Two thirds of all employment positions are made available in the areas of metal processing, dental industry electronics and <a href="/wiki/Electro-technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Electro-technology">electro-technology</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mail_order" title="Mail order">mail order</a> companies (Bader, Klingel, Wenz) with their sales volumes in the order of millions of Euros occupies a leading position in Germany. <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">Tourism</a> is gaining importance. In this respect the city benefits from its favorable Three-Valleys location at the gateway to the Black Forest, and related to this, from the starting points of a large number of hiking, cycling and waterway routes. The <a href="/wiki/European_long-distance_paths" title="European long-distance paths">European long-distance trail</a> <a href="/wiki/European_walking_route_E1" class="mw-redirect" title="European walking route E1">E1</a> passes through Pforzheim. It is also the starting point of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Forest_Hiking_Routes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Black Forest Hiking Routes (page does not exist)">Black Forest Hiking Routes</a> <a href="/wiki/Westweg" title="Westweg">Westweg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mittelweg" title="Mittelweg">Mittelweg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ostweg" title="Ostweg">Ostweg</a>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_wikipedia"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_wikipedia">[109]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic">Traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_8" title="Bundesautobahn 8">Federal Freeway A8</a> (<a href="/wiki/Perl,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Perl, Germany">Perl</a>â<a href="/wiki/Bad_Reichenhall" title="Bad Reichenhall">Bad Reichenhall</a>) runs by just to the north of the city. The city can be accessed via four freeway exits. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstate_Road_B10&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Interstate Road B10 (page does not exist)">Interstate Road B10</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lebach" title="Lebach">Lebach</a>â<a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=B294&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="B294 (page does not exist)">B294</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gundelfingen" title="Gundelfingen">Gundelfingen</a>â<a href="/wiki/Bretten" title="Bretten">Bretten</a>) run through the city. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=B463_Interstate_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="B463 Interstate Road (page does not exist)">B463 Interstate Road</a> running toward <a href="/wiki/Nagold" title="Nagold">Nagold</a> has its starting point here.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pforzheim_Hauptbahnhof" title="Pforzheim Hauptbahnhof">Pforzheim Hauptbahnhof</a> (central station) is located on the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe%E2%80%93M%C3%BChlacker_railway" title="KarlsruheâMÃ¼hlacker railway">KarlsruheâMÃ¼hlacker line</a>, connecting to Stuttgart. In addition there are two railway lines into the <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Enz_Valley_Railway" title="Enz Valley Railway">Enz Valley Railway</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bad_Wildbad" title="Bad Wildbad">Bad Wildbad</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nagold_Valley_Railway" title="Nagold Valley Railway">Nagold Valley Railway</a> to Nagold. Pforzheim is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Stadtbahn_Karlsruhe" class="mw-redirect" title="Stadtbahn Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe Light Rail network</a>. Other public transportation services in the city area are provided by buses of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim_Municipal_Transport&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pforzheim Municipal Transport (page does not exist)">Pforzheim Municipal Transport</a>, subsidiary of Veolia Transport Company (SVP) and several other transportation companies. They all offer unified fares within the framework of the <i>Pforzheim-Enzkreis Verkehrsverbund</i>.</p>
<p>Between 1931 and 1968 a <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> connection existed between <a href="/wiki/Ittersbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Ittersbach">Ittersbach</a> and Pforzheim, operated by Pforzheim Municipal Transportation Company (<i>SVP</i>). Before that (<i>since 1899</i>) the railroad belonged to the BLEAG (Baden Local Railway Inc., Badische-Lokaleisenbahn-Aktiengesellschaft). The only remaining <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> service "S 5" connecting Pforzheim to <a href="/wiki/Bietigheim-Bissingen" title="Bietigheim-Bissingen">Bietigheim-Bissingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6rth_am_Rhein" title="WÃ¶rth am Rhein">WÃ¶rth am Rhein</a> is operated by <a href="/wiki/Albtal-Verkehrs-Gesellschaft" title="Albtal-Verkehrs-Gesellschaft">Albtal-Verkehrs-Gesellschaft</a> (Albtal Transportation Company), which since 2002 also operates the Enz Valley Light Rail route to Bad Wildbad.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_wikipedia"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_wikipedia">[110]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_institutions">Educational institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pforzheim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Educational institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pforzheim_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Pforzheim University of Applied Sciences">Pforzheim University of Applied Sciences</a> (Hochschule Pforzheim â Hochschule fuer Gestaltung, Technik und Wirtschaft) enrolls about 5400 students. It was formed in 1992 by way of merging the former Pforzheim School of Design (Fachhochschule fuer Gestaltung) and Pforzheim Business School (Fachhochschule fuer Wirtschaft) and additionally establishing the Faculty of Engineering. The Pforzheim School of Design had its roots in the <b>Ducal Academy of Arts and Crafts and Technical School for the Metal Processing Industry</b>, established 1877. The Pforzheim Business School was the successor institution of the <b>National Business College</b>, which was established in 1963. The campuses of the Faculty of Design and the Faculties of Economics and Engineering are located at separate sites in the city area. The <b>Pforzheim University of Applied Sciences</b> fosters international exchange. Among other relationships, it is affiliated with the NIEBES Association and has close academic ties to <a href="/wiki/Osijek" title="Osijek">Osijek</a> University of <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> and academic exchange programs with many institutions abroad, among them <a href="/wiki/Auburn_University" title="Auburn University">Auburn University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wyoming" title="University of Wyoming">University of Wyoming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Institute_of_Technology" title="Illinois Institute of Technology">Illinois Institute of Technology</a>, in Chicago, of the United States of America.</li>
<li>The <b>Goldsmith and Watchmaking Vocational School</b> is the only school of its kind in Europe. It is attended by many students from abroad.</li>
<li>The general qualification for university admission (Abitur) can be obtained through an education at the <b><a href="/wiki/Reuchlin-Gymnasium" title="Reuchlin-Gymnasium">Reuchlin-Highschool</a></b>, the <b>Kepler-Highschool</b>, the <b>Hebel-Highschool</b>, the <b>Theodor-Heuss-Highschool</b>, the <b>Hilda-Highschool</b>, the <b>Schiller-Highschool</b>, the <b>Fritz-Erler-Highschool</b> (economics-oriented highschool), the <b>Heinrich-Wieland-Highschool</b> (technology-oriented highschool), the <b>Johanna-Wittum-Highschool</b> (home economics-oriented highschool), as well as the <b>Waldorfschule</b>.</li>
<li>Pforzheim also has many schools providing the mandatory general elementary and secondary education (<b>Grundschule</b>, <b>Realschule</b>) as well an institution which is dedicated to further education of grown-ups (<b>Volkshochschule</b>). There are also several state-run vocational schools leading to professional diplomas in the crafts and trades.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_hist1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_hist1">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_wikipedia"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pforzheim#endnote_wikipedia">[115]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<li><span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_station"><b><a href="#ref_station">^</a></b></span> Pforzheim is situated in a valley and also spread out across the adjacent hill slopes. On the northern slope there is a level, narrow plateau that is about 100&#160;meters wide and about 2&#160;kilometers in length. The railway facilities, including the main station and what used to be the freight loading facilities, are located on this plateau. This is the only level space that can possibly be used for railway facilities there. If the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">RAF</a> would have been only aiming at destroying the railway facilities, a few aircraft would have sufficed to finish this job in a short time. There was no need to bomb an area that was wider than one kilometer (0.6 miles) and had a length of more than three kilometers (1.9 miles) using more than 360 <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Lancasters</a>, as was the case in the big raid on 23 February. The existence and size of the plateau on the northern slope can be verified by examining any topographic map featuring the Pforzheim city area, and the map showing the destroyed city area. (refer to <a href="#References">References</a> Map of destroyed town area). And besides that, the area bombardment obviously was not even effective in destroying the railway facilities, because less than one month after the big raid in mid-March the railway facilities were bombed again several times by the <a href="/wiki/USAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAF">USAF</a>, this time focussing mainly on the suspected military target, not civilian estates (refer to <a href="#References">References</a> Pforzheimer Zeitung of 4 March 2005).</li>

<p><b>Solingen</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈzoːlɪŋən]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Solingen.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e8/De-Solingen.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Solingen.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, Germany. It is located on the northern edge of the region called <a href="/wiki/Bergisches_Land" title="Bergisches Land">Bergisches Land</a>, south of the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruhr area">Ruhr area</a>, and with a 2009 population<sup id="cite_ref-Bev.C3.B6lkerungsstand_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bev.C3.B6lkerungsstand-2">[2]</a></sup> of 161,366 is after <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal" title="Wuppertal">Wuppertal</a> the second largest city in the Bergisches Land. It is a member of the regional authority of the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>.</p>
<p>Solingen is called the "City of Blades", since it has long been renowned for the manufacturing of fine swords, knives, scissors and razors made by famous firms such as Dreiturm, <a href="/wiki/DOVO_Solingen" title="DOVO Solingen">DOVO Solingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCsthof" title="Wüsthof">Wüsthof</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._A._Henckels" title="J. A. Henckels">J. A. Henckels</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6ker" title="Böker">Böker</a>, Klauberg, Eickhorn-Solingen, and numerous other manufacturers.</p>
<p>In Medieval times, the <a href="/wiki/Swordsmith" class="mw-redirect" title="Swordsmith">swordsmiths</a> of Solingen coined the town's image, which is preserved to this date. In the latter part of the 17th century, a group of swordsmiths from Solingen broke their guild oaths by taking their sword-making secrets with them to <a href="/wiki/Shotley_Bridge" title="Shotley Bridge">Shotley Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> in England.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solingen lies southwest of <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal" title="Wuppertal">Wuppertal</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bergisches_Land" title="Bergisches Land">Bergisches Land</a>. The city has an area of 89.45 square kilometres (34.54&#160;sq&#160;mi), of which roughly 50% is used for agriculture, horticulture, or forestry. The city's border is 62 kilometres (39&#160;mi) long, and the city's dimensions are 15.6 kilometres (9.7&#160;mi) east to west and 11.7 kilometres (7.3&#160;mi) north to south. The <a href="/wiki/Wupper" title="Wupper">Wupper river</a>, a right tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, flows through the city for 26 kilometres (16&#160;mi). The city's highest point at 276 metres (906&#160;ft) is in the northern borough of <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A4frath" title="Gräfrath">Gräfrath</a> at the Light Tower, previously the <a href="/wiki/Water_tower" title="Water tower">water tower</a>, and the lowest point at 53 metres (174&#160;ft) is in the southwest.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_administration">City administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: City administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

The municipal councils are responsible for many of the boroughs' important administrative affairs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SOL001.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/SOL001.JPG/220px-SOL001.JPG" width="220" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/SOL001.JPG 1.5x" data-file-width="326" data-file-height="337" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SOL001.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Coins issued after World War I by the City of Solingen</div>
</div>

<p>Solingen was first mentioned in 1067 by a chronicler who called the area "Solonchon". Early variations of the name included "Solengen", "Solungen", and "Soleggen", although the modern name seems to have been in use since the late 14th and early 15th centuries.</p>
<p>Blacksmith smelters, dating back to over 2000 years, have been found around the town adding to Solingen's fame as a Northern Europe blacksmith centre. Swords from Solingen have turned up in places such as the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms in the British Isles. Northern Europe prized the quality of Solingen's manufactured weaponry, and they were traded across the European continent. Solingen today remains the knife-centre of Germany.</p>
<p>It was a tiny village for centuries, but became a fortified town in the 15th century.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Interwar_Period">Interwar Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Interwar Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1929 Ohligs, located in the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Province" title="Rhine Province">Rhine Province</a>, 17 miles (27&#160;km) by rail north of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> became part of Solingen. Its chief manufactures were <a href="/wiki/Cutlery" title="Cutlery">cutlery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Household_hardware" title="Household hardware">hardware</a>, and there were iron-foundries and <a href="/wiki/Flour_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Flour mill">flour mills</a>. Other industries are brewing, dyeing, weaving and brick-making.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> the Old Town was completely destroyed by an <a href="/wiki/Airstrike" title="Airstrike">air raid</a> of the British air force in 1944; 1,800 people died and over 1,500 people were badly injured.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> As such, there are few pre-war sights in the centre.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solingen's population doubled between the years 1880 and 1890 due to the incorporation of the city Dorp into Solingen in 1889, at which time the population reached 36,000. The population again received a large boost on August 1, 1929 through the incorporation of Ohligs, Wald, Höhscheid, and Gräfrath into the city limits. This brought the population above the 100,000 mark, which gave Solingen the distinction of "large city" (<i>Großstadt</i>). The number of inhabitants peaked in 1971 with 177,899 residents, and the 2006 population figure was 163,263.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Travel">Travel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Travel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Solingen_Hauptbahnhof" title="Solingen Hauptbahnhof">Solingen Hauptbahnhof</a> is served by <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr_S-Bahn" title="Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn">Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn</a> <a href="/wiki/S1_(Rhine-Ruhr_S-Bahn)" title="S1 (Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn)">line S1</a> from <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf_Airport" title="Düsseldorf Airport">Düsseldorf Airport</a> Station. S-Bahn <a href="/wiki/S7_(Rhine-Ruhr_S-Bahn)" title="S7 (Rhine-Ruhr S-Bahn)">line S7</a> links Solingen (including the station nearest the town centre, <a href="/wiki/Solingen_Mitte_station" title="Solingen Mitte station">Solingen Mitte</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solingen_Gr%C3%BCnewald_station" title="Solingen Grünewald station">Solingen-Grünewald</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal_Hauptbahnhof" title="Wuppertal Hauptbahnhof">Wuppertal</a> via <a href="/wiki/Remscheid_Hauptbahnhof" title="Remscheid Hauptbahnhof">Remscheid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remscheid-Lennep_station" title="Remscheid-Lennep station">Remscheid-Lennep</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal-Ronsdorf_station" title="Wuppertal-Ronsdorf station">Wuppertal-Ronsdorf</a>. This line has been operated by <a href="/wiki/Abellio_Deutschland" title="Abellio Deutschland">Abellio Deutschland</a> starting 15 Dec. 2013. The <a href="/wiki/Rhein-Wupper-Bahn" title="Rhein-Wupper-Bahn">Rhein-Wupper-Bahn</a> (RB 48) runs over the <a href="/wiki/Gruiten%E2%80%93K%C3%B6ln-Deutz_railway" title="Gruiten–Köln-Deutz railway">Gruiten–Köln-Deutz line</a> to Bonn-Mehlem via <a href="/wiki/Opladen_station" title="Opladen station">Opladen</a> and <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ln_Hauptbahnhof" title="Köln Hauptbahnhof">Cologne</a>. It has been operated by <a href="/wiki/National_Express" title="National Express">National Express</a> starting 13 Dec. 2015.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trolleybus">Trolleybus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Trolleybus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Netzplan_Oberleitungsbus_Solingen.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Netzplan_Oberleitungsbus_Solingen.png/220px-Netzplan_Oberleitungsbus_Solingen.png" width="220" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Netzplan_Oberleitungsbus_Solingen.png/330px-Netzplan_Oberleitungsbus_Solingen.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Netzplan_Oberleitungsbus_Solingen.png/440px-Netzplan_Oberleitungsbus_Solingen.png 2x" data-file-width="1939" data-file-height="1995" /></a>

<p><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup><br />
Solingen has a <a href="/wiki/Trolleybus" title="Trolleybus">Trolleybus</a> network, one of only three in Germany remaining besides <a href="/wiki/Eberswalde" title="Eberswalde">Eberswalde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esslingen_am_Neckar" title="Esslingen am Neckar">Esslingen am Neckar</a>. The network centres on <i>Graf-Wilhelm-Platz</i> (Count William Square).</p>
<dl>
<dt>History</dt>
</dl>
<p>The first trolleybus was brought into service on 19 June 1952. The network was a conversion of the previous <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">tram</a> services. Conversion from tramway was completed on 2 December 1959. Extensions to the system were opened in 1981–82 – Schlagbaum to Hasselstraße (2.6 kilometres (1.6&#160;mi)) and Höhscheid to Brockenberg (0.8 kilometres (0.50&#160;mi)) respectively – and in 1993 from Aufderhöhe to Mangenberg/Graf-Wilhem-Platz (8.2 kilometres (5.1&#160;mi)).</p>
<p>The mid-1990s saw plans to replace the trolleybuses with diesel buses, but this was never pursued; trolleybuses being preferred over <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel</a> vehicles because of superior acceleration and better suitability for the hilly terrain.</p>

<dl>
<dt>Network</dt>
</dl>
<p>As of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 6 lines are in operation. The older lines (681–684) are served every ten minutes, and the newer lines (685–686, opened 22 August 1993) run every half-hour, although they are duplicated by each other for the majority of their route. Routes 681 and 682 interchange with the city's principal railway station – Solingen Hbf – which lies in the western <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburbs</a>. Line 683 – at 14.5 kilometres (9.0&#160;mi), by far the network's longest – also connects to the <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal_Schwebebahn" class="mw-redirect" title="Wuppertal Schwebebahn">Wuppertal Schwebebahn</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal-Vohwinkel_station" title="Wuppertal-Vohwinkel station">Vohwinkel</a>, the northern end of the route and the western terminus of the Schwebebahn. The southern extent of 683 is the town of <a href="/wiki/Burg_an_der_Wupper" class="mw-redirect" title="Burg an der Wupper">Burg an der Wupper</a>, which contains <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Burg_(Solingen)" class="mw-redirect" title="Schloss Burg (Solingen)">Schloss Burg</a> (Burg Castle). Burg is also home to the world's only trolleybus <a href="/wiki/Turntable_(railroad)" class="mw-redirect" title="Turntable (railroad)">turntable</a>, owing to lack of space to provide a full turning circle. This precludes the use of <a href="/wiki/Articulated_vehicle" title="Articulated vehicle">articulated vehicles</a> like on the rest of the network. Until November 2009 this turntable was in regular use for line 683. Since November 2009 line 683 has been extended to Burger Bahnhof. On the new section, the buses use their diesel engine instead of electricity, as no overhead wires were constructed here.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Fleet</dt>
</dl>
<p>As of early 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the fleet stands at 49 vehicles: 15 articulated <a href="/wiki/VDL_Berkhof" title="VDL Berkhof">Berkhof</a> buses (2001/2), 20 articulated <a href="/wiki/Van_Hool" title="Van Hool">Van Hool</a> buses (2002/3), and 14 three-<a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a> <a href="/wiki/MAN_SE" title="MAN SE">MAN</a> buses (1986-7). The latter are due for replacement during 2008. The power supply is 600&#160;<a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">V</a>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airtraffic">Airtraffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Airtraffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nearest Airports are Düsseldorf Airport and Cologne/Bonn Airport. Both International Airports can be reached by train from Solingen-Hauptbahnhof (change trains at Köln-Messe/Deutz Station for the S-Bahn 13 to Cologne/Bonn Airport). Other easily reached airports are the airports of Frankfurt am Main (ICE train stop), Dortmund (railway station "Holzwickede" on the RE7 trainline) and the low cost airport Weeze (coaches from Düsseldorf Hauptbahnhof).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Solingen has belonged from its beginnings to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Cologne" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Cologne">Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Cologne</a> (<i>Erzbistum Köln</i>), and more specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Archdeacon" title="Archdeacon">Archdeaconry</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Probst" title="Probst">Probst</a> (<i>provost</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Cunibert" title="Cunibert">St. Kunibert</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Deanery" title="Deanery">deanery</a> of Deutz. Although the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> gradually made gains in the city, which was under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Berg_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Berg (state)">Counts of Berg</a>, the population by and large remained Catholic for a while. The Catholic community was newly endowed by the local lord in 1658 and in 1701 received a new church building. In 1827 Solingen became the seat of its own deanery within the newly defined Archdiocese of Cologne, to which the city's current parishes still belong.</p>
<p>As mentioned, the Reformation only gradually gained a foothold in Solingen. A <a href="/wiki/Reformed_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformed church">reformed church</a> affiliated with the Bergisch <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a> was established in 1590, and the city's <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> became reformed in 1649. <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutherans</a> had been present in Solingen since the beginning of the 17th century, and a Lutheran congregation was founded in 1635. In 1672 a formalized religious agreement was reached between the city's religious groups. The Reformation was also introduced in Gräfrath in 1590, where a church council was apparently established in 1629. The Reformed and Lutheran churches were formed into a <a href="/wiki/United_and_uniting_churches" title="United and uniting churches">united church community</a> in 1838 following the general <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Union_(Evangelical_Christian_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Prussian Union (Evangelical Christian Church)">merger of Reformed and Lutheran churches</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> in 1817.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> parishes originally belonged to the district synod of Lennep, today part of the city <a href="/wiki/Remscheid" title="Remscheid">Remscheid</a>. A new synod was established in Solingen in 1843, and the city acquired its own <a href="/wiki/Superintendent_(ecclesiastical)" title="Superintendent (ecclesiastical)">superintendent</a>, a form of church administrator. This formed the basis for the present-day Church District of Solingen, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_the_Rhineland" title="Evangelical Church in the Rhineland">Evangelical Church in the Rhineland</a>. With the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Free_churches" class="mw-redirect" title="Free churches">free churches</a>, most Protestant churches belong to the Church District of Solingen.</p>
<p>Today approximately 34% of Solingen's population belongs to Protestant churches, and roughly 26% belong to Catholic churches. Other church communities in Solingen include <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">Greek Orthodox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Evangelical_Free_Church_Congregations_in_Germany" title="Union of Evangelical Free Church Congregations in Germany">Evangelical Free</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Brethren" title="Plymouth Brethren">Brethren</a>), <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">Methodist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostal</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Salvation_Army" title="The Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Free_church" title="Free church">free</a> churches. <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Apostolic_Church" title="New Apostolic Church">New Apostolic Church</a> also have communities in Solingen.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baseball">Baseball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Baseball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Solingen_Alligators" title="Solingen Alligators">Solingen Alligators</a> are a baseball and softball club from Solingen. The club was founded in 1991 and the first men's team was promoted to the first division of the Baseball Bundesliga for the 2003 season. It has played there in every season since, winning the league championship in 2006 and 2014. The club claims over 250 members.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chess">Chess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Chess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Schachgesellschaft Solingen e.V. 1868</i> is best known for its chess team, which plays in the Schachbundesliga (<a href="/wiki/Chess_Bundesliga" title="Chess Bundesliga">Chess Bundesliga</a>), the top tier of the German chess league system, and is the most successful club in German chess history, having won a record 12 national titles (1969, 1971, 1972, 1973, 1974, 1975, 1980, 1980/81, 1986/87, 1987/88, 1996/97 and 2015/16), three national cups (1986, 2006 und 2009) and 2 European cups (1976 and 1990).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Handball">Handball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solingen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Handball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Team_handball" class="mw-redirect" title="Team handball">handball</a></i>, Solingen's most successful team is <i><a href="/wiki/Bergischer_HC" title="Bergischer HC">Bergischer HC</a></i>, playing in the top-tier <a href="/wiki/Handball-Bundesliga" title="Handball-Bundesliga">Handball-Bundesliga</a> which they were promoted to for the second time in 2013, reaching 15th place in the <a href="/wiki/2013%E2%80%9314_Handball-Bundesliga" title="2013–14 Handball-Bundesliga">2013–14 campaign</a> and therefore staying in the top flight for a second consecutive season. <i>BHC</i> originates from a 2006 cooperation between the <i>SG Solingen</i> and rivals <i>LTV Wuppertal</i> from the nearby <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal" title="Wuppertal">city of the same name</a>. The club advertises itself as a representative of the entire <a href="/wiki/Bergisches_Land" title="Bergisches Land">Bergisches Land</a> region. The team plays its home games at both Solingen's <i>Klingenhalle</i> (2,600 seats) and Wuppertal's <i>Uni-Halle</i> (3,200 seats).</p>

<p>In May 1955, the city of Solingen took over the <a href="/wiki/Partnership" title="Partnership">partnership</a> of the German general cargo ship <i>Solingen</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_America_Line" title="Hamburg America Line">Hamburg-American Packet Transit Actien-Gesellschaft (Hapag)</a>.</p>

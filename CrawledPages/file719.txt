<p><i><b>Washington Monthly</b></i> is a bimonthly <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonprofit">nonprofit</a> <a href="/wiki/Magazine" title="Magazine">magazine</a> of <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> that is based in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> The magazine is known for its annual ranking of American colleges and universities, which serve as an alternative to the <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report">U.S. News &amp; World Report</a></i> rankings.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Washington_Monthly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The magazine's founder is <a href="/wiki/Charles_Peters" title="Charles Peters">Charles Peters</a>, who started the magazine in 1969 and continued to write the "Tilting at Windmills" column in each issue until 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Monthly01022014_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monthly01022014-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Glastris" title="Paul Glastris">Paul Glastris</a>, former speechwriter for <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>, has been <i>Washington Monthly'</i>s editor-in-chief since 2001. In 2008, the magazine switched from a monthly to a bimonthly publication schedule, citing high publication costs.</p>
<p>Diane Straus Tucker is the magazine's current publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-Masthead_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Masthead-2">[2]</a></sup> Past staff editors of the magazine include <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Alter" title="Jonathan Alter">Jonathan Alter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Branch" title="Taylor Branch">Taylor Branch</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Fallows" title="James Fallows">James Fallows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Green_(journalist)" title="Joshua Green (journalist)">Joshua Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Ignatius" title="David Ignatius">David Ignatius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Kaus" title="Mickey Kaus">Mickey Kaus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Lemann" title="Nicholas Lemann">Nicholas Lemann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suzannah_Lessard" title="Suzannah Lessard">Suzannah Lessard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jon_Meacham" title="Jon Meacham">Jon Meacham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Noah" title="Timothy Noah">Timothy Noah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Nocera" title="Joe Nocera">Joe Nocera</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Steven_Waldman" title="Steven Waldman">Steven Waldman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT042202_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT042202-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, the liberal watchdog and advocacy group <a href="/wiki/Common_Cause" title="Common Cause">Common Cause</a> considered acquiring <i>Washington Monthly</i>, but the deal fell apart.<sup id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contents_and_viewpoint">Contents and viewpoint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Washington_Monthly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contents and viewpoint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The politics of <i>Washington Monthly</i> are often considered <a href="/wiki/Centre-left_politics" title="Centre-left politics">center-left</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PoliticsUnspun_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PoliticsUnspun-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYMag122415_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYMag122415-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Forbes091406_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes091406-8">[8]</a></sup> Founder Charles Peters refers to himself as a <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> Democrat and advocates the use of government to address social problems. His columns also frequently emphasized the importance of a vigilant "<a href="/wiki/Fourth_estate" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth estate">fourth estate</a>" in keeping government honest.</p>
<p><i>Washington Monthly</i> features a continuing <a href="/wiki/Weblog" class="mw-redirect" title="Weblog">blog</a>; "Political Animal" was written principally by <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Drum" title="Kevin Drum">Kevin Drum</a> for several years, with frequent guest contributions by <i>Washington Monthly's</i> current and alumni editors. In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Benen" title="Steve Benen">Steve Benen</a> took over as lead blogger; in 2012, he was succeeded by Ed Kilgore.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Kilgore left the magazine in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-WaMo112015_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaMo112015-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to "Political Animal," the magazine's website also hosts "Ten Miles Square," a general blog featuring posts from staff and political scientists, which debuted in 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and "College Guide," a blog about higher education, which the magazine began offering in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="College_rankings">College rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Washington_Monthly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: College rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Washington Monthly's</i> annual <a href="/wiki/College_and_university_rankings" title="College and university rankings">college and university rankings</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> a deliberate alternative college guide to <i><a href="/wiki/U.S._News_and_World_Report" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. News and World Report">U.S. News and World Report</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> College Rankings</i> among domestic publications, began as a research report in 2005. It was introduced as an official set of rankings in the September 2006 issue.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its "National Universities Rankings", most recently published in 2016, began as a research report in 2005, with rankings appearing in the September 2006 issue. It ranks colleges on numerous metrics revolving around academic quality, faculty, and alumni outcomes as well as factoring in "contribution to the public good in numerous categories".</p>
<p>The following are elements in the Washington Monthly rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Academic quality: a survey of the institution's academic structure, and general program</li>
<li>Retention: first year retention rate, and graduation rate of the institutions</li>
<li>Faculty resources: average class size, faculty degree level, student-faculty ratio, and proportion of full-time faculty</li>
<li>Social Mobility: recruiting and graduating low-income students</li>
<li>Research: producing cutting-edge scholarship and <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">PhD</a>s</li>
<li>Service: encouraging students to give something back to their country</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Washington_Monthly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Washington Monthly</i> receives financial support from the <a href="/wiki/Lumina_Foundation" title="Lumina Foundation">Lumina Foundation</a> to provide coverage of post-secondary education-related issues.<sup id="cite_ref-lumina_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lumina-19">[19]</a></sup> The magazine has also received funding from the <a href="/wiki/Schumann_Center_for_Media_and_Democracy" title="Schumann Center for Media and Democracy">Schumann Center for Media and Democracy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Corporation_of_New_York" title="Carnegie Corporation of New York">Carnegie Corporation of New York</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-carnegie_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carnegie-21">[21]</a></sup> and individual supporters, including <a href="/wiki/Warren_Buffett" title="Warren Buffett">Warren Buffett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Markos_Kounalakis" title="Markos Kounalakis">Markos Kounalakis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT042202_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT042202-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

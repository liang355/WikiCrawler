<p><b>Thomas Andrew</b> "<b>Tom</b>" <b>Lehrer</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɛr/: 'err' in 'merry'">ɛr</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born April 9, 1928) is a retired American musician, <a href="/wiki/Singer-songwriter" title="Singer-songwriter">singer-songwriter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>. He has lectured on <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Musical_theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Musical theater">musical theater</a>. He is best known for the pithy, humorous songs he recorded in the 1950s and 1960s.</p>
<p>His work often parodies popular song forms, though he usually creates original melodies when doing so. A notable exception is "<a href="/wiki/The_Elements_(song)" title="The Elements (song)">The Elements</a>", where he sets the names of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> to the tune of the <a href="/wiki/Major-General%27s_song" class="mw-redirect" title="Major-General's song">Major-General's song</a> from <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_Penzance" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirates of Penzance">Pirates of Penzance</a></i>. Lehrer's early work typically dealt with non-topical subject matter and was noted for its <a href="/wiki/Black_humor" class="mw-redirect" title="Black humor">black humor</a> in songs such as "Poisoning Pigeons in the Park". In the 1960s, he produced a number of songs dealing with social and political issues of the day, particularly when he wrote for the U.S. version of the television show <i><a href="/wiki/That_Was_the_Week_That_Was" title="That Was the Week That Was">That Was the Week That Was</a></i>. Despite their topical subjects and references, the popularity of these songs has endured; Lehrer quoted a friend's explanation: "Always predict the worst and you'll be hailed as a prophet."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 1970s, he mostly retired from public performances to devote his time to teaching mathematics and music theatre at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz" title="University of California, Santa Cruz">University of California, Santa Cruz</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_Lehrer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lehrer was born in 1928 to a Jewish family and grew up in Manhattan's <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bensmith_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensmith-2">[2]</a></sup> Although he was raised Jewish, Lehrer became an <a href="/wiki/Agnostic" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnostic">agnostic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He began studying classical piano at the age of seven, but was more interested in the popular music of the age. Eventually, his mother also sent him to a popular-music piano teacher.<sup id="cite_ref-Songs_1997_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Songs_1997-4">[4]</a></sup> At this early age, he began writing show tunes, which eventually helped him as a satirical composer and writer in his years of lecturing at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> noting the influence one of his professors <a href="/wiki/Irving_Kaplansky" title="Irving Kaplansky">Irving Kaplansky</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kapslecture_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kapslecture-6">[6]</a></sup> and later at other universities.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lehrer attended the <a href="/wiki/Horace_Mann_School" title="Horace Mann School">Horace Mann School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Riverdale,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Riverdale, New York">Riverdale, New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bensmith_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensmith-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-toobin_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toobin-8">[8]</a></sup> He also attended <a href="/wiki/Camp_Androscoggin" title="Camp Androscoggin">Camp Androscoggin</a>, both as a camper and a counselor.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Lehrer was considered a child prodigy and entered <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> at the age of 15 after graduating from <a href="/wiki/Loomis_Chaffee_School" title="Loomis Chaffee School">Loomis Chaffee School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bensmith_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensmith-2">[2]</a></sup> As a mathematics undergraduate student at Harvard College, he began to write <a href="/wiki/Comic_song" title="Comic song">comic songs</a> to entertain his friends, including "<a href="/wiki/Fight_Fiercely,_Harvard" title="Fight Fiercely, Harvard">Fight Fiercely, Harvard</a>" (1945). Those songs were later named <i>The Physical Revue</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> a joking reference to a leading scientific journal, <i>The <a href="/wiki/Physical_Review" title="Physical Review">Physical Review</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_and_military_career">Academic and military career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_Lehrer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic and military career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lehrer earned his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> in mathematics (<a href="/wiki/Latin_honors" title="Latin honors"><i>magna cum laude</i></a>) from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He received his <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">MA</a> degree the next year, and was inducted into <a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa_Society" title="Phi Beta Kappa Society">Phi Beta Kappa</a>. He taught classes at <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a>, Harvard, <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_College" title="Wellesley College">Wellesley</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz" title="University of California, Santa Cruz">University of California, Santa Cruz</a>.</p>
<p>He remained in Harvard's doctoral program for several years, taking time out for his musical career and to work as a researcher at the <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_Scientific_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Alamos Scientific Laboratory">Los Alamos Scientific Laboratory</a>. He was drafted into the U.S. Army from 1955 to 1957, working at the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a>. (Lehrer has stated that he invented the <a href="/wiki/Jell-O_shots" class="mw-redirect" title="Jell-O shots">Jell-O Shot</a> during this time, as a means of circumventing liquor restrictions.)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> These experiences became fodder for songs, e.g., "<a href="/wiki/Fight_Fiercely,_Harvard" title="Fight Fiercely, Harvard">Fight Fiercely, Harvard</a>", "The Wild West Is Where I Want to Be" and "It Makes a Fellow Proud to Be a Soldier".</p>
<p>Despite holding a master's degree in an era when American conscripts often lacked a <a href="/wiki/High_school_diploma" title="High school diploma">high school diploma</a>, Lehrer served as an <a href="/wiki/Enlisted_soldier" class="mw-redirect" title="Enlisted soldier">enlisted soldier</a>, achieving the rank of <a href="/wiki/Specialist_(rank)" title="Specialist (rank)">Specialist Third Class</a> (later retitled "Specialist-4" and currently "Specialist"), which he described as being a "<a href="/wiki/Corporal#United_States_Army" title="Corporal">corporal</a> <a href="/wiki/Minister_without_portfolio" title="Minister without portfolio">without portfolio</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1960, Lehrer returned to full-time studies at Harvard,<sup id="cite_ref-toobin_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toobin-8">[8]</a></sup> but in 1965 gave up on his mathematical dissertation about the subject of <a href="/wiki/Mode_(statistics)" title="Mode (statistics)">modes</a> in statistics, after working on it intermittently for 15 years.<sup id="cite_ref-bensmith_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensmith-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1962, he taught in the <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> department at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1972, he joined the faculty of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz" title="University of California, Santa Cruz">University of California, Santa Cruz</a>, teaching an introductory course entitled "The Nature of Mathematics" to liberal arts majors—"Math for Tenors", according to Lehrer. He also taught a class in <a href="/wiki/Musical_theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Musical theater">musical theater</a>. He occasionally performed songs in his lectures, primarily those relating to the topic.<sup id="cite_ref-kapslecture_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kapslecture-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, Lehrer taught his last mathematics class (on the topic of <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinity</a>) and retired from academia.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He has remained in the area, and in 2003 said he still "hangs out" around the University of California, Santa Cruz.<sup id="cite_ref-smh_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smh-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_publications">Mathematical publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_Lehrer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mathematical publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">American Mathematical Society</a> database lists him as co-author of two papers:</p>
<p><cite class="citation journal">R. E. Fagen; T. A. Lehrer (March 1958). <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_Lehrer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lehrer was mainly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Musical_theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Musical theater">musical theater</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Nachman_(journalist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerald Nachman (journalist)">Gerald Nachman</a>'s book <i>Seriously Funny</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> the Broadway musical <i><a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Face_It!" title="Let's Face It!">Let's Face It!</a></i> (by <a href="/wiki/Cole_Porter" title="Cole Porter">Cole Porter</a>) made an early and lasting impression on him. Lehrer's style consists of <a href="/wiki/Parody_music" title="Parody music">parodying</a> various forms of <a href="/wiki/Popular_song" class="mw-redirect" title="Popular song">popular song</a>. For example, his appreciation of <a href="/wiki/List_song" title="List song">list songs</a> led him to write "<a href="/wiki/The_Elements_(song)" title="The Elements (song)">The Elements</a>", which lists the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> to the tune of <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Major-General%27s_Song" title="Major-General's Song">Major-General's Song</a>".</p>
<p>Author <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a> recounted in his second autobiographical volume <i>In Joy Still Felt</i> seeing Lehrer perform in a Boston nightclub on October 9, 1954, during which he sang very cleverly about Jim getting it from Louise, and Sally from Jim, "and after a while you gathered the 'it' to be venereal disease [the song was likely "<a href="/wiki/I_Got_It_From_Agnes" class="mw-redirect" title="I Got It From Agnes">I Got It From Sally</a> (in later versions 'Agnes')"]. Suddenly, as the combinations grew more grotesque, you realized he was satirizing every perversion known to mankind without using a single naughty phrase. It was clearly unsingable (in those days) outside a nightclub." Asimov also recalled a song that dealt with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_subway" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston subway">Boston subway</a> system, making use of the stations leading into town from Harvard, observing that the local subject-matter rendered the song useless for general distribution. Lehrer subsequently granted Asimov permission to print the lyrics to the subway song in his book. "I haven't gone to nightclubs often," said Asimov, "but of all the times I have gone, it was on this occasion that I had by far the best time."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1953, inspired by the success of his performances, Lehrer paid $15 for some studio time to record <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_by_Tom_Lehrer" title="Songs by Tom Lehrer">Songs by Tom Lehrer</a></i>. The initial pressing was 400 copies. At the time, radio stations would not give Lehrer air time because of his controversial subjects. He sold his album on campus at Harvard for $3 (equivalent to $27.00 today), while "several stores near the Harvard campus sold it for $3.50, taking only a minimal markup as a kind of community service. Newsstands on campus sold it for the same price."<sup id="cite_ref-barryhansen_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barryhansen-20">[20]</a></sup> After one summer, he started to receive mail orders from all parts of the country (as far away as San Francisco, after <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">The Chronicle</a></i> wrote an article on the record). Interest in his recordings was spread by word of mouth; friends and supporters brought their records home and played them for their friends, who then also wanted a copy.<sup id="cite_ref-Jim_Bessman_1997_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jim_Bessman_1997-21">[21]</a></sup> Lehrer later recalled, "Lacking exposure in the media, my songs spread slowly. Like herpes, rather than ebola."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album—which included the macabre "I Hold Your Hand in Mine", the mildly risqué "Be Prepared", and "<a href="/wiki/Lobachevsky_(song)" title="Lobachevsky (song)">Lobachevsky</a>" (regarding plagiarizing mathematicians)—became a cult success via <a href="/wiki/Word_of_mouth" title="Word of mouth">word of mouth</a>, despite being <a href="/wiki/Self-publishing" title="Self-publishing">self-published</a> and without promotion. Lehrer embarked on a series of <a href="/wiki/Concert_tour" class="mw-redirect" title="Concert tour">concert tours</a> and in 1959 recorded a second album, which was released in two versions: the songs were the same, but <i><a href="/wiki/More_of_Tom_Lehrer" title="More of Tom Lehrer">More of Tom Lehrer</a></i> was studio-recorded while <i><a href="/wiki/An_Evening_Wasted_with_Tom_Lehrer" title="An Evening Wasted with Tom Lehrer">An Evening Wasted with Tom Lehrer</a></i> was recorded live in concert. In 2013, Lehrer recalled the studio session for "Poisoning Pigeons in the Park," which referred to the practice of controlling pigeons in Boston with strychnine-treated corn:<sup id="cite_ref-faulkner_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faulkner-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>"The copyist arrived at the last minute with the parts and passed them out to the band... And there was no title on it, and there was no lyrics. And so they ran through it, 'what a pleasant little waltz'... And the engineer said, '"Poisoning Pigeons in the Park,' take one," and the piano player said, '"What?"' and literally fell off the stool."<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-24">[24]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Lehrer's major breakthrough in the United Kingdom came with the doctor of music <a href="/wiki/Honoris_causa" class="mw-redirect" title="Honoris causa">honoris causa</a>, which the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> conferred to <a href="/wiki/Princess_Margaret" class="mw-redirect" title="Princess Margaret">Princess Margaret</a> on 4 December 1957 "in the full knowledge", as the public orator Professor J.R. Sutherland stated, "that the Princess is a connoisseur of music and a performer of skill and distinction, her taste being catholic, ranging from Mozart to the <a href="/wiki/Calypso_music" title="Calypso music">calypso</a> and from opera to the songs of Miss <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Lillie" title="Beatrice Lillie">Beatrice Lillie</a> and Tom Lehrer.“<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This prompted significant interest in Lehrer's works and helped secure distributors for his material in the UK. It was there that his music achieved real popularity, as a result of the proliferation of university newspapers referring to the material, and the willingness of the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> to play his songs on the radio (something that was a rarity in the United States). By the end of the 1950s, Lehrer had sold 370,000 records.<sup id="cite_ref-bensmith_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensmith-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1960, Lehrer essentially retired from touring in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-bensmith_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensmith-2">[2]</a></sup> In the early 1960s, he was employed as the resident songwriter for the U.S. edition of <i><a href="/wiki/That_Was_The_Week_That_Was" class="mw-redirect" title="That Was The Week That Was">That Was The Week That Was</a></i> (TW3), a satirical television show.<sup id="cite_ref-barryhansen_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barryhansen-20">[20]</a></sup> <span id="NewMath"></span> An increased proportion of his output became overtly political, or at least topical, on subjects such as education ("<a href="/wiki/New_Math_(song)" class="mw-redirect" title="New Math (song)">New Math</a>"), the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> ("The Vatican Rag", the tune which has been claimed to be based on the 1910 song the Spaghetti Rag<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Race_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Race relations">race relations</a> ("National Brotherhood Week"), air and water <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> ("Pollution"), American militarism ("Send the Marines"), <a href="/wiki/World_War_III" title="World War III">World War III</a> "pre-nostalgia" ("So Long, Mom", premiered by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Allen" title="Steve Allen">Steve Allen</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation" title="Nuclear proliferation">nuclear proliferation</a> ("Who's Next?" and "MLF Lullaby"). He also wrote a song that famously satirized the alleged amorality of rocket scientist <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a>, who worked for Nazi Germany before working for the United States. ("'Once the rockets are up, who cares where they come down? That's not my department,' says Wernher von Braun.") Lehrer did not appear on the television show; his songs were performed by a female vocalist, <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Ames" title="Nancy Ames">Nancy Ames</a>, and his lyrics were often altered by the network censors. Lehrer later performed the songs on the album <i><a href="/wiki/That_Was_The_Year_That_Was" class="mw-redirect" title="That Was The Year That Was">That Was The Year That Was</a></i> (1965), so that, in his words, people could hear the songs the way they were intended to be heard. In 1966, for <a href="/wiki/David_Frost" title="David Frost">David Frost</a>'s further BBC television programme <i><a href="/wiki/The_Frost_Report" title="The Frost Report">The Frost Report</a></i> Lehrer was invited to contribute some of his classic compositions. <i>The Frost Report</i> was transmitted live, and he pre-recorded all his segments at one performance. Though Lehrer was not featured in every edition, they were slotted in at an appropriate part of each show. At least two of his offerings were songs not included on any of his LPs: a reworking of Noël Coward’s "That is the End of the News" (with some new lyrics) and a comic explanation of how Britain might adapt to the <a href="/wiki/Decimal_Day" title="Decimal Day">coming of decimal currency</a>.</p>
<p>During the spring of 1960, Lehrer toured Australia and New Zealand, performing a total of 33 concerts "to great acclaim".<sup id="cite_ref-barryhansen_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barryhansen-20">[20]</a></sup> Yet this occurred during a time in which he was "banned, censored, mentioned in several houses of parliament and threatened with arrest", after which he quipped that it was "the highlight of his life". In particular, "Be Prepared" drew advance brickbats in Brisbane from the chief of police, which he sang anyway, prompting wrath in Adelaide where he signed a petition that he wouldn't sing five songs in that territory. Nonetheless, Lehrer debuted several unreleased songs in Australia including "The Masochism Tango" which couldn't yet be banned.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1967, Lehrer was persuaded to make a short tour in Norway and Denmark, where he performed some of the songs from the television program. The performance in Oslo, Norway, on September 10 was recorded on video tape and aired locally later that autumn; this program was released on DVD some 40 years later. On that tour, a concert at the <i>Studenterforeningen</i> (student association) in Copenhagen, Denmark, where a prominent international guest was invited annually, was also televised; Lehrer commented onstage that he might be America's "revenge for <a href="/wiki/Victor_Borge" title="Victor Borge">Victor Borge</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the summer of 1967, Lehrer composed and performed on piano original songs in a Dodge automobile "industrial" film that was distributed primarily to automobile dealers and shown at promotional events. Set in a fictional American wild west town, the full title is <i>The Dodge Rebellion Theatre presents Ballads For '67</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-barryhansen_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barryhansen-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Working with <a href="/wiki/Joe_Raposo" title="Joe Raposo">Joe Raposo</a>, Lehrer attempted to adapt <a href="/wiki/Sweeney_Todd" title="Sweeney Todd">Sweeney Todd</a> as a Broadway musical to star <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Colonna_(entertainer)" title="Jerry Colonna (entertainer)">Jerry Colonna</a>. Although a few songs were started, Lehrer noted that "Nothing ever came of it, and of course twenty years later <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Sondheim" title="Stephen Sondheim">Stephen Sondheim</a> beat me to the punch." <sup id="cite_ref-Todd_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Todd-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The record deal with <a href="/wiki/Reprise_Records" title="Reprise Records">Reprise Records</a> for the <i>That Was The Year That Was</i> album also gave Reprise distribution rights for his earlier recordings, as Lehrer wanted to wind up his own record imprint. The Reprise issue of <i>Songs by Tom Lehrer</i> was a stereo re-recording. This version was not issued on CD, but the songs were issued on the live <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Lehrer_Revisited" class="mw-redirect" title="Tom Lehrer Revisited">Tom Lehrer Revisited</a></i> CD. The [live] recording included bonus tracks "L-Y" and "Silent E", two of the ten songs that Lehrer wrote for the PBS children's educational series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Electric_Company_(1971_TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Electric Company (1971 TV series)">The Electric Company</a></i>. Lehrer later commented that worldwide sales of the recordings under Reprise surpassed 1.8 million units in 1996. That same year, the album <i>That Was The Year That Was</i> went gold.<sup id="cite_ref-Jim_Bessman_1997_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jim_Bessman_1997-21">[21]</a></sup> The album liner notes (and Lehrer himself in one routine) promote his songs with self-deprecating humor. ("I know it's very bad form to quote one's own reviews, but there is something the <i>New York Times</i> said about me [in 1958], that I have always treasured: '<i>Mr. Lehrer's muse</i> [is] <i>not fettered by such inhibiting factors as taste.</i>")</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Departure_from_the_music_scene">Departure from the music scene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_Lehrer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Departure from the music scene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1970s, Lehrer concentrated on teaching mathematics and musical theater, although he also wrote ten songs for the educational children's television show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Electric_Company" title="The Electric Company">The Electric Company</a></i>. His last public performance took place in 1972, on a fundraising tour for Democratic US presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/George_McGovern" title="George McGovern">George McGovern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bensmith_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensmith-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is an <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban legend</a> that Lehrer gave up <a href="/wiki/Political_satire" title="Political satire">political satire</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a> in 1973. He did comment that awarding the prize to Kissinger made political satire obsolete,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> but has denied that he stopped creating satire as a form of protest, asserting that he had stopped several years prior.<sup id="cite_ref-avclub_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avclub-35">[35]</a></sup> Another mistaken belief is that he was sued for <a href="/wiki/Slander_and_libel" class="mw-redirect" title="Slander and libel">libel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a>, the subject of one of his songs, and forced to relinquish his royalties to von Braun. Lehrer denied this in a 2003 interview.<sup id="cite_ref-smh_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smh-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>When asked about his reasons for abandoning his musical career in an interview in the book accompanying his CD box set (released in 2000), he cited a simple lack of interest, a distaste for touring, and boredom with performing the same songs repeatedly. He observed that when he was moved to write and perform songs, he did, and when he was not, he did not, and that after a while he simply lost interest. Although Lehrer was "a hero of the anti-nuclear, civil rights left", covering its political issues in many of his songs, and even though he shared the <a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">New Left</a>'s opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Vietnam war">Vietnam war</a>, he disliked the aesthetics of <a href="/wiki/1960s_counterculture" class="mw-redirect" title="1960s counterculture">1960s counterculture</a> and stopped performing as the movement gained momentum.<sup id="cite_ref-bensmith_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensmith-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lehrer's musical career was brief; he pointed out that he had performed a mere 109 shows and written 37 songs over 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Nevertheless, he developed a significant following in the United States and abroad.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revivals_and_discographic_reissues">Revivals and discographic reissues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_Lehrer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Revivals and discographic reissues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1980 <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Foolery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tom Foolery">Tom Foolery</a></i>, a revue of his songs created and produced by <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Mackintosh" title="Cameron Mackintosh">Cameron Mackintosh</a>, was a hit on the London stage. Although not its instigator, Lehrer eventually gave the stage production his full support and updated several of his lyrics for the show (such as "Who's Next", in which <a href="/wiki/Neiman-Marcus" class="mw-redirect" title="Neiman-Marcus">Neiman-Marcus</a> [or in the San Francisco production, <a href="/wiki/J.C._Penney" class="mw-redirect" title="J.C. Penney">J.C. Penney</a>], not Alabama, gets the bomb). <i>Tom Foolery</i> contained 27 songs and led to more than 200 productions,<sup id="cite_ref-Jim_Bessman_1997_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jim_Bessman_1997-21">[21]</a></sup> including an <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">Off-Broadway</a> production at the <a href="/wiki/Village_Gate" title="Village Gate">Village Gate</a>, which ran for 120 performances in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In conjunction with the <i>Tom Foolery</i> premiere in 1980 at the <a href="/wiki/Criterion_Theatre" title="Criterion Theatre">Criterion Theatre</a> in London, Lehrer made a rare TV appearance on <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Parkinson_(TV_series)" title="Parkinson (TV series)">Parkinson</a></i> show, where he sang "I Got It from Agnes".<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, Lehrer wrote "That's Mathematics" for the closing credits to a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Sciences_Research_Institute" title="Mathematical Sciences Research Institute">Mathematical Sciences Research Institute</a> video<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> celebrating the proof of <a href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem" title="Fermat's Last Theorem">Fermat's Last Theorem</a>.</p>
<p>On June 7 and 8, 1998, Lehrer performed in public for the first time in 25 years at the <a href="/wiki/Lyceum_Theatre,_London" title="Lyceum Theatre, London">Lyceum Theatre, London</a> as part of the gala show <i><a href="/wiki/Hey,_Mr._Producer!" title="Hey, Mr. Producer!">Hey, Mr. Producer!</a></i> celebrating the career of impresario <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Mackintosh" title="Cameron Mackintosh">Cameron Mackintosh</a>, who had been the producer of <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Foolery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tom Foolery">Tom Foolery</a></i>. The June 8 show was his only performance to date before <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a>. Lehrer sang "Poisoning Pigeons in the Park" and an updated version of the nuclear proliferation song "Who's Next?" The DVD of the event includes the former song.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000 a boxed set of CDs, <i>The Remains of Tom Lehrer</i>, was released by <a href="/wiki/Rhino_Entertainment" title="Rhino Entertainment">Rhino Entertainment</a>. It included live and studio versions of his first two albums, <i>That Was The Year That Was</i>, the songs he wrote for <i>The Electric Company</i>, and some previously unreleased material. It was accompanied by a small hardbound book containing an introduction by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Demento" title="Dr. Demento">Dr. Demento</a> and lyrics for all the songs.</p>
<p>In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Shout!_Factory" title="Shout! Factory">Shout! Factory</a> launched a reissue campaign, making his long out-of-press albums available digitally. They also issued a CD/DVD combo called <i>The Tom Lehrer Collection</i>, which includes his best-loved songs, plus a DVD featuring an Oslo concert.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_legacy">Musical legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tom_Lehrer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Musical legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sardonic composer <a href="/wiki/Randy_Newman" title="Randy Newman">Randy Newman</a> said of Lehrer, "He's one of the great American songwriters without a doubt, right up there with everybody, the top guys. As a lyricist, as good as there's been in the last half of the 20th century."<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-24">[24]</a></sup> Singer and comedian <a href="/wiki/Dillie_Keane" title="Dillie Keane">Dillie Keane</a> has acknowledged<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Lehrer's influence on her work.</p>
<p>Lehrer was praised by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Demento" title="Dr. Demento">Dr. Demento</a> as "the best musical satirist of the twentieth century." Other artists who cite Lehrer as an influence include <a href="/wiki/%22Weird_Al%22_Yankovic" title="&quot;Weird Al&quot; Yankovic">"Weird Al" Yankovic</a>, whose work generally addresses more popular and less technical or political subjects,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and educator and scientist <a href="/wiki/H._Paul_Shuch" title="H. Paul Shuch">H. Paul Shuch</a>, who tours under the stage name Dr. SETI and calls himself "a cross between <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> and Tom Lehrer: he sings like Sagan and lectures like Lehrer."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lehrer has commented that he doubts his songs had any real effect on those not already critical of the establishment: "I don't think this kind of thing has an impact on the unconverted, frankly. It's not even preaching to the converted; it's titillating the converted... I'm fond of quoting <a href="/wiki/Peter_Cook" title="Peter Cook">Peter Cook</a>, who talked about the satirical Berlin <a href="/wiki/Kabarett" title="Kabarett">kabaretts</a> of the 1930s, which did so much to stop the rise of Hitler and prevent the Second World War."<sup id="cite_ref-avclub_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avclub-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003 he commented that his particular brand of political satire is more difficult in the modern world: "The real issues I don't think most people touch. The Clinton jokes are all about Monica Lewinsky and all that stuff and not about the important things, like the fact that he wouldn't ban land mines... I'm not tempted to write a song about George W. Bush. I couldn't figure out what sort of song I would write. That's the problem: I don't want to satirise George Bush and his puppeteers, I want to vaporize them."<sup id="cite_ref-smh_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smh-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gene_Weingarten" title="Gene Weingarten">Gene Weingarten</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> interviewed Lehrer off the record in a February 2008 phone call. When Weingarten asked if there was anything he could print for the record, Lehrer responded, "Just tell the people that I am voting for <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Obama</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The play <i><a href="/wiki/Letters_from_Lehrer" title="Letters from Lehrer">Letters from Lehrer</a></i> by Canadian <a href="/wiki/Richard_Greenblatt_(playwright)" title="Richard Greenblatt (playwright)">Richard Greenblatt</a> was performed by him at <a href="/wiki/CanStage" class="mw-redirect" title="CanStage">CanStage</a> in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, from January 16 to February 25, 2006. It followed Lehrer's musical career, the meaning of several songs, the politics of the time, and Greenblatt's own experiences with Lehrer's music, while playing some of Lehrer's songs. There are currently no plans for more performances, although low-quality audio recordings have been on the internet.</p>
<p>Stylistically influenced performers include American political satirist <a href="/wiki/Mark_Russell" title="Mark Russell">Mark Russell</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Canadian comedian and songwriter <a href="/wiki/Randy_Vancourt" title="Randy Vancourt">Randy Vancourt</a> and the British duo <a href="/wiki/Kit_and_The_Widow" title="Kit and The Widow">Kit and The Widow</a>. British medical satirists <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Transplants" title="Amateur Transplants">Amateur Transplants</a> acknowledge the debt they owe to Lehrer on the back of their first album, <i><a href="/wiki/Fitness_to_Practice" title="Fitness to Practice">Fitness to Practice</a></i>. Their songs "The Menstrual Rag" and "The Drugs Song" are to the tunes of Lehrer's "The Vatican Rag" and "The Elements" (the tune of the "Major-General's Song" from <i>The <a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_Penzance" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirates of Penzance">Pirates of Penzance</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a>) respectively. Their second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Unfit_to_Practise" title="Unfit to Practise">Unfit to Practise</a></i>, opens with an update of Lehrer's "The Masochism Tango" and is called "Masochism Tango 2008".</p>
<p>In 1967, Swedish actor <a href="/wiki/Lars_Ekborg" title="Lars Ekborg">Lars Ekborg</a>, outside Sweden most known for his part in Ingmar Bergman's "<a href="/wiki/Summer_with_Monika" title="Summer with Monika">Summer with Monika</a>" made an album called "I Tom Lehrers vackra värld" ("In the beautiful world of Tom Lehrer"), with 12 of Lehrer's songs interpreted in Swedish. Lehrer wrote in a letter to the producer Per–Anders Boquist that "not knowing any Swedish, I am obviously not equipped to judge, but it sounds to me as though Mr Ekborg is perfect for the songs", along with further compliments to pianist Leif Asp for unexpected additional flourishes.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, Argentinian singer <a href="/wiki/Nacha_Guevara" title="Nacha Guevara">Nacha Guevara</a> sang Spanish versions of several Lehrer's songs for the show/live album "Este es el año que es".<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lehrer's song "<a href="/wiki/The_Old_Dope_Peddler" title="The Old Dope Peddler">The Old Dope Peddler</a>" is sampled in rapper <a href="/wiki/2_Chainz" title="2 Chainz">2 Chainz</a>'s song "Dope Peddler", on his 2012 debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Based_on_a_T.R.U._Story" title="Based on a T.R.U. Story">Based on a T.R.U. Story</a>.</i> The following year, Lehrer said he was "very proud" to have his song sampled "literally sixty years after I recorded it." Lehrer went on to describe his official response to the request to use his song: "As sole copyright owner of 'The Old Dope Peddler,' I grant you motherfuckers permission to do this. Please give my regards to Mr. Chainz, or may I call him 2?"<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lehrer has said of his musical career, "If, after hearing my songs, just one human being is inspired to say something nasty to a friend, or perhaps to strike a loved one, it will all have been worth the while."<sup id="cite_ref-Songs_1997_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Songs_1997-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

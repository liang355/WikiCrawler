<p><b>Gelsenkirchen</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˌɡɛlzn̩ˈkɪʁçn̩]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Gelsenkirchen.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3b/De-Gelsenkirchen.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Gelsenkirchen.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in the <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> state of Germany. It is located in the northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr" title="Ruhr">Ruhr</a> area. Its population in 2015 was c.&#160;260,000.</p>
<p>Gelsenkirchen was first documented in 1150, but it remained a tiny village until the 19th century, when the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> led to the growth of the entire area. In 1840, when the mining of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> began, 6,000 inhabitants lived in Gelsenkirchen; in 1900 the population had increased to 138,000.</p>
<p>In the early 20th century, Gelsenkirchen was the most important coal mining town in Europe. It was called the "city of a thousand fires" for the flames of mine gases flaring at night. In 1928, Gelsenkirchen was merged with the adjoining cities of <a href="/wiki/Buer,_Germany" title="Buer, Germany">Buer</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Horst,_Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Horst, Gelsenkirchen (page does not exist)">Horst</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelsenkirchen-Horst" class="extiw" title="de:Gelsenkirchen-Horst">de</a>)</span>. The city bore the name Gelsenkirchen-Buer, until it was renamed Gelsenkirchen in 1930. During the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi era</a> Gelsenkirchen remained a centre of coal production and <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refining</a>, and for this reason it was bombed in Allied air raids during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. There are no longer colliers in Gelsenkirchen with the city searching for a new image, having been hit for decades with one of the highest unemployment rates in Germany. Today Germany's largest <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> plant is located in the city. In Gelsenkirchen-Scholven there is a coal-fired power station with the tallest chimneys in Germany (302 m).</p>
<p>Gelsenkirchen is home of the famous <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> club <a href="/wiki/FC_Schalke_04" title="FC Schalke 04">Schalke 04</a>, which is named after the borough Schalke, while the club's stadium, the <a href="/wiki/Veltins-Arena" title="Veltins-Arena">Veltins-Arena</a>, is located in the borough of Erle.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_and_medieval_times">Ancient and medieval times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient and medieval times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the part of town now called Buer was first mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Heribert_of_Cologne" title="Heribert of Cologne">Heribert I</a> in a document as <i>Puira</i> in 1003, there were hunting people on a hill north of the <a href="/wiki/Emscher" title="Emscher">Emscher</a> as early as the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> – and therefore earlier than 1000 BC. They did not live in houses as such, but in small yards gathered together near each other. Later, the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> pushed into the area. In about 700, the region was settled by the <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxons</a>. A few other parts of town which today lie in Gelsenkirchen's north end were mentioned in documents from the early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, some examples being: <i>Raedese</i> (nowadays <i>Resse</i>), Middelvic (<i>Middelich</i>, today part of Resse), <i>Sutheim</i> (<i>Sutum</i>; today part of Beckhausen) and <i>Sculven</i> (nowadays <i>Scholven</i>). Many nearby farming communities were later identified as <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">iuxta Bure</span></i> ("near Buer").</p>
<p>It was about 1150 when the name <i>Gelstenkerken</i> or <i>Geilistirinkirkin</i> appeared up for the first time. At about the same time, the first <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church</a> in town was built in what is now Buer. This <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">ecclesia Buron</span></i> ("church at Buer") was listed in a directory of <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> churches by the sexton from <a href="/wiki/Deutz,_Cologne" title="Deutz, Cologne">Deutz</a>, Theodericus. This settlement belonged to the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Mark" title="County of Mark">Mark</a>. However, in ancient times and even in the Middle Ages, only a few dozen people actually lived in the settlements around the Emscher basin.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrialisation">Industrialisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industrialisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Up until the middle of the 19th century, the area in and around Gelsenkirchen was only thinly settled and almost exclusively agrarian. In 1815, after temporarily belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duchy">Grand Duchy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Berg_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Berg (state)">Berg</a>, the land now comprising the city of Gelsenkirchen passed to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>, which assigned it to the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Westphalia" title="Province of Westphalia">province of Westphalia</a>. Whereas the Gelsenkirchen of that time – not including today's north-end communities, such as Buer – was put in the <i><a href="/wiki/Amt_(subnational_entity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amt (subnational entity)">Amt</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Wattenscheid" title="Wattenscheid">Wattenscheid</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bochum" title="Bochum">Bochum</a> district, in the governmental <a href="/wiki/Arnsberg_(region)" title="Arnsberg (region)">region of Arnsberg</a>, Buer, which was an <i>Amt</i> in its own right, was along with nearby Horst joined to <a href="/wiki/Recklinghausen_(district)" title="Recklinghausen (district)">Recklinghausen district</a> in the governmental <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_(region)" title="Münster (region)">region of Münster</a>. This arrangement came to an end only in 1928.</p>
<p>After the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> – lovingly known as "Black Gold" – in the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr" title="Ruhr">Ruhr</a> area in 1840, and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialisation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>–<a href="/wiki/Minden" title="Minden">Minden</a> Railway and the Gelsenkirchen Main Railway Station were opened. In 1868, Gelsenkirchen became the seat of an <i>Amt</i> within the Bochum district which encompassed the communities of Gelsenkirchen, Braubauerschaft (since 1900, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen_Bismarck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gelsenkirchen Bismarck (page does not exist)">Bismarck</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelsenkirchen-Bismarck" class="extiw" title="de:Gelsenkirchen-Bismarck">de</a>)</span>), Schalke, Heßler, Bulmke and Hüllen.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Grillo" title="Friedrich Grillo">Friedrich Grillo</a> founded the Corporation for Chemical Industry (<i>Aktiengesellschaft für Chemische Industrie</i>) in Schalke in 1872, as well as founding the <i>Vogelsang &amp; Co.</i> with the Grevel family (later <i><a href="/wiki/Schalker_Eisenh%C3%BCtte_Maschinenfabrik" title="Schalker Eisenhütte Maschinenfabrik">Schalker Eisenhütte Maschinenfabrik</a></i>), and also the Schalke Mining and Ironworks Association (<i>Schalker Gruben- und Hüttenverein</i>). A year later, and once again in Schalke, he founded the Glass and Mirror Factory Incorporated (<i>Glas- und Spiegel-Manufaktur AG</i>).</p>
<p>After Gelsenkirchen had become an important heavy-industry hub, it was raised to city in 1875.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_city">Independent city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Independent city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1885, after Bochum district was split up, Gelsenkirchen became the seat of its own district (<i>Kreis</i>), which would last until 1926. The cities of Gelsenkirchen and Wattenscheid, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Amt_(administrative_division)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amt (administrative division)">Ämter</a> of Braubauerschaft (in 1900, Bismarck), Schalke, Ückendorf, <a href="/wiki/Wanne-Eickel" class="mw-redirect" title="Wanne-Eickel">Wanne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wattenscheid" title="Wattenscheid">Wattenscheid</a> all belonged to the Gelsenkirchen district. A few years later, in 1896, Gelsenkirchen split off from Gelsenkirchen district to become an <a href="/wiki/Independent_city" title="Independent city">independent city</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">kreisfreie Stadt</span>). In 1891, Horst was split off from the <i>Amt</i> of Buer, which itself was raised to city status in 1911, and to an independent city status the next year. Meanwhile, Horst became the seat of its own <i>Amt</i>. In 1924, the rural community of <a href="/wiki/Flugwerk_Deutschland_GmbH" title="Flugwerk Deutschland GmbH">Rotthausen</a>, which until then had belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a> district, was made part of the Gelsenkirchen district.</p>
<p>In 1928, under the Prussian local government reforms, the cities of Gelsenkirchen and Buer along with the <i>Amt</i> of Horst together became a new <i>kreisfreie Stadt</i> called Gelsenkirchen-Buer, effective on 1 April that year. From that time, the whole city area belonged to the governmental district of Münster. In 1930, on the city's advice, the city's name was changed to <i>Gelsenkirchen</i>, effective 21 May. By this time, the city was home to about 340,000 people.</p>
<p>In 1931, the Gelsenkirchen Mining Corporation (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Gelsenkirchener Bergwerks-Aktien-Gesellschaft</span>) founded the Gelsenberg Petrol Corporation (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Gelsenberg-Benzin-AG</span>). In 1935, the Hibernia Mining Company founded the <i>Hydrierwerk Scholven AG GE-Buer</i> <a href="/wiki/Coal_liquefaction" title="Coal liquefaction">Coal liquefaction</a> plant. Scholven/Buer began operation in 1936 and achieved a capacity of "<i>200,000 tons/year of finished product, mainly aviation base gasoline.</i>"<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20071108041008/http://www.fischer-tropsch.org/Bureau_of_Mines/info_circ/ic_7375/ic_7375.htm">[1]</a> After 1937, Gelsenberg-Benzin-AG opened the Nordstern plant for converting bituminous coal to synthetic oil.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazi_Germany">Nazi Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nazi Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/November_9_in_German_history" title="November 9 in German history">9 November 1938</a> <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a> antisemitic riots destroyed Jewish businesses, dwellings and cemeteries, and a <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> in Buer and one in downtown Gelsenkirchen. However, a new downtown Gelsenkirchen synagogue was opened post-World War II (long after the war), on 1 February 2007.</p>
<p>Gelsenkirchen was a target of <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">strategic bombing during World War II</a>, particularly during the 1943 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Ruhr" title="Battle of the Ruhr">Battle of the Ruhr</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oil_Campaign_of_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil Campaign of World War II">Oil Campaign</a>. Three quarters of Gelsenkirchen was destroyed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and many above-ground <a href="/wiki/Air-raid_shelter" class="mw-redirect" title="Air-raid shelter">air-raid shelters</a> such as near the town hall in Buer are in nearly original form.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oberst" title="Oberst">Oberst</a> <a href="/wiki/Werner_M%C3%B6lders" title="Werner Mölders">Werner Mölders</a> the legendary <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> Fighter pilot was born here.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gelsenberg_Lager" title="Gelsenberg Lager">Gelsenberg Lager</a> subcamp of <a href="/wiki/KZ_Buchenwald" class="mw-redirect" title="KZ Buchenwald">KZ Buchenwald</a> was established in 1944<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> to provide <a href="/wiki/Forced_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced labor">forced labor</a> of about 2000 Hungarian women and girls for Gelsenberg-Benzin-AG. About 150 died during September 1944 bombing raids (shelters and protection ditches were forbidden to them).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1933 to 1945, the city's mayor was the appointed <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> Carl Engelbert Böhmer. In 1994, the Institute for City History opened the documentation centre "Gelsenkirchen under National Socialism" (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Dokumentationsstätte "Gelsenkirchen im Nationalsozialismus"</span>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_war">After the war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: After the war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 17 December 1953, the <i>Kokerei Hassel</i> went into operation, billed as Germany's "first new <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coking_plant&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Coking plant (page does not exist)">coking plant</a>" since the war. When <a href="/wiki/Postal_code" title="Postal code">postal codes</a> (<i>Postleitzahlen</i>) were introduced in 1961, Gelsenkirchen was one of the few cities in <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> to be given two codes: Buer was given 466, while Gelsenkirchen got 465. These were in use until 1 July 1993. The "first <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">comprehensive school</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>" was opened in 1969. Scholven-Chemie AG (the old hydrogenation plant) merged with Gelsenberg-Benzin-AG to form the new corporation VEBA-Oel AG. In 1987, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> celebrated <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a> before 85,000 people at Gelsenkirchen's Parkstadion. The Pope also became an honorary member of <a href="/wiki/FC_Schalke_04" title="FC Schalke 04">FC Schalke 04</a>.</p>
<p>In 1997, the Federal Garden Show (<i>Bundesgartenschau</i> or <i>BUGA</i>) was held on the grounds of the disused <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zeche_Nordstern&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Zeche Nordstern (page does not exist)">Nordstern</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeche_Nordstern" class="extiw" title="de:Zeche Nordstern">de</a>)</span> <a href="/wiki/Coalmine" class="mw-redirect" title="Coalmine">coalmine</a> in Horst. In 1999, the last phase of the <a href="/wiki/Internationale_Bauausstellung_Emscher_Park" title="Internationale Bauausstellung Emscher Park">Internationale Bauausstellung Emscher Park</a>, an undertaking that brought together many cities in North Rhine-Westphalia, was held. <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">Coke</a> was produced at the old Hassel coking works for the last time on 29 September 1999. This marked the shutdown of the last coking plant in Gelsenkirchen, after being a coking town for more than 117 years. In the same year, Shell Solar Deutschland AG took over production of <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> equipment. On 28 April 2000, the Ewald-Hugo colliery closed – Gelsenkirchen's last colliery. Three thousand coalminers lost their jobs. In 2003, Buer celebrated its thousandth anniversary of first documentary mention, and FC Schalke 04 celebrated on 4 May 2004 its hundredth anniversary.</p>
<p>Today, Gelsenkirchen is a centre for sciences, services, and production, with good infrastructure.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_history">Jewish history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jewish history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> community of Gelsenkirchen was officially established in 1874, relatively late compared to the Jewish <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashkenazi">Ashkenazi</a> communities in Germany. In a list of 1829 to determine the salary for the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Rabbi" title="Chief Rabbi">Chief Rabbi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalia</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abraham_Sutro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abraham Sutro (page does not exist)">Abraham Sutro</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Sutro" class="extiw" title="de:Abraham Sutro">de</a>)</span>, three families were named: the families of Ruben Levi, Reuben Simon, and Herz Heimann families.<sup id="cite_ref-talmud.de_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-talmud.de-5">[5]</a></sup> With the growth of the town during the second half of the 20th century, its Jewish population also grew bigger, with about 120 Jews living in town in 1880, and a <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> established in 1885. With the growth of the community, a bigger building was built to serve as the community school.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The community continued to grow and around 1,100 Jews were living in Gelsenkirchen in 1901, a number that reached its peak of 1,300 individuals in 1933. At the turn of the 20th century the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Judaism" title="Reform Judaism">Reform Jewish</a> community was the most dominant among all Jewish communities in town, and after an <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organ</a> was installed inside the synagogue, and most prayers performed mostly in German instead of traditional Hebrew, the town <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">orthodox</a> community decided to stop its attendance of the synagogue and tried to establish a new orthodox community, led by Dr. Max Meyer, Dr. Rubens and Abraham Fröhlich, most of them living on Florastraße.<sup id="cite_ref-talmud.de_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-talmud.de-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, another Jewish orthodox congregation of Polish Jews was found in town.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1908, a lot on Wanner Straße was purchased and served the community as its cemetery until 1936, today containing about 400 graves.<sup id="cite_ref-talmud.de_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-talmud.de-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, another cemetery was built in 1927 in the suburb of <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%9Cckendorf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ückendorf (page does not exist)">Ückendorf</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%9Cckendorf" class="extiw" title="de:Ückendorf">de</a>)</span>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazi_Germany_2">Nazi Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nazi Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With the rise of <a href="/wiki/Hitler" class="mw-redirect" title="Hitler">Hitler</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Socialism" class="mw-redirect" title="National Socialism">National Socialism</a> in 1933, Jewish life in Gelsenkirchen was still relatively quiet. In August 1938, 160 Jewish businesses were still open in town. In October 1938, though, an official ban restricted these businesses and all Jewish doctors became unemployed. In the same month, the Jewish community of town was expelled. Between 1937 and 1939, the Jewish population of Gelsenkirchen dropped from 1,600 to 1,000. During <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>, the town synagogue was destroyed, after two thirds of the town's Jewish population had already left. On 27 January 1942, 350 among the 500 remaining Jews in town were deported to the <a href="/wiki/Riga_Ghetto" title="Riga Ghetto">Riga Ghetto</a>; later, the last remaining Jews were deported to Warsaw and <a href="/wiki/Theresienstadt_concentration_camp" title="Theresienstadt concentration camp">Theresienstadt concentration camp</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Gelsenkirchen_transport">The Gelsenkirchen transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Gelsenkirchen transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 31 March 1942, a Nazi deportation train set out from Gelsenkirchen and, carrying 48 Jews from the town area, made its way to the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Ghetto" title="Warsaw Ghetto">Warsaw Ghetto</a>. The train was the first to deport Jews to Warsaw and not to <a href="/wiki/Trawniki_concentration_camp" title="Trawniki concentration camp">Trawniki concentration camp</a> in southern Poland, as used before. After it left Gelsenkirchen, the train was boarded by other Jews from <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Münster">Münster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dortmund" title="Dortmund">Dortmund</a> and a few other stops along the way, and mostly by the Jews of <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, 500 in number. The arrival of this transport from Westphalia and Upper Saxony was recorded in his diaries by Adam Czerniakov, the last chairman of the Warsaw Ghetto <a href="/wiki/Judenrat" title="Judenrat">Judenrat</a>. He stated that those older than 68 were allowed to stay in Germany. The majority of these deportees were killed later on the different death sites around modern day Poland.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="After_World_War_II">After World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: After World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1946, 69 Jews returned to Gelsenkirchen and in 1958, a synagogue and cultural centre were built for the remaining community. In 2005, about 450 Jews were living in town. During the last decade of the 20th century, a noted number of Jews came to the town, after emigrating out of the former USSR. This situation made it necessary to extend the synagogue. Eventually, a new and bigger synagogue was built to serve the increasing Jewish community of Gelsenkirchen. The current community practices Orthodox Judaism, even though no family practices it at home.<sup id="cite_ref-talmud.de_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-talmud.de-5">[5]</a></sup> On 16 May 2014, antisemitic graffiti were painted on the town synagogue.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sites">Sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The building at Husemannstraße 75 belonged to Dr. Max Meyer, who built it between 1920 and 1921. A <a href="/wiki/Mezuzah" title="Mezuzah">mezuzah</a> sign can still be seen on the top right side of the door.<sup id="cite_ref-talmud.de_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-talmud.de-5">[5]</a></sup> On Florastraße, near Kennedyplatz, (formerly Schalker Straße 45), stands the house of the Tepper family, a Jewish family that vanished during the <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">Holocaust</a>. As part of the national <a href="/wiki/Stolperstein" title="Stolperstein">Stolperstein</a> project, five bricks, commemorating the Jewish inhabitants, were installed outside the house.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

According to a study by the <a href="/wiki/Bertelsmann_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Bertelsmann Foundation">Bertelsmann Foundation</a>, Gelsenkirchen is, after <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a>, Germany's fourth business-friendliest city.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gelsenkirchen lies on <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">autobahns</a> <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_2" title="Bundesautobahn 2">A 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_40" title="Bundesautobahn 40">A 40</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_42" title="Bundesautobahn 42">A 42</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_52" title="Bundesautobahn 52">A 52</a>, as well as on Bundesstraßen (Federal Highways) B 224, B 226 and B 227. <a href="/wiki/Gelsenkirchen_Hauptbahnhof" title="Gelsenkirchen Hauptbahnhof">Gelsenkirchen Hauptbahnhof</a> (central station) lies at the junction of the <a href="/wiki/Duisburg%E2%80%93Dortmund_railway" title="Duisburg–Dortmund railway">Duisburg–Dortmund</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Essen%E2%80%93Gelsenkirchen_railway" title="Essen–Gelsenkirchen railway">Essen–Gelsenkirchen</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wanne-Eickel%E2%80%93Hamburg_railway" title="Wanne-Eickel–Hamburg railway">Gelsenkirchen–Münster</a> lines.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rhine%E2%80%93Herne_Canal" title="Rhine–Herne Canal">Rhine–Herne Canal</a> has a commercial-industrial harbour in Gelsenkirchen. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen_Harbour&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gelsenkirchen Harbour (page does not exist)">Gelsenkirchen Harbour</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafen_Gelsenkirchen" class="extiw" title="de:Hafen Gelsenkirchen">de</a>)</span> has a yearly turnover of 2 million tonnes and a water surface area of about 1.2 square kilometres (0.5 square miles), one of Germany's biggest and most important canal harbours, and is furthermore connected to <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a>'s railway network at Gelsenkirchen Hauptbahnhof.</p>
<p>Local transport in Gelsenkirchen is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Bochum/Gelsenkirchen" title="Trams in Bochum/Gelsenkirchen">Bochum/Gelsenkirchen tramway network</a> and buses run by the <a href="/wiki/BOGESTRA" title="BOGESTRA">Bochum-Gelsenkirchener Straßenbahn AG</a> (BOGESTRA), as well as by buses operated by Vestische Straßenbahnen GmbH in the city's north (despite its name, it nowadays runs only buses). Some <a href="/wiki/Stadtbahn" title="Stadtbahn">Stadtbahn</a> and tram lines are operated by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruhrbahn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ruhrbahn (page does not exist)">Ruhrbahn</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruhrbahn" class="extiw" title="de:Ruhrbahn">de</a>)</span>. All these services have an integrated fare structure within the <a href="/wiki/Verkehrsverbund_Rhein-Ruhr" title="Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Ruhr">VRR</a>. There are three tram lines, one light rail line, and about 50 bus routes in Gelsenkirchen.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gelsenkirchen is the headquarters of the <i>Verband Lokaler Rundfunk in Nordrhein-Westfalen e.V. (VLR)</i> (Network of Local Radio in North Rhine-Westphalia Registered Association). REL (<i>Radio Emscher-Lippe</i>) is also headquartered in Gelsenkirchen.</p>
<p>Among newspapers, the <i>Buersche Zeitung</i> was a daily till 2006. The <i><a href="/wiki/Ruhr_Nachrichten" title="Ruhr Nachrichten">Ruhr Nachrichten</a></i> ceased publication in Gelsenkirchen in April 2006. Now, the <i><a href="/wiki/Westdeutsche_Allgemeine_Zeitung" title="Westdeutsche Allgemeine Zeitung">Westdeutsche Allgemeine Zeitung</a></i> is the only local newspaper in Gelsenkirchen. The local station <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Emscher-Lippe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Radio Emscher-Lippe (page does not exist)">Radio Emscher-Lippe</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_Emscher-Lippe" class="extiw" title="de:Radio Emscher-Lippe">de</a>)</span> also reports the local news.</p>
<p>There is also a free weekly newspaper, the <i>Stadtspiegel Gelsenkirchen</i>, along with monthly, or irregular, local publications called the <i>Familienpost</i> and the <i>Beckhausener Kurier</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_science">Education and science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Education and science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gelsenkirchen has 51 <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school" title="Elementary school">elementary schools</a> (36 public schools, 12 Catholic schools, 3 Protestant schools), 8 <i><a href="/wiki/Hauptschule" title="Hauptschule">Hauptschulen</a></i>, 6 <i><a href="/wiki/Realschule" title="Realschule">Realschulen</a></i>, 7 <i><a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Gymnasien</a></i>, and 4 <i><a href="/wiki/Education_in_Germany" title="Education in Germany">Gesamtschulen</a></i>, among which the <i>Gesamtschule Bismarck</i>, as the only comprehensive school run by the Westphalian branch of the Evangelical (<a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a>) Church, warrants special mention.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschule</a> Gelsenkirchen</i>, founded in 1992, has also campuses in <a href="/wiki/Bocholt,_Germany" title="Bocholt, Germany">Bocholt</a> and Recklinghausen with the following course offerings: <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">Economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Science">Computer Science</a>, Engineering Physics, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_Engineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical Engineering">Electrical Engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_Engineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanical Engineering">Mechanical Engineering</a>, and Supply and Disposal Engineering.</p>
<p>There is a <i><a href="/wiki/Volkshochschule#Germany_and_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Volkshochschule">Volkshochschule</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Adult_education" title="Adult education">adult education</a> as well as a city library with three branches.</p>
<p>The Institute for Underground Infrastructure, founded in 1994 and associated with the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_University_Bochum" title="Ruhr University Bochum">Ruhr University Bochum</a>, provides a wide range of research, certification, and colsulting services. The <a href="/wiki/Science_park" title="Science park">science park</a> created in 1995 by <a href="/wiki/Internationale_Bauausstellung_Emscher_Park" title="Internationale Bauausstellung Emscher Park">Internationale Bauausstellung Emscher Park</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wissenschaftspark_Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wissenschaftspark Gelsenkirchen (page does not exist)">Wissenschaftspark Gelsenkirchen</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wissenschaftspark_Gelsenkirchen" class="extiw" title="de:Wissenschaftspark Gelsenkirchen">de</a>)</span>, provides a pathway to restructure the local economy from coal- and steel-based industries to solar energy and project management.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gelsenkirchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:080110_schalke_arena_germany.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/080110_schalke_arena_germany.JPG/220px-080110_schalke_arena_germany.JPG" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/080110_schalke_arena_germany.JPG/330px-080110_schalke_arena_germany.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/080110_schalke_arena_germany.JPG/440px-080110_schalke_arena_germany.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1360" /></a>

<p>Gelsenkirchen is home of the <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga" title="Bundesliga">Bundesliga</a> club <a href="/wiki/FC_Schalke_04" title="FC Schalke 04">FC Schalke 04</a>. Schalke's home ground, <a href="/wiki/Veltins-Arena" title="Veltins-Arena">Veltins-Arena</a>. It was one of 12 German cities to host matches during the <a href="/wiki/2006_FIFA_World_Cup" title="2006 FIFA World Cup">2006 FIFA World Cup</a>, hosting matches between <a href="/wiki/Poland_national_football_team" title="Poland national football team">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecuador_national_football_team" title="Ecuador national football team">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina_national_football_team" title="Argentina national football team">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serbia_national_football_team" title="Serbia national football team">Serbia and Montenegro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_football_team" title="Portugal national football team">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico_national_football_team" title="Mexico national football team">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_men%27s_national_soccer_team" title="United States men's national soccer team">USA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic_national_football_team" title="Czech Republic national football team">Czech Republic</a>.</p>
<p>German football players <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0lkay_G%C3%BCndo%C4%9Fan" title="İlkay Gündoğan">İlkay Gündoğan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesut_%C3%96zil" title="Mesut Özil">Mesut Özil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Neuer" title="Manuel Neuer">Manuel Neuer</a> were born in Gelsenkirchen. German football manager <a href="/wiki/Michael_Skibbe" title="Michael Skibbe">Michael Skibbe</a> was also born in Gelsenkirchen.</p>
<p>Since 1912, Gelsenkirchen owns the <a href="/wiki/Harness_racing" title="Harness racing">harness racing</a> track <a href="/wiki/Trabrennbahn_Gelsenkirchen" title="Trabrennbahn Gelsenkirchen">Trabrennbahn Gelsenkirchen</a> (also referred as GelsenTrabPark).</p>

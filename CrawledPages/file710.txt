			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Museum of Comparative Zoology</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Museum of Comparative Zoology</b>, full name "The Louis Agassiz Museum of Comparative Zoology", often abbreviated simply to "MCZ", is the <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a> museum located on the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>. It is one of three natural history research museums at Harvard whose public face is the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Harvard Museum of Natural History">Harvard Museum of Natural History</a>. Harvard MCZ's collections consist of some 21 million specimens, of which several thousand are on rotating display at the public museum. The current director of the Museum of Comparative Zoology is <a href="/wiki/James_Hanken" title="James Hanken">James Hanken</a>, the Louis Agassiz Professor of Zoology at Harvard University.</p>
<p>Many of the exhibits in the public museum have not only zoological interest but also historical significance. Past exhibits have included a <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> <a href="/wiki/Sand_dollar" title="Sand dollar">sand dollar</a> which was found by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> in 1834, Captain Cook's <a href="/wiki/Mamo" title="Mamo">mamo</a>, and two <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasants</a> that once belonged to <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, now on loan to Mount Vernon in Virginia.</p>
<p>The Harvard Museum of Natural History is physically connected to the <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Museum_of_Archaeology_and_Ethnology" title="Peabody Museum of Archaeology and Ethnology">Peabody Museum of Archaeology and Ethnology</a>; for visitors, one admission ticket grants access to both museums. The research collections of the Museum of Comparative Zoology are not open to the public.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Museum of Comparative Zoology was founded in 1859 through the efforts of zoologist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Agassiz" title="Louis Agassiz">Louis Agassiz</a>, and the museum used to be referred to as "The Agassiz" after its founder. Agassiz designed the collection to illustrate the variety and <a href="/wiki/Comparative_anatomy" title="Comparative anatomy">comparative relationships</a> of <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> life.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Departments">Departments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Departments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum comprises twelve departments: <a href="/wiki/Biological_Oceanography" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological Oceanography">Biological Oceanography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entomology" title="Entomology">Entomology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herpetology" title="Herpetology">Herpetology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ichthyology" title="Ichthyology">Ichthyology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate_paleontology" title="Invertebrate paleontology">Invertebrate Paleontology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate_zoology" title="Invertebrate zoology">Invertebrate Zoology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mammalogy" title="Mammalogy">Mammalogy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_invertebrates" title="Marine invertebrates">Marine invertebrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malacology" title="Malacology">Malacology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">Ornithology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_Genetics" class="mw-redirect" title="Population Genetics">Population Genetics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate_Paleontology" class="mw-redirect" title="Vertebrate Paleontology">Vertebrate Paleontology</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mayr" title="Ernst Mayr">Ernst Mayr Library</a> and its archives join in supporting the work of the museum. The Ernst Mayr Library is a founding member of the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.biodiversitylibrary.org">Biodiversity Heritage Library</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum publishes two journals: the <i>Bulletin of the Museum of Comparative Zoology at Harvard College</i>, first published in 1869,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and <i>Breviora</i>, first published in 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Displays">Displays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Displays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In contrast to numerous more modern museums, the Harvard Museum of Natural History has many hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Taxidermy" title="Taxidermy">stuffed animals</a> on display, from the collections of the Museum of Comparative Zoology. Notable exhibits include <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whale</a> <a href="/wiki/Skeleton" title="Skeleton">skeletons</a>, the largest <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtle</a> <a href="/wiki/Animal_shell" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal shell">shell</a> ever found (eight feet long), "the Harvard <a href="/wiki/Mastodon" title="Mastodon">mastodon</a>", a 42-foot (13&#160;m) long <i><a href="/wiki/Kronosaurus" title="Kronosaurus">Kronosaurus</a></i> skeleton, the skeleton of a <a href="/wiki/Dodo" title="Dodo">dodo</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Coelacanth" title="Coelacanth">coelacanth</a> preserved in fluid. The two-story Great Mammal Hall was renovated in 2009 in celebration of the 150th anniversary of founding of the Museum of Comparative Zoology.</p>
<p>New and changing exhibitions in the Harvard Museum of Natural History include "Evolution" (2008); "The Language of Color" (2008 to 2013); "Arthropods: Creatures that Rule" (2006); "New England Forests" (2011); and "Mollusks: Shelled Masters of the Marine Realm" (2012).</p>

<p>The <b>Peabody Museum of Archaeology and Ethnology</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museum</a> affiliated with <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>. Founded in 1866, the Peabody Museum is one of the oldest and largest museums focusing on <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropological</a> material, with particular focus on the <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">ethnography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaeology_of_the_Americas" title="Archaeology of the Americas">archaeology of the Americas</a>. The Museum is caretaker to over 1.2 million objects, some 900 linear feet of documents, 2,000 maps and site plans, and approximately 500,000 photographs.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Museum is located at Divinity Avenue on the Harvard University campus. The Museum is one of the four Harvard Museums of Science &amp; Culture open to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Museum_of_Archaeology_and_Ethnology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Museum was established as a gift from <a href="/wiki/George_Peabody" title="George Peabody">George Peabody</a>, a native of South Danvers (now <a href="/wiki/Peabody,_Massachusetts" title="Peabody, Massachusetts">Peabody</a>), a wealthy American financier and philanthropist on October 8, 1866.<sup id="cite_ref-Watson--pg4-5_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson--pg4-5-3">[3]</a></sup> Peabody committed $150,000 to be used, according to the terms of the trust, to establish the position of Peabody Professor-Curator, to purchase artifacts, and to construct a building to house its collections. Peabody directed his trustees to organize the construction of "a suitable fireproof museum building, upon land to be given for that purpose, free of cost or rental, by the President and Fellows of Harvard College."<sup id="cite_ref-Watson--pg4-5_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watson--pg4-5-3">[3]</a></sup> The Museum opened its first exhibition consisting of a small number of prehistoric artifacts from the <a href="/wiki/Merrimack_Valley" title="Merrimack Valley">Merrimack Valley</a> in Harvard University's Boylston Hall in 1867. In 1877, the long-awaited museum building was completed and ready for occupancy. The building that houses the Peabody was expanded in 1888 and again in 1913.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections">Collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Museum_of_Archaeology_and_Ethnology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peabody Museum is steward to archaeological, ethnographic, <a href="/wiki/Osteology" title="Osteology">osteological</a>, and archival collections from many countries and covering millions of years of human cultural, social, and biological history, with particular focus on the cultures of North and South America and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific Islands</a>, as well as collections from Africa, Europe, and Asia.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The Peabody’s archaeological and ethnographic holdings from North America form more than a quarter of its collections, with artifacts from many parts of the continent and spanning 10,000 years, including from the earliest excavations in the Northeast and Mimbres collections from the Southwest, the <a href="/wiki/Grace_Nicholson" title="Grace Nicholson">Grace Nicholson</a> Collection of California baskets, and the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_and_Clark" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis and Clark">Lewis and Clark</a> Collection.</li>
<li>Central America. The Museum’s Central American collection focuses on archaeological materials from eastern <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, lower <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> islands and Central <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. The Museum hosts a large collection of <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Mayan</a> material culture from <a href="/wiki/Cop%C3%A1n" title="Copán">Copán</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holmul" title="Holmul">Holmul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labna" title="Labna">Labna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piedras_Negras_(Maya_site)" title="Piedras Negras (Maya site)">Piedras Negras</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uaxactun" title="Uaxactun">Uaxactun</a>, stone sculptures from <a href="/wiki/Cop%C3%A1n" title="Copán">Copán</a>, fine artifacts from the Sacred Cenote of <a href="/wiki/Chich%C3%A9n_Itza" class="mw-redirect" title="Chichén Itza">Chichén Itza</a>, and 600 plaster casts of monuments at important Central American sites.</li>
<li>South America. Some of the Peabody’s earliest accessions, collected by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Agassiz" title="Louis Agassiz">Louis Agassiz</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Emanuel_Agassiz" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander Emanuel Agassiz">Alexander Emanuel Agassiz</a>, form the main part of the Peabody's South American ethnographic collections. These include the collection of nineteenth- and twentieth-century <a href="/wiki/Featherwork" title="Featherwork">featherwork</a> <a href="/wiki/Headdress" class="mw-redirect" title="Headdress">headdresses</a> and ornaments from the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Basin">Amazon Basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andean_textiles" title="Andean textiles">Andean textiles</a> and the <a href="/wiki/William_Farabee" class="mw-redirect" title="William Farabee">William Farabee</a> collection of <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peruvian</a> ceremonial and domestic objects. Important archaeological collections include <a href="/wiki/Chim%C3%BA_culture" title="Chimú culture">Chimú</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazca_culture" title="Nazca culture">Nazca</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Moche_(culture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Moche (culture)">Moche</a> pottery; <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca</a> and Chimú metalwork; and a collection of prehistoric-period textiles.</li>
<li>Asia. The Museum’s Asian holdings include one of the earliest collections of objects made and used by the <a href="/wiki/Ainu_people" title="Ainu people">Ainu people</a>, Japan’s indigenous people; <a href="/wiki/Japanese_ceramics" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese ceramics">Japanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_ceramics" title="Chinese ceramics">Chinese ceramics</a> and porcelains; colorful textiles made by the <a href="/wiki/Kachin_people" title="Kachin people">Kachin people</a> living in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> (Burma) and contiguous areas of India and China, by the <a href="/wiki/Shan_people" title="Shan people">Shan people</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> hide costumes and carved wooden household items. Archaeological materials dominate the Asia collections with an extensive collection of excavated artifacts from <a href="/wiki/Tepe_Yahya" title="Tepe Yahya">Tepe Yahya</a> (Iran) and <a href="/wiki/Tarsus,_Mersin" title="Tarsus, Mersin">Tarsus</a> (Turkey).</li>
<li>Africa. The Museum’s holdings include over 20,000 items in four significant collections. The three principal collections, gathered from <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a> during the first half of the twentieth century, include a diverse range of objects used in daily or ritual life. The fourth collection contains more than 200 musical instruments including drums and hand pianos. Archaeological collections are represented by <a href="/wiki/George_Andrew_Reisner" title="George Andrew Reisner">George Andrew Reisner</a>'s excavations in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>.</li>
<li>Oceania. Collected by eighteenth-century Boston merchants, traders, and researchers during their Pacific voyages the 23,000 items of this collection include <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a> <a href="/wiki/Tapa_cloth" title="Tapa cloth">tapa</a> figures and carved wooden statues; <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaiian</a> <a href="/wiki/Feather_cloak" title="Feather cloak">feather cloaks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mahiole" title="Mahiole">Mahiole</a> feather helmets; <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Maori</a> carved <a href="/wiki/Whakairo" title="Whakairo">whakairo</a> door panels, bowls, and human figures; <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Javanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Wayang" title="Wayang">wayang</a> <a href="/wiki/Shadow_puppet" class="mw-redirect" title="Shadow puppet">shadow puppets</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesian</a> canoes and <a href="/wiki/Shell_jewelry" title="Shell jewelry">shell jewelry</a>.</li>
<li>Europe. In addition to <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a> collections from France, especially from the site of <a href="/wiki/Abri_Pataud" title="Abri Pataud">Abri Pataud</a> where <a href="/wiki/Cro-Magnon" title="Cro-Magnon">Cro-Magnon</a> man once lived, there are materials from <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> through <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Europe" title="Iron Age Europe">Iron Age Europe</a>, with the notable collection of the <a href="/wiki/Duchess_Marie_Antoinette_of_Mecklenburg" title="Duchess Marie Antoinette of Mecklenburg">Duchess Marie Antoinette of Mecklenburg</a> materials, excavated at <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_Archaeological_Site_in_Va%C4%8De" title="Hallstatt Archaeological Site in Vače">Hallstatt Archaeological Site in Vače</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, surveyed by her mother <a href="/wiki/Princess_Marie_of_Windisch-Graetz" title="Princess Marie of Windisch-Graetz">Princess Marie of Windisch-Graetz</a>. The collection also includes a portion of the French archaeologist and political activist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Laurent_Gabriel_de_Mortillet" title="Louis Laurent Gabriel de Mortillet">Louis Laurent Gabriel de Mortillet</a>’s collections from Central Europe, a <a href="/wiki/Venus_figurine" class="mw-redirect" title="Venus figurine">Venus figurine</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Grimaldi_Man" title="Grimaldi Man">Grimaldi Man</a> caves in Italy, and Neolithic <a href="/wiki/Stone_tool" title="Stone tool">stone tools</a> from northwestern Europe.</li>
<li>Paintings and Drawing Collections. Numbering nearly 200 paintings and 950 works on paper, the collection of artwork is a complementary addition to the object collections. About half, representing the David I. Bushnell, Jr. Collection of American Art, contains works by Alexander de Batz, <a href="/wiki/George_Catlin" title="George Catlin">George Catlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bird_King" title="Charles Bird King">Charles Bird King</a>, George Gibbs, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Kern" title="Edward Kern">Edward Kern</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Webber" title="John Webber">John Webber</a>, and over 130 oils, watercolors, and drawings by <a href="/wiki/Seth_Eastman" class="mw-redirect" title="Seth Eastman">Seth Eastman</a>, the pictorial historian of native North Americans. There are also painted portraits of Native Americans by <a href="/wiki/Elbridge_Ayer_Burbank" title="Elbridge Ayer Burbank">Elbridge Ayer Burbank</a>, some being the only extant representation of the subject. Other significant pieces include the works of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charlot" title="Jean Charlot">Jean Charlot</a> depicting the monumental architecture of Mesoamerica, twentieth-century works by Native American artists, an extensive series of <a href="/wiki/Inuit_art" title="Inuit art">Inuit</a> prints, and thirty-eight works depicting people and places in India and Tibet by Andre Chéronnet Champollion.</li>
<li>Archival Collections. The holdings of both the records and photographic archives present another dimension to the scholarly work of the anthropologists and archaeologists whose collected artifacts reside in the Peabody. The papers and manuscripts housed in the archives include institutional (departmental and Museum) records, special collections, and materials associated with over 70 anthropological/archaeological expeditions, dozens of faculty and researchers, and other research projects. The <a href="/wiki/Daguerreotype" title="Daguerreotype">daguerreotypes</a>, colored <a href="/wiki/Transparency_(projection)" title="Transparency (projection)">transparencies</a>, and other photographic images in the Peabody’s collections number about half a million from the archaeology and ethnology of many countries, with particular emphasis in Native American cultures and portraits, Mexico, northern Central America, China and Southwest Asia, Africa, and <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_art" title="Pre-Columbian art">pre-Columbian</a> objects and art.</li>
<li>Osteological Collections. The osteological collections of the Peabody Museum derive from more than eighty countries on six continents and include human and non-human <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primate</a> remains, fossils, and casts. These are collected mainly from the Americas, Eurasia, and Africa. Approximately 40 percent of the more than 18,500 human and non-human individuals (MNI) currently represented in the collections are from the United States. The Museum also has anatomical teaching and <a href="/wiki/Hominid" class="mw-redirect" title="Hominid">hominid</a> cast collections.</li>
</ul>
<p><i><small>Source: The Peabody Museum, Collections by Area</small></i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_exhibitions">Permanent exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Museum_of_Archaeology_and_Ethnology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Permanent exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><i>Change &amp; Continuity: Hall of the North American Indian</i> explores North American cultures through the objects produced by <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a> of the nineteenth century. The Changes and Continuity exhibit considers historic interactions between native peoples and Europeans during a period of profound social change.</li>
<li><i>Day of the Dead/Día de los Muertos</i> includes of a <a href="/wiki/Day_of_the_Dead" title="Day of the Dead">Day of the Dead</a> altar or <i>offrenda</i> in the "Encounters with the Americas" gallery. It represents the original <a href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">Aztec</a> origins of the holiday and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> symbols incorporated into the tradition.</li>
<li><i>Digging Veritas: The Archaeology and History of the Indian College and Student Life at Colonial Harvard</i> uses archaeological finds from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Yard" title="Harvard Yard">Harvard Yard</a>, historic maps, and other sources to reveal how students lived at <a href="/wiki/Colonial_history_of_the_United_States" title="Colonial history of the United States">colonial</a> Harvard, and the role of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_College" title="Indian College">Indian College</a> in Harvard’s early years.</li>
<li><i>Encounters with the Americas</i> explores the native cultures of <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> before and after <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>' arrival in 1492</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temporary_exhibitions">Temporary exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Museum_of_Archaeology_and_Ethnology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temporary exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><i>Wiyohpiyata: Lakota Images of the Contested West</i>: in this exhibition, co-curators Castle McLaughlin and <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota</a> artist Butch Thunder Hawk use ambient sound, motion, scent, and historic and contemporary <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> art to animate nineteenth century Lakota drawings from a warrior’s ledger collected at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Little_Bighorn" title="Battle of the Little Bighorn">Little Bighorn battlefield</a>. This exhibit presents Lakota perspectives on westward expansion while exploring culturally-shaped relationships between words, objects, and images.</li>
<li><i>All the World Is Here: Harvard’s Peabody Museum and the Invention of American Anthropology</i> traces the Peabody Museum’s early days under its second director, <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Ward_Putnam" title="Frederic Ward Putnam">Frederic Ward Putnam</a>, including its role in the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">1893 World’s Fair</a>, with over 600 objects from Asia, Oceania, and the Americas.</li>
</ul>
<p>Source: The Peabody Museum, Current Exhibitions<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

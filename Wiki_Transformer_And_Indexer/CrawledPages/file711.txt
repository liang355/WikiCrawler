<p>The <b>Carpenter Center for the Visual Arts</b> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> is the only building actually designed by <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and one of only two in the Americas (the other is the <a href="/wiki/Curutchet_House" title="Curutchet House">Curutchet House</a> in <a href="/wiki/La_Plata" title="La Plata">La Plata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>). Le Corbusier designed it with the collaboration of Chilean architect <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_Jullian_de_la_Fuente" title="Guillermo Jullian de la Fuente">Guillermo Jullian de la Fuente</a> at his 35 rue de Sèvres studio; the on-site preparation of the construction plans was handled by the office of <a href="/wiki/Josep_Llu%C3%ADs_Sert" title="Josep Lluís Sert">Josep Lluís Sert</a>, then dean of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Graduate_School_of_Design" title="Harvard Graduate School of Design">Harvard Graduate School of Design</a>. He had formerly worked in Le Corbusier's atelier and had been instrumental in winning him the commission. The building was completed in 1962.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commission">Commission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carpenter_Center_for_the_Visual_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Commission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the mid-1950s, the idea of creating a place for the visual arts at Harvard began to take shape. A new department dedicated to the visual arts was created, and the need for a building to house the new department arose. A budget was set for $1.3 million, and the proposal was included in a Harvard fundraising program. The project immediately elicited a response from St. Vrain Carpenter, an alumnus who supplied $1.5 million for the proposed design center.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The donation propelled the project forward, and the Committee for the Practice of Visual Arts began to look for an architect to undertake the project. Originally, the committee had recommended that the building be designed by "a first rate American architect" who would be in the company of Charles Bulfinch and Walter Gropius, among others. However, José Luis Sert, who was at the time Dean of the Graduate School of Design and chairman of the committee suggested that his friend and previous collaborator, Le Corbusier, be asked to design the building. Delayed due to scheduling and payment conflicts, Le Corbusier eventually accepted and made his first of two visits to Cambridge in 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">Design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carpenter_Center_for_the_Visual_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After much debate, a site was chosen between Quincy and Prescott Streets, abiding by the original proposal for the building.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The allotted space was quite small, so the completed building presents itself as a compact, roughly cylindrical mass bisected by an S-shaped ramp on the third floor. Le Corbusier's earliest design showed a much more pronounced ramp that further separated the two parts of the central mass. However, the early design created the problem of too much disruption of the central mass. This problem auditorium reconciled by using a pinwheel effect so that in the finally executed design, the two halves meet at a vertical core that houses an elevator. The concrete ramp is cantilevered from this central spine and stands atop a few pilotis. The landing at the top of the ramp is located in the core of the building and leads to various studios and exhibition spaces seen through glass windows and doors, providing views into the building's instructional and displaying functions without interrupting the activities in progress.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The exterior of the Carpenter Center presents itself very differently from different angles. From Prescott Street looking toward the curved studio space, one can see the brise-soleil that are placed perpendicular to the direction of the central portion of the ramp, making only their narrow ends visible from the street. The Quincy Street view, however, reveals ondulatoires on this studio's exterior curve, which interfere with the building's curve less than the brise-soleil do on the opposite side. On the ramp from Quincy street just before entering the building, one sees grids of square and rectangles of the windows, brise-soleils, and studio spaces, rather than the curves of the two halves of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Le_Corbusier's_Five_Points"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Le_Corbusier.27s_Five_Points">Le Corbusier's Five Points</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carpenter_Center_for_the_Visual_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Le Corbusier's Five Points">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During his career, Le Corbusier developed a set of architectural principles that dictated his technique, which he called "the Five Points of a New Architecture" and were most evident in his <a href="/wiki/Villa_Savoye" title="Villa Savoye">Villa Savoye</a>. The five points are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pilotis" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilotis">Pilotis</a> – Replacement of supporting walls by a grid of <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete_column" title="Reinforced concrete column">reinforced concrete columns</a> that bears the structural load is the basis of the new aesthetic.</li>
<li>The free designing of the ground plan—the absence of supporting walls—means the house is unrestrained in its internal use.</li>
<li>The free design of the <a href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade" class="mw-redirect" title="Façade">façade</a>—separating the exterior of the building from its structural function—sets the façade free from structural constraints.</li>
<li>The horizontal window, which cuts the façade along its entire length, lights rooms equally.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roof_garden" title="Roof garden">Roof gardens</a> on a flat roof can serve a domestic purpose while providing essential protection to the concrete roof.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Because the Carpenter Center was to be his only building in America, he felt it should be a synthesis of his architectural principles and therefore incorporated his Five Points into its design.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carpenter_Center_for_the_Visual_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The building now houses the department of Visual and Environmental Studies of the University, and is the venue for screenings by the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Film_Archive" title="Harvard Film Archive">Harvard Film Archive</a>.</p>
<p>Le Corbusier never actually saw the building. He was invited to the opening ceremony, but he declined the invitation on account of his poor health.</p>
<p>The French artist <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Huyghe" title="Pierre Huyghe">Pierre Huyghe</a> explored the creation of the building in his 2004 work <i>This Is Not A Time For Dreaming".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carpenter_Center_for_the_Visual_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Le Corbusier later became well-known as being a major influence for the <a href="/wiki/Urban_renewal" title="Urban renewal">urban renewal</a> projects that replaced <a href="/wiki/Scollay_Square" title="Scollay Square">Scollay Square</a> with <a href="/wiki/Boston_City_Hall_Plaza" title="Boston City Hall Plaza">Boston City Hall Plaza</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_End,_Boston" title="West End, Boston">West End</a> of Boston.</p>

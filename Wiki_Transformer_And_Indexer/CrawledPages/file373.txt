<p><b>Amberg</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈambɛrk]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Amberg.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5b/Amberg.ogg" class="internal" title="Amberg.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a town in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is located in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Palatinate" title="Upper Palatinate">Upper Palatinate</a>, roughly halfway between <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth" title="Bayreuth">Bayreuth</a>. In 2013, over 41,000 people lived in the town.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town was first mentioned in 1034, at that time under the name <b>Ammenberg</b>. It became an important trading centre in the Middle Ages, exporting mainly <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> and iron products. In 1269, together with <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a>, the town became subordinate to the <a href="/wiki/Wittelsbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Wittelsbach">Wittelsbach</a> dynasty that ruled Bavaria.</p>
<p>In 1329 the town and the entire Upper Palatinate fell to a branch line of the <a href="/wiki/Wittelsbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Wittelsbach">Wittelsbach</a> family and was no longer part of the duchy of Bavaria. Although in geographic terms it was regarded as Bavarian and the region was part of the Bavarian circle in the organization of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Circle" title="Imperial Circle">Imperial Circles</a>. The rulers of Upper Palatinate were open-minded towards <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>. In the 16th century the town turned to <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran church">Lutheranism</a>, but attempts of the ruling family to introduce the more radical <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a> failed due to the reluctance of its citizens.</p>
<p>In 1628 Amberg and Upper Palatinate became part of the electorate of Bavaria. The inhabitants were given the choice: return to <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> or leave the town forever. Many families left the town and fled to the <a href="/wiki/Free_Imperial_Cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Imperial Cities">Free Imperial Cities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>.</p>
<p>On 24&#160;August 1796, during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a>, the city and its environs were the locale of a major <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Amberg" title="Battle of Amberg">battle</a> at which 35,000&#160;French, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Jourdan" class="mw-redirect" title="Jean Baptiste Jourdan">Jean Baptiste Jourdan</a> fought with 40,000 Austrians under the command of <a href="/wiki/Archduke_Charles,_Duke_of_Teschen" title="Archduke Charles, Duke of Teschen">Archduke Charles</a>; the French suffered significantly more losses in this Austrian victory. Amberg was the regional capital of Upper Palatinate until 1810 when power was transferred to the larger city of <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a>.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, when Bavaria fell into the American Sector, Amberg was home to Pond Barracks, a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> post. <a href="/wiki/I.F.Stone" class="mw-redirect" title="I.F.Stone">I.F.Stone</a> writes about it in his book <a href="/wiki/Underground_to_Palestine" title="Underground to Palestine">Underground to Palestine</a> (pp.&#160;31ff). The post was closed in 1992 and the facility turned over to the local community for housing, most of it for social housing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_History">Jewish History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Jewish History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> had settled in Amberg before 1294, when the first documentation can be found. Shortly after, in 1298, thirteen of the town Jews died during the <a href="/wiki/Rindfleisch_massacres" class="mw-redirect" title="Rindfleisch massacres">Rindfleisch massacres</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jvl_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jvl-2">[2]</a></sup> Nevertheless, in 1347 six families received permission to settle in Amberg and twenty years after, in 1367, a <a href="/wiki/Yeshivah" class="mw-redirect" title="Yeshivah">Yeshivah</a> was opened in it, though the Jewish community was expelled from Amberg in 1403.<sup id="cite_ref-jvl_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jvl-2">[2]</a></sup> Upon the expulsion, the <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> was annexed to the nearby <a href="/wiki/Local_church" title="Local church">church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Twelve Jews remained in town in 1942. The few survivors returned to the town after 1945, and a <a href="/wiki/Displaced_persons_camp" title="Displaced persons camp">displaced persons camp</a> named <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://lamoth.info/index.php?p=digitallibrary/digitalcontent&amp;id=467">Amberg</a> - located nearby the town - housed mostly <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://collections.ushmm.org/search/catalog/pa1170155">refugees and survivors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result of immigration from the former <a href="/wiki/USSR" class="mw-redirect" title="USSR">USSR</a> to <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> population in town grew to about 275 in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-jvl_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jvl-2">[2]</a></sup> A <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.maven.co.il/synagogues/C3389Y41640RX">synagogue</a> exists in town nowadays.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">Sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A defining feature of the town is the <i>Stadtbrille</i> (literally: town spectacles) – a bridge, originally a part of the town fortifications, whose arches reflected on the river waters resemble a pair of spectacles.</p>
<p>Other tourist attractions in Amberg include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Market Square, which contains the Gothic town hall (built in 1358) and the late-Gothic parish church of St. Martin</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Palace,_Amberg" title="Electoral Palace, Amberg">New Palace</a>, the former residence of the <a href="/wiki/Count_palatine" title="Count palatine">counts of the Rhenish Palatinate</a>, built at the beginning of the 15th century and renovated in 1603</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>A well-preserved section of the medieval walls and gates</li>
<li>The baroque <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> monastery on the Hill of Our Lady Help of Christians (Germ. <i>Mariahilfberg</i>) above the town. This hill was given its name during the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> in 1633/4 when the locals beseeched the Virgin Mary to rid them of the plague.</li>
<li>The '<a href="/wiki/Eh%27h%C3%A4usl" title="Eh'häusl">Little Wedding House</a>' (local German dialect <i>Eh’häusl</i>), claimed by town authorities to be the world's smallest hotel. Built in 1728, the 2 metre wide hotel was 'sold' to young couples for one night to circumvent laws prohibiting marriages between poor people.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The town museum (Stadtmuseum Amberg) includes exhibits on life and industry in Amberg, the history of clothing and works of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Mathias_Prechtl" title="Michael Mathias Prechtl">Michael Mathias Prechtl</a> and houses travelling exhibitions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Air Museum (Luftmuseum), opened in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

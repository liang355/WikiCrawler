<p><b>Crimson</b> is a strong, <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> color, inclining to <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a>. It originally meant the <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kermes_(dye)" title="Kermes (dye)">kermes dye</a> produced from a <a href="/wiki/Scale_insect" title="Scale insect">scale insect</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kermes_vermilio" title="Kermes vermilio">Kermes vermilio</a></i>, but the name is now sometimes also used as a generic term for slightly bluish-red colors that are between black and <a href="/wiki/Rose_(color)" title="Rose (color)">rose</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Crimson</b> (NR4) is produced using the dried bodies of the <a href="/wiki/Kermes_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kermes (genus)">kermes</a> insect, which were gathered commercially in Mediterranean countries, where they live on the <a href="/wiki/Kermes_oak" class="mw-redirect" title="Kermes oak">kermes oak</a>, and sold throughout Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Kermes dyes have been found in burial wrappings in Anglo-Scandinavian <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>. They fell out of use with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Cochineal" title="Cochineal">cochineal</a>, because although the dyes were comparable in quality and color intensity it needed ten to twelve times as much kermes to produce the same effect as cochineal.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Carmine" title="Carmine">Carmine</a></b> is the name given to the dye made from the dried bodies of the female cochineal, although the name <b>crimson</b> is sometimes applied to these dyes too. Cochineal appears to have been brought to Europe during the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> by the Spaniard <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a>, and the name 'carmine' is derived from the French <i>carmin</i>. It was first described by Mathioli in 1549. The pigment is also called <i>cochineal</i> after the insect from which it is made.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Alizarin" title="Alizarin">Alizarin</a></b> (PR83) is a pigment that was first synthesized in 1868 by the German <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemists</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gr%C3%A4be" title="Carl Gräbe">Carl Gräbe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Liebermann" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl Liebermann">Carl Liebermann</a> and replaced the natural pigment <a href="/wiki/Madder_lake" class="mw-redirect" title="Madder lake">madder lake</a>. Alizarin crimson is a dye bonded onto <a href="/wiki/Alum" title="Alum">alum</a> which is then used as a pigment and mixed with <a href="/wiki/Ochre" title="Ochre">ochre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sienna" title="Sienna">sienna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Umber" title="Umber">umber</a>. It is not totally colorfast.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>crimson</i> has been recorded in English since 1400,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and its earlier forms include <i>cremesin</i>, <i>crymysyn</i> and <i>cramoysin</i> (cf. cramoisy, a crimson cloth). These were adapted via <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Spanish">Old Spanish</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>cremesinus</i> (also <i>kermesinus</i> or <i>carmesinus</i>), the dye produced from <i><a href="/wiki/Kermes_(genus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kermes (genus)">Kermes</a></i> scale insects, and can be traced back to Arabic <i>qermez</i> ("red"), also borrowed in <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> <i>kırmızı</i> and many other languages, e.g. German <i>Karmesin</i>, Italian <i>cremisi</i>, French <i>cramoisi</i>, Portuguese <i>carmesim</i>, etc. (via Latin). The ultimate source may be Sanskrit कृमिज <i>kṛmi-jā</i> meaning "worm-made".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A shortened form of <i>carmesinus</i> also gave the Latin <i>carminus</i>, from which comes <a href="/wiki/Carmine" title="Carmine">carmine</a>.</p>
<p>Other cognates include the Persian <i>qermez</i> (or <i>ghermez</i>) "red", <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">Old Church Slavonic</a> <i>čruminu</i>, archaic <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> чермный (<i>čermnyj</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian</a> <i>crven</i> "red". Cf. also <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermilion</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dyes">Dyes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dyes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Carmine" title="Carmine">Carmine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kermes_(dye)" title="Kermes (dye)">Kermes (dye)</a></div>

Carminic acid</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Carmine <a href="/wiki/Natural_dye" title="Natural dye">dyes</a>, which give crimson and related red and purple colors, are based on an <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carminic_acid" title="Carminic acid">carminic acid</a>. <b>Carmine lake</b> is an aluminium or aluminium-tin <a href="/wiki/Lake_pigment" title="Lake pigment">lake</a> of cochineal extract, and <b>crimson lake</b> is prepared by striking down an infusion of cochineal with a 5 <a href="/wiki/Percent" class="mw-redirect" title="Percent">percent</a> <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alum" title="Alum">alum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_tartrate" title="Potassium tartrate">cream of tartar</a>. <b>Purple lake</b> is prepared like carmine lake with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">lime</a> to produce the deep purple tone. Carmine dyes tend to fade quickly.</p>
<p>Carmine dyes were once widely prized in both the Americas and in Europe. They were used in paints by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> and for the crimson fabrics of the <a href="/wiki/Hussars" class="mw-redirect" title="Hussars">Hussars</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turks</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Red_coat_(British_army)" class="mw-redirect" title="Red coat (British army)">British Redcoats</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">Royal Canadian Mounted Police</a>.</p>
<p>Nowadays carmine dyes are used for coloring foodstuffs, medicines and cosmetics. As a <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additive</a> in the European Union, carmine dyes are designated <a href="/wiki/Carmine" title="Carmine">E120</a>, and are also called <b>cochineal</b> and <b>Natural Red 4</b>. Carmine dyes are also used in some <a href="/wiki/Oil_paint" title="Oil paint">oil paints</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watercolor" class="mw-redirect" title="Watercolor">watercolors</a> used by artists.</p>

<p><b>IU Crimson</b>, along with <a href="/wiki/Cream_(colour)" title="Cream (colour)">cream</a>, is an official color for <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_Bloomington" title="Indiana University Bloomington">Indiana University</a> and its athletic teams, the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Hoosiers" title="Indiana Hoosiers">Indiana Hoosiers</a>. The official IU Crimson is Pantone® 201.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> However, in the 1970s former basketball coach <a href="/wiki/Bob_Knight" title="Bob Knight">Bob Knight</a> and football coach <a href="/wiki/Lee_Corso" title="Lee Corso">Lee Corso</a> started using uniforms that were more scarlet or bright red.<sup id="cite_ref-colors_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colors-18">[18]</a></sup> During the same time, cream gave way almost universally to white. But those colors reverted mostly to cream and crimson in the early 2000s, after then-athletics director Michael McNeely decided that the team uniforms needed to reflect the school's official colors of cream and crimson. Indiana cheerleaders still chant "Go Big Red".<sup id="cite_ref-colors_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colors-18">[18]</a></sup> The changes over the years has led to some clashing of colors in some varsity sport uniforms, as is the case with the baseball team's jackets being a different color than their caps and uniforms.<sup id="cite_ref-colors_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colors-18">[18]</a></sup> Athletic Director Fred Glass said, "My view is that we're an awfully big and diverse place. I think cream and crimson and 'Go Big Red' can survive in one place."<sup id="cite_ref-colors_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colors-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Oklahoma Crimson</b> and Oklahoma Cream are the official colors for the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oklahoma" title="University of Oklahoma">University of Oklahoma</a>, and its athletic teams, known as the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Sooners" title="Oklahoma Sooners">Sooners</a>. In the fall of 1895, Miss May Overstreet was asked to chair a committee to select the colors of the university. The committee decided the colors should be crimson and cream and an elaborate display of the colors was draped above a platform before the student body.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Alabama Crimson</b>, along with <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a>, is an official color for the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama" title="University of Alabama">University of Alabama</a> and its athletic teams, the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Crimson_Tide" title="Alabama Crimson Tide">Alabama Crimson Tide</a>. The specification provided by the office of university relations refers to the <a href="/wiki/Pantone" title="Pantone">Pantone Matching System</a>, color PMS 201.<sup id="cite_ref-HD_Crimson_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HD_Crimson-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FTD_Crimson_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FTD_Crimson-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-About_Crimson_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About_Crimson-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are different versions of the origin of the nickname "Crimson Tide." The football team was originally called the Crimson White. The first nickname newspapers used was The Thin Red Line. Hugh Roberts of the Birmingham Age-Herald is credited with being the first to use the term Crimson Tide in 1907 when Alabama tied a heavily favored Alabama Polytechnic Institute (Auburn) 6-6. The game was played in heavy rain and the field was red mud. The Thin Red Line was equal to the task and became the Crimson Tide.<sup id="cite_ref-History_of_color_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_color-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Worcester Polytechnic Institute Crimson</b>, a shade of red, is one of the official colors of <a href="/wiki/Worcester_Polytechnic_Institute" title="Worcester Polytechnic Institute">Worcester Polytechnic Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WPI_Logo_Usage_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WPI_Logo_Usage-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_nature">In nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: In nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Algae">Algae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Algae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Red_tide" title="Red tide">crimson tide</a> which sometimes occurs on beaches is caused by a type of algae known as <i><a href="/wiki/Karenia_brevis" title="Karenia brevis">Karenia brevis</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birds">Birds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Birds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Comb_(anatomy)" title="Comb (anatomy)">coxscomb</a> of a rooster is a deep crimson color</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Crimson_sunbird" title="Crimson sunbird">crimson sunbird</a> is the national bird of Singapore</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt>Literature</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/George_R.R._Martin" class="mw-redirect" title="George R.R. Martin">George R.R. Martin</a>'s series A Song Of Ice and Fire, crimson is the family color of House Lannister</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Nobility</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_Royal_Hussars" class="mw-redirect" title="The King's Royal Hussars">The King's Royal Hussars</a> still wear crimson trousers as successors to the 11th Hussars (the "Cherrypickers")</li>
<li>In Polish, <i>karmazyn</i> (crimson) is a synonym for a <a href="/wiki/Magnate" title="Magnate">magnate</a>, i.e., a member of the rich, high <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Religion</dt>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>In texts of the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a>, crimson stands for tests and sacrifice, among other things<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rhubarb" title="Rhubarb">Rhubarb</a> is sometimes poetically referred to as <i>crimson stalks</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>, crimson is the color of the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Ordnance Corps (United States Army)">Ordnance Corps</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="School_colors">School colors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: School colors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Multiple <a href="/wiki/Fraternities_and_Sororities" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraternities and Sororities">Greek letter organizations</a> use crimson as one of their official colors:<a href="/wiki/Delta_Sigma_Theta" title="Delta Sigma Theta">Delta Sigma Theta</a> (ΔΣΘ), <a href="/wiki/Kappa_Alpha_Psi" title="Kappa Alpha Psi">Kappa Alpha Psi</a> (ΚΑΨ), and <a href="/wiki/Kappa_Alpha_Order" title="Kappa Alpha Order">Kappa Alpha Order</a> (ΚΑ).</li>
<li>Crimson is the school color of several universities, including: <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas" title="University of Kansas">University of Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University_(Bloomington)" class="mw-redirect" title="Indiana University (Bloomington)">Indiana University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea_University" title="Korea University">Korea University</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico_State_University" title="New Mexico State University">New Mexico State University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph%27s_University" title="Saint Joseph's University">Saint Joseph's University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_University" title="Tuskegee University">Tuskegee University</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama" title="University of Alabama">University of Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Belgrano" title="University of Belgrano">University of Belgrano</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Denver" title="University of Denver">University of Denver</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mississippi" title="University of Mississippi">University of Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska_at_Omaha" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Nebraska at Omaha">University of Nebraska at Omaha</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oklahoma" title="University of Oklahoma">University of Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Utah" title="University of Utah">University of Utah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_University" title="Washington State University">Washington State University</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Worcester_Polytechnic_Institute" title="Worcester Polytechnic Institute">Worcester Polytechnic Institute</a></li>
<li>The daily newspaper at Harvard is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Harvard_Crimson" title="The Harvard Crimson">The Harvard Crimson</a></i></li>
<li>The daily newspaper at Alabama is called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crimson_White" title="The Crimson White">The Crimson White</a></i></li>
<li>Harvard's athletic teams are the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Crimson" title="Harvard Crimson">Crimson</a>, and those of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama" title="University of Alabama">University of Alabama</a> are the Crimson Tide</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vexillology">Vexillology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crimson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Vexillology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Crimson is the national color of <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> and forms the background of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Nepal" title="Flag of Nepal">country's flag</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and it also appears on the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Poland" title="Flag of Poland">flag of Poland</a> as well as being the main color on the flag of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a></p>

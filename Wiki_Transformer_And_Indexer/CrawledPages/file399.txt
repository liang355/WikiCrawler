<p><b>Karlsruhe</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈkaɐ̯lsˌʁuːə]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Karlsruhe.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/32/De-Karlsruhe.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Karlsruhe.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; formerly <b>Carlsruhe</b><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) is the second-largest <a href="/wiki/Cities_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Cities of Germany">city</a> in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>, in southwest <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, near the French-German border. It has a population of 307,755. The city is the seat of the two highest courts in Germany: the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Constitutional_Court_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Constitutional Court of Germany">Federal Constitutional Court</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Court_of_Justice_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Court of Justice of Germany">Federal Court of Justice</a>. Its most remarkable building is <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Palace" title="Karlsruhe Palace">Karlsruhe Palace</a>, which was built in 1715.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:49th_Parallel_Karlsruhe.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/49th_Parallel_Karlsruhe.JPG/220px-49th_Parallel_Karlsruhe.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/49th_Parallel_Karlsruhe.JPG/330px-49th_Parallel_Karlsruhe.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/49th_Parallel_Karlsruhe.JPG/440px-49th_Parallel_Karlsruhe.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:49th_Parallel_Karlsruhe.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/49th_parallel_north" title="49th parallel north">49th parallel north</a> in the Karlsruhe Stadtgarten</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Karlsruhe lies completely to the right of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, and almost completely on the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Rhine_Plain" title="Upper Rhine Plain">Upper Rhine Plain</a>. It contains the <a href="/wiki/Turmberg" title="Turmberg">Turmberg</a> in the east, and also lies on the borders of the <a href="/wiki/Kraichgau" title="Kraichgau">Kraichgau</a> leading to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Black_Forest" title="Northern Black Forest">Northern Black Forest</a>.</p>
<p>The Rhine, one of the world's most important shipping routes forms the western limits of the city, beyond which lie the towns of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximiliansau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Maximiliansau (page does not exist)">Maximiliansau</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6rth_am_Rhein" title="Wörth am Rhein">Wörth am Rhein</a> in the German state of <a href="/wiki/Rhineland-Palatinate" title="Rhineland-Palatinate">Rhineland-Palatinate</a>. The city centre is approximately 7.5&#160;km (4.7&#160;mi) from the river, as measured from the Marktplatz (Market Square). The two tributaries of the Rhine, the <a href="/wiki/Alb" title="Alb">Alb</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pfinz" title="Pfinz">Pfinz</a> flow through the city from the Kraichgau to eventually join the Rhine.</p>
<p>The city lies at an altitude between 100&#160;m and 322&#160;m (near the communications tower In the suburb of Grünwettersbach). Its geographical coordinates are <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Karlsruhe&amp;params=49_00_N_8_24_E_type:city(282595)_region:DE-BW"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">49°00′N</span> <span class="longitude">8°24′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">49.000°N 8.400°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">49.000; 8.400</span></span></span></a></span>; the <a href="/wiki/49th_parallel_north" title="49th parallel north">49th parallel</a> runs through the city centre, which puts it at the same latitude as much of the <a href="/wiki/Canada-United_States_border" class="mw-redirect" title="Canada-United States border">Canada-United States border</a>, the cities <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> (Canada), <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> (France), <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> (Germany) and <a href="/wiki/Hulunbuir" title="Hulunbuir">Hulunbuir</a> (China). Its course is marked by a stone and painted line in the <i>Stadtgarten</i> (municipal park). The total area of the city is 173.46&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (66.97&#160;sq&#160;mi), and hence is the 30th largest city in Germany measured by land area. The longest North-South distance is 16.8&#160;km (10.4&#160;mi) and 19.3&#160;km (12.0&#160;mi) in the East-West direction.</p>
<p>Karlsruhe is part of the urban area of Karlsruhe/Pforzheim, to which certain other towns in the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_(district)" title="Karlsruhe (district)">district of Karlsruhe</a> like <a href="/wiki/Bruchsal" title="Bruchsal">Bruchsal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ettlingen" title="Ettlingen">Ettlingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stutensee" title="Stutensee">Stutensee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rheinstetten" title="Rheinstetten">Rheinstetten</a>, as well as the city of <a href="/wiki/Pforzheim" title="Pforzheim">Pforzheim</a> belong.</p>

MiRO oil refinery</div>

<p>The city was planned with the palace tower (<i>Schloss</i>) at the center and 32&#160;streets radiating out from it like the <a href="/wiki/Spokes" class="mw-redirect" title="Spokes">spokes</a> of a wheel, or the ribs of a <a href="/wiki/Fan_(implement)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fan (implement)">folding fan</a>, so that one <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> for Karlsruhe in German is the "fan city" (<i>Fächerstadt</i>). Almost all of these streets survived until today. Because of this city layout, in <a href="/wiki/Metric_geometry" class="mw-redirect" title="Metric geometry">metric geometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_metric" title="Karlsruhe metric">Karlsruhe metric</a> refers to a measure of distance that assumes travel is only possible along radial streets and along circular avenues around the centre.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city centre is the oldest part of town and lies south of the palace in the quadrant defined by nine of the radial streets. The central part of the palace runs east-west, with two wings, each at a 45°&#160;angle, directed southeast and southwest (i.e.,&#160;parallel with the streets marking the boundaries of the quadrant defining the city center).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Market_square" title="Market square">market square</a> lies on the street running south from the palace to <a href="/wiki/Ettlingen" title="Ettlingen">Ettlingen</a>. The market square has the <a href="/wiki/Town_hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Town hall">town hall</a> (<i>Rathaus</i>) to the west, the main <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> church (<i>Evangelische Stadtkirche</i>) to the east, and the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_William,_Margrave_of_Baden-Durlach" title="Charles III William, Margrave of Baden-Durlach">Margrave Charles III William</a> in <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Pyramid" title="Karlsruhe Pyramid">a pyramid</a> in the buildings, resulting in Karlsruhe being one of only three large cities in Germany where buildings are laid out in the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">neoclassical</a> style.</p>
<p>The area north of the palace is a park and forest. Originally the area to the east of the palace consisted of gardens and forests, some of which remain, but the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Institute_of_Technology" title="Karlsruhe Institute of Technology">Karlsruhe Institute of Technology</a> (founded in 1825), <a href="/wiki/Wildparkstadion" title="Wildparkstadion">Wildparkstadion</a> football stadium, and residential areas have been built there. The area west of the palace is now mostly residential.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Karlsruhe experiences an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Cfb</i>) and its winter climate is milder, compared to most other German cities, except for the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Rhur#Climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine-Rhur">Rhine-Ruhr</a> area. Summers are also hotter than elsewhere in the country and it is one of the sunniest cities in Germany, like the <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Palatinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine Palatinate">Rhine-Palatinate</a> area. Precipitation is almost evenly spread throughout the year. In 2008, the weather station in Karlsruhe, which had been operating since 1876, was closed; it was replaced by a weather station in <a href="/wiki/Rheinstetten" title="Rheinstetten">Rheinstetten</a>, south of Karlsruhe.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to legend, the name <i>Karlsruhe</i>, which translates as "Charles’ repose", was given to the new city after a hunting trip when Margrave <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_William,_Margrave_of_Baden-Durlach" title="Charles III William, Margrave of Baden-Durlach">Charles III William</a> of <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Baden-Durlach" class="mw-redirect" title="Margraviate of Baden-Durlach">Baden-Durlach</a>, woke from a dream in which he dreamt of founding his new city. A variation of this story claims that he built the new palace in order to find peace from his wife.</p>
<p>Charles William founded the city on June 17, 1715, after a dispute with the citizens of his previous capital, <a href="/wiki/Durlach" title="Durlach">Durlach</a>. The founding of the city is closely linked to the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Palace" title="Karlsruhe Palace">palace</a>. Karlsruhe became the capital of <a href="/wiki/Baden-Durlach" class="mw-redirect" title="Baden-Durlach">Baden-Durlach</a> and in 1771 of the united <a href="/wiki/Baden" title="Baden">Baden</a> until 1945. Built in 1822, the <i>Ständehaus</i> was the first parliament building in a German State. In the aftermath of the democratic revolution of 1848, a republican government was elected here.</p>
<p>Karlsruhe was visited by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> during his time as the American envoy to France; when <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Charles_L%27Enfant" title="Pierre Charles L'Enfant">Pierre Charles L'Enfant</a> was planning the layout of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, Jefferson passed to him maps of 12 European towns to consult, one of which was a sketch he had made of Karlsruhe during his visit.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1860, the first-ever international professional <a href="/wiki/Convention_(meeting)" title="Convention (meeting)">convention</a> of chemists, the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Congress" title="Karlsruhe Congress">Karlsruhe Congress</a>, was held in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of the central area, including the palace, was reduced to rubble by <a href="/wiki/Allied_bombing_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Allied bombing of Germany">Allied bombing</a> during World War II but was rebuilt after the war. Located in the American zone of the <a href="/wiki/Allied-occupied_Germany" title="Allied-occupied Germany">post-war Allied occupation</a>, Karlsruhe was home to an <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">American military</a> base, established in 1945. In 1995, the bases <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Army_installations_in_Germany#Former_installations" title="List of United States Army installations in Germany">closed</a>, and their facilities were turned over to the city of Karlsruhe.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Main sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Stadtgarten</i> is a recreational area near the main railway station (<i>Hauptbahnhof</i>) and was rebuilt for the 1967 <a href="/wiki/Federal_Garden_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Garden Show">Federal Garden Show</a> (<i>Bundesgartenschau</i>). It is also the site of the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Zoo" title="Karlsruhe Zoo">Karlsruhe Zoo</a>.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Durlach" title="Durlach">Durlacher</a> <a href="/wiki/Turmberg" title="Turmberg">Turmberg</a></i> has a look-out tower (hence its name). It is a former <a href="/wiki/Keep" title="Keep">keep</a> dating back to the 13th century.</p>
<p>The city has two botanical gardens: the municipal <i><a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_Karlsruhe" title="Botanischer Garten Karlsruhe">Botanischer Garten Karlsruhe</a></i>, which forms part of the Palace complex, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_der_Universit%C3%A4t_Karlsruhe" title="Botanischer Garten der Universität Karlsruhe">Botanischer Garten der Universität Karlsruhe</a></i>, which is maintained by the university.</p>

<p>The <i>Marktplatz</i> has a <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Pyramid" title="Karlsruhe Pyramid">stone pyramid</a> marking the grave of the city's founder. Built in 1825, it is the emblem of Karlsruhe. The city is nicknamed the "fan city" (<i>die Fächerstadt</i>) because of its design layout, with straight streets radiating fan-like from the Palace.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Palace" title="Karlsruhe Palace">Karlsruhe Palace</a> (<i>Schloss</i>) is an interesting piece of architecture; the adjacent <i>Schlossgarten</i> includes the Botanical Garden with a palm, cactus and orchid house, and walking paths through the woods to the north.</p>
<p>The so-called <i>Kleine Kirche</i> (Little Church), built between 1773 and 1776, is the oldest church of Karlsruhe's city centre.</p>
<p>The architect <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Weinbrenner" title="Friedrich Weinbrenner">Friedrich Weinbrenner</a> designed many of the city's most important sights. Another sight is the <i>Rondellplatz</i> with its 'Constitution Building Columns' (1826). It is dedicated to Baden's first constitution in 1818, which was one of the most liberal of its time. The <i>Münze</i> (mint), erected in 1826/27, was also built by Weinbrenner.</p>

<p>The St. Stephan parish church is one of the masterpieces of neoclassical church architecture in.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Weinbrenner, who built this church between 1808 and 1814, orientated it to the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon, Rome</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Karlsruhe_Grabkapelle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Karlsruhe_Grabkapelle.jpg/220px-Karlsruhe_Grabkapelle.jpg" width="220" height="369" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Karlsruhe_Grabkapelle.jpg/330px-Karlsruhe_Grabkapelle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Karlsruhe_Grabkapelle.jpg/440px-Karlsruhe_Grabkapelle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="1676" /></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Neo-Gothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Gothic">neo-Gothic</a> Grand Ducal Burial Chapel, built between 1889 and 1896, is a <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum" title="Mausoleum">mausoleum</a> rather than a church, and is located in the middle of the forest.</p>
<p>The main cemetery of Karlsruhe is the oldest park-like cemetery in Germany. The <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">crematorium</a> was the first to be built in the style of a church.</p>
<p>Karlsruhe is also home to a Museum of Natural History, an opera house (the <a href="/wiki/Badisches_Staatstheater_Karlsruhe" title="Badisches Staatstheater Karlsruhe">Baden State Theatre</a>), as well as a number of independent theatres and art galleries. The <a href="/wiki/Staatliche_Kunsthalle_Karlsruhe" title="Staatliche Kunsthalle Karlsruhe">State Art Gallery</a>, built in 1846 by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_H%C3%BCbsch" title="Heinrich Hübsch">Heinrich Hübsch</a>, displays paintings and sculptures from six centuries, particularly from France, Germany and Holland. Karlsruhe's newly renovated art museum is one of the most important art museums in <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>. Further cultural attractions are scattered throughout Karlsruhe's various incorporated suburbs. Established in 1924, the Scheffel Association is the largest <a href="/wiki/Literary_society" title="Literary society">literary society</a> in Germany.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Today the <i>Prinz-Max-Palais</i>, built between 1881 and 1884 in neoclassical style, houses the organisation and includes its museum.</p>

<p>Due to population growth in the late 19th century, Karlsruhe developed several suburban areas (<i><a href="/wiki/Vorstadt" title="Vorstadt">Vorstadt</a></i>) in the <i><a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%BCnderzeit" title="Gründerzeit">Gründerzeit</a></i> and especially <a href="/wiki/Art_nouveau" class="mw-redirect" title="Art nouveau">art nouveau</a> styles of architecture, with many preserved examples.</p>
<p>Karlsruhe is also home to the <i>Majolika-Manufaktur</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> the only art-ceramics pottery studio in Germany.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Founded in 1901, it is located in the <i>Schlossgarten</i>. A 'blue streak' (<i>Blauer Strahl</i>) consisting of 1,645 ceramic tiles, connects the studio with the Palace. It is the world's largest ceramic artwork.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Another tourist attraction is the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Art_and_Media_Karlsruhe" title="Center for Art and Media Karlsruhe">Centre for Art and Media</a> (<i>Zentrum für Kunst und Medientechnologie</i>, or ZKM), which is located in a converted ammunition factory.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Justice">Justice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Justice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Karlsruhe is the seat of the German <a href="/wiki/Federal_Constitutional_Court_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Constitutional Court of Germany">Federal Constitutional Court</a> <i>(Bundesverfassungsgericht)</i> and the highest <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeals" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeals">Court of Appeals</a> in civil and criminal cases, the <i><a href="/wiki/Bundesgerichtshof" class="mw-redirect" title="Bundesgerichtshof">Bundesgerichtshof</a></i>. The courts came to Karlsruhe after World War II, when the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Baden_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Baden Germany">Baden</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Württemberg">Württemberg</a> were merged. <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, capital of Württemberg, became the capital of the new province (<a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg-Baden" title="Württemberg-Baden">Württemberg-Baden</a> in 1945 and <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> in 1952). In compensation for the state authorities relocated to Stuttgart, Karlsruhe applied to become the seat of the high court.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health">Public health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Public health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are four hospitals: The municipal <i>Klinikum Karlsruhe</i> provides the maximum level of medical services, the <i>St. Vincentius-Kliniken</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Deaconess" title="Deaconess">Diakonissen</a>krankenhaus</i>, connected to the Catholic and Protestant churches, respectively, offer central services, and the private <i>Paracelsus-Klinik</i> basic medical care, according to state hospital demand planning.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Germany's largest <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a> is located in Karlsruhe, at the western edge of the city, directly on the river <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>. The <i>Technologieregion Karlsruhe</i> is a loose confederation of the region's cities in order to promote high tech industries; today, about 20% of the region's jobs are in research and development. <a href="/wiki/EnBW" title="EnBW">EnBW</a>, one of Germany's biggest <a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">electric utility</a> companies and a revenue of 19.2&#160;billion € in 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> is headquartered in the city.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_activities">Internet activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Internet activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Karlsruhe" title="University of Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe Institute of Technology</a> providing services until the late 1990, Karlsruhe became known as the <i>internet capital</i> of Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/DENIC" title="DENIC">DENIC</a>, Germany's <a href="/wiki/Network_Information_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Network Information Centre">Network Information Centre</a>, has since moved to Frankfurt, though, where <a href="/wiki/DE-CIX" class="mw-redirect" title="DE-CIX">DE-CIX</a> is located.</p>
<p>Two major <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">internet service providers</a>, <a href="/wiki/WEB.DE" class="mw-redirect" title="WEB.DE">WEB.DE</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Schlund%2Bpartner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schlund+partner (page does not exist)">schlund+partner</a>/<a href="/wiki/1%261_Internet" title="1&amp;1 Internet">1&amp;1</a>, now both owned by <a href="/wiki/United_Internet" title="United Internet">United Internet</a>&#160;AG, are located at Karlsruhe.</p>
<p>The library of the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Institute_of_Technology" title="Karlsruhe Institute of Technology">Karlsruhe Institute of Technology</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruher_Virtueller_Katalog" title="Karlsruher Virtueller Katalog">Karlsruher Virtueller Katalog</a>, the first internet site that allowed researchers worldwide (for free) to search multiple library catalogues worldwide.</p>
<p>In the year 2000 the regional online "newspaper" <i>ka‑news.de</i> was created. As a daily newspaper, it not only provides the news, but also informs readers about upcoming events in Karlsruhe and surrounding areas.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Verkehrsbetriebe_Karlsruhe" title="Verkehrsbetriebe Karlsruhe">Verkehrsbetriebe Karlsruhe</a> (VBK) operates the city's urban public transport network, comprising seven tram routes and a network of bus routes. This network is well developed and all city areas can be reached round the clock by tram and a night bus system. The <a href="/wiki/Turmbergbahn" title="Turmbergbahn">Turmbergbahn</a> <a href="/wiki/Funicular" title="Funicular">funicular railway</a>, to the east of the city centre, is also operated by the VBK.</p>
<p>The VBK is also a partner, with the <a href="/wiki/Albtal-Verkehrs-Gesellschaft" title="Albtal-Verkehrs-Gesellschaft">Albtal-Verkehrs-Gesellschaft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a>, in the operation of the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Stadtbahn" title="Karlsruhe Stadtbahn">Karlsruhe Stadtbahn</a>, the rail system that serves a larger area around the city. This system makes it possible to reach other towns in the region, like <a href="/wiki/Ettlingen" title="Ettlingen">Ettlingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6rth_am_Rhein" title="Wörth am Rhein">Wörth am Rhein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pforzheim" title="Pforzheim">Pforzheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bad_Wildbad" title="Bad Wildbad">Bad Wildbad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bretten" title="Bretten">Bretten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruchsal" title="Bruchsal">Bruchsal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heilbronn" title="Heilbronn">Heilbronn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baden-Baden" title="Baden-Baden">Baden-Baden</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Freudenstadt" title="Freudenstadt">Freudenstadt</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a> right from the city centre. The Stadtbahn is well known in transport circles around the world for pioneering the concept of operating trams on train tracks, to achieve a more effective and attractive <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a> system, to the extent that this is often known as the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_model" title="Karlsruhe model">Karlsruhe model</a> <a href="/wiki/Tram-train" title="Tram-train">tram-train</a> system.</p>
<p>Karlsruhe is well-connected via road and rail, with <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a> connections going to <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>/<a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freiburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Freiburg">Freiburg</a>/<a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a> from <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Hauptbahnhof" title="Karlsruhe Hauptbahnhof">Karlsruhe Hauptbahnhof</a>. Since June 2007 it has been connected to the <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a> network, reducing travel time to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> to only three hours (previously it had taken five hours).</p>

<p>Two ports on the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> provide transport capacity on <a href="/wiki/Cargo_ship" title="Cargo ship">cargo ships</a>, especially for <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_products" class="mw-redirect" title="Petroleum products">petroleum products</a>.</p>
<p>The nearest airport is part of the <a href="/wiki/Baden_Airpark" class="mw-redirect" title="Baden Airpark">Baden Airpark</a> (officially <i>Flughafen Karlsruhe/<a href="/wiki/Baden-Baden" title="Baden-Baden">Baden-Baden</a></i>) about 45&#160;km (28&#160;mi) southwest of Karlsruhe, with regular connections to airports in Germany and Europe in general. <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt International Airport</a> can be reached in about an hour and a half by car (one hour by <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a>); <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart_Airport" title="Stuttgart Airport">Stuttgart Airport</a> can be reached in about one hour (about an hour and a half by train and <a href="/wiki/S-Bahn" class="mw-redirect" title="S-Bahn">S‑Bahn</a>).</p>
<p>Two interesting facts in transportation history are that both <a href="/wiki/Karl_Drais" title="Karl Drais">Karl Drais</a>, the inventor of the bicycle, as well as <a href="/wiki/Karl_Benz" title="Karl Benz">Karl Benz</a>, the inventor of the automobile were born in Karlsruhe. Benz was born in Mühlburg, which later became a borough of Karlsruhe (in 1886). Benz also studied at the Karlsruhe University. It also is interesting that Benz’s wife <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Benz" title="Bertha Benz">Bertha</a> took the world's first long distance-drive with an automobile from <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a> to Karlsruhe-Grötzingen and <a href="/wiki/Pforzheim" title="Pforzheim">Pforzheim</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Benz_Memorial_Route" title="Bertha Benz Memorial Route">Bertha Benz Memorial Route</a>). Their professional lives led both men to the neighboring city of Mannheim, where they first applied their most famous inventions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_community">Jewish community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Jewish community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jews settled in Karlsruhe soon after its founding.<sup id="cite_ref-Singer1906_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singer1906-16">[16]</a></sup> They were attracted by the numerous privileges granted by its founder to settlers, without discrimination as to creed. Official documents attest the presence of several Jewish families at Karlsruhe in 1717.<sup id="cite_ref-Singer1906_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singer1906-16">[16]</a></sup> A year later the city council addressed to the margrave a report in which a question was raised as to the proportion of municipal charges to be borne by the newly arrived Jews, who in that year formed an organized congregation, with Rabbi Nathan Uri Kohen of <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> at its head. A document dated 1726 gives the names of twenty-four Jews who had taken part in an election of municipal officers.</p>
<p>As the city grew, permission to settle there became less easily obtained by Jews, and the community developed more slowly. A 1752 Jewry ordinance stated Jews were forbidden to leave the city on Sundays and Christian holidays, or to go out of their houses during church services, but they were exempted from service by court summonses on Sabbaths. They could sell wine only in inns owned by Jews and graze their cattle, not on the <a href="/wiki/Commons" title="Commons">commons</a>, but on the wayside only. <a href="/wiki/Nethaneel_Weil" class="mw-redirect" title="Nethaneel Weil">Nethanael Weill</a> was a rabbi in Karlsruhe from 1750 until his death.</p>
<p>In 1783, by a decree issued by Margrave <a href="/wiki/Charles_Frederick,_Grand_Duke_of_Baden" title="Charles Frederick, Grand Duke of Baden">Charles Frederick of Baden</a>, the Jews ceased to be <a href="/wiki/Serf" class="mw-redirect" title="Serf">serfs</a>, and consequently could settle wherever they pleased. The same decree freed them from the <i>Todfall</i> tax, paid to the clergy for each Jewish burial. In commemoration of these changes special prayers were prepared by the acting rabbi Jedidiah Tiah Weill, who, succeeding his father in 1770, held the office until 1805.</p>
<p>In 1808 the new constitution of what at that time, during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_era" title="Napoleonic era">Napoleonic era</a>, had become the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Baden" title="Grand Duchy of Baden">Grand Duchy of Baden</a> granted Jews citizenship status; a subsequent edict, in 1809, constitutionally acknowledged Jews as a religious group.<sup id="cite_ref-Dubnow1920_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dubnow1920-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The latter edict provided for a hierarchical organization of the Jewish communities of Baden, under the umbrella of a central council of Baden Jewry (Oberrat der Israeliten Badens), with its seat in Karlsruhe,<sup id="cite_ref-Dubnow1920_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dubnow1920-17">[17]</a></sup> and the appointment of a chief rabbi of Karlsruhe, as the spiritual head of the Jews in all of Baden.<sup id="cite_ref-Singer1906_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singer1906-16">[16]</a></sup> The first chief rabbi of Karlsruhe and Baden was Rabbi Asher Loew, who served from 1809 until his death in 1837.<sup id="cite_ref-Oelsner_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oelsner-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Complete <a href="/wiki/Emancipation" title="Emancipation">emancipation</a> was given in 1862, Jews were elected to city council and Baden parliament, and from 1890 were appointed judges. Jews were persecuted in the <a href="/wiki/Hep-Hep_riots" title="Hep-Hep riots">'Hep-Hep' riots</a> that occurred in 1819; and anti-Jewish demonstrations were held in 1843, 1848, and the 1880s. The well-known German-Israeli artist <a href="/wiki/Leo_Kahn" title="Leo Kahn">Leo Kahn</a> studied in Karlsruhe before leaving for France and Israel in the 1920s and 1930s.</p>
<p>Today, there are about 900 members in the Jewish community, many of whom are recent immigrants from Russia, and an orthodox rabbi.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Karlsruhe has memorialized its Jewish community and notable pre-war synagogues with a memorial park.<sup id="cite_ref-alemannia-judaica_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alemannia-judaica-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Karlsruhe_and_the_Holocaust">Karlsruhe and the Holocaust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Karlsruhe and the Holocaust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1933, 3,358 Jews lived in Karlsruhe. The Jewish community owned buildings and property, such as two synagogues, one on Karl-Friedrich-Straße and one on Kronenstraße, two elderly citizens' homes, a Jewish school, a hospital, welfare institutions, and several Jewish cemeteries. During the first years of the Nazi regime, the community continued to function, particularly to prepare Jews for emigration. On October 28, 1938, all male Polish Jews living in Karlsruhe were deported to Poland. Synagogues were destroyed on <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>, 9–10 November 1938. Most of the men were arrested and sent to <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">Dachau concentration camp</a>, but were released after they had furnished proof that they intended to emigrate. In October 1940, 895 Jews were expelled during <a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Wagner-B%C3%BCrckel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Operation Wagner-Bürckel (page does not exist)">Operation Wagner-Bürckel</a> and interned by the French <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy</a> authorities in <a href="/wiki/Camp_Gurs" class="mw-redirect" title="Camp Gurs">Gurs</a> in southern France. Most of these were then deported from there to <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz" class="mw-redirect" title="Auschwitz">Auschwitz</a> (via the <a href="/wiki/Drancy_deportation_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Drancy deportation camp">Drancy deportation camp</a>, on the outskirts of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>) between August and November 1942. Most of the 429 remaining Jews and other so‑called "non-<a href="/wiki/Aryan_race" title="Aryan race">Aryans</a>" were deported to the east between 1941 and 1944. In 1945 there were only 18 Jews in Karlsruhe. More than 1,000 of them had been killed between 1933 and 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Baden Central Jewish Council was reorganized in 1948. A new synagogue was built in 1969.</p>
<p>Some 113 holocaust victims are commemorated by brass plaques on Karlsruhe's sidewalks, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Stolperstein" title="Stolperstein">Stolpersteine</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Karlsruhe is a renowned research and study centre, with one of Germany's finest institutions of higher education.</p>
<h3><span id="Technology,_engineering,_and_business"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology.2C_engineering.2C_and_business">Technology, engineering, and business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Technology, engineering, and business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Karlsruhe University">Karlsruhe University</a> (<i>Universität Karlsruhe-TH</i>), the oldest technical university in Germany, is home to the <i><a href="/wiki/Forschungszentrum_Karlsruhe" class="mw-redirect" title="Forschungszentrum Karlsruhe">Forschungszentrum Karlsruhe</a></i> (Karlsruhe Research Center), where engineering and scientific research is performed in the areas of health, earth, and environmental sciences. The <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Karlsruhe University of Applied Sciences">Karlsruhe University of Applied Sciences</a> (<i>Hochschule Karlsruhe-HS</i>) is the largest university of technology in the state of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>, offering both professional and academic education in <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> sciences and business. In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Karlsruhe" title="University of Karlsruhe">University of Karlsruhe</a> joined the <i><a href="/wiki/Forschungszentrum_Karlsruhe" class="mw-redirect" title="Forschungszentrum Karlsruhe">Forschungszentrum Karlsruhe</a></i> to form the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Institute_of_Technology" title="Karlsruhe Institute of Technology">Karlsruhe Institute of Technology</a> (KIT).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_arts">The arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Fine_Arts,_Karlsruhe" title="Academy of Fine Arts, Karlsruhe">Academy of Fine Arts, Karlsruhe</a> is one of the smallest universities in Germany, with average 300 students, but it is known as one of the most significant academies of fine arts. The <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_University_of_Arts_and_Design" title="Karlsruhe University of Arts and Design">Karlsruhe University of Arts and Design</a> (HfG) was founded to the same time as its sister institution, the Center for Art and Media Karlsruhe (<i><a href="/wiki/Zentrum_f%C3%BCr_Kunst_und_Medientechnologie" class="mw-redirect" title="Zentrum für Kunst und Medientechnologie">Zentrum für Kunst und Medientechnologie</a></i>). The HfG teaching and research focuses on <a href="/wiki/New_media" title="New media">new media</a> and <a href="/wiki/Media_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Media art">media art</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hochschule_f%C3%BCr_Musik_Karlsruhe" title="Hochschule für Musik Karlsruhe">Hochschule für Musik Karlsruhe</a> is a music <a href="/wiki/Music_school" title="Music school">conservatory</a> that offers degrees in <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">composition</a>, music performance, education, and radio <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a>. Since 1989 it has been located in the Gottesaue Palace.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_education">International education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: International education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Karlshochschule_International_University" title="Karlshochschule International University">Karlshochschule International University</a> (formerly known as <i>Merkur Internationale Fachhochschule</i>) was founded in 2004. As a foundation-owned, state-approved <a href="/wiki/Management_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Management school">management school</a>, Karlshochschule offers <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate_education" title="Undergraduate education">undergraduate education</a> in both German and English, focusing on international and <a href="/wiki/Intercultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Intercultural">intercultural</a> <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a>, as well as service- and culture-related industries. Furthermore, an international consecutive <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leadership_studies" title="Leadership studies">leadership studies</a> is offered in English.</p>
<h3><span id="European_Institute_of_Innovation_and_Technology_(EIT)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Institute_of_Innovation_and_Technology_.28EIT.29">European Institute of Innovation and Technology (EIT)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: European Institute of Innovation and Technology (EIT)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/European_Institute_of_Innovation_and_Technology" title="European Institute of Innovation and Technology">European Institute of Innovation and Technology</a></div>
<p>Karlsruhe hosts one of the <a href="/wiki/European_Institute_of_Innovation_and_Technology" title="European Institute of Innovation and Technology">European Institute of Innovation and Technology</a>'s Knowledge and Innovation Communities (KICs) focusing on <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable energy</a>. Other co‑centres are based in <a href="/wiki/Grenoble" title="Grenoble">Grenoble</a>, France (CC Alps Valleys); <a href="/wiki/Eindhoven" title="Eindhoven">Eindhoven</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">Leuven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> (CC <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">Benelux</a>); <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, Spain (CC Iberia); <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>, Poland (CC PolandPlus); and <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, Sweden (CC Sweden).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_Education">University of Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: University of Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The University of Education Karlsruhe was founded in 1962. It is specialized in educational processes. The University has about 3700 students and 180 full-time researchers and lecturers. It offers a wide range of educational studies, like teaching profession for primary and secondary schools (both optional with a European Teaching Certificate profile), Bachelor programs that specializes in Early Childhood Education and in Health and Leisure Education, Master programs in Educational Science, Intercultural Education, Migration and Multilingualism. Furthermore, the University of Education Karlsruhe offers a Master program for Biodiversity and Environmental Education.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1999 the <a href="/wiki/Zentrum_f%C3%BCr_Kunst_und_Medientechnologie" class="mw-redirect" title="Zentrum für Kunst und Medientechnologie">ZKM</a> (<i>Zentrum für Kunst und Medientechnologie</i>, Centre for Art and Media) was opened. Within a short time it built up a worldwide reputation as a cultural institution. Linking new media theory and practice, the ZKM is located in a former weapons factory. Among the institutes related to the ZKM are the <i><a href="/wiki/Staatliche_Hochschule_f%C3%BCr_Gestaltung" class="mw-redirect" title="Staatliche Hochschule für Gestaltung">Staatliche Hochschule für Gestaltung</a></i> (State University of Design), whose president is philosopher <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sloterdijk" title="Peter Sloterdijk">Peter Sloterdijk</a> and the Museum for Contemporary Art.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karlsruhe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Every year in July there is a large open-air festival lasting three days called simply <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Das_Fest&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Das Fest (page does not exist)">Das Fest</a></i> ("The Festival").<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Baden State Theatre has sponsored the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A4ndel" class="mw-redirect" title="Händel">Händel</a> Festival since 1978.</p>
<p>The city hosted the 23rd and 31st <a href="/wiki/European_Juggling_Convention" title="European Juggling Convention">European Juggling Conventions</a> (EJC) in 2000 and 2008.</p>
<p>In July the <a href="/w/index.php?title=African_Summer_Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="African Summer Festival (page does not exist)">African Summer Festival</a> is held in the city's Nordstadt. Markets, drumming workshops, exhibitions, a varied children's programme, and musical performances take place during the three days festival.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the past Karlsruhe has been the host of <a href="/wiki/LinuxTag" title="LinuxTag">LinuxTag</a> (the biggest Linux event in Europe) and until 2006 hosted the annual Linux Audio Conference.<sup id="cite_ref-lac_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lac-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Visitors and locals watched the total <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a> at noon on August 11, 1999. The city was not only located within the eclipse path but was one of the few within Germany not plagued by bad weather.</p>

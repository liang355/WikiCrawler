<p><b>Postgraduate education</b>, or <b>graduate education</b> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, involves learning and studying for <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">academic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Professional_degree" title="Professional degree">professional degrees</a>, academic or professional certificates, academic or professional diplomas, or other qualifications for which a first or <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> generally is required, and it is normally considered to be part of <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a>. In North America, this level is generally referred to as <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate school</a> (or sometimes colloquially as <b>grad school</b>).</p>
<p>The organization and structure of postgraduate education varies in different countries, as well as in different institutions within countries. This article outlines the basic types of courses and of teaching and examination methods, with some explanation of their history.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_postgraduate_qualification">Types of postgraduate qualification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of postgraduate qualification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two main types of degrees studied for at the postgraduate level: academic and vocational degrees.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Degrees">Degrees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Degrees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term <i>degree</i> in this context means the moving from one stage or level to another (from <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>degré</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>dē-</i> + <i>gradus</i>), and first appeared in the 13th century.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Universit%C3%A4t_Bologna_Deutsche_Nation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Universit%C3%A4t_Bologna_Deutsche_Nation.jpg/220px-Universit%C3%A4t_Bologna_Deutsche_Nation.jpg" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Universit%C3%A4t_Bologna_Deutsche_Nation.jpg/330px-Universit%C3%A4t_Bologna_Deutsche_Nation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Universit%C3%A4t_Bologna_Deutsche_Nation.jpg/440px-Universit%C3%A4t_Bologna_Deutsche_Nation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="574" data-file-height="550" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Universit%C3%A4t_Bologna_Deutsche_Nation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Although systems of higher education date back to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>, the concept of <i>postgraduate</i> education depends upon the system of awarding degrees at different levels of study, and can be traced to the workings of European <a href="/wiki/Medieval_university" title="Medieval university">medieval universities</a>, mostly Italians.<sup id="cite_ref-Lexikon_des_Mittelalters:_Doctor.2C_doctoratus_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lexikon_des_Mittelalters:_Doctor.2C_doctoratus-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lexikon_des_Mittelalters:_Licentia_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lexikon_des_Mittelalters:_Licentia-2">[2]</a></sup> University studies took six years for a bachelor's degree and up to twelve additional years for a master's degree or doctorate. The first six years taught the faculty of the arts, which was the study of the seven <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal arts">liberal arts</a>: arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, music theory, grammar, logic, and rhetoric. The main emphasis was on logic. Once a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> degree had been obtained, the student could choose one of three faculties—<a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a>—in which to pursue master's or doctor's degrees.</p>
<p>The degrees of master (from Latin <i>magister</i>) and doctor (from Latin <i>doctor</i>) were for some time equivalent, "the former being more in favour at Paris and the universities modeled after it, and the latter at Bologna and its derivative universities. At Oxford and Cambridge a distinction came to be drawn between the Faculties of Law, Medicine, and Theology and the Faculty of Arts in this respect, the title of Doctor being used for the former, and that of Master for the latter."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Because theology was thought to be the highest of the subjects, the doctorate came to be thought of as higher than the master's.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main significance of the higher, postgraduate degrees was that they licensed the holder to teach ("doctor" comes from Latin <i>docere</i>, "to teach").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In most countries, the hierarchy of postgraduate degrees is as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">Master's degrees</a>.
<dl>
<dd>These are sometimes placed in a further hierarchy, starting with degrees such as the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a> (from Latin <i>Magister artium</i>; M.A.) and <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> (from Latin <i>Magister scientiæ</i>; M.Sc.) degrees, then the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Philosophy" title="Master of Philosophy">Master of Philosophy</a> degree (from Latin <i>Magister philosophiæ</i>; M.Phil.), and finally the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Letters" title="Master of Letters">Master of Letters</a> degree (from Latin <i>Magister litterarum</i>; M.Litt.) (all formerly known in France as <a href="/wiki/DEA_(former_French_degree)" class="mw-redirect" title="DEA (former French degree)">DEA</a> or DESS before 2005, and nowadays Masters too). In the UK, master's degrees may be taught or by research: taught master's degrees include the Master of Science and Master of Arts degrees which last one year and are worth 180 <a href="/wiki/Credit_Accumulation_and_Transfer_Scheme" title="Credit Accumulation and Transfer Scheme">CATS</a> credits (equivalent to 90 ECTS European credits), whereas the master's degrees by research include the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Research" title="Master of Research">Master of Research</a> degree (M.Res.) which also lasts one year and is worth 180 CATS or 90 ECTS credits (the difference compared to the Master of Science and Master of Arts degrees being that the research is much more extensive) and the Master of Philosophy degree which lasts two years. In Scottish Universities, the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Philosophy" title="Master of Philosophy">Master of Philosophy</a> degree tends to be by research or higher master's degree and the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Letters" title="Master of Letters">Master of Letters</a> degree tends to be the taught or lower master's degree. In many fields such as <a href="/wiki/Clinical_social_work" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinical social work">clinical social work</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Library_science" title="Library science">library science</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, a master's is the <a href="/wiki/Terminal_degree" title="Terminal degree">terminal degree</a>. Professional degrees such as the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Architecture" title="Master of Architecture">Master of Architecture</a> degree (M.Arch.) can last to three and a half years to satisfy professional requirements to be an architect. Professional degrees such as the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">Master of Business Administration</a> degree (M.B.A.) can last up to two years to satisfy the requirement to become a knowledgeable business leader.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">Doctorates</a>.
<dl>
<dd>These are often further divided into academic and professional doctorates. An academic doctorate can be awarded as a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> degree (from Latin <i>Doctor philosophiæ</i>; Ph.D. or D.Phil.) or as a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">Doctor of Science</a> degree (from Latin <i>Doctor scientiæ</i>; D.Sc.). The Doctor of Science degree can also be awarded in specific fields, such as a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Doctor_of_Science_in_Mathematics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Doctor of Science in Mathematics (page does not exist)">Doctor of Science in Mathematics</a> degree (from Latin <i>Doctor scientiarum mathematic arum</i>; D.Sc.Math.), a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Doctor_of_Agricultural_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Doctor of Agricultural Science (page does not exist)">Doctor of Agricultural Science</a> degree (from Latin <i>Doctor scientiarum agrariarum</i>; D.Sc.Agr.), a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Business_Administration" title="Doctor of Business Administration">Doctor of Business Administration</a> degree (D.B.A.), etc. In some parts of Europe, doctorates are divided into the Doctor of Philosophy degree or "junior doctorate", and the "higher doctorates" such as the Doctor of Science degree, which are generally awarded to highly distinguished professors. A doctorate is the <a href="/wiki/Terminal_degree" title="Terminal degree">terminal degree</a> in most fields. In the United States, there is little distinction between a Doctor of Philosophy degree and a Doctor of Science degree. In the UK, Doctor of Philosophy degrees are often equivalent to 540 <a href="/wiki/Credit_Accumulation_and_Transfer_Scheme" title="Credit Accumulation and Transfer Scheme">CATS</a> credits or 270 <a href="/wiki/European_Credit_Transfer_and_Accumulation_System" title="European Credit Transfer and Accumulation System">ECTS</a> European credits, but this is not always the case as the credit structure of doctoral degrees is not officially defined.</dd>
</dl>
</li>
</ol>
<p>In the UK and countries whose education systems were founded on the British model, such as the US, the master's degree was for a long time the only postgraduate degree normally awarded, while in most European countries apart from the UK, the master's degree almost disappeared. In the second half of the 19th century, however, US universities began to follow the European model by awarding doctorates, and this practice spread to the UK. Conversely, most European universities now offer master's degrees parallelling or replacing their regular system, so as to offer their students better chances to compete in an international market dominated by the American model.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the UK, an equivalent formation to doctorate is the NVQ 5 or QCF 8.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorary_degrees">Honorary degrees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Honorary degrees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most universities award honorary degrees, usually at the postgraduate level. These are awarded to a wide variety of people, such as artists, musicians, writers, politicians, businesspeople, etc., in recognition of their achievements in their various fields. (Recipients of such degrees do not normally use the associated titles or letters, such as "Dr".)</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-degree_qualifications">Non-degree qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-degree qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Postgraduate education can involve studying for qualifications such as <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_certificate" title="Postgraduate certificate">postgraduate certificates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_diploma" title="Postgraduate diploma">postgraduate diplomas</a>. They are sometimes used as steps on the route to a degree, as part of the training for a specific career, or as a qualification in an area of study too narrow to warrant a full degree course.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Admission">Admission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Admission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Argentina, the admission to a Postgraduate program at an Argentine University requires the full completion of any undergraduate course, called in Argentina "carrera de grado" (v.gr. <a href="/wiki/Licentiate" class="mw-redirect" title="Licentiate">Licenciado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering_degree" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering degree">Ingeniero</a> or Lawyer degree).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While a significant portion of postgraduate students finance their tuition and living costs with teaching or research work at private and state-run institutions, international institutions, such as the Fulbright Program and the Organization of American States (OAS), have been known to grant full scholarships for tuition with apportions for housing.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Degree_requirements">Degree requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Degree requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon completion of at least two years' research and course work as a postgraduate student, a candidate must demonstrate truthful and original contributions to his or her specific field of knowledge within a frame of academic excellence.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Master and Doctoral candidate's work should be presented in a dissertation or thesis prepared under the supervision of a tutor or director, and reviewed by a postgraduate Committee. This Committee should be composed of examiners external to the program, and at least one of them should also be external to the institution.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_postgraduate_degrees">Types of postgraduate degrees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Types of postgraduate degrees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Programmes are divided into coursework-based and research-based degrees. Coursework programs typically include qualifications such as <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Graduate Certificate, six month full-time coursework</li>
<li>Graduate Diploma, twelve month full-time coursework</li>
<li>Masters (of Arts, Science or other discipline), twelve to 24 months coursework sometimes including a six-month dissertation like the Australian undergraduate honours degree</li>
<li>Professional Doctorates, which are usually more strenuous and of a longer duration than a master's degree, e.g. 36 months in duration.</li>
</ul>
<p>Research degrees generally consist of either Masters or Doctorate programs. In some disciplines it is acceptable to go straight from the undergraduate degree into a Ph.D. program if one achieves a very good Honors degree (see Admissions below), and in others, it may be encouraged or expected or simply advantageous in varying amounts for the student to first undertake a research Masters before applying to Ph.D. programs. Research master's degrees may be still called an M.A. or M.Sc., like a course work Masters, or may have a special appellation, e.g. M.Phil. Doctorate programs may lead to the award of a Ph.D. or a D.Phil. depending on the university or faculty.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Letters" title="Doctor of Letters">D.Litt</a> is a higher research degree for exemplary achievement.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Admission_2">Admission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Admission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally, the Australian higher education system follows that of its British counterpart (with some notable exceptions). Entrance is decided by merit, entrance to coursework-based programmes is usually not as strict; most universities usually require a "Credit" average as entry to their taught programmes in a field related to their previous undergraduate. On average, however, a strong "Credit" or "Distinction" average is the norm for accepted students. Not all coursework programs require the student to already possess the relevant undergraduate degree, they are intended as "conversion" or professional qualification programs, and merely any relevant undergraduate degree with good grades is required.</p>
<p>Ph.D. entrance requirements in the higher ranked schools typically require a student to have <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_research" title="Postgraduate research">postgraduate research</a> honours or a master's degree by research, or a master's with a significant research component. Entry requirements depend on the subject studied and the individual university. The minimum duration of a Ph.D. programme is two years, but completing within this time span is unusual, with Ph.D.s usually taking an average of three to four years to be completed.</p>
<p>Most of the confusion with Australian postgraduate programmes occurs with the research-based programmes, particularly scientific programmes. Research degrees generally require candidates to have a minimum of a second-class four-year honours undergraduate degree to be considered for admission to a Ph.D. programme (M.Phil. are an uncommon route).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In science, a British first class honours (3 years) is not equivalent to an Australian first class honours (1 year research postgraduate programme that requires a completed an undergraduate (pass) degree with a high grade-point average).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In scientific research, it is commonly accepted that an Australian postgraduate honours is equivalent to a British master's degree (in research). There has been some debate over the acceptance of a three-year honours degree (as in the case of graduates from British universities) as equivalent entry requirement to graduate research programmes (M.Phil., Ph.D.) in Australian universities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The letters of Honours programmes also added to the confusion. For example: B.Sc. (Hons) are the letters gained for postgraduate research honours at the University of Queensland. B.Sc. (Hons) does not indicate that this honours is postgraduate qualification. Difficulty also arises between different universities in Australia—some universities have followed the UK system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_programs">Professional programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Professional programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many professional programs such as medical and dental school require a previous bachelors for admission and are considered graduate or <a href="/wiki/Graduate_Entry" class="mw-redirect" title="Graduate Entry">Graduate Entry</a> programs even though they culminate in a bachelor's degree. Example, the <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Medicine_and_Surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Bachelor of Medicine and Surgery">Bachelor of Medicine (MBBS)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">Bachelor of Dentistry (BDent)</a>.</p>
<p>There has also been some confusion over the conversion of the different marking schemes between British, US, and Australian systems for the purpose of assessment for entry to graduate programmes. The Australian grades are divided into four categories: High Distinction, Distinction, Credit, and Pass (though many institutions have idiosyncratic grading systems). Assessment and evaluation based on the Australian system is not equivalent to British or US schemes because of the "low-marking" scheme used by Australian universities. For example, a British student who achieves 70+ will receive an A grade, whereas an Australian student with 70+ will receive a Distinction which is not the highest grade in the marking scheme.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_2">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Australian government usually offer full funding (fees and a monthly stipend) to its citizens and permanent residents who are pursuing research-based higher degrees. There are also highly competitive scholarships for international candidates who intend to pursue research-based programmes. Taught-degree scholarships (certain master's degrees, Grad. Dip., Grad. Cert., D.Eng., D.B.A.) are almost non-existent for international students, so they are usually required to be self-funded.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Degree_requirements_2">Degree requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Degree requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Requirements for the successful completion of a taught master's programme are that the student pass all the required modules. Some universities require eight taught modules for a one-year programme, twelve modules for a one-and-a-half-year programme, and twelve taught modules plus a thesis or dissertation for a two-year programme. The academic year for an Australian postgraduate programme is typically two semesters (eight months of study).</p>
<p>Requirements for research-based programmes vary among universities. Generally, however, a student is not required to take taught modules as part of their candidacy. It is now common that first-year Ph.D. candidates are not regarded as permanent Ph.D. students for fear that they may not be sufficiently prepared to undertake independent research. In such cases, an alternative degree will be awarded for their previous work, usually an M.Phil. or M.Sc. by research.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Admission_3">Admission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Admission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Brazil, a Bachelor's, Licenciate or <i>Technologist</i> degree is required in order to enter a graduate program, called <i>pós-graduação</i>. Generally, in order to be accepted, the candidate must have above average grades and it is highly recommended to be initiated on scientific research through government programs on undergraduate areas, as a complement to usual coursework.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_3">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The competition for <a href="/wiki/List_of_universities_in_Brazil" class="mw-redirect" title="List of universities in Brazil">public universities</a> is very large, as they are the most prestigious and respected universities in Brazil. Public universities do not charge fees for any level/course. Funding, similar to wages, is available but is usually granted by public agencies linked to the university in question (i.e. <a href="/wiki/FAPESP" class="mw-redirect" title="FAPESP">FAPESP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coordenadoria_de_Aperfei%C3%A7oamento_de_Pessoal_de_N%C3%ADvel_Superior" class="mw-redirect" title="Coordenadoria de Aperfeiçoamento de Pessoal de Nível Superior">CAPES</a>, <a href="/wiki/CNPq" class="mw-redirect" title="CNPq">CNPq</a>, etc.), given to the students previously ranked based on internal criteria.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Degree_requirements_3">Degree requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Degree requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two types of <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Postgraduate">postgraduate</a>; <i>lato sensu</i> (Latin for "in broad sense"), which generally means a specialization course in one area of study, mostly addressed to professional practice, and <i>stricto sensu</i> (Latin for "in narrow sense"), which means a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> or <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">Doctorate</a>, encompassing broader and profound activities of scientific research.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_programs">Types of programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Types of programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Graduate certificates (sometimes called "postgraduate certificates")</li>
<li>Master's degree</li>
<li>Doctoral degree</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Admission_4">Admission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Admission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Admission to a graduate certificate program requires a university degree (or in some cases, a diploma with years of related experience). English speaking colleges require proof of English language proficiency such as IELTS. Some colleges may provide English language upgrading to students prior to the start of their graduate certificate program.</p>
<p>Admission to a master's program generally requires a bachelor's degree in a related field, with sufficiently high grades usually ranging from <a href="/wiki/Academic_grading_in_North_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic grading in North America">B+</a> and higher (note that different schools have different <a href="/wiki/Letter_grade" class="mw-redirect" title="Letter grade">letter grade</a> conventions, and this requirement may be significantly higher in some faculties), and recommendations from professors. Some schools require samples of the student's writing as well as a research proposal. Some programs require Graduate Record Exams (GRE) in both the general examination and the examination for its specific discipline, with minimum scores for admittance. At English-speaking universities, applicants from countries where English is not the primary language are required to submit scores from the Test of English as a Foreign Language (<a href="/wiki/TOEFL" class="mw-redirect" title="TOEFL">TOEFL</a>). Nevertheless, some French speaking universities, like HEC Montreal, also requires candidates to submit TOEFL score or to pass their own English test.</p>
<p>Admission to a doctoral program typically requires a master's degree in a related field, sufficiently high grades, recommendations, samples of writing, a research proposal, and typically an interview with a prospective supervisor. Requirements are often set higher than those for a master's program. In exceptional cases, a student holding an honours B.A. with sufficiently high grades and proven writing and research abilities may be admitted directly to a Ph.D. program without the requirement to first complete a master's. Many Canadian graduate programs allow students who start in a master's to "reclassify" into a Ph.D. program after satisfactory performance in the first year, bypassing the master's degree.</p>
<p>Graduate students must usually declare their research goal or submit a research proposal upon entering grad school; in the case of master's degrees, there will be some flexibility (that is, one is not held to one's research proposal, although major changes, for example from premodern to modern history, are discouraged). In the case of Ph.D.s, the research direction is usually known as it will typically follow the direction of the master's research.</p>
<p>Master's degrees can possibly be completed in one year but normally take at least two; they typically do not exceed five years. Doctoral degrees require a minimum of two years but frequently take much longer, not usually exceeding six years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_4">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Graduate students may take out <a href="/wiki/Student_loans_in_Canada" title="Student loans in Canada">student loans</a>, but instead they often work as <a href="/wiki/Teaching_assistant" title="Teaching assistant">teaching</a> or <a href="/wiki/Research_assistant" title="Research assistant">research assistants</a>. Students often agree, as a condition of acceptance to a programme, not to devote more than twelve hours per week to work or outside interests.</p>
<p>Funding is available to first-year masters students whose transcripts reflect exceptionally high grades; this funding is normally given in the second year.</p>
<p>Funding for Ph.D. students comes from a variety of sources, and many universities waive tuition fees for doctoral candidates.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Funding is available in the form of <a href="/wiki/Scholarships" class="mw-redirect" title="Scholarships">scholarships</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bursary" title="Bursary">bursaries</a> and other awards, both private and public.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Degree_requirements_4">Degree requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Degree requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Graduate certificates require between eight and sixteen months of study. The length of study depends on the program. Graduate certificates primarily involve coursework. However, some may require a research project or a work placement.</p>
<p>Both master's and doctoral programs may be done by <a href="/wiki/Coursework" title="Coursework">coursework</a> or <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> or a combination of the two, depending on the subject and <a href="/wiki/Faculty_(division)" title="Faculty (division)">faculty</a>. Most faculties require both, with the emphasis on research, and with coursework being directly related to the field of research.</p>
<p>Master's candidates undertaking research are typically required to complete a <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">thesis</a> comprising some original research and ranging from seventy to two-hundred pages. Some fields may require candidates to study at least one foreign language if they have not already earned sufficient foreign-language credits. Some faculties require candidates to <a href="/wiki/Thesis_defence" class="mw-redirect" title="Thesis defence">defend their thesis</a>, but many do not. Those that do not, often have a requirement of taking two additional courses, at minimum, in lieu of preparing a thesis.</p>
<p>Ph.D. candidates undertaking research must typically complete a thesis, or <a href="/wiki/Dissertation" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissertation">dissertation</a>, consisting of original research representing a significant contribution to their field, and ranging from two-hundred to five-hundred pages. Most Ph.D. candidates will be required to sit <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_examination" title="Comprehensive examination">comprehensive examinations</a>—examinations testing general knowledge in their field of specialization—in their second or third year as a prerequisite to continuing their studies, and must defend their thesis as a final requirement. Some faculties require candidates to earn sufficient credits in a third or fourth foreign language; for example, most candidates in modern Japanese topics must demonstrate ability in English, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Standard_Mandarin" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Mandarin">Mandarin</a>, while candidates in pre-modern Japanese topics must demonstrate ability in English, Japanese, <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">Classical Chinese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Classical_Japanese" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Japanese">Classical Japanese</a>.</p>
<p>At English-speaking Canadian universities, both master's and Ph.D. theses may be presented in English or in the language of the subject (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> for <a href="/wiki/German_literature" title="German literature">German literature</a>, for example), but if this is the case an extensive abstract must be also presented in English. In exceptional circumstances, a thesis may be presented in French.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The exception to this rule is McGill University, where all work can be submitted in either English or French, unless the purpose of the course of study is acquisition of a language.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>French-speaking universities have varying sets of rules; some (e.g. HEC Montreal<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>) will accept students with little knowledge of French if they can communicate with their supervisors (usually in English).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_context">Specific context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Specific context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For historical reasons dating back to the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution_of_1789" class="mw-redirect" title="French Revolution of 1789">French Revolution of 1789</a>, France has a dual education system, with <a href="/wiki/Grandes_%C3%89coles" class="mw-redirect" title="Grandes Écoles">Grandes Écoles</a> on one side, and <a href="/wiki/Universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Universities">universities</a> on the other hand, with the <i>Grandes Écoles</i> being considered as much more prestigious. The Grandes écoles deliver the French <a href="/wiki/Dipl%C3%B4me_d%27ing%C3%A9nieur" class="mw-redirect" title="Diplôme d'ingénieur">diplôme d'ingénieur</a>, which is ranked as a master's degree.</p>
<p>Note that France ranks a Doctorate in health sciences (i.e. physician, surgeon, dentist, veterinarian diplomas) as equivalent to a master's degree in any other discipline,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> to account for the difficulty gap between getting a medical degree and getting non health related doctoral degrees, the latter requiring much tougher research.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Admission_5">Admission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Admission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are 87 <a href="/wiki/Public_universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Public universities">public universities</a> in France, and also some private universities, and they are based upon the European education ladder including bachelors, Masters, and Ph.D.s. Students gain each degree though the successful completion of a predetermined number of years in education, gaining credits via the <a href="/wiki/European_Credit_Transfer_System" class="mw-redirect" title="European Credit Transfer System">European Credit Transfer System</a> (ECTS). There are over 300 doctoral programs that collaborate with 1200 research laboratories and centers. Each degree has a certain set of national diplomas that are all of equal value, irrespective of where they were issued. There are also other diplomas that are exclusive to France and are very hard to attain.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany_and_the_Netherlands">Germany and the Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Germany and the Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some programs in the traditional German system and the traditional Dutch system, there is no legal distinction between "undergraduate" and "postgraduate". In such programs, all education aims towards the master's degree, whether introductory (Bachelor's level) or advanced (master's level). These one-tier programmes take between 4.5 and 5 years.</p>
<p>In the meantime, Germany introduced the <a href="/wiki/Bologna_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Bologna process">Bologna process</a> with a separation between Bachelor and Master programmes in many fields, except for education studies, law and other specially regulated subjects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Nigeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Admission to a postgraduate degree programme in Nigeria requires a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> with at least a Second Class Lower Division (not less than 2.75/5). Admission to Doctoral programmes requires an Academic master's degree with a minimum <a href="/wiki/Weighted_average" class="mw-redirect" title="Weighted average">weighted average</a> of 60% (B average or 4.00/5). In addition to this, applicants may be subjected to written and oral examinations depending on the school. Most universities with high numbers of applicants have more stringent admission processes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Postgraduate degrees in Nigeria include M.A., M.Sc., M.Ed., M.Eng., LL.M, M.Arch., M.Agric., M.Phil., PhD. The master's degree typically take 18–36 months with students undertaking coursework and presenting seminars and a dissertation. The doctoral degree is for a minimum of 36 months and may involve coursework alongside the presentation of seminars and a research thesis. Award of postgraduate degrees requires a defence of the completed research before a panel of examiners comprising external and internal examiners, Head of Department, Departmental Postgraduate Coordinator, Representative(s) of Faculty and Postgraduate School, and any other member of staff with a PhD in the department/faculty.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Admission_6">Admission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Admission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Admission to undertake a research degree in the UK typically requires a good bachelor's degree, or Scottish M.A., (at least <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification#Second-class_honours" title="British undergraduate degree classification">lower second</a>, but usually an upper second or <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification#First-class_honours" title="British undergraduate degree classification">first class</a>). In some institutions <a href="/wiki/Doctoral" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctoral">Doctoral</a> candidates are initially admitted to a Masters in Research Philosophy (<a href="/wiki/Master_of_Philosophy" title="Master of Philosophy">M.Phil.</a> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=M.Res.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="M.Res. (page does not exist)">M.Res.</a>), then later transfer to a Ph.D./D.Phil. if they can show satisfactory progress in their first 8–12 months of study.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Candidates for the degree of Doctor of Education (<a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Education" title="Doctor of Education">Ed.D</a>) are typically required to hold a good bachelor's degree as well as an appropriate master's degree before being admitted.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_5">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Funding for postgraduate study in the UK is awarded competitively, and usually is disseminated by institution (in the form of a certain allocation of studentships for a given year) rather than directly to individuals. There are a number of scholarships for master's courses, but these are relatively rare and dependent on the course and class of undergraduate degree obtained (usually requiring at least a lower second). Most master's students are self-funded.</p>
<p>Funding is available for some Ph.D./D.Phil. courses. As at the master's level, there is more funding available to those in the sciences than in other disciplines. Such funding generally comes from <a href="/wiki/UK_Research_Councils" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Research Councils">Research Councils</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Engineering_and_Physical_Sciences_Research_Council" title="Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council">Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council</a> (EPSRC), <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Humanities_Research_Council" title="Arts and Humanities Research Council">Arts and Humanities Research Council</a> (AHRC), <a href="/wiki/Medical_Research_Council_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical Research Council (UK)">Medical Research Council</a> (MRC) and others. Masters students may also have the option of a Postgraduate loan introduced by the British Government in 2016.</p>
<p>For overseas students, most major funding applications are due as early as twelve months or more before the intended graduate course will begin. This funding is also often highly competitive. The most widely available, and thus important, award for overseas students is the Overseas Research Student (ORS) Award, which pays the difference in university fees between an overseas student and a British or EU resident. However, a student can only for one university apply for the ORS Award, often before he or she knows whether they have been accepted. As of the 2009/2010 academic year, the <a href="/wiki/Higher_Education_Funding_Council_for_England" title="Higher Education Funding Council for England">HEFCE</a> has cancelled the Overseas Research Student Award scheme for English and Welsh universities.<sup id="cite_ref-ORSAS_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ORSAS-19">[19]</a></sup> The state of the scheme for Scottish and Northern Irish universities is currently unclear.</p>
<p>Students studying part-time for a master's degree can apply for income-based <a href="/wiki/Jobseeker%27s_Allowance" title="Jobseeker's Allowance">Jobseeker's Allowance</a> provided their timetabled hours are fewer than 16 hours per week. This also entitles the student to <a href="/wiki/Housing_benefit" class="mw-redirect" title="Housing benefit">housing benefit</a> provided by their local <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">council</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Full-time students (of any type) are not normally eligible for state benefits, including during vacation time.<sup id="cite_ref-Jobcentre_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jobcentre-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Degree_requirements_5">Degree requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Degree requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Additionally, doctoral students who have advanced to candidacy but not filed a dissertation ("ABD," for "<a href="/wiki/All_but_dissertation" title="All but dissertation">all but dissertation</a>") often receive master's degrees and an additional master's called a Master of Philosophy, or M.Phil., or <a href="/wiki/C.Phil." class="mw-redirect" title="C.Phil.">C.Phil.</a> "Candidate in Philosophy" degree. The master's component of a doctorate program often requires one or two years, and some students, because doctoral programs are sometimes better-funded, apply for doctoral programs while only intending to earn a master's degree.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This is generally not acceptable and, if a student's advisor learns of the student's plans, can result in early termination.</p>
<p>Many graduate programs require students to pass one or several examinations in order to demonstrate their competence as scholars.<sup id="cite_ref-Ph.D.Process_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ph.D.Process-22">[22]</a></sup> In some departments, a comprehensive examination is often required in the first year of doctoral study, and is designed to test a student's background undergraduate-level knowledge. Examinations of this type are more common in the sciences and some social sciences, and relatively unknown in most humanities disciplines.</p>
<p>Some graduate students perform teaching duties, often serving as graders, tutors, or teaching assistants. In some departments, they can be promoted to <a href="/wiki/Lecturer" title="Lecturer">Lecturer</a> status, a position that comes with more responsibilities.</p>
<p>Doctoral students generally spend roughly their first two to three years doing coursework, and begin research by their second year if not before. Many master's and all specialist students will perform research culminating in a paper, presentation, and defense of their research. This is called the master's thesis (or, for Educational Specialist students, the specialist paper). However, many US master's degree programs do not require a master's thesis, focusing instead primarily on course work or on "practicals" or "workshops". Such "real-world" experience may typically require a candidate work on a project alone or in a team as a consultant, or consultants, for an outside entity approved or selected by the academic institution, and under faculty supervision.</p>
<p>In the second and third years of study, doctoral programs often require students to pass more examinations.<sup id="cite_ref-Ph.D.Process_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ph.D.Process-22">[22]</a></sup> Programs often require a Qualifying Examination ("Quals"), a Ph.D. Candidacy Examination ("Candidacy"), or a General Examination ("Generals") designed to test the students' grasp of a broad sample of their discipline, or one or several Special Field Examinations ("Specials") which test students in their narrower selected areas of specialty within the discipline. If these examinations are held orally, they may be known colloquially as "orals". For some social science and many humanities disciplines, where graduate students may or may not have studied the discipline at the undergraduate level, these exams will be the first set, and be based either on graduate coursework or specific preparatory reading (sometimes up to a year's work in reading). In all cases, comprehensive exams are normally both stressful and time-consuming and must be passed to be allowed to proceed on to the thesis. Passing such examinations allows the student to stay, begin doctoral research, and rise to the status of a doctoral candidate while failing usually results in the student leaving the program or re-taking the test after some time has passed (usually a semester or a year). Some schools have an intermediate category, passing at the master's level, which allows the student to leave with a master's without having completed a master's thesis.</p>
<p>For the next several years, the doctoral candidate primarily performs his or her research. Usually this lasts three to eight years, though a few finish more quickly and some take substantially longer. In total, the typical doctoral degree takes between four and eight years from entering the program to completion though this time varies depending upon the department, thesis topic, and many other factors.</p>
<p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> degrees take five to six years on average, but <a href="/wiki/Observational_astronomy" title="Observational astronomy">observational astronomy</a> degrees take six to seven due to limiting factors of weather, while <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_astronomy" title="Theoretical astronomy">theoretical astronomy</a> degrees take five. Though there is substantial variation among universities, departments, and individuals, <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> doctorates on average take somewhat longer to complete than <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural science</a> doctorates. These differences are due to the differing nature of research between the humanities and some social sciences and the natural sciences, and to the differing expectations of the discipline in coursework, languages and length of thesis. However, time required to complete a <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> also varies according to the candidate's abilities and choice of research. Some students may also choose to remain in a program if they fail to win an academic position, particularly in disciplines with a tight job market; by remaining a student, they can retain access to libraries and university facilities, while also retaining an academic affiliation, which can be essential for conferences and job-searches.</p>
<p>Traditionally, doctoral programs were only intended to last three to four years and, in some disciplines (primarily the natural sciences), with a helpful advisor, and a light teaching load, it is possible for the degree to be completed in that amount of time. However, increasingly many disciplines, including most humanities, set their requirements for coursework, languages and the expected extent of thesis research by the assumption that students will take five years minimum or six to seven years on average; competition for jobs within these fields also raises expectations on the length and quality of theses considerably.</p>
<p>In some disciplines, doctoral programs can average seven to ten years. <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">Archaeology</a>, which requires long periods of research, tends towards the longer end of this spectrum. The increase in length of degree is a matter of great concern for both students and universities, though there is much disagreement on potential solutions to this problem.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_6">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postgraduate_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many departments, especially those in which students have research or teaching responsibilities, offer tuition-forgiveness and a stipend that pays for most expenses. At some elite universities, there may be a minimum stipend established for all Ph.D. students, as well as a tuition waiver. The terms of these stipends vary greatly, and may consist of a scholarship or fellowship, followed by teaching responsibilities. At many elite universities, these stipends have been increasing, in response both to student pressure and, especially, to competition among the elite universities for graduate students.</p>
<p>In some fields, research positions are more coveted than teaching positions because student researchers are typically paid to work on the dissertation they are required to complete anyway, while teaching is generally considered a distraction from one's work. Research positions are more typical of science disciplines; they are relatively uncommon in humanities disciplines, and where they exist, they rarely allow the student to work on their own research.</p>
<p>Departments often have money for limited discretionary funding to supplement minor expenses such as research trips and travel to conferences.</p>
<p>A few students can attain outside <a href="/wiki/Scholarship" title="Scholarship">fellowships</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> (NSF) and <a href="/wiki/National_Physical_Science_Consortium" title="National Physical Science Consortium">National Physical Science Consortium</a> (NPSC). Funding differs greatly by departments and universities; some universities give five years of full funding to all Ph.D. students, though often with a teaching requirement attached; other universities do not.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foreign students are typically funded the same way as domestic (US) students, although federally subsidized student and parent loans and work-study assistance are generally limited to US citizens and nationals, permanent residents, and approved refugees.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Moreover, some funding sources (such as many NSF fellowships) may only be awarded to domestic students. Other factors contributing to possible financial difficulties include high costs to visit their families back home, supporting one's family who is not allowed to work due to <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">immigration</a> laws, tuition that is steep by world standards, and large fees: <a href="/wiki/Visa_(document)" class="mw-redirect" title="Visa (document)">visa</a> fees by <a href="/wiki/US_Citizenship_and_Immigration_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="US Citizenship and Immigration Services">US Citizenship and Immigration Services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">surveillance</a> fees (such as Student and Exchange Visitor Information Systems, or <a href="/wiki/SEVIS" class="mw-redirect" title="SEVIS">SEVIS</a><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>) by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">United States Department of Homeland Security</a>.</p>

<p><b>Lisa Randall</b> (born June 18, 1962) is an American <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physicist" class="mw-redirect" title="Theoretical physicist">theoretical physicist</a> working in <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmology</a>. She is the Frank B. Baird, Jr. Professor of Science on the physics faculty of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HU_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HU-1">[1]</a></sup> Her research includes elementary particles, fundamental forces and <a href="/wiki/Extra_dimensions" title="Extra dimensions">extra dimensions</a> of space. She studies the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">supersymmetry</a>, possible solutions to the <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy_problem" title="Hierarchy problem">hierarchy problem</a> concerning the relative weakness of gravity, cosmology of extra dimensions, <a href="/wiki/Baryogenesis" title="Baryogenesis">baryogenesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_inflation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmological inflation">cosmological inflation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NAS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAS-2">[2]</a></sup> She contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Randall%E2%80%93Sundrum_model" title="Randall–Sundrum model">Randall–Sundrum model</a>, first published in 1999 with <a href="/wiki/Raman_Sundrum" title="Raman Sundrum">Raman Sundrum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisa_Randall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Randall was born in <a href="/wiki/Queens,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Queens, New York">Queens</a>, New York City. She is an alumna of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_College_Summer_Studies_in_Mathematics" title="Hampshire College Summer Studies in Mathematics">Hampshire College Summer Studies in Mathematics</a> and graduated from <a href="/wiki/Stuyvesant_High_School" title="Stuyvesant High School">Stuyvesant High School</a> in 1980,<sup id="cite_ref-edge_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edge-4">[4]</a></sup> where she was a classmate of fellow physicist and science popularizer <a href="/wiki/Brian_Greene" title="Brian Greene">Brian Greene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-edge_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edge-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-colmag_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colmag-5">[5]</a></sup> She won first place in the 1980 <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Science_Talent_Search" class="mw-redirect" title="Westinghouse Science Talent Search">Westinghouse Science Talent Search</a> at the age of 18. At <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, Randall earned both a BA in physics (1983) and a PhD in theoretical particle physics (1987) under <a href="/wiki/Howard_Georgi" title="Howard Georgi">Howard Georgi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HU_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HU-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academia">Academia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisa_Randall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Randall researches <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a> at Harvard, where she is a professor of theoretical physics. Her research concerns <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particles" class="mw-redirect" title="Elementary particles">elementary particles</a> and fundamental forces, and has involved the study of a wide variety of models, the most recent involving <a href="/wiki/Randall%E2%80%93Sundrum_model" title="Randall–Sundrum model">extra dimensions of space</a>. She has also worked on <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">supersymmetry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> observables, <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_inflation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmological inflation">cosmological inflation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baryogenesis" title="Baryogenesis">baryogenesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_unified_theories" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand unified theories">grand unified theories</a>, and <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>.</p>
<p>After her graduate work at Harvard, Randall held professorships at <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> before returning to Harvard in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-CV_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CV-6">[6]</a></sup> Professor Randall was the first tenured woman in the Princeton physics department and the first tenured female <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physicist" class="mw-redirect" title="Theoretical physicist">theoretical physicist</a> at Harvard. (<a href="/wiki/Melissa_Franklin" title="Melissa Franklin">Melissa Franklin</a> was the first tenured woman in the Harvard physics department.)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WIP_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WIP-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_Books">Popular Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisa_Randall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popular Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Randall's books <i><a href="/wiki/Warped_Passages" title="Warped Passages">Warped Passages: Unraveling the Mysteries of the Universe's Hidden Dimensions</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Knocking_on_Heaven%E2%80%99s_Door:_How_Physics_and_Scientific_Thinking_Illuminate_the_Universe_and_the_Modern_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Knocking on Heaven’s Door: How Physics and Scientific Thinking Illuminate the Universe and the Modern World">Knocking on Heaven’s Door: How Physics and Scientific Thinking Illuminate the Universe and the Modern World</a></i> have both been on <i>New York Times</i> 100 notable books lists.<sup id="cite_ref-HU_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HU-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between the hardback and paperback release of <i>Knocking on Heaven's Door</i>, the quest for the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a> was actually completed, a subject discussed in the book. Scientists at the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> found a particle identified as the Higgs boson.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> She said about the discovery, that even if people don't understand everything about it, "what an exciting thing it is that people are excited that there is something fundamentally new that has been discovered."<sup id="cite_ref-PI_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PI-11">[11]</a></sup> Randall has an e-book entitled <i>Higgs Discovery: The Power of Empty Space</i>. Before the Large Hadron Collider was operating, she wrote an article explaining the discoveries that were expected from using it.<sup id="cite_ref-TY_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TY-12">[12]</a></sup> She was commonly asked about the misconception that the LHC could make black holes that could destroy the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> She answered that it was "not even conceivable unless space and gravity are very different from what we thought."<sup id="cite_ref-TY_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TY-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Randall wrote the <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> <i>Hypermusic Prologue: A Projective Opera in Seven Planes</i> on the invitation of the composer, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A8ctor_Parra" title="Hèctor Parra">Hèctor Parra</a>, who was inspired by her book <i>Warped Passages</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_Organizations">Professional Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisa_Randall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Professional Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Randall is a member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> (2004) and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> (2008),<sup id="cite_ref-NAS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAS-2">[2]</a></sup> and a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a>.</p>
<p>Randall has helped organize numerous conferences and has been on the editorial board of several major theoretical physics journals.<sup id="cite_ref-HU_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HU-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CV_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CV-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisa_Randall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In autumn 2004, she was the most cited theoretical physicist of the previous five years. Professor Randall was featured in <a href="/wiki/Seed_(magazine)" title="Seed (magazine)"><i>Seed</i> magazine</a>'s "2005 Year in Science Icons" and in <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a>'</i>s "Who's Next in 2006" as "one of the most promising theoretical physicists of her generation." In 2007, Randall was named one of <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i> magazine's</a> 100 Most Influential People (<a href="/wiki/Time_100" title="Time 100"><i>Time</i> 100</a>) under the section for "Scientists &amp; Thinkers". Randall was given this honor for her work regarding the evidence of a higher dimension.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other honors:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" class="mw-redirect" title="Phi Beta Kappa">Phi Beta Kappa</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Gemant_Award" title="Andrew Gemant Award">Andrew Gemant Award</a>, 2012</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisa_Randall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an interview she was asked if she believes in God.</p>
<p>"...I probably don't believe in God. I think it's a problem that people are considered immoral if they're not religious. That's just not true. This might earn me some enemies, but in some ways they may be even more moral. If you do something for a religious reason, you do it because you'll be rewarded in an afterlife or in this world. That's not quite as good as something you do for purely generous reasons."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Randall's sister, <a href="/wiki/Dana_Randall" title="Dana Randall">Dana Randall</a>, is a professor of computer science at <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Tech</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

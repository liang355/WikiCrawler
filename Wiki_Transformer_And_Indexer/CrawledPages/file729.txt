<p><b>The Princeton Review</b> is a <a href="/wiki/College_admission" class="mw-redirect" title="College admission">college admission</a> services company offering <a href="/wiki/Test_preparation" title="Test preparation">test preparation</a> services, tutoring and admissions resources, online courses, and books published by <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>. The company has more than 4,000 teachers and tutors in the United States and Canada and international franchises in 14 other countries. The company is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Natick,_Massachusetts" title="Natick, Massachusetts">Natick, Massachusetts</a>, and is privately held. It is not associated with <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_history">Corporate history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Princeton_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Corporate history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Princeton Review was founded in 1981 by <a href="/wiki/John_Katzman" title="John Katzman">John Katzman</a>, who—shortly after leaving college—taught <a href="/wiki/SAT" title="SAT">SAT</a> preparation to 15 students in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He served as CEO until 2007, and was replaced by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Perik&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michael Perik (page does not exist)">Michael Perik</a>. In March 2010, Perik resigned and was replaced by John M. Connolly. In April 2010, the company sold $48&#160;million in stock for $3 per share, and a short time later was accused of fraud in a class action suit filed by a Michigan retirement fund, which claimed The Princeton Review leadership exaggerated earnings to boost its stock price.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2012, the company was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Charlesbank_Capital" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlesbank Capital">Charlesbank Capital</a>, a private equity fund, for $33&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On August 1, 2014, the Princeton Review brand name and operations were bought for an undisclosed sum by <a href="/wiki/Tutor.com" title="Tutor.com">Tutor.com</a>, an <a href="/wiki/IAC_(company)" title="IAC (company)">IAC company</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mandy_Ginsburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mandy Ginsburg (page does not exist)">Mandy Ginsburg</a> became CEO. The company is no longer affiliated with its former parent, Education Holdings 1, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Princeton_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Princeton_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Test preparation providers have been criticized in the past on the grounds that their courses claim larger score increases than they deliver.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ranking_schools">Ranking schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Princeton_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ranking schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>College rankings, including those published by the Princeton Review, have been criticized for failing to be accurate or comprehensive by assigning objective rankings formed from subjective opinions.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Princeton Review officials counter that their rankings are unique in that they rely on student opinion and not just on statistical data.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002 an <a href="/wiki/American_Medical_Association" title="American Medical Association">American Medical Association</a> affiliated program, A Matter of Degree,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> funded by the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wood_Johnson_Foundation" title="Robert Wood Johnson Foundation">Robert Wood Johnson Foundation</a>, criticized the Princeton Review list of Best <a href="/wiki/Party_school#Criticism_of_party_school_lists" title="Party school">Party Schools</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> published an editorial titled "Sobering Statistics"<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> in August 2002 and stated, "the doctor's group goes too far in suggesting that the rankings contribute to the problem (of campus drinking)." The editorial noted the fact that among the schools the AMA program was then funding as part of its campaign against campus drinking, six of 10 of those schools calling for The Princeton Review to "drop the annual ranking...had made (Princeton Review's) past top-party-school lists: many times for some. That's no coincidence." The editorial commended The Princeton Review for reporting the list, calling it "a public service" for "student applicants and their parents".</p>
<p>Rankings for <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a>-related lists have also been criticized as inaccurate due to outdated methodologies.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Princeton Review bases its LGBT-Friendly and LGBT-Unfriendly <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> top twenty ranking lists, which asks undergraduates: "Do students, faculty, and administrators at your college treat all persons equally regardless of their sexual orientations and gender identify/expression?" The Princeton Review also publishes <i>The Gay &amp; Lesbian Guide to College Life</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy_concerns">Privacy concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Princeton_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Privacy concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2016, the company was criticized by privacy rights advocates who worry that a company that owns online dating and college preparation services could amass data and exploit it in a way that preys on unsuspecting consumers, particularly younger people.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> "Do parents know that when their underage kids enroll for exam prep or tutoring, personal information may be shared with hookup sites that could then target their kids to become customers?" asked one critic, who concluded that the company "makes no guarantee that data sharing among its entities will not include those customers whose sole aim is to improve their grades and test scores."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Indeed, another critic points out that The Princeton Review "policy states 'we may collect certain information from your computer each time you visit our site'—information like data 'regarding your academic and extracurricular activities and interests.' That information can be used to 'send you email notices and offers; perform research and analysis about your use of or interest in our products, services or products or services offered by others; [and] develop and display content and advertising tailored to your interests on our site and other sites.'"<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

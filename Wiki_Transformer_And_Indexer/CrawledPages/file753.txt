<p><b>Rowing</b>, often referred to as <b>crew</b> in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sport</a> with origins back to Ancient Egyptian times. It is based on propelling a boat (<a href="/wiki/Racing_shell" title="Racing shell">racing shell</a>) on water using <a href="/wiki/Oar_(sport_rowing)" title="Oar (sport rowing)">oars</a>. By pushing against the water with an oar, a force is generated to move the boat. The sport can be either recreational, where the focus is on learning the technique of rowing, or competitive, where athletes race against each other in boats.<sup id="cite_ref-Speed_Rower.2C_Competitive_Rowing_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speed_Rower.2C_Competitive_Rowing-3">[2]</a></sup> There are a number of different boat classes in which athletes compete, ranging from an individual shell (called a <a href="/wiki/Single_scull" title="Single scull">single scull</a>) to an eight-person shell with <a href="/wiki/Coxswain" title="Coxswain">coxswain</a> (called a <a href="/wiki/Coxed_eight" class="mw-redirect" title="Coxed eight">coxed eight</a>).</p>
<p>Modern rowing as a competitive sport can be traced to the early 10th century when races were held between professional <a href="/wiki/Watermen" class="mw-redirect" title="Watermen">watermen</a> on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, United Kingdom. Often prizes were offered by the London Guilds and <a href="/wiki/Livery_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Livery Companies">Livery Companies</a>. <a href="/wiki/Amateurism_in_sport" class="mw-redirect" title="Amateurism in sport">Amateur</a> competition began towards the end of the 18th century with the arrival of "boat clubs" at the British <a href="/wiki/Public_school_(United_Kingdom)" title="Public school (United Kingdom)">public schools</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westminster_School" title="Westminster School">Westminster School</a>. Similarly, clubs were formed at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>, with a race held between <a href="/wiki/Brasenose_College_Boat_Club" title="Brasenose College Boat Club">Brasenose College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jesus_College_Boat_Club_(Oxford)" title="Jesus College Boat Club (Oxford)">Jesus College</a> in 1815. At the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> the first recorded races were in 1827. Public rowing clubs were beginning at the same time; in England <a href="/wiki/Leander_Club" title="Leander Club">Leander Club</a> was founded in 1818, in Germany <a href="/wiki/Der_Hamburger_und_Germania_Ruder_Club" title="Der Hamburger und Germania Ruder Club">Der Hamburger und Germania Ruder Club</a> was founded in 1836 and in the United States Narragansett Boat Club was founded in 1838 and <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Boat_Club" title="Detroit Boat Club">Detroit Boat Club</a> was founded in 1839. In 1843, the first American college rowing club was formed at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Rowing_Federation" title="International Rowing Federation">International Rowing Federation</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Fédération Internationale des Sociétés d'Aviron</i></span>, abbreviated FISA), responsible for international governance of rowing, was founded in 1892 to provide regulation at a time when the sport was gaining popularity. Across six continents, 150 countries now have rowing federations that participate in the sport.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rowing is one of the oldest <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic sports</a>. Though it was on the programme for the 1896 games, racing did not take place due to bad weather,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> but male rowers have competed since the <a href="/wiki/1900_Summer_Olympics" title="1900 Summer Olympics">1900</a>. Women's rowing was added to the Olympic programme in <a href="/wiki/1976_Summer_Olympics" title="1976 Summer Olympics">1976</a>. Today, only fourteen boat classes race at the Olympics: <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[note 2]</a></sup> Each year the <a href="/wiki/World_Rowing_Championships" title="World Rowing Championships">World Rowing Championships</a> is staged by FISA with 22 boat classes raced. In Olympic years only the non-Olympic boat classes are raced at the World Championships. The <a href="/wiki/European_Rowing_Championships" title="European Rowing Championships">European Rowing Championships</a> are held annually, along with three <a href="/wiki/World_Rowing_Cup" title="World Rowing Cup">World Rowing Cups</a> in which each event earns a number of points for a country towards the World Cup title. Since <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Paralympics" title="2008 Summer Paralympics">2008</a>, rowing has also been competed at the <a href="/wiki/Paralympic_Games" title="Paralympic Games">Paralympic Games</a>.</p>
<p>Major domestic competitions take place in dominant rowing nations and include <a href="/wiki/The_Boat_Race" title="The Boat Race">The Boat Race</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henley_Royal_Regatta" title="Henley Royal Regatta">Henley Royal Regatta</a> in the United Kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Rowing_Championships" title="Australian Rowing Championships">Australian Rowing Championships</a> in Australia, the <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Yale_Regatta" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Yale Regatta">Harvard-Yale Regatta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_Charles_Regatta" title="Head of the Charles Regatta">Head of the Charles Regatta</a> in the United States, and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Henley_Regatta" title="Royal Canadian Henley Regatta">Royal Canadian Henley Regatta</a> in Canada. Many other competitions often exist for racing between clubs, schools, and universities in each nation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_information">Basic information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DMURC_mens_8%2B_at_BUCS_Regatta_2010.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/DMURC_mens_8%2B_at_BUCS_Regatta_2010.png/250px-DMURC_mens_8%2B_at_BUCS_Regatta_2010.png" width="250" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/DMURC_mens_8%2B_at_BUCS_Regatta_2010.png/375px-DMURC_mens_8%2B_at_BUCS_Regatta_2010.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/DMURC_mens_8%2B_at_BUCS_Regatta_2010.png/500px-DMURC_mens_8%2B_at_BUCS_Regatta_2010.png 2x" data-file-width="1565" data-file-height="923" /></a>

Rowers in a <a href="/wiki/Eight_(rowing)" title="Eight (rowing)">coxed eight</a> (8+), a <i><a href="/wiki/Sweep_(rowing)" title="Sweep (rowing)">sweep</a></i> rowing boat</div>

<p>While rowing, the athlete sits in the boat facing toward the stern, and uses the <a href="/wiki/Oars" class="mw-redirect" title="Oars">oars</a> which are held in place by the <a href="/wiki/Oarlock" class="mw-redirect" title="Oarlock">oarlocks</a> to propel the boat forward (towards the <a href="/wiki/Bow_(ship)" title="Bow (ship)">bow</a>). This may be done on a canal, river, lake, sea, or other large bodies of water. The sport requires strong core balance, physical strength, flexibility, and cardiovascular endurance.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whilst the action of rowing and equipment used remains fairly consistent throughout the world, there are many different types of competition. These include endurance races, <a href="/wiki/Head_race" title="Head race">time trials</a>, stake racing, <a href="/wiki/Bumps_race" title="Bumps race">bumps racing</a>, and the side-by-side format used in the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_games" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympic games">Olympic games</a>. The many different formats are a result of the long <a href="/wiki/History_of_rowing" class="mw-redirect" title="History of rowing">history of the sport</a>, its development in different regions of the world, and specific local requirements and restrictions.</p>
<p>There are two forms of rowing:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <i><b><a href="/wiki/Sweep_(rowing)" title="Sweep (rowing)">sweep</a></b></i> or <i>sweep-oar</i> rowing, each rower has one oar, held with both hands. This is generally done in pairs, fours, and eights. In some regions of the world, each rower in a sweep boat is referred to either as <i><a href="/wiki/Port_(nautical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Port (nautical)">port</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Starboard" class="mw-redirect" title="Starboard">starboard</a></i>, depending on which side of the boat the rower's oar extends to. In other regions, the port side is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Stroke_(rowing)" title="Stroke (rowing)">stroke side</a>, and the starboard side as <a href="/wiki/Bow_(rowing)" title="Bow (rowing)">bow side</a>; this applies even if the stroke oarsman is rowing on bow side and/or the bow oarsman on stroke side.</li>
<li>In <i><b><a href="/wiki/Sculling" title="Sculling">sculling</a></b></i> each rower has two oars (or <i>sculls</i>), one in each hand. Sculling is usually done without a <a href="/wiki/Coxswain_(rowing)" title="Coxswain (rowing)">coxswain</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Quad_scull" title="Quad scull">quads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Double_scull" title="Double scull">doubles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Single_scull" title="Single scull">singles</a>. The oar in the sculler's right hand extends to port, and the oar in the left hand extends to starboard.</li>
</ul>

<p>The rowing stroke may be characterized by two fundamental reference points. The <i>catch</i>, which is placement of the oar blade in the water, and the <i>extraction</i>, also known as the <i>finish</i> or <i>release</i>, when the rower removes the oar blade from the water. The action between catch and release is the first phase of the stroke that propels the boat.</p>
<p>At the catch the rower places the blade in the water and applies pressure to the oar by pushing the seat toward the bow of the boat by extending the legs, thus pushing the boat through the water. The point of placement of the blade in the water is a relatively fixed point about which the oar serves as a lever to propel the boat. As the rower's legs approach full extension, the rower pivots the torso toward the bow of the boat and then finally pulls the arms towards his or her chest. The hands meet the chest right above the diaphragm.</p>
<p>At the end of the stroke, with the blade still in the water, the hands drop slightly to unload the oar so that spring energy stored in the bend of the oar gets transferred to the boat, which eases removing the oar from the water and minimizes energy wasted on lifting water above the surface (splashing).</p>
<p>The recovery phase follows the drive. The recovery starts with the extraction and involves coordinating the body movements with the goal to move the oar back to the catch position. In extraction, the rower pushes down on the oar handle to quickly lift the blade from the water and rapidly rotates the oar so that the blade is parallel to the water. This process is sometimes referred to as <i>feathering the blade</i>. Simultaneously, the rower pushes the oar handle away from the chest. The blade emerges from the water square and feathers immediately once clear of the water. After feathering and extending the arms, the rower pivots the body forward. Once the hands are past the knees, the rower compresses the legs which moves the seat towards the stern of the boat. The leg compression occurs relatively slowly compared to the rest of the stroke, which affords the rower a moment to recover, and allows the boat to glide through the water. The gliding of the boat through the water during recovery is often called <i>run</i>.</p>
<p>A controlled slide is necessary to maintain momentum and achieve optimal boat run. However, various teaching methods disagree about the optimal relation in timing between drive and recovery. Near the end of the recovery, the rower squares the blade into perpendicular orientation with respect to the water, and begins another stroke.<sup id="cite_ref-Speed_Rower.2C_Competitive_Rowing_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speed_Rower.2C_Competitive_Rowing-3">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breathing_during_a_rowing_stroke">Breathing during a rowing stroke</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breathing during a rowing stroke">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two schools of thought with respect to the appropriate breathing technique during the rowing motion: Full lungs at the catch and empty lungs at the catch.</p>
<p>With the full lung technique, rowers exhale during the stroke and inhale during the recovery. In laboured circumstances, rowers will take a quick pant at the end of the stroke before taking a deep breath on the recovery that fills the lungs by the time the catch is reached.</p>
<p>In the empty-lung technique, rowers inhale during the drive, and exhale during the recovery so that they have empty lungs at the catch. Because the knees come up to the chest when the lungs are empty, this technique allows the rower to reach a little bit further than if the lungs were full of air. Full lungs at the release also can help the rower to maintain a straighter back, a style encouraged by many coaches.</p>
<p>A scientific study of the benefits of entrained breathing technique in relatively fit, but untrained rowers did not show any physiological or psychological benefit to either technique.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rowing_propulsion">Rowing propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rowing propulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rowing is a cyclic (or intermittent) form of propulsion such that in the quasi-steady state the motion of the system (the system comprising the rower, the oars, and the boat), is repeated regularly. In order to maintain the steady-state propulsion of the system without either accelerating or decelerating the system, the sum of all the external forces on the system, averaged over the cycle, must be zero. Thus, the average drag (retarding) force on the system must equal the average propulsion force on the system. The drag forces consist of aerodynamic drag on the superstructure of the system (components of the boat situated above the waterline), as well as the hydrodynamic drag on the submerged portion of the system. The propulsion forces are the forward reaction of the water on the oars while in the water. Note also that the oar can be used to provide a drag force (a force acting against the forward motion) when the system is brought to rest.</p>
<p>Although the oar can be conveniently thought of as a lever with a "fixed" pivot point in the water, the blade moves sideways and sternwards through the water, so that the magnitude of the propulsion force developed is the result of a complex interaction between unsteady fluid mechanics (the water flow around the blade) and solid mechanics and dynamics (the handle force applied to the oar, the oar's inertia and bending characteristic, the acceleration of the boat and so on).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_from_other_watercraft">Distinction from other watercraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distinction from other watercraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Watercraft_rowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Watercraft rowing">Watercraft rowing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rowing_Sport_Oarlock.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Oars are held in an oarlock at the end of outriggers attached to the side of the boat</div>

<p>The distinction between rowing and other forms of water transport, such as <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoeing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kayak" title="Kayak">kayaking</a>, is that in rowing the oars are held in place at a pivot point that is in a fixed position relative to the boat, this point is the load point for the oar to act as a second class <a href="/wiki/Lever#First-class_levers" title="Lever">lever</a> (the blade fixed in the water is the fulcrum). In flatwater rowing, the boat (also called a <i>shell</i> or <i>fine boat</i>) is narrow to avoid <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a>, and the oars are attached to oarlocks at the end of <a href="/wiki/Outriggers" class="mw-redirect" title="Outriggers">outriggers</a> extending from the sides of the boat.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> Racing boats also have sliding seats to allow the use of the legs in addition to the body to apply power to the oar. Racing shells are inherently unstable, much like racing kayaks or canoes. The rowing boats require oars on both sides to prevent them from rolling over.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fitness_and_health">Fitness and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fitness and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rowing is one of the few non-weight bearing sports that <a href="/wiki/Exercise" class="mw-redirect" title="Exercise">exercises</a> all the major muscle groups, including <a href="/wiki/Quadriceps_femoris_muscle" title="Quadriceps femoris muscle">quads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biceps" title="Biceps">biceps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triceps" class="mw-redirect" title="Triceps">triceps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latissimus_dorsi_muscle" title="Latissimus dorsi muscle">lats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gluteal_muscles" title="Gluteal muscles">glutes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_abdomen" class="mw-redirect" title="Human abdomen">abdominal</a> muscles. The sport also improves <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardiovascular">cardiovascular</a> endurance and <a href="/wiki/Muscular_strength" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscular strength">muscular strength</a>. High-performance rowers tend to be tall and muscular: although extra weight does increase the drag on the boat, the larger athletes' increased power tends to be more significant. The increased power is achieved through increased length of leverage on the oar through longer limbs of the athlete. In multi-person boats (2,4, or 8), the lightest person typically rows in the bow seat at the front of the boat.</p>
<p>Rowing is a low impact activity with movement only in defined ranges, so twist and sprain injuries are rare. However, the repetitive rowing action can put strain on <a href="/wiki/Knee_joint" class="mw-redirect" title="Knee joint">knee joints</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Human_vertebral_column" class="mw-redirect" title="Human vertebral column">spine</a> and the tendons of the forearm, and <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> of these are the most common rowing injuries. If one rows with poor technique, especially rowing with a curved rather than straight back, other injuries may surface, including back pains. Blisters occur for almost all rowers, especially in the beginning of one's rowing career, as every stroke puts pressure on the hands, though rowing frequently tends to harden hands and generate protective calluses. Holding the oars too tightly or making adjustments to technique may cause recurring or new blisters, as it is common to feather the blade (previously described). Another common injury is getting "track bites", thin cuts on the back of one's calf or thigh caused by contact with the seat tracks at either end of the stroke.</p>

<p>Ever since the earliest recorded references to rowing, the sporting element has been present. An Egyptian funerary inscription of 1430 BC records that the warrior <a href="/wiki/Amenhotep_II" title="Amenhotep II">Amenhotep</a> (Amenophis) II was also renowned for his feats of oarsmanship. In the <a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> mentions rowing forming part of the funeral games arranged by <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a> in honour of his father.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell.2C_Richard_1997_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell.2C_Richard_1997-11">[9]</a></sup> In the 13th century, <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venetian</a> festivals called <i>regata</i> included boat races among others.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first known "modern" rowing races began from competition among the professional <a href="/wiki/Watermen" class="mw-redirect" title="Watermen">watermen</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> that provided ferry and taxi service on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. Prizes for wager races were often offered by the London <a href="/wiki/Guilds" class="mw-redirect" title="Guilds">Guilds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Livery_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Livery Companies">Livery Companies</a> or wealthy owners of riverside houses.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnell.2C_Richard_1997_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnell.2C_Richard_1997-11">[9]</a></sup> The oldest surviving such race, <a href="/wiki/Doggett%27s_Coat_and_Badge" title="Doggett's Coat and Badge">Doggett's Coat and Badge</a> was first contested in 1715 and is still held annually from <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge" title="London Bridge">London Bridge</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> During the 19th century these races were to become numerous and popular, attracting large crowds. Prize matches amongst professionals similarly became popular on other rivers throughout Great Britain in the 19th century, notably on the <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne" title="River Tyne">Tyne</a>. In America, the earliest known race dates back to 1756 in New York, when a pettiauger defeated a Cape Cod whaleboat in a race.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Amateur competition in England began towards the end of the 18th century. Documentary evidence from this period is sparse, but it is known that the Monarch Boat Club of <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a> and the Isis Club of <a href="/wiki/Westminster_School" title="Westminster School">Westminster School</a> were both in existence in the 1790s. The Star Club and Arrow Club in London for gentlemen amateurs were also in existence before 1800. At the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> bumping races were first organised in 1815 when <a href="/wiki/Brasenose_College_Boat_Club" title="Brasenose College Boat Club">Brasenose College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jesus_College_Boat_Club_(Oxford)" title="Jesus College Boat Club (Oxford)">Jesus College</a> boat clubs had the first annual race<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_rowing_history_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_rowing_history-15">[13]</a></sup> while at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> the first recorded races were in 1827.<sup id="cite_ref-Penn_Rowing_History_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penn_Rowing_History-16">[14]</a></sup> Brasenose beat Jesus to win Oxford University's first Head of the River; the two clubs claim to be the oldest established boat clubs in the world. <a href="/wiki/The_Boat_Race" title="The Boat Race">The Boat Race</a> between Oxford University and Cambridge University first took place in 1829, and was the second intercollegiate sporting event (following the first Varsity Cricket Match by 2 years). The interest in the first Boat Race and subsequent matches led the town of <a href="/wiki/Henley-on-Thames" title="Henley-on-Thames">Henley-on-Thames</a> to begin hosting an <a href="/wiki/Henley_Royal_Regatta" title="Henley Royal Regatta">annual regatta</a> in 1839.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Founded in 1818, <a href="/wiki/Leander_Club" title="Leander Club">Leander Club</a> is the world's oldest public rowing club.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> The second oldest club which still exists is the <a href="/wiki/Der_Hamburger_und_Germania_Ruder_Club" title="Der Hamburger und Germania Ruder Club">Der Hamburger und Germania Ruder Club</a> which was founded 1836 and marked the beginning of rowing as an organized sport in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> During the 19th century, as in England, wager matches in North America between professionals became very popular attracting vast crowds. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.rownbc.org/?id=club-info/index">Narragansett Boat Club</a> was founded in 1838 exclusively for rowing. During an 1837 parade in Providence, R.I, a group of boatmen were pulling a longboat on wheels, which carried the oldest living survivor of the 1772 Gaspee Raid. They boasted to the crowd that they were the fastest rowing crew on the Bay. A group of Providence locals took issue with this and challenged them to race, which the Providence group summarily won. The six-man core of that group went on the following year to found NBC in 1838.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> Detroit Boat Club was founded in 1839 and is the second oldest continuously-operated rowing club in the U.S. In 1843, the first American college rowing club was formed at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Yale_Regatta" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Yale Regatta">Harvard-Yale Regatta</a> is the oldest intercollegiate sporting event in the United States,<a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://rowinghistory.net/Time%20Line/TL%20-1849images.htm">http://rowinghistory.net/Time%20Line/TL%20-1849images.htm</a><sup id="cite_ref-Harvard-yale_reggata_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard-yale_reggata-22">[20]</a></sup> having been contested every year since 1852 (excepting interruptions for wars).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schuylkill_Navy">Schuylkill Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Schuylkill Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_Navy" title="Schuylkill Navy">Schuylkill Navy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

Philadelphia's iconic <a href="/wiki/Boathouse_Row" title="Boathouse Row">Boathouse Row</a>, Home of the Schuylkill Navy</div>

<p>The Schuylkill Navy is an association of amateur rowing clubs of <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>. Founded in 1858, it is the oldest amateur athletic governing body in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Boathouse_Row_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boathouse_Row-23">[21]</a></sup> The member clubs are all on the <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill River</a> where it flows through <a href="/wiki/Fairmount_Park" title="Fairmount Park">Fairmount Park</a> in Philadelphia, mostly on the historic <a href="/wiki/Boathouse_Row" title="Boathouse Row">Boathouse Row</a>.</p>
<p>By charter, the Schuylkill Navy’s object is "to secure united action among the several Clubs and to promote amateurism on the Schuylkill River."<sup id="cite_ref-Schuylkill_Navy_Records_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schuylkill_Navy_Records-24">[22]</a></sup> Over the years, the group has had a role in certain ceremonial and state functions.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> The success of the Schuylkill Navy and similar organizations contributed heavily to the extinction of professional rowing and the sport's current status as an amateur sport.<sup id="cite_ref-Jefferson_1983_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jefferson_1983-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>At its founding, it had nine clubs; today, there are 12: <a href="/wiki/Fairmount_Rowing_Association" title="Fairmount Rowing Association">Fairmount Rowing Association</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crescent_Boat_Club" title="Crescent Boat Club">Crescent Boat Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bachelors_Barge_Club" title="Bachelors Barge Club">Bachelors Barge Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_Barge_Club" title="University Barge Club">University Barge Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta_Boat_Club" title="Malta Boat Club">Malta Boat Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vesper_Boat_Club" title="Vesper Boat Club">Vesper Boat Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/College_Boat_Club" title="College Boat Club">College Boat Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penn_Athletic_Club_Rowing_Association" title="Penn Athletic Club Rowing Association">Penn Athletic Club Rowing Association</a> (Penn AC), <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Girls%27_Rowing_Club" title="Philadelphia Girls' Rowing Club">Philadelphia Girls' Rowing Club</a> (PGRC), <a href="/wiki/Gillin_Boat_Club" title="Gillin Boat Club">Gillin Boat Club</a> and St. Joseph's University and St. Joseph's Prep. At least 23 other clubs have belonged to the Navy at various times.<sup id="cite_ref-Schuylkill_Navy_Records_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schuylkill_Navy_Records-24">[22]</a></sup> Many of the clubs have a rich history, and have produced a large number of Olympians and world-class competitors.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FISA">FISA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: FISA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/International_Rowing_Federation" title="International Rowing Federation">International Rowing Federation</a></div>
<p>The sport's governing body is formally known as the "Fédération Internationale des Sociétés d'Aviron" (English translation: <i>International Federation of Rowing Associations</i>), though, the majority of the time, either the <a href="/wiki/Initialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Initialism">initialism</a> "FISA" or the English co-name, World Rowing, which the organization "uses for 'commercial purposes,'"<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup> is used to refer to it. Founded by representatives from France, Switzerland, Belgium, <a href="/wiki/Adriatica" title="Adriatica">Adriatica</a> (now a part of Italy), and Italy in <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a> on 25 June 1892,<sup id="cite_ref-Virtual_Library_Rowing_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Virtual_Library_Rowing-29">[27]</a></sup> FISA is the oldest international sports federation in the Olympic movement.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>FISA first organized a <a href="/wiki/European_Rowing_Championships" title="European Rowing Championships">European Rowing Championships</a> in 1893.<sup id="cite_ref-Virtual_Library_Rowing_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Virtual_Library_Rowing-29">[27]</a></sup> An annual <a href="/wiki/World_Rowing_Championships" title="World Rowing Championships">World Rowing Championships</a> was introduced in 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard-yale_reggata_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard-yale_reggata-22">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AUS_Rowing_History_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AUS_Rowing_History-31">[29]</a></sup> Rowing has also been conducted at the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> since <a href="/wiki/1900_Summer_Olympics" title="1900 Summer Olympics">1900</a> (cancelled at the first modern Games in <a href="/wiki/1896_Summer_Olympics" title="1896 Summer Olympics">1896</a> due to bad weather).<sup id="cite_ref-Olympic.org_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olympic.org-32">[30]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Racing_shell" title="Racing shell">Racing shell</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Racing_shell" title="Racing shell">Racing boats</a> (often called <i>shells</i>) are long, narrow, and broadly semi-circular in cross-section in order to reduce drag to a minimum. There is some trade off between boat speed and stability in choice of hull shape. They usually have a fin towards the rear, to help prevent roll and yaw and to increase the effectiveness of the rudder.</p>
<p>Originally made from <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, shells are now almost always made from a <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite material</a> (usually a double skin of <a href="/wiki/Carbon-fibre_reinforced_plastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon-fibre reinforced plastic">carbon-fibre reinforced plastic</a> with a sandwich of honeycomb material) for strength and weight advantages. FISA rules specify minimum weights for each class of boat so that no individual team will gain a great advantage from the use of expensive materials or technology.</p>
<p>There are several different types of boats. They are classified using:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Number of rowers</b>. In all forms of modern competition the number is either 1, 2, 4, or 8.</li>
<li><b>Position of <a href="/wiki/Coxswain_(rowing)" title="Coxswain (rowing)">coxswain</a></b> (also referred to as cox). Boats are either coxless (<i>straight</i>), bow-coxed (also called <a href="/wiki/Bowloader" title="Bowloader">bowloaders</a>), or stern-coxed.</li>
</ul>
<p>Although sculling and sweep boats are generally identical to each other (except having different <a href="/wiki/Outrigger" title="Outrigger">riggers</a>), they are referred to using different names:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sweep: <a href="/wiki/Coxless_pair" title="Coxless pair">coxless pair</a> (or straight pair) (2-), <a href="/wiki/Coxed_pair" title="Coxed pair">coxed pair</a> (2+), <a href="/wiki/Coxless_four" title="Coxless four">Coxless four</a> (or straight four) (4-), <a href="/wiki/Coxed_four" title="Coxed four">coxed four</a> (4+), <a href="/wiki/Eight_(rowing)" title="Eight (rowing)">eight</a> (8+) (always coxed)</li>
<li>Sculling: <a href="/wiki/Single_scull" title="Single scull">single scull</a> (1x), <a href="/wiki/Double_scull" title="Double scull">double scull</a> (2x), triple scull (3x) (very rare), <a href="/wiki/Quad_scull" title="Quad scull">quad (or quadruple) scull</a> (4x), <a href="/wiki/Octuple_scull" title="Octuple scull">octuple scull</a> (8x) (always coxed, and mainly for juniors and exhibition)</li>
<li>Sweep/Sculling: Queep, 2 scullers and 2 sweepers (very rare) Randan, 1 sculler and 2 sweepers (very rare)</li>
</ul>
<p>With the smaller boats, specialist versions of the shells for sculling can be made lighter. The riggers in sculling apply the forces symmetrically to each side of the boat, whereas in sweep oared racing these forces are staggered alternately along the boat. The sweep oared boat has to be stiffer to handle these unmatched forces, so consequently requires more bracing and is usually heavier – a pair (2-) is usually a more robust boat than a double scull (2x) for example, and being heavier is also slower when used as a double scull. In theory this could also apply to the 4x and 8x, but most rowing clubs cannot afford to have a dedicated large hull which might be rarely used and instead generally opt for versatility in their fleet by using stronger shells which can be rigged for either sweep rowing or sculling. The symmetrical forces also make sculling more efficient than rowing: the double scull is faster than the coxless pair, and the quadruple scull is faster than the coxless four.</p>
<p>One additional boat is the <i>queep</i>, a coxed or non-coxed shell. The bow and stroke positions have a set of sculling riggers and two and three have a sweep set. These shells have been used in the UK and recently at a club in Victoria BC, Canada. In addition to the queep the <i>trop</i> and the <i>coxed trop</i> are become more mainstream. They are mainly rowed in central Canada. The trop shell consists of three people where the bow has a pair of sculling oars, and 2,3 each a sweeping oar. A coxed trop is the same configuration as the trop plus a coxed seated at the stern of the boat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many adjustments can be made to the equipment to accommodate the physiques of the crew. Collectively these adjustments are known as the boat's <a href="/wiki/Boat_rigging_(sport_rowing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boat rigging (sport rowing)">rigging</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steering">Steering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Steering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Single, double, and quad sculls are usually steered by the scullers pulling harder on one side or the other. In other boats, there is a <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudder</a>, controlled by the coxswain, if present, or by one of the crew. In the latter case, the rudder cable is attached to the toe of one of his shoes which can pivot about the ball of the foot, moving the cable left or right. The bowman may steer since he has the best vision when looking over his shoulder. On straighter courses, the strokesman may steer, since he can point the stern of the boat at some landmark at the start of the course. On international courses, landmarks for the steersmen, consisting of two aligned poles, may be provided.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blades">Blades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Blades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oar_(sport_rowing)" title="Oar (sport rowing)">Oar (sport rowing)</a></div>
<p>Blades, otherwise known as oars to amateurs or non rowers, are used to propel the boat. They are long (sculling: 250–300&#160;cm; rowing 340–360&#160;cm) poles with one flat end about 50&#160;cm long and 25&#160;cm wide, called the blade. Classic blades were made out of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, but modern blades are made from more expensive and durable <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">synthetic</a> material, the most common being <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a>.</p>
<p>An 'oar' is often referred to as a <i>blade</i> in the case of sweep oar rowing and as a <i>scull</i> in the case of sculling. A sculling oar is shorter and has a smaller blade area than the equivalent sweep oar. The combined blade area of a pair of sculls is however greater than that of a single sweep oar, so the oarsman when sculling is working against more water than when rowing sweep-oared. He is able to do this because the body action in sculling is more anatomically efficient (due to the symmetry).</p>
<p>The <i>spoon</i> of oars is normally painted with the colours of the club to which they belong. This greatly simplifies identification of boats at a distance. As many sports teams have logos printed on their jerseys, rowing clubs have specifically painted blades that each team is associated with.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indoor_rowing">Indoor rowing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Indoor rowing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Indoor rowing (on ergometer, or tank) is a way to train technique and strength by going through the same motions as rowing, with resistance. Indoor rowing is helpful when there are no rowable bodies of water near by, or weather conditions don't permit rowing.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rowing_tank">Rowing tank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Rowing tank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rowing_tank" title="Rowing tank">rowing tank</a></div>
<p>A rowing tank is an indoor facility which attempts to mimic the conditions rowers face on open water. Rowing tanks are primarily used for off-season rowing, muscle specific conditioning and technique training, or simply when bad weather doesn't allow for open water training.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ergometer">Ergometer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ergometer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p><a href="/wiki/Indoor_rower" title="Indoor rower">Ergometer</a> rowing machines (colloquially <i>ergs</i> or <i>ergo</i>) simulate the rowing action and provide a means of training on land when waterborne training is restricted, and of measuring rowing fitness. Ergometers do not simulate the lateral balance challenges, the exact resistance of water, or the exact motions of true rowing including the sweep of the oar handles. For that reason ergometer scores are generally not used as the sole selection criterion for crews (colloquially <i>"ergs don't float"</i>), and technique training is limited to the basic body position and movements. However, this action can still allow a comparable workout to those experienced on the water.</p>
<p>Sometimes, slides are placed underneath the erg to try to simulate the movement of being on the water. It allows the machine to move back and forth smoothly as if there is water beneath you. The slides can be connected in rows or columns so that rowers are forced to move together on the ergometer, similar to how they would match up their rhythm in a boat.</p>
<p>Indoor rowing has become popular as a sport in its own right with numerous indoor competitions (and the annual World Championship <a href="/wiki/CRASH-B_Sprints" title="CRASH-B Sprints">CRASH-B Sprints</a> in Boston) during the winter off-season.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Perhaps one of the most common brand of ergometers is Concept2.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> The company offers multiple types of models , including the Model D, Model E, and the dynamic rower. An updated <a href="/wiki/Rowperfect" class="mw-redirect" title="Rowperfect">Rowperfect</a> brand of dynamic rowers, RP3, produces ergometers that more naturally mimic the feel and resistance of rowing in a shell on the water. It additionally, shows a dynamic force curve of power that provides the rower with detailed information about their stroke which they can use to improve technique and get stronger.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Damage">Damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most commonly damaged piece of rowing equipment is the skeg, which is a metal or plastic fin that comes out of the bottom of the boat to help maintain stability, and to assist in steering. Since the skeg sticks out below the hull of the boat it is the most vulnerable to damage, however it is relatively easy to replace skegs by gluing a new one on. Hull damage is also a significant concern both for maintaining equipment, and for rower safety. Hull damage can be caused by submerged logs, poor strapping to trailers, and collisions with other boats, docks, rocks, etc.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Boats are conveyed to competitions on special trailers accommodating up to 20 boats.</p>
<h2><span id="Boat_storage,_boat_houses,_and_boat_centers"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Boat_storage.2C_boat_houses.2C_and_boat_centers">Boat storage, boat houses, and boat centers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Boat storage, boat houses, and boat centers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Racing boats are stored in <a href="/wiki/Boat_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Boat house">boat houses</a>. These are specially designed storage areas which usually consist of a long two-story building with a large door at one end which leads out to a pontoon or slipway on the river or lakeside. The boats are stored on racks (horizontal bars, usually metal) on the ground floor. Oars, riggers, and other equipment is stored around the boats. Boat houses are typically associated with <a href="/wiki/Rowing_club" title="Rowing club">rowing clubs</a> and include some social facilities on the upper floor: a cafe, bar, or gym.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rowers may take part in the sport for their leisure or they may row competitively. There are different types of competition in the sport of rowing. In the U.S. all types of races are referred to as <i>regattas</i> whereas this term is only used in the UK for head-to-head or multi-lane races (such as those that take place at <a href="/wiki/Dorney_Lake" title="Dorney Lake">Dorney Lake</a>), which generally take place in the summer season. Time trials occur in the UK during the winter, and are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Head_race" title="Head race">Head races</a>. In the US, head races (usually about 5k, depending on the body of water) are rowed in the fall, while 2k sprint races are rowed in the spring and summer.</p>
<p>Rowing is unusual in the demands it places on competitors. The standard <a href="/wiki/Rowing_World_Championships" class="mw-redirect" title="Rowing World Championships">world championship</a> race distance of 2,000&#160;metres is long enough to have a large endurance element, but short enough (typically 5.5 to 7.5&#160;minutes) to feel like a sprint. This means that rowers have some of the highest power outputs of athletes in any sport.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> At the same time the motion involved in the sport compresses the rowers' lungs, limiting the amount of oxygen available to them. This requires rowers to tailor their breathing to the stroke, typically inhaling and exhaling twice per stroke, unlike most other sports such as cycling where competitors can breathe freely.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Side_by_side">Side by side</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Side by side">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most races that are held in the spring and summer feature <a href="/wiki/Regatta" title="Regatta">side by side</a> racing, or sprint racing, sometimes called a regatta; all the boats start at the same time from a stationary position and the winner is the boat that crosses the finish line first. The number of boats in a race typically varies between two (which is sometimes referred to as a <i>dual race</i>) to six, but any number of boats can start together if the course is wide enough.</p>

<p>The standard length races for the Olympics and the <a href="/wiki/World_Rowing_Championships" title="World Rowing Championships">World Rowing Championships</a> is 2 kilometres (1.24&#160;mi) long, 1.5 kilometres (0.93&#160;mi) - 2 kilometres (1.24&#160;mi) for US high school races on the east coast and 1,000&#160;m for <i>masters</i> rowers (rowers older than 27). However the race distance can and does vary from <i>dashes</i> or sprints, which may be 500 metres (1,640&#160;ft) long, to races of marathon or ultra-marathon length races such as the <a href="/wiki/Tour_du_Lac" title="Tour du Lac">Tour du Léman</a> in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> which is 160 kilometres (99&#160;mi),<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup> and the 2&#160;day, 185-kilometre (115&#160;mi) Corvallis to Portland Regatta<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> held in Oregon, USA. In the UK, regattas are generally between 500 metres (1,640&#160;ft) and 2 kilometres (1.24&#160;mi) long.</p>
<p>A feature of the end of twentieth century rowing was the development of non-olympic multicrew racing boats, typically fixed seat-gigs, pilot boats and in Finland church- or longboats. The most usual craft in races held around the coasts of Britain during summer months is the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_pilot_gig" title="Cornish pilot gig">Cornish pilot gig</a>, most typically in the south-west, with crews of 6 from local towns and races of varying distances. The <a href="/wiki/Cornish_pilot_gig" title="Cornish pilot gig">Cornish pilot gig</a> was designed and built to ferry harbour and river pilots to and from ships in fierce coastal waters. The boat needed to be stable and fast with the large crew hence making it ideal for its modern racing usage. In Finland 14-oared churchboats race throughout the summer months, usually on lakes, and often with mixed crews. The largest gathering sees over 7000 rowers mainly rowing the 60 kilometres (37&#160;mi) course at Sulkava<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup> near the eastern border over a long weekend in mid July. The weekend features the World Masters churchboat event which also includes a 2 kilometres (1.24&#160;mi) dash.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two traditional non-standard distance shell races are the annual <a href="/wiki/Boat_Race" class="mw-redirect" title="Boat Race">Boat Race</a> between <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Yale_Boat_Race" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Yale Boat Race">Harvard-Yale Boat Race</a> which cover courses of approximately 4 miles (6.44&#160;km). The <a href="/wiki/Henley_Royal_Regatta" title="Henley Royal Regatta">Henley Royal Regatta</a> is also raced upon a non-standard distance at 2,112&#160;meters (1&#160;mile, 550&#160;yards).</p>
<p>In general, multi-boat competitions are organized in a series of rounds, with the fastest boats in each heat qualifying for the next round. The losing boats from each heat may be given a second chance to qualify through a <a href="/wiki/Repechage" title="Repechage">repechage</a>. The World Rowing Championships offers multi-lane racing in heats, finals and repechages. At Henley Royal Regatta two crews compete side by side in each round, in a straightforward <a href="/wiki/Single-elimination_tournament" title="Single-elimination tournament">knock-out format</a>, with no repechages.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Head_races">Head races</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Head races">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Head_race" title="Head race">Head race</a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Head_race" title="Head race">Head races</a></b> are <a href="/wiki/Time_trial" title="Time trial">time trial</a> / processional races that take place from autumn (fall) to early spring (depending on local conditions). Boats begin with a rolling start at <a href="/wiki/Interval_(time)" class="mw-redirect" title="Interval (time)">intervals</a> of 10 – 20&#160;seconds, and are timed over a set distance. Head courses usually vary in length from 2,000 metres (1.24&#160;mi) to 12,000 metres (7.46&#160;mi), though there are longer races such as the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Rowing_Marathon" title="Boston Rowing Marathon">Boston Rowing Marathon</a> and shorter such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pairs_Head&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pairs Head (page does not exist)">Pairs Head</a>.</p>
<p>The oldest, and arguably most famous, head race is the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_River_Race" title="Head of the River Race">Head of the River Race</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Fairbairn" title="Steve Fairbairn">Steve Fairbairn</a> in 1926 which takes place each March on the river <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">Thames</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, United Kingdom. Head racing was exported to the United States in the 1950s, and the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_Charles" class="mw-redirect" title="Head of the Charles">Head of the Charles Regatta</a> held each October on the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, United States is now the largest rowing event in the world. The Head of the Charles, along with the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_Schuylkill_Regatta" title="Head of the Schuylkill Regatta">Head of the Schuylkill</a> in Philadelphia and the Head of the Connecticut, are considered to be the three “fall classics.”<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>These processional races are known as <i>Head Races</i>, because, as with bumps racing, the fastest crew is awarded the title <i>Head of the River</i> (as in "head of the class"). It was not deemed feasible to run bumps racing on the Tideway, so a timed format was adopted and soon caught on.</p>
<p>Time trials are sometimes used to determine who competes in an event where there is a limited number of entries, for example the qualifying races for Henley Royal Regatta, and <i>rowing on</i> and <i>getting on</i> for the Oxford and Cambridge <a href="/wiki/Bumps_race" title="Bumps race">Bumps races</a> respectively.</p>

<p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Bumps_race" title="Bumps race">bumps race</a></b> is a multi-day race beginning with crews lined up along the river at set intervals. They start simultaneously and all pursue the boat ahead while avoiding being bumped by a boat from behind. If a crew overtakes or makes physical contact with the crew ahead, a <i>bump</i> is awarded. As a result, damage to boats and equipment is common during bumps racing. To avoid damage the cox of the crew being bumped may concede the bump before contact is actually made. The next day, the bumping crew will start ahead of any crews that have been bumped. The positions at the end of the last race are used to set the positions on the first day of the races the next year. Oxford and Cambridge Universities hold bumps races for their respective colleges twice a year, and there are also <i>Town Bumps</i> races in both cities, open to non-university crews. Oxford's races are organised by City of Oxford Rowing Club<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[39]</a></sup> and Cambridge's are organised by the <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_Rowing_Association" title="Cambridgeshire Rowing Association">Cambridgeshire Rowing Association</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stake_races">Stake races</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Stake races">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stake format was often used in early American races. Competitors line up at the start, race to a stake, moored boat, or buoy some distance away, and return. The 180° turn requires mastery of steering. These races are popular with spectators because one may watch both the start and finish. Usually only two boats would race at once to avoid collision. The Green Mountain Head Regatta continues to use the stake format but it is run as a head race with an interval start.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup> A similar type of racing is found in UK and Irish coastal rowing, where a number of boats race out to a given point from the coast and then return fighting rough water all the way. In Irish coastal rowing the boats are in individual lanes with the races consisting of up to 3 turns to make the race distance 2.3&#160;km.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> are held every four years, where only select boat classes are raced (14 in total):</p>
<ul>

<p>At the end of each year, the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_Rowing_Associations" class="mw-redirect" title="International Federation of Rowing Associations">FISA</a> holds the World Rowing Championships with events in 22 different boat classes. Athletes generally consider the Olympic classes to be premier events .<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During Olympic years only non-Olympic boats compete at the World Championships.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules_of_racing">Rules of racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Rules of racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many differing sets of rules governing racing, and these are generally defined by the governing body of the sport in a particular country—e.g.,&#160;<a href="/wiki/British_Rowing" title="British Rowing">British Rowing</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rowing_Australia" title="Rowing Australia">Rowing Australia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/USRowing" title="USRowing">USRowing</a> in the United States. In international competitions, the rules are set out by the world governing body, the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_des_Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9s_d%27Aviron" class="mw-redirect" title="Fédération Internationale des Sociétés d'Aviron">Fédération Internationale des Sociétés d'Aviron</a> (FISA). The rules are mostly similar but do vary; for example, British Rowing requires <a href="/wiki/Coxswain" title="Coxswain">coxswains</a> to wear buoyancy aids at all times, whereas FISA rules do not.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rowing_crew">Rowing crew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Rowing crew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boat_positions">Boat positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Boat positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boat_positions_(sport_rowing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boat positions (sport rowing)">Boat positions (sport rowing)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BoatPositions.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The boat positions within an 8+ rowing shell</div>

<p>In all boats, with the exception of single sculls, each rower is numbered in sequential order, low numbers at the bow, up to the highest at the stern. The person seated on the first seat is called the bowman, or just 'bow', whilst the rower closest to the stern is called the 'strokeman' or just 'stroke'. There are some exceptions to this – some UK coastal rowers, and in France, Spain, and Italy rowers number from stern to bow.</p>
<p>In addition to this, certain crew members have other titles and roles. In an 8+ the stern pair are responsible for setting the stroke rate and rhythm for the rest of the boat to follow. The middle four (sometimes called the "engine room" or "power house") are usually the less technical, but more powerful rowers in the crew, whilst the bow pair are the more technical and generally regarded as the pair to set up the balance of the boat. They also have most influence on the line the boat steers.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coxswain">Coxswain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Coxswain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coxswain_(rowing)" title="Coxswain (rowing)">Coxswain (rowing)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Coxswain_(rowing)" title="Coxswain (rowing)">coxswain</a> (or simply the cox) is the member who sits in the boat facing the bow, steers the boat, and coordinates the power and rhythm of the rowers - by communicating to the crew through a device called a <a href="/wiki/Cox_box" title="Cox box">cox box</a> and speakers. They usually sit in the stern of the boat, except in <a href="/wiki/Bowloader" title="Bowloader">bowloaders</a> where the coxswain lies in the bow. Bowloader are usually seen as the <a href="/wiki/Coxed_four" title="Coxed four">coxed four</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coxed_pair" title="Coxed pair">coxed pair</a> type of boat.</p>
<p>It is an advantage for the coxswain to be light, as this requires less effort for the crew to propel the boat. In many competitive events there is a minimum weight set for the coxswain to prevent unfair advantage.</p>
<ul>
<li>International (FISA) - 55 kilograms (121.25&#160;lb) (Men's, U23 men's, junior men's boats), 50 kilograms (110.23&#160;lb) (Women's, U23 women's, junior women's and mixed boats)<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>UK (<a href="/wiki/British_Rowing" title="British Rowing">British Rowing</a>) - 55 kilograms (121.25&#160;lb) (Open and mixed boats), 50 kilograms (110.23&#160;lb) (Women's boats), 45 kilograms (99.21&#160;lb) (J15 and younger boats)<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>US (<a href="/wiki/USRowing" title="USRowing">USRowing</a>) - 120 pounds (54&#160;kg) (Men's boats), 110 pounds (50&#160;kg) (Women's boats)<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>If a coxswain is under the minimum weight allowance (underweight) they may have to carry weights in the boat such as sandbags.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weight_classes">Weight classes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Weight classes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In most levels of rowing there are different weight classes – typically "open" (or referred to as "heavyweight") and lightweight. Competitive rowing favours tall, muscular athletes due to the additional leverage height provides in pulling the oar through the water as well as the explosive power needed to propel the boat at high speed.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavyweight">Heavyweight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Heavyweight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Heavyweight rowers of both sexes tend to be very tall, broad-shouldered, have long arms and legs as well as tremendous cardiovascular capacity and very low body fat ratios. Olympic or International level heavyweight male oarsmen are typically anywhere between 190&#160;cm and 206&#160;cm (6'3" to 6'9") tall with most being around 198&#160;cm (6'6") and weighing approximately 102&#160;kg (225&#160;lb) with about 6 to 7% body fat.</p>
<p>Heavyweight women are slightly shorter at around 186&#160;cm (6'1") and lighter than their male counterparts.</p>
<p>Some rowing enthusiasts claim that the disproportionate number of tall rowers is simply due to the unfair advantage that tall rowers have on the ergometer. This is due to the ergometer's inability to properly simulate the larger rowers drag on a boat due to weight. Since the ergometer is used to assess potential rowers, results on the ergometer machine play a large role in a rower's career success. Thus, many erg scores are weight-adjusted, as heavyweights typically find it easier to get better erg scores. Also, since crew selection has favored tall rowers long before the advent of the ergometer,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[45]</a></sup> and bigger, taller crews are almost universally faster than smaller, shorter crews on the water, being tall is a definite advantage ultimately having little to do with the ergometer.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lightweight">Lightweight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Lightweight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lightweight_rowing" title="Lightweight rowing">Lightweight rowing</a></div>
<p>Unlike most other non-combat sports, rowing has a special weight category called <i>lightweight</i> (Lwt for short). According to FISA, this weight category was introduced "to encourage more universality in the sport especially among nations with less statuesque people". The first lightweight events were held at the World Championships in <a href="/wiki/1974_World_Rowing_Championships" title="1974 World Rowing Championships">1974</a> for men and <a href="/wiki/1985_World_Rowing_Championships" title="1985 World Rowing Championships">1985</a> for women. Lightweight rowing was added to the Olympics in <a href="/wiki/1996_Summer_Olympics" title="1996 Summer Olympics">1996</a>.</p>
<p>At international level the limits are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Men: Crew average 70&#160;kg (154&#160;lb) – no rower over 72.5&#160;kg (160&#160;lb)</li>
<li>Women: Crew average 57&#160;kilograms (125&#160;lb) – no rower over 59&#160;kg (130&#160;lb)</li>
</ul>
<p>The Olympic lightweight boat classes are limited to; Men's double (LM2x), Women's double (LW2x).</p>
<p>At the junior level (in the United States), regattas require each rower to weigh in at least two hours before their race; they are sometimes given two chances to make weight at smaller regattas, with the exception of older more prestigious regattas, which allow only one opportunity to make weight. For juniors in the United States, the lightweight cutoff for men is 150.0&#160;lb.; for women, it is 130.0&#160;lb. In the fall the weight limits are increased for women, with the cutoff being 135&#160;lb.</p>
<p>At the collegiate level (in the United States), the lightweight weight requirements can be different depending on competitive season. For fall regattas (typically head races), the lightweight cutoff for men is 165.0&#160;lb. and 135.0&#160;lb. for women. In the spring season (typically sprint races), the lightweight cutoff for men is 160.0&#160;lb., with a boat average of 155.0&#160;lb. for the crew; for women, the lightweight cutoff is 130.0&#160;lb.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women">Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rowing" title="Women's rowing">Women's rowing</a></div>
<p>Women row in all boat classes, from single scull to coxed eights, across the same age ranges and standards as men, from junior amateur through university-level to elite athlete.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[48]</a></sup> Typically men and women compete in separate crews although mixed crews and mixed team events also take place.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[49]</a></sup> Coaching for women is similar to that for men.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[50]</a></sup> The world's first women's rowing team was formed in 1896 at the <a href="/wiki/Furnivall_Sculling_Club" title="Furnivall Sculling Club">Furnivall Sculling Club</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first international women's races were the 1954 <a href="/wiki/European_Rowing_Championships" title="European Rowing Championships">European Rowing Championships</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WR2_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WR2-54">[52]</a></sup> The introduction of women's <a href="/wiki/Rowing_at_the_1976_Summer_Olympics" title="Rowing at the 1976 Summer Olympics">rowing at the 1976 Summer Olympics</a> in Montreal increased the growth of women's rowing because it created the incentive for national rowing federations to support women's events. <a href="/wiki/Rowing_at_the_2012_Summer_Olympics" title="Rowing at the 2012 Summer Olympics">Rowing at the 2012 Summer Olympics</a> in London included six events for women compared with eight for men.<sup id="cite_ref-WRfeature1_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WRfeature1-55">[53]</a></sup> In the US, rowing is an NCAA sport for women but not for men;<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[54]</a></sup> though it is one of the country's oldest collegiate sports, the difference is in large part due to the requirements of <a href="/wiki/Title_IX" title="Title IX">Title IX</a>.</p>
<p>At the international level, women's rowing traditionally has been dominated by Eastern European countries, such as Romania, Russia, and Bulgaria, although other countries such as Germany, Canada, the Netherlands, Great Britain and New Zealand often field competitive teams.<sup id="cite_ref-WRfeature1_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WRfeature1-55">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[55]</a></sup> The United States also has had very competitive crews, and in recent years these crews have become even more competitive given the surge in women's <a href="/wiki/College_rowing_(United_States)" title="College rowing (United States)">collegiate rowing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[56]</a></sup></p>

<p>Adaptive rowing is a special category of races for those with physical disabilities. Under FISA rules there are 5 boat classes for adaptive rowers; mixed (2 men and 2 women plus cox) LTA (Legs, Trunk, Arms), mixed intellectual disability (2 men and 2 women plus cox) LTA (Legs, Trunk, Arms), mixed (1 man and 1 woman) TA (Trunk and Arms), and men's and women's AS (Arms and Shoulders). Events are held at the World Rowing Championships and were also held at the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Paralympics" title="2008 Summer Paralympics">2008 Summer Paralympics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology_and_event_nomenclature">Terminology and event nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rowing_(sport)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Terminology and event nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_rowing_terms" title="Glossary of rowing terms">Glossary of rowing terms</a></div>
<p>Rowing events use a systematic <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nomenclature" class="extiw" title="wikt:nomenclature">nomenclature</a> for the naming of events, so that age, gender, ability and size of boat can all be expressed in a few numbers and letters. The first letter to be used is 'L' or 'Lt' for lightweight. If absent then the crew is open weight. This can be followed by either a 'J' or 'B' to signify junior (under 19 years) or under 23 years respectively. If absent the crew is open age (the letter 'O' is sometimes used). Next is either an 'M' or 'W' to signify if the crew are men or women. Then there is a number to show how many athletes are in the boat (1,2,4 or 8). An 'x' following the number indicates a sculling boat. Finally either a + or – is added to indicate whether the boat is coxed or coxswainless.</p>
<p>Some events will use an experience rating to separate races. In the UK boats are classed as "Elite", "Senior", "Intermediate 1/2/3" or "Novice", depending on the number of wins the athletes have accumulated. Masters events use age ranges to separate crews of older rowers.</p>
<p>Examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>M8+ or 8+ <i>men's eight</i> (Always coxed. Sometimes written as 8o for "8-oared".)</li>
<li>W4- <i>women's coxless four (or "straight four")</i></li>
<li>LM2- <i>lightweight men's coxless pair</i></li>
<li>BM1x <i>men's single sculls under age 23</i></li>
<li>JW4x <i>junior women's quad</i></li>
<li>Masters WC2x <i>masters women's double sculls with average crew age between 43–50</i></li>
<li>Mixed Masters 8+ <i>coxed eight with 4 women and 4 men as rowers and a coxswain of either gender</i></li>
</ul>

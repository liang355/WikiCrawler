<p><b>Münster</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈmʏnstɐ]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-M%C3%BCnster.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/35/De-M%C3%BCnster.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Münster.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a>: <i>Mönster</i>; <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>Monasterium</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> μοναστήριον <i>monastērion</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a>") is an <a href="/wiki/Independent_city" title="Independent city">independent city</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is in the northern part of the state and is considered to be the cultural centre of the <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalia</a> region. It is also capital of the local government region <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_(region)" title="Münster (region)">Münsterland</a>. Münster was the location of the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Rebellion" title="Münster Rebellion">Anabaptist rebellion</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> and the site of the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Westphalia" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Westphalia">Treaty of Westphalia</a> ending the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> in 1648. Today it is known as the bicycle capital of Germany.</p>
<p>Münster gained the status of a <i>Großstadt</i> (major city) with more than 100,000 inhabitants in 1915.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster1900-1945_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster1900-1945-2">[2]</a></sup> Currently there are 300,000<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> people living in the city, with about 55,500 students, only some of whom are recorded in the official population statistics as having their <a href="/wiki/Primary_residence" title="Primary residence">primary residence</a> in Münster.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="History of Münster">History of Münster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="Timeline of Münster">Timeline of Münster</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 793, <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> sent out <a href="/wiki/Ludger" title="Ludger">Ludger</a> as a missionary to evangelise the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_(region)" title="Münster (region)">Münsterland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup> In 797, Ludger founded a school that later became the Cathedral School.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_Paulinum" title="Gymnasium Paulinum">Gymnasium Paulinum</a> traces its history back to this school.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup> Ludger was ordained as the first <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_M%C3%BCnster" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Münster">bishop of Münster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup> The first cathedral was completed by 850.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup> The combination of <a href="/wiki/Ford_(crossing)" title="Ford (crossing)">ford</a> and crossroad, market place, episcopal administrative centre, library and school, established Münster as an important centre.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1040, <a href="/wiki/Henry_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry III, Holy Roman Emperor">Heinrich III</a> became the first king of Germany to visit Münster.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_and_early_modern_period">Middle Ages and early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle Ages and early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="Prince-Bishopric of Münster">Prince-Bishopric of Münster</a> was a leading member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; overflow:hidden; width:auto; max-width:1008px;">

<p>In 1534, the <a href="/wiki/Anabaptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabaptist">Anabaptists</a> led by <a href="/wiki/John_of_Leiden" title="John of Leiden">John of Leiden</a>, took power in the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Rebellion" title="Münster Rebellion">Münster Rebellion</a> and founded a democratic proto-socialistic state. They claimed all property, burned all books except the Bible, and called it the "New Jerusalem". John of Leiden believed he would lead the elect from Münster to capture the entire world and purify it of evil with the sword in preparation for the <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Coming of Christ">Second Coming of Christ</a> and the beginning of the Millennium. They went so far as to require all citizens to be naked as preparation for the Second Coming. However, the town was recaptured in 1535; the Anabaptists were tortured to death, their corpses were exhibited in metal baskets (often confused with cages), which can still be seen hanging from the Tower of <a href="/wiki/Lambert_of_Maastricht" title="Lambert of Maastricht">St. Lambert</a>'s steeple.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Part of the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> of 1648 was held in Münster.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnsterhistory_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnsterhistory-6">[6]</a></sup> This ended the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years' War">Eighty Years' War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnsterhistory_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnsterhistory-6">[6]</a></sup> It also guaranteed the future of the prince-bishop and the diocese; the area was to be exclusively <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="18th,_19th_and_early_20th_centuries"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="18th.2C_19th_and_early_20th_centuries">18th, 19th and early 20th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 18th, 19th and early 20th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The last outstanding palace of the German baroque period was created according to plans by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Conrad_Schlaun" title="Johann Conrad Schlaun">Johann Conrad Schlaun</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnster793-1800_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnster793-1800-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/University_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="University of Münster">University of Münster</a> (today called "Westphalian Wilhelms-University", WWU) was established in 1780. Now a major European centre for excellence in education and research with large faculties in the arts, humanities, theology, sciences, business and law. Currently there are about 40,000 undergraduate and postgraduate students enrolled. In 1802 Münster was conquered by <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>. It was also part of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Berg" title="Grand Duchy of Berg">Grand Duchy of Berg</a> between 1806 and 1811 and the <a href="/wiki/Lippe_(department)" title="Lippe (department)">Lippe</a> department of the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">First French Empire</a> between 1811 and 1813, before returning to Prussian rule. It became the capital of the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Westphalia" title="Province of Westphalia">province of Westphalia</a>. A century later in 1899 the city's harbour started operations when the city was linked to the <a href="/wiki/Dortmund-Ems_Canal" class="mw-redirect" title="Dortmund-Ems Canal">Dortmund-Ems Canal</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the 1940s The Bishop of Münster, Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Clemens_August_Graf_von_Galen" title="Clemens August Graf von Galen">Clemens August Graf von Galen</a>, was one of the most prominent critics of the Nazi government. In retaliation for his success (<i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> described Bishop von Galen as "the most obstinate opponent of the National Socialist anti-Christian program"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>), Münster was heavily garrisoned during World War II, and five large complexes of barracks are still a feature of the city. Münster was the headquarters (Hauptsitz) for the 6th Military District (Wehrkreis) of the German Wehrmacht, under the command of Infantry General (General der Infanterie) <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Glokke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gerhard Glokke (page does not exist)">Gerhard Glokke</a>. Originally made up of <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">Battle of France</a> it was expanded to include the <a href="/wiki/Eupen" title="Eupen">Eupen</a> - <a href="/wiki/Malmedy" title="Malmedy">Malmedy</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>. The headquarters controlled military operations in Münster, <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wuppertal" title="Wuppertal">Wuppertal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld" title="Bielefeld">Bielefeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coesfeld" title="Coesfeld">Coesfeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paderborn" title="Paderborn">Paderborn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herford" title="Herford">Herford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minden" title="Minden">Minden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Detmold" title="Detmold">Detmold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lingen" class="mw-redirect" title="Lingen">Lingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="Osnabrück">Osnabrück</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recklinghausen" title="Recklinghausen">Recklinghausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gelsenkirchen" title="Gelsenkirchen">Gelsenkirchen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>. Münster was the home station for the <a href="/wiki/VI_Corps_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="VI Corps (Germany)">VI</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=XXIII_Corps_(Germany)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="XXIII Corps (Germany) (page does not exist)">XXIII Infantry Corps</a> (Armeekorps), as well as the XXXIII and LVI <a href="/wiki/Panzerkorps" class="mw-redirect" title="Panzerkorps">Panzerkorps</a>. Münster was also the home of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Light_Division_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="1st Light Division (Germany)">6th</a>, <a href="/wiki/16th_Infantry_Division_(Wehrmacht)" <p>A secondary target of the <a href="/wiki/Oil_Campaign_of_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil Campaign of World War II">Oil Campaign of World War II</a>, Münster was bombed on October 25, 1944 by 34 diverted <a href="/wiki/B-24_Liberator" class="mw-redirect" title="B-24 Liberator">B-24 Liberator</a> bombers, during a mission to a nearby primary target, the <a href="/wiki/Scholven_Power_Station" title="Scholven Power Station">Scholven</a>/<a href="/wiki/Buer,_Germany" title="Buer, Germany">Buer</a> synthetic oil plant at Gelsenkirchen. About 91% of the Old City and 63% of the entire city was destroyed by Allied air raids.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The US <a href="/wiki/17th_Airborne_Division_(United_States)" title="17th Airborne Division (United States)">17th Airborne Division</a>, employed in a standard infantry role and not in a parachute capacity, attacked Münster with the British <a href="/wiki/6th_Guards_Tank_Brigade_(United_Kingdom)" title="6th Guards Tank Brigade (United Kingdom)">6th Guards Tank Brigade</a> on 2 April 1945 in a ground assault and fought its way into the contested city centre, which was cleared in urban combat on the following day.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar_period">Postwar period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Postwar period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1946 to 1998, there was a Latvian secondary school in Münster,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and in 1947, one of the largest of about 93 Latvian libraries in the West was established in Münster.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1950s the Old City was rebuilt to match its pre-war state, though many of the surrounding buildings were replaced with cheaper modern structures. It was also for several decades a garrison town for the British forces stationed in West Germany.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-reunification">Post-reunification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-reunification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, Münster won an honourable distinction: the LivCom-Award for the most livable city in the world with a population between 200,000 and 750,000.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Münster is famous and liked for its bicycle friendliness and for the student character of the city that is due to the influence of its university, the Westfälische Wilhelms Universität Münster.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_position">Geographic position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geographic position">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Münster is situated on the river <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnstersche_Aa" title="Münstersche Aa">Aa</a>, approximately 15 kilometres (9 miles) south of its confluence with the <a href="/wiki/Ems_(river)" title="Ems (river)">Ems</a> in the Westphalian Bight, a landscape studded with dispersed settlements and farms, the so-called "Münsterland". The <a href="/wiki/Wolstonian_Stage" title="Wolstonian Stage">Wolstonian</a> <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sediments</a> of the mountain ridge called "Münsterländer Kiessandzug" cross the city from north to south. The highest elevation is the Mühlenberg in the northwest of Münster, 97&#160;metres <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a>. The lowest elevation is at the Ems with 44&#160;m <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a>. The city centre is 60&#160;m above sea level, measured at the <a href="/wiki/Prinzipalmarkt" title="Prinzipalmarkt">Prinzipalmarkt</a> in front of the historic city hall.</p>
<p>The Dutch city of <a href="/wiki/Enschede" title="Enschede">Enschede</a> is about 65&#160;km (40&#160;mi) northwest of Münster. Other major cities nearby include <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="Osnabrück">Osnabrück</a>, about 44&#160;km (27&#160;mi) to the north, <a href="/wiki/Dortmund" title="Dortmund">Dortmund</a>, about 61&#160;km (38&#160;mi) to the south, and <a href="/wiki/Bielefeld" title="Bielefeld">Bielefeld</a>, about 62&#160;km (39&#160;mi) to the east.</p>
<p>Münster is one of the 42 <a href="/wiki/Urban_agglomeration" title="Urban agglomeration">agglomeration areas</a> and one of Germany's biggest cities in terms of area. But this includes substantial sparsely populated, rural districts which were formerly separate local government authorities until they were <a href="/wiki/Amalgamation_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amalgamation (politics)">amalgamated</a> in 1975. Thus nearly half the city's area is agricultural, resulting in a low population density of approximately 900 inhabitants per km².</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_density">Population density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Population density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city's built-up area is quite extensive. There are no skyscrapers and few high-rise buildings but very many detached houses and mansions. Still the population density reaches about 15,000 inhabitants per km² in the city centre.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Calculating the population density based on the actual populated area results in approximately 2890 inhabitants per km².<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Münster's urban area of 302.91 square kilometres (116.95&#160;sq&#160;mi) is distributed into 57.54 square kilometres (22.22&#160;sq&#160;mi) covered with buildings while 0.99&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (0.38&#160;sq&#160;mi) are used for maintenance and 25.73&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (9.93&#160;sq&#160;mi) for traffic areas, 156.61&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (60.47&#160;sq&#160;mi) for agriculture and recreation, 8.91&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (3.44&#160;sq&#160;mi) are covered by water, 56.69&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (21.89&#160;sq&#160;mi) is forested and 6.23&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (2.41&#160;sq&#160;mi) is used otherwise.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The perimeter has a length of 107 kilometres (66 miles), the largest extend of the urban area in north–south direction is 24.4&#160;km (15.2&#160;mi), in east–west direction 20.6&#160;km (12.8&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A well-known saying in Münster is <i>"Entweder es regnet oder es läuten die Glocken. Und wenn beides zusammen fällt, dann ist Sonntag"</i> ("Either it rains or the church bells ring. And if both occur at the same time, it's Sunday."), but in reality the rainfall with approximately 758&#160;mm (29.8 inches) per year is close to the average rainfall in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-weatherDE_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weatherDE-19">[19]</a></sup> The perception of Münster as a rain-laden city isn't caused by the absolute amount of rainfall but by the above-average number of rainy days with relatively small amounts of rainfall. The average temperature is 9.4&#160;°C (48.9&#160;°F) with approximately 1500 sun hours per year.<sup id="cite_ref-weatherDE_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weatherDE-19">[19]</a></sup> Consequently, Münster is in the bottom fifth in comparison with other German cities. The winter in Münster is fairly mild and <a href="/wiki/Snowfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowfall">snowfall</a> is unusual. The temperature during summertime meets the average in Germany. The highest daily rainfall was registered on July 28, 2014: One weather station of the MeteoGroup reported a rainfall of 122.2 l / m², the State Environment Agency registered at one of its stations 292 l / m² during seven hours.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The record rainfall led to severe flooding throughout the city and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Greven" title="Greven">Greven</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjacent_cities_and_districts">Adjacent cities and districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Adjacent cities and districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Münster borders on the following cities and municipalities, named clockwise and beginning in the northwest: <a href="/wiki/Altenberge" title="Altenberge">Altenberge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greven" title="Greven">Greven</a> (<a href="/wiki/Steinfurt_(district)" title="Steinfurt (district)">District of Steinfurt</a>), <a href="/wiki/Telgte" title="Telgte">Telgte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Everswinkel" title="Everswinkel">Everswinkel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sendenhorst" title="Sendenhorst">Sendenhorst</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drensteinfurt" title="Drensteinfurt">Drensteinfurt</a> (<a href="/wiki/Warendorf_(district)" title="Warendorf (district)">District of Warendorf</a>), as well as <a href="/wiki/Ascheberg" title="Ascheberg">Ascheberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senden" title="Senden">Senden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Havixbeck" title="Havixbeck">Havixbeck</a> (<a href="/wiki/Coesfeld_(district)" title="Coesfeld (district)">District of Coesfeld</a>).</p>

<p>The city is divided into six administrative districts or <a href="/wiki/Stadtbezirk" title="Stadtbezirk">Stadtbezirke</a>: "Mitte" (Middle), "Nord" (North), "Ost" (East), "West", "Süd-Ost" (South-East) and "Hiltrup". Each district is represented by a council of 19 representatives elected in local elections. Heading each council is the district mayor, or <i>Bezirksvorsteher</i>. Every district is subdivided into residential quarters (<i>Wohnbereiche</i>). This official term, however, is not used in common speech, as there are no discrete definitions of the individual quarters. The term "Stadtteil" is used instead, mainly referring to the incorporated communities. The districts are also divided into 45 statistical districts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Münster has approximately 300,000 inhabitants, and more than 10,000 others who have their secondary residence in the city. The city has about 50,000 resident foreigners.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The life-expectancy in Münster is 76.3&#160;years for males and 83.1&#160;years for females. The average age of Münster's residents was 40 in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is considered the "creative desk of Westphalia".<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy-26">[26]</a></sup> Greater Münster is home to many industries such as those of public authorities, consulting companies, insurance companies, banks, computer centres, publishing houses, advertising and design.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy-26">[26]</a></sup> The service sector has created several thousand jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy-26">[26]</a></sup> Retailers have approximately 1.9&#160;billion euro turnover.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy-26">[26]</a></sup> The city still has traditional merchants' townhouses as well as modern outlets.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCnsterEconomy-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The job market situation in Münster is "comparatively good".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Of the approximately 130,000 employees subject to social insurance contribution more than 80% work in the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Tertiary sector">tertiary sector</a>, about 17% work in the <a href="/wiki/Secondary_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary sector">secondary sector</a> and 1% work in the <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary sector">primary sector</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Cathedral" title="Münster Cathedral">St. Paul's Cathedral</a>, built in the 13th century in a mixture of late Romanesque and early Gothic styles. It was completely restored after World War II. It includes an <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_clock" title="Astronomical clock">astronomical clock</a> of 1540, adorned with hand-painted zodiac symbols, which traces the movement of the planets, and plays a <a href="/wiki/Glockenspiel" title="Glockenspiel">Glockenspiel</a> tune every noon.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Prinzipalmarkt" title="Prinzipalmarkt">Prinzipalmarkt</a></i>, the main shopping street in the city centre with the Gothic <a href="/wiki/Historical_City_Hall_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="Historical City Hall of Münster">town hall</a> (14th century) in which the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> treaty which put an end to the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> was signed in 1648. Immediately north of the <i>Prinzipalmarkt</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Roggenmarkt_(M%C3%BCnster)" title="Roggenmarkt (Münster)"><i>Roggenmarkt</i></a>.</li>
<li>St Lambert's Church (1375), with three cages hanging from its tower above the clock face. In 1535 these cages were used to display the corpses of <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Leiden" class="mw-redirect" title="Jan van Leiden">Jan van Leiden</a> and other leaders of the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Rebellion" title="Münster Rebellion">Münster Rebellion</a>, who promoted polygamy and renunciation of all property.</li>
<li>The <i>Schloss</i> (palace), built in 1767–87 as residence for the prince-bishops by the Baroque architect Johann Conrad Schlaun and Wilhelm Ferdinand Lipper. Now the administrative centre for the University.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_M%C3%BCnster" title="Botanischer Garten Münster">Botanischer Garten Münster</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a> founded in 1803.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Zwinger_(M%C3%BCnster)" title="Zwinger (Münster)">Zwinger</a> fortress built in 1528. Used from the 18th to the 20th century as a prison. During World War II, the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> used the Zwinger also for executions.</li>
<li>"Krameramtshaus" (1589), an old guild house, which housed the delegation from the Netherlands during the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a>.</li>
<li>Stadthaus (1773)</li>
<li>Haus Rüschhaus (1743–49), a country estate situated in Nienberge, built by Johann Conrad Schlaun for himself</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbdrostenhof_Palace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Erbdrostenhof Palace (page does not exist)">Erbdrostenhof</a> (1749–53), a Baroque palace, also built by Schlaun, residence of <a href="/wiki/Droste-Vischering" class="mw-redirect" title="Droste-Vischering">Droste zu Vischering</a> noble family and birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Mary_of_the_Divine_Heart" title="Mary of the Divine Heart">Blessed Mary of the Divine Heart</a>.</li>
<li>Clemenskirche (1745–53), a Baroque church, also built by Schlaun</li>
<li>Signal-Iduna Building (1961), the first high-rise building in Münster.</li>
<li>LVM-Building, high-rise building near the Aasee.</li>
<li>LBS-Building, location of Münster's first zoo. Some old structures of the former zoo can be found in the park around the office building. Also the "Tuckesburg", the strange-looking house of the zoo-founder, is still intact.</li>
<li>"Münster Arkaden" (2006), new shopping centre between Prinzipalmarkt and the Pablo Picasso Museum of Graphic Art.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Münster is home to many institutions of higher education, including the <a href="/wiki/University_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="University of Münster">University of Münster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule_M%C3%BCnster" title="Fachhochschule Münster">University of Applied Sciences</a>. The city also has 92 Schools of primary and secondary education. The city has 47,000 students.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bicycling">Bicycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Bicycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Münster claims to be the bicycle capital of Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-BicyclingM.C3.BCnsterMarketing_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BicyclingM.C3.BCnsterMarketing-30">[30]</a></sup> It states that in 2007, vehicle traffic (36.4%) fell below traffic by bicycle (37.6%),<sup id="cite_ref-BicyclesM.C3.BCnsterWebsite_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BicyclesM.C3.BCnsterWebsite-31">[31]</a></sup> even though it is unclear how such a figure is defined. The city maintains an extensive network for bicycles including the popular "Promenade" which encircles Münster's city centre. While motorised vehicles are banned, there are paths for pedestrians. Additional <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_paths" class="mw-redirect" title="Bicycle paths">bicycle paths</a> link all city districts with the inner city and special traffic lights provide signals for bicyclists.<sup id="cite_ref-BicyclesM.C3.BCnsterWebsite_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BicyclesM.C3.BCnsterWebsite-31">[31]</a></sup> Bicycle stations in Münster offer bicycle rentals.<sup id="cite_ref-BicyclesM.C3.BCnsterWebsite_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BicyclesM.C3.BCnsterWebsite-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Train">Train</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Train">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Hauptbahnhof" title="Münster Hauptbahnhof">Münster's Central Station</a> is on the <a href="/wiki/Wanne-Eickel%E2%80%93Hamburg_railway" title="Wanne-Eickel–Hamburg railway">Wanne-Eickel–Hamburg railway</a>. The city is connected by <a href="/wiki/Intercity_(Deutsche_Bahn)" title="Intercity (Deutsche Bahn)">Intercity trains</a> to a lot of other German major cities.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transportation">Public transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Public transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, Münster had a historic <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_M%C3%BCnster" title="Trams in Münster">tramway system</a>, but later closed in 1954. Today, Münster does have some public transportation, which includes bus expresses,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> sightseeing buses,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> "waterbuses",<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and bicycle rentals.<sup id="cite_ref-BicyclesM.C3.BCnsterWebsite_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BicyclesM.C3.BCnsterWebsite-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is home to <a href="/wiki/SC_Preu%C3%9Fen_M%C3%BCnster" title="SC Preußen Münster">Preußen Münster</a> which was founded on 30 April 1906. The main section is football and the team plays at <a href="/wiki/Preu%C3%9Fenstadion" title="Preußenstadion">Preußenstadion</a>. Other important sports teams include the USC Münster e.V. volleyball club.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="British_forces">British forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: British forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/British_Forces_Germany" title="British Forces Germany">British Forces Germany</a></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Münster became a major station within <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck_Garrison" title="Osnabrück Garrison">Osnabrück Garrison</a>, part of <a href="/wiki/British_Forces_Germany" title="British Forces Germany">British Forces Germany</a>. Their presence was gradually reduced, yet there are still many active military bases. The last forces left Münster on 4 July 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<li>1824, 31 December, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Altum" title="Bernard Altum">Bernard Altum</a>; † 1 February 1900 in Eberswalde, zoologist, ornithologist and forest scientist</li>
<li>1833, January 26, <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Ney" class="mw-redirect" title="Elisabeth Ney">Elisabeth Ney</a>; † June 29, 1907 in Austin / Texas, sculptor</li>

<p><b>Transcendentalism</b> is a philosophical movement that developed in the late 1820s and 1830s in the eastern United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It arose as a reaction to or protest against the general state of <a href="/wiki/Intellectualism" title="Intellectualism">intellectualism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spirituality</a> at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarian</a> church as taught at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Divinity_School" title="Harvard Divinity School">Harvard Divinity School</a> was of particular interest.</p>
<p>Transcendentalism emerged from English and German Romanticism, the Biblical criticism of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder" title="Johann Gottfried Herder">Johann Gottfried Herder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schleiermacher" title="Friedrich Schleiermacher">Friedrich Schleiermacher</a>, the skepticism of <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford-1">[1]</a></sup> and the transcendental philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_Idealism" class="mw-redirect" title="German Idealism">German Idealism</a>. Miller and Versluis regard <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Swedenborg" title="Emanuel Swedenborg">Emanuel Swedenborg</a> as a pervasive influence on transcendentalism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller195049_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller195049-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVersluis200117_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVersluis200117-6">[6]</a></sup> It was also influenced by Hindu texts on philosophy of the mind and spirituality, especially the <a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">Upanishads</a>.</p>
<p>A core belief of transcendentalism is in the inherent goodness of people and nature. Adherents believe that society and its institutions have corrupted the purity of the individual, and they have faith that people are at their best when truly "<a href="/wiki/Self-Reliance" title="Self-Reliance">self-reliant</a>" and independent.</p>
<p>Transcendentalism emphasizes subjective intuition over objective empiricism. Adherents believe that individuals are capable of generating completely original insights with as little attention and deference to past masters as possible.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Transcendentalism is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a>, the dominant religious movement in Boston in the early nineteenth century. It started to develop after Unitarianism took hold at Harvard University, following the elections of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ware_(Unitarian)" title="Henry Ware (Unitarian)">Henry Ware</a> as the Hollis Professor of Divinity in 1805 and of <a href="/wiki/John_Thornton_Kirkland" title="John Thornton Kirkland">John Thornton Kirkland</a> as President in 1810. Transcendentalism was not a rejection of Unitarianism; rather, it developed as an organic consequence of the Unitarian emphasis on free conscience and the value of intellectual reason. The transcendentalists were not content with the sobriety, mildness, and calm rationalism of Unitarianism. Instead, they longed for a more intense spiritual experience. Thus, transcendentalism was not born as a counter-movement to Unitarianism, but as a parallel movement to the very ideas introduced by the Unitarians.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transcendental_Club">Transcendental Club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transcendental Club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ralph_Waldo_Emerson_ca1857.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson_ca1857.jpg/220px-Ralph_Waldo_Emerson_ca1857.jpg" width="220" height="334" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson_ca1857.jpg/330px-Ralph_Waldo_Emerson_ca1857.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson_ca1857.jpg/440px-Ralph_Waldo_Emerson_ca1857.jpg 2x" data-file-width="452" data-file-height="686" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ralph_Waldo_Emerson_ca1857.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a></div>
</div>

<p>Transcendentalism became a coherent movement and a sacred organization with the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Transcendental_Club" title="Transcendental Club">Transcendental Club</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a> on September 8, 1836 by prominent New England intellectuals, including George Putnam (1807–78, the Unitarian minister in <a href="/wiki/Roxbury,_Boston" title="Roxbury, Boston">Roxbury</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Henry_Hedge" title="Frederic Henry Hedge">Frederic Henry Hedge</a>. From 1840, the group frequently published in their journal <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dial" title="The Dial">The Dial</a></i>, along with other venues.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_wave_of_transcendentalists">Second wave of transcendentalists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Second wave of transcendentalists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the late 1840s, Emerson believed the movement was dying out, and even more so after the death of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Fuller" title="Margaret Fuller">Margaret Fuller</a> in 1850. "All that can be said," Emerson wrote, "is that she represents an interesting hour and group in American cultivation."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> There was, however, a second wave of transcendentalists, including <a href="/wiki/Moncure_Conway" class="mw-redirect" title="Moncure Conway">Moncure Conway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octavius_Brooks_Frothingham" class="mw-redirect" title="Octavius Brooks Frothingham">Octavius Brooks Frothingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Longfellow" title="Samuel Longfellow">Samuel Longfellow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Benjamin_Sanborn" title="Franklin Benjamin Sanborn">Franklin Benjamin Sanborn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Notably, the transgression of the spirit, most often evoked by the poet's prosaic voice, is said to endow in the reader a sense of purposefulness. This is the underlying theme in the majority of transcendentalist essays and papers—all of which are centered on subjects which assert a love for individual expression.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Though the group was mostly made up of struggling <a href="/wiki/Aestheticism" title="Aestheticism">aesthetes</a>, the wealthiest among them was <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Gray_Ward" title="Samuel Gray Ward">Samuel Gray Ward</a>, who, after a few contributions to <i>The Dial</i>, focused on his banking career.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beliefs">Beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Transcendentalists are strong believers in the power of the individual. Their beliefs are closely linked with those of the <a href="/wiki/Romantics" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantics">Romantics</a>, but differ by an attempt to embrace or, at least, to not oppose the empiricism of science.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transcendental_knowledge">Transcendental knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transcendental knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transcendentalists desire to ground their religion and philosophy in principles not based on, or falsifiable by, physical experience, but deriving from the inner spiritual or mental essence of the human.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Transcendentalism merged English and German Romanticism, the Biblical criticism of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder" title="Johann Gottfried Herder">Herder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schleiermacher" class="mw-redirect" title="Schleiermacher">Schleiermacher</a>, and the skepticism of <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">Hume</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford-1">[1]</a></sup> and the transcendental philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a> (and of <a href="/wiki/German_Idealism" class="mw-redirect" title="German Idealism">German Idealism</a> more generally), interpreting Kant's a priori categories as a priori knowledge.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Early transcendentalists were largely unacquainted with <a href="/wiki/German_philosophy" title="German philosophy">German philosophy</a> in the original and relied primarily on the writings of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">Thomas Carlyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Cousin" title="Victor Cousin">Victor Cousin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germaine_de_Sta%C3%ABl" title="Germaine de Staël">Germaine de Staël</a>, and other English and French commentators for their knowledge of it. The transcendental movement can be described as an American outgrowth of English Romanticism.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individualism">Individualism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Individualism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transcendentalists believe that society and its institutions—particularly organized religion and political parties—corrupt the purity of the individual. They have faith that people are at their best when truly "self-reliant" and independent. It is only from such real individuals that true community can form. Even with this necessary individuality, transcendentalists also believe that all people possess a piece of the "<a href="/wiki/Over-soul" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-soul">Over-soul</a>" (God). Because the Over-soul is one, this unites all people as one being.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (July 2015)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> Emerson alludes to this concept in the introduction of the <i>American Scholar</i> address, "that there is One Man, - present to all particular men only partially, or through one faculty; and that you must take the whole society to find the whole man."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Such an ideal is in harmony with Transcendentalist individualism, as each person is empowered to behold within him or herself a piece of the divine Over-soul.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_religions">Indian religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Indian religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transcendentalism has been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Indian_religions" title="Indian religions">Indian religions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVersluis1993_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVersluis1993-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVersluis20013_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVersluis20013-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[note 1]</a></sup> Thoreau in <i><a href="/wiki/Walden" title="Walden">Walden</a></i> spoke of the Transcendentalists' debt to Indian religions directly:</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_David_Thoreau_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Henry_David_Thoreau_2.jpg/220px-Henry_David_Thoreau_2.jpg" width="220" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Henry_David_Thoreau_2.jpg/330px-Henry_David_Thoreau_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Henry_David_Thoreau_2.jpg/440px-Henry_David_Thoreau_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1978" data-file-height="2081" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_David_Thoreau_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the morning I bathe my intellect in the stupendous and cosmogonal philosophy of the Bhagavat Geeta, since whose composition years of the gods have elapsed, and in comparison with which our modern world and its literature seem puny and trivial; and I doubt if that philosophy is not to be referred to a previous state of existence, so remote is its sublimity from our conceptions. I lay down the book and go to my well for water, and lo! there I meet the servant of the Brahmin, priest of Brahma, and Vishnu and Indra, who still sits in his temple on the Ganges reading the Vedas, or dwells at the root of a tree with his crust and water-jug. I meet his servant come to draw water for his master, and our buckets as it were grate together in the same well. The pure Walden water is mingled with the sacred water of the Ganges.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Idealism">Idealism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Idealism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transcendentalists differ in their interpretations of the practical aims of will. Some adherents link it with utopian social change; <a href="/wiki/Orestes_Brownson" title="Orestes Brownson">Brownson</a>, for example, connected it with early socialism, but others consider it an exclusively individualist and idealist project. Emerson believed the latter; in his 1842 lecture "<a href="/wiki/The_Transcendentalist" title="The Transcendentalist">The Transcendentalist</a>", he suggested that the goal of a purely transcendental outlook on life was impossible to attain in practice:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>You will see by this sketch that there is no such thing as a transcendental <i>party</i>; that there is no pure transcendentalist; that we know of no one but prophets and heralds of such a philosophy; that all who by strong bias of nature have leaned to the spiritual side in doctrine, have stopped short of their goal. We have had many harbingers and forerunners; but of a purely spiritual life, history has afforded no example. I mean, we have yet no man who has leaned entirely on his character, and eaten angels' food; who, trusting to his sentiments, found life made of miracles; who, working for universal aims, found himself fed, he knew not how; clothed, sheltered, and weaponed, he knew not how, and yet it was done by his own hands. ...Shall we say, then, that transcendentalism is the <a href="/wiki/Saturnalia" title="Saturnalia">Saturnalia</a> or excess of Faith; the presentiment of a faith proper to man in his integrity, excessive only when his imperfect obedience hinders the satisfaction of his wish.</p>
</blockquote>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_New_Thought" title="History of New Thought">History of New Thought</a></div>
<p>Transcendentalism is, in many aspects, the first notable American intellectual movement. It has inspired succeeding generations of American intellectuals, as well as some literary movements.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Transcendentalism influenced the growing movement of "Mental Sciences" of the mid-19th century, which would later become known as the <a href="/wiki/New_Thought" title="New Thought">New Thought</a> movement. New Thought considers Emerson its intellectual father.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emma_Curtis_Hopkins" title="Emma Curtis Hopkins">Emma Curtis Hopkins</a> "the teacher of teachers", <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Holmes" title="Ernest Holmes">Ernest Holmes</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Religious_Science" title="Religious Science">Religious Science</a>, the Fillmores, founders of <a href="/wiki/Unity_Church" title="Unity Church">Unity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Malinda_Cramer" title="Malinda Cramer">Malinda Cramer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nona_L._Brooks" title="Nona L. Brooks">Nona L. Brooks</a>, the founders of <a href="/wiki/Divine_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Divine Science">Divine Science</a>, were all greatly influenced by Transcendentalism.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 19th century, under the influence of Ralph Waldo Emerson (who had been a Unitarian minister)<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> and other transcendentalists, Unitarianism began its long journey from liberal Protestantism to its present more pluralist form.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Transcendentalism also influenced <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ram_Mohan_Roy" title="Ram Mohan Roy">Ram Mohan Roy</a> (1772–1833), the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Brahmo_Samaj" title="Brahmo Samaj">Brahmo Samaj</a>, rejected Hindu mythology, but also the Christian trinity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris2009268_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris2009268-23">[22]</a></sup> He found that <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a> came closest to true Christianity,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris2009268_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris2009268-23">[22]</a></sup> and had a strong sympathy for the Unitarians,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKipf19793_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKipf19793-24">[23]</a></sup> who were closely connected to the Transcendentalists.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVersluis1993_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVersluis1993-15">[15]</a></sup> Ram Mohan Roy founded a missionary committee in Calcutta, and in 1828 asked for support for missionary activities from the American Unitarians.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKipf19797-8_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKipf19797-8-25">[24]</a></sup> By 1829, Roy had abandoned the Unitarian Committee,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKipf197915_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKipf197915-26">[25]</a></sup> but after Roy's death, the Brahmo Samaj kept close ties to the Unitarian Church,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris2009268-269_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris2009268-269-27">[26]</a></sup> who strived towards a rational faith, social reform, and the joining of these two in a renewed religion.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKipf19793_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKipf19793-24">[23]</a></sup> Its theology was called "<a href="/wiki/Neo-Vedanta" title="Neo-Vedanta">neo-Vedanta</a>" by Christian commentators,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHalbfass19959_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHalbfass19959-28">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERinehart2004192_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERinehart2004192-29">[28]</a></sup> and has been highly influential in the modern popular understanding of Hinduism,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKing2002_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKing2002-30">[29]</a></sup> but also of modern western spirituality, which re-imported the Unitarian influences in the disguise of the seemingly age-old Neo-Vedanta.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKing2002_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKing2002-30">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESharf1995_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharf1995-31">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESharf2000_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharf2000-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_figures">Major figures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Major figures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sarah_Margaret_Fuller_engraving.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Sarah_Margaret_Fuller_engraving.jpg/220px-Sarah_Margaret_Fuller_engraving.jpg" width="220" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Sarah_Margaret_Fuller_engraving.jpg/330px-Sarah_Margaret_Fuller_engraving.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Sarah_Margaret_Fuller_engraving.jpg/440px-Sarah_Margaret_Fuller_engraving.jpg 2x" data-file-width="688" data-file-height="760" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sarah_Margaret_Fuller_engraving.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Margaret_Fuller" title="Margaret Fuller">Margaret Fuller</a></div>

<p>Major figures in the transcendentalist movement were <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Ralph Waldo Emerson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Fuller" title="Margaret Fuller">Margaret Fuller</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amos_Bronson_Alcott" title="Amos Bronson Alcott">Amos Bronson Alcott</a>. Other prominent transcendentalists included <a href="/wiki/Louisa_May_Alcott" title="Louisa May Alcott">Louisa May Alcott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Timothy_Brooks" title="Charles Timothy Brooks">Charles Timothy Brooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orestes_Brownson" title="Orestes Brownson">Orestes Brownson</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Ellery_Channing_(poet)" title="William Ellery Channing (poet)">William Ellery Channing</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Channing" title="William Henry Channing">William Henry Channing</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Freeman_Clarke" title="James Freeman Clarke">James Freeman Clarke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Pearse_Cranch" title="Christopher Pearse Cranch">Christopher Pearse Cranch</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Sullivan_Dwight" title="John Sullivan Dwight">John Sullivan Dwight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convers_Francis" title="Convers Francis">Convers Francis</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Furness" title="William Henry Furness">William Henry Furness</a>, Frederic Henry Hedge, <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_Judd" title="Sylvester Judd">Sylvester Judd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Parker" title="Theodore Parker">Theodore Parker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Palmer_Peabody" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Palmer Peabody">Elizabeth Palmer Peabody</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Ripley_(transcendentalist)" title="George Ripley (transcendentalist)">George Ripley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Treadwell_Stone" title="Thomas Treadwell Stone">Thomas Treadwell Stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jones_Very" title="Jones Very">Jones Very</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">Walt Whitman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early in the movement's history, the term "Transcendentalists" was used as a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pejorative" class="extiw" title="wikt:pejorative">pejorative</a> term by critics, who were suggesting their position was beyond sanity and reason.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Hawthorne" title="Nathaniel Hawthorne">Nathaniel Hawthorne</a> wrote a novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blithedale_Romance" title="The Blithedale Romance">The Blithedale Romance</a></i> (1852), satirizing the movement, and based it on his experiences at <a href="/wiki/Brook_Farm" title="Brook Farm">Brook Farm</a>, a short-lived utopian community founded on transcendental principles.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a> wrote a story, "<a href="/wiki/Never_Bet_the_Devil_Your_Head" title="Never Bet the Devil Your Head">Never Bet the Devil Your Head</a>" (1841), in which he embedded elements of deep dislike for transcendentalism, calling its followers "Frogpondians" after the pond on <a href="/wiki/Boston_Common" title="Boston Common">Boston Common</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> The narrator ridiculed their writings by calling them "metaphor-run" lapsing into "mysticism for mysticism's sake",<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> and called it a "disease." The story specifically mentions the movement and its flagship journal <i>The Dial</i>, though Poe denied that he had any specific targets.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> In Poe's essay "<a href="/wiki/The_Philosophy_of_Composition" title="The Philosophy of Composition">The Philosophy of Composition</a>" (1846), he offers criticism denouncing "the excess of the suggested meaning... which turns into prose (and that of the very flattest kind) the so-called poetry of the so-called transcendentalists."<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcendentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Versluis: "In <i>American Transcendentalism and Asian religions</i>, I detailed the immense impact that the Euro-American discovery of Asian religions had not only on European Romanticism, but above all, on American Transcendentalism. There I argued that the Transcendentalists' discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Bhagavad-Gita" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhagavad-Gita">Bhagavad-Gita</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Upanishads" title="Upanishads">Upanishads</a>, and other world scriptures was critical in the entire movement, pivotal not only for the well-known figures like Emerson and Thoreau, but also for lesser known figures like Samuel Johnson and William Rounsville Alger. That Transcendentalism emerged out of this new knowledge of the world's religious traditions I have no doubt."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVersluis20013_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVersluis20013-16">[16]</a></sup></span></li>

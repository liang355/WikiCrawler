<p><b>Cottbus</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈkɔtbʊs]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Cottbus.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/02/De-Cottbus.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Cottbus.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Lower_Sorbian_language" title="Lower Sorbian language">Lower Sorbian</a>: <span lang="dsb" xml:lang="dsb"><i>Chóśebuz</i></span>) is a university city and the second-largest city in <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, Germany. Situated around 125&#160;km (78&#160;mi) southeast of Berlin, on the River <a href="/wiki/Spree" title="Spree">Spree</a>, Cottbus is also a major railway junction with extensive <a href="/wiki/Siding_(rail)" title="Siding (rail)">sidings</a>/depots. Although only a small <a href="/wiki/Sorbs" title="Sorbs">Sorbian</a> minority lives in Cottbus itself, the city is considered as the political and cultural center of the Sorbs in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Lusatia" title="Lower Lusatia">Lower Lusatia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">Spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cottbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until the beginning of the twentieth century the spelling of the city's name was contentious. In <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> the spelling "Kottbus" was preferred, and it is still used in respect of the capital's <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Kottbusser Tor</i></span> ("Cottbus Gate"), but locally the traditional spelling "Cottbus" (which defies standard German-language rules) was preferred, and this is now used in most circumstances. Because the official spelling used locally before the spelling reforms of 1996 had contravened even the standardized spelling rules already in place, the <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/St%C3%A4ndiger_Ausschuss_f%C3%BCr_geographische_Namen" class="extiw" title="de:Ständiger Ausschuss für geographische Namen">Standing Committee for Geographical Names</a> stress their urgent recommendation that geographical names should respect the <a href="/wiki/German_orthography_reform_of_1996" title="German orthography reform of 1996">national spelling standards</a>. In this context it is to be noted that to identify a citizen of the city either "Cottbuser" or "Cottbusser" may be used.</p>

<p>The settlement was established in the 10th century, when <a href="/wiki/Sorbs" title="Sorbs">Sorbs</a> erected a <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> on a sandy island in the River <a href="/wiki/Spree" title="Spree">Spree</a>. The first recorded mention of the town's name was in 1156. In the 13th century <a href="/wiki/Ostsiedlung" title="Ostsiedlung">German settlers</a> came to the town and thereafter lived side-by-side with the Sorbs. In <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Times" title="Medieval Times">medieval times</a> Cottbus was known for <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, and the town's drapery was exported throughout Brandenburg, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Saxony" title="Electorate of Saxony">Saxony</a>. In 1445 Cottbus was acquired by the <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Margraviate of Brandenburg</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>. In 1514 Jan Rak founded the <i>Universitas Serborum</i>, a Sorbian gymnasium, in the city. In 1701 the city became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>. It was also ruled by Saxony between 1807 and 1813. In 1815 the surrounding districts of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Lusatia" title="Upper Lusatia">Upper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lower_Lusatia" title="Lower Lusatia">Lower Lusatia</a> were ceded by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Saxony" title="Kingdom of Saxony">Kingdom of Saxony</a> to Prussia. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Cottbus was taken by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> on 22 April 1945.</p>
<p>From 1949 until <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a> in 1990, Cottbus was part of the <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">German Democratic Republic</a> (East Germany).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_education">Culture and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cottbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Culture and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nsg-cottbus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Nsg-cottbus.jpg/220px-Nsg-cottbus.jpg" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Nsg-cottbus.jpg/330px-Nsg-cottbus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Nsg-cottbus.jpg/440px-Nsg-cottbus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="618" /></a>

<p>Cottbus is the cultural centre of the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Sorbian_language" title="Lower Sorbian language">Lower Sorbian</a> minority. Many signs in the town are bilingual, and there is a Lower Sorbian-medium <i><a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Gymnasium</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Sorbian_Quarter" class="mw-redirect" title="Sorbian Quarter">Sorbian Quarter</a>, but Sorbian is rarely spoken on the streets.</p>
<p>Next to Cottbus is the famous <a href="/w/index.php?title=Branitz_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Branitz Park (page does not exist)">Branitz Park</a>, created by Prince <a href="/wiki/Hermann_von_P%C3%BCckler-Muskau" class="mw-redirect" title="Hermann von Pückler-Muskau">Hermann von Pückler-Muskau</a> after 1845. Schloss Branitz (Branitz Castle) was rebuilt by Gottfried Semper in a late Baroque style between 1846 and 1852, and the gardens laid by Prince Hermann feature two pyramids. One of these, the Seepyramide, is in the middle of an artificial lake and serves as his mauseoleum.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cottbus is also home of the <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg_University_of_Technology" title="Brandenburg University of Technology">Brandenburg University of Technology</a> (BTU) and the maths/science-oriented Max-Steenbeck-Gymnasium, which is named after the physicist <a href="/wiki/Max_Steenbeck" title="Max Steenbeck">Max Steenbeck</a>.</p>
<p>Every year Cottbus is host to the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">East European</a> <a href="/wiki/Film_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Film Festival">Film Festival</a>.</p>
<p>Cottbus has a <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> team called <a href="/wiki/FC_Energie_Cottbus" title="FC Energie Cottbus">FC Energie Cottbus</a>, which currently play in the <a href="/wiki/3._Liga" title="3. Liga">3. Liga</a>. Their home matches are played at the city's <a href="/wiki/Stadion_der_Freundschaft_(Cottbus)" title="Stadion der Freundschaft (Cottbus)">Stadion der Freundschaft</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_generation">Power generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cottbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Power generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several lignite-fired <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a> in the area around Cottbus (Lausitz). The biggest stations are "<a href="/wiki/Schwarze_Pumpe_power_station" title="Schwarze Pumpe power station">Schwarze Pumpe</a>" (1600 MW), "<a href="/wiki/Boxberg_Power_Station" title="Boxberg Power Station">Boxberg</a>" (1900 MW) and "<a href="/wiki/J%C3%A4nschwalde_Power_Station" title="Jänschwalde Power Station">Jänschwalde</a>" (3000 MW).</p>

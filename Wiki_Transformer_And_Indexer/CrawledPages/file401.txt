<p><b>Krefeld</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈkʁeːfɛlt]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Krefeld.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/40/De-Krefeld.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Krefeld.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>), also known as <b>Crefeld</b> until 1929, is a city in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is located northwest of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>, its centre lying just a few kilometres to the west of the river <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>; the borough of <a href="/wiki/Uerdingen" title="Uerdingen">Uerdingen</a> is situated directly on the Rhine. Krefeld is accessed by the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">autobahns</a> <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_57" title="Bundesautobahn 57">A57</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>–<a href="/wiki/Nijmegen" title="Nijmegen">Nijmegen</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_44" title="Bundesautobahn 44">A44</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a>–<a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>–<a href="/wiki/Dortmund" title="Dortmund">Dortmund</a>–<a href="/wiki/Kassel" title="Kassel">Kassel</a>).</p>
<p>Krefeld is also called the "Velvet and Silk City".</p>
<p>Krefeld's residents speak <i><a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">Hochdeutsch</a></i>, the standard German taught to all people in Germany. However, the native dialect is a <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a> variety, sometimes locally called <i>Krefelder Plattdeutsch</i>, <i>Krieewelsch Platt</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Plattdeutsch" class="mw-redirect" title="Plattdeutsch">Plattdeutsch</a></i>, or sometimes simply <i>Platt</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Uerdingen_line" title="Uerdingen line">Uerdingen line</a> <a href="/wiki/Isogloss" title="Isogloss">isogloss</a>, separating general dialectical areas in Germany and neighbouring Germanic-speaking countries, runs through and is named after Krefeld's Uerdingen district, originally an independent municipality.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krefeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town originated in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> times when the <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">legions</a> founded the military camp of <b>Gelduba</b> (today the borough of Gellep). Records first mention Krefeld itself in 1105 under the name of <i>Krinvelde</i>. <a href="/wiki/Uerdingen" title="Uerdingen">Uerdingen</a>, originally an independent town east of Krefeld, founded in 1255, became in medieval times larger and more important than Krefeld.</p>
<p>In February 1598, Walburga, wife of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_van_Nieuwenaar" title="Adolf van Nieuwenaar">Adolf van Nieuwenaar</a>, and last Countess of Limburg and Moers, gifted the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Moers" title="County of Moers">County of Moers</a>, which included Krefeld, to <a href="/wiki/Maurice,_Prince_of_Orange" title="Maurice, Prince of Orange">Maurice, Prince of Orange</a>. After her death in 1600, <a href="/wiki/John_William,_Duke_of_J%C3%BClich-Cleves-Berg" title="John William, Duke of Jülich-Cleves-Berg">John William of Cleves</a> took possession of these lands, but Maurice successfully defended his heritage in 1601. Krefeld and Moers would remain under the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Orange" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Orange">House of Orange</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age" title="Dutch Golden Age">Dutch Golden Age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-peele_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peele-2">[2]</a></sup> The growth of the town began in that century, partially because Krefeld was one of few towns spared the horrors of the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> (1618–1648). The town of Uerdingen, incorporated into Krefeld in the 20th century, was less fortunate, almost ceasing to exist, destroyed at the hands of troops from <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a> during the Thirty Years' War. In 1683 a group of thirteen <a href="/wiki/Mennonite" title="Mennonite">Mennonite</a> families left Krefeld to re-settle in Pennsylvania in order to enjoy religious freedom. They crossed the Atlantic on the ship <i>Concord</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and founded the settlement of <a href="/wiki/Germantown,_Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Germantown, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Germantown</a> (now incorporated in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>), thus beginning the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Dutch" title="Pennsylvania Dutch">Pennsylvania Dutch</a> ethnic identity.</p>
<p>After the death of <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III of Orange</a> in 1702, Krefeld passed to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-peele_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peele-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Krefeld" title="Battle of Krefeld">Battle of Krefeld</a> occurred nearby in 1758 during the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>. Krefeld and Uerdingen were included within the Prussian <a href="/wiki/Province_of_J%C3%BClich-Cleves-Berg" title="Province of Jülich-Cleves-Berg">Province of Jülich-Cleves-Berg</a> in 1815 (after 1822 the <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Province" title="Rhine Province">Rhine Province</a>). In 1872 Krefeld became an <a href="/wiki/Independent_city" title="Independent city">independent city</a> within Rhenish Prussia. In 1918 during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World Warthe</a> <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgian Army">Belgian Army</a> used it as a base during the <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Rhineland" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupation of the Rhineland">occupation of the Rhineland</a>.</p>
<p>In 1929 Krefeld and Uerdingen merged to form <b>Krefeld-Uerdingen</b>; in 1940 the name was shortened to simply <i>Krefeld</i>.</p>
<p>On December 11, 1941, during World War II, a detailed report on the transport of Jews from Krefeld and its surroundings listed 1007 Jews from Krefeld and Duisburg, were deported to the Šķirotava Railway Station near <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a>, later to become <a href="/wiki/Jungfernhof_concentration_camp" title="Jungfernhof concentration camp">Jungfernhof concentration camp</a>. They were transported in freezing conditions with no drinking-water for more than two days.<sup id="cite_ref-yadvashemRiga_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yadvashemRiga-4">[4]</a></sup> Almost immediately upon arrival these Jews were shot in the <a href="/wiki/Rumbula_massacre" title="Rumbula massacre">Rumbula forest massacre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-deportations_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deportations-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 21 June 1943 British bombs destroyed large parts of east of the city; a firestorm consumed most of the city center (apart from the central train station, which remained intact apart from minor damage). On 3 March 1945 US troops entered Krefeld, among them the later U.S. Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The town became part of the new state of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Jews_of_Krefeld">The Jews of Krefeld</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krefeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Jews of Krefeld">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jews were listed as citizens of Krefeld from 1617. In 1764 a <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> was erected, and by 1812, under French rule, the town included 196 Jewish families, with three Jewish-owned banks. Under <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>, the town became the capital for the surrounding Jewish communities including over 5000 Jews, and by 1897 they comprised 1.8% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-yadvashemDB_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yadvashemDB-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1846 a Jewish representative was voted onto the town's municipal council, while rising antisemitism was noted during these elections.<sup id="cite_ref-yadvashemDB_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yadvashemDB-6">[6]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Reform_Judaism" title="Reform Judaism">reform</a> synagogue was built in 1876, arousing opposition from the <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox</a> community. A Jewish school existed in the town, with more than 200 students around 1900.<sup id="cite_ref-yadvashemDB_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yadvashemDB-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1938 during <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>, the two synagogues were attacked.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1941 following an order from <a href="/wiki/Hitler" class="mw-redirect" title="Hitler">Hitler</a> to deport the German Jews to the east, Jews from the town were sent to the area around Riga<sup id="cite_ref-yadvashemRiga_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yadvashemRiga-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-yadvashemDB_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yadvashemDB-6">[6]</a></sup> and murdered there.<sup id="cite_ref-deportations_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deportations-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1945, the U.S. Army occupied the city and placed <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a>, then an Army private and later Secretary of State of the United States, in charge of the city administration.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 a new synagogue, library and Jewish cultural center were erected on the location of one of the demolished synagogues. Around 1100 Jews were reported to live in and around Krefeld at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayors_of_Krefeld_from_1848">Mayors of Krefeld from 1848</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krefeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mayors of Krefeld from 1848">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krefeld&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Autobahn_A44_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Autobahn_A44_1.jpg/220px-Autobahn_A44_1.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Autobahn_A44_1.jpg/330px-Autobahn_A44_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Autobahn_A44_1.jpg/440px-Autobahn_A44_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2304" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Autobahn_A44_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_44" title="Bundesautobahn 44">Bundesautobahn 44</a> towards <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Krefeld is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a> network with several stations, including its main station, <a href="/wiki/Krefeld_Hauptbahnhof" title="Krefeld Hauptbahnhof">Krefeld Hauptbahnhof</a>. They are served by <a href="/wiki/Intercity_(Deutsche_Bahn)" title="Intercity (Deutsche Bahn)">Intercity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regional-Express" title="Regional-Express">Regional-Express</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regionalbahn" title="Regionalbahn">Regionalbahn</a> trains. The <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Rheinbahn" title="Rheinbahn">Rheinbahn</a> operates a <a href="/wiki/Stadtbahn" title="Stadtbahn">Stadtbahn</a> service to the centrally located Rheinstraße stop. This line was the first electric <a href="/wiki/Inter-city_rail" title="Inter-city rail">inter-city rail</a> line in Europe, established in 1898, and commonly called the <a href="/w/index.php?title=K-Bahn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="K-Bahn (page does not exist)">K-Bahn</a> because of the letter "K" used to denote the trains to Krefeld. Nowadays, in the <a href="/wiki/Verkehrsverbund_Rhein-Ruhr" title="Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Ruhr">VRR</a> notation, it is called U76, with the morning and afternoon express trains numbered as U70, the line number there coloured red instead of the usual blue used for <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit_in_Germany#U-Bahn" title="Rapid transit in Germany">U-Bahn</a> lines. The term <i>K-Bahn</i>, however, prevails in common usage.</p>
<p>The city of Krefeld itself operates <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Krefeld" title="Trams in Krefeld">four tramway</a> and several <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a> lines under the umbrella of <a href="/wiki/SWK_MOBIL" title="SWK MOBIL">SWK MOBIL</a>, a city-owned company. Since 2010, 19 of the oldest trams of the type <a href="/wiki/DUEWAG" class="mw-redirect" title="DUEWAG">DUEWAG</a> GT8 were replaced by modern barrier-free trams of the type <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Transportation" title="Bombardier Transportation">Bombardier</a> <a href="/wiki/Flexity_Outlook" title="Flexity Outlook">Flexity Outlook</a>. SWK Mobil owns an option to buy another 19 trams of the same type to replace the last 19 DUEWAG M8 trams. The whole tram fleet will then be barrier-free. Next to that the city plans to extend the line 044 in Krefeld-Hüls to connect the northern district of Hüls with the Krefeld downtown area.</p>

<p>Since 1964,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the city has hosted an "honors program in foreign language (German) studies" for high school students from <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The program annually places approximately thirty carefully selected high school juniors with families in and around Krefeld for intensive German language training.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Since 1973, the <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">fire services</a> of Krefeld and twin city <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a> have played each other in an annual 'friendly' football match.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

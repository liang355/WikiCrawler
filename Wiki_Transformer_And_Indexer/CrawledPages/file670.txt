<p><br />
In <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <b>divestment</b> or <b>divestiture</b> is the reduction of some kind of <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">asset</a> for financial, ethical, or political objectives or sale of an existing business by a firm. A divestment is the opposite of an <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investment</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motives">Motives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Divestment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Motives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Firms may have several motives for divestitures:</p>
<ol>
<li>a firm may divest (sell) businesses that are not part of its core operations so that it can focus on what it does best. For example, <a href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastman Kodak">Eastman Kodak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/Future_Group" title="Future Group">Future Group</a> and many other firms have sold various businesses that were not closely related to their core businesses.</li>
<li>to obtain funds. Divestitures generate funds for the firm because it is selling one of its businesses in exchange for cash. For example, <a href="/wiki/CSX_Corporation" title="CSX Corporation">CSX Corporation</a> made divestitures to focus on its core railroad business and also to obtain funds so that it could pay off some of its existing debt.</li>
<li>a firm's "break-up" value is sometimes believed to be greater than the value of the firm as a whole. In other words, the sum of a firm's individual asset liquidation values exceeds the market value of the firm's combined assets. This encourages firms to sell off what would be worth more when liquidated than when retained.</li>
<li>divesting a part of a firm may enhance stability. <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a>, for example, divested its chip division - <a href="/wiki/NXP_Semiconductors" title="NXP Semiconductors">NXP</a> - because the chip market was so volatile and unpredictable that NXP was responsible for the majority of Philips's stock fluctuations while it represented only a very small part of Philips NV.</li>
<li>divesting a part of a company may eliminate a division which is under-performing or even failing.</li>
<li>regulatory authorities may demand divestiture, for example in order to create competition.</li>
<li>pressure from shareholders for social reasons (sometimes also called <a href="/wiki/Disinvestment" title="Disinvestment">disinvestment</a>). Examples include <a href="/wiki/Disinvestment_from_South_Africa" title="Disinvestment from South Africa">disinvestment from South Africa</a> in the former era of <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> (now ended), and more recent calls for <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_divestment" title="Fossil fuel divestment">fossil fuel divestment</a> in response to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divestment_for_financial_goals">Divestment for financial goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Divestment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Divestment for financial goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Often the term is used as a means to grow financially in which a company sells off a business unit in order to focus their resources on a market it judges to be more profitable, or promising. Sometimes, such an action can be a <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spin-off</a>. In the United States, divestment of certain parts of a company can occur when required by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> before a merger with another firm is approved. A company can divest assets to wholly owned subsidiaries.</p>
<p>It is a process of selling an asset. The largest and likely the most famous<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2013)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> corporate divestiture in history was the 1984 <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a>-mandated <a href="/wiki/The_break_up_of_AT%26T" class="mw-redirect" title="The break up of AT&amp;T">breakup</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bell_System" title="Bell System">Bell System</a> into <a href="/wiki/Old_AT%26T" class="mw-redirect" title="Old AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a> and the seven <a href="/wiki/Baby_Bell" class="mw-redirect" title="Baby Bell">Baby Bells</a>.</p>
<p>Of the 1000 largest global companies, those that are actively involved in both acquiring and divesting create as much as 1.5 to 4.7 percentage points higher shareholder returns than those primarily focused on acquisitions.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_divestment">Method of divestment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Divestment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Method of divestment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some firms are using technology to facilitate the process of divesting some divisions. They post the information about any division that they wish to sell on their website so that it is available to any firm that may be interested in buying the division. For example, Alcoa has established an online showroom of the divisions that are for sale. By communicating the information online, Alcoa has reduced its hotel, travel, and meeting expenses.</p>
<p>Firms use transitional service agreements to increase the strategic benefits of divestitures.</p>
<p>Divestment execution includes five critical work streams: governance, tax, carve-out financial statements, deal-basis information, and operational separation.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Companies often create cross-disciplined teams composed of IT, HR, legal, tax, and other key business units, to implement a business separation.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalization" title="Economic liberalization">economic liberalization</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_India" title="Economy of India">Indian economy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Finance_(India)#Department_of_Disinvestments" title="Ministry of Finance (India)">Ministry of Finance</a> of India had set up a separate <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Finance_(India)#Department_of_Disinvestments" title="Ministry of Finance (India)">Department of Disinvestments</a>.</p>

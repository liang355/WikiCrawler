<p><b>Bochum</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈboːxʊm]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Bochum.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/37/De-Bochum.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Bochum.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Westphalian_language" title="Westphalian language">Westphalian</a>: <i>Baukem</i>) is a city in the state of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and part of the <a href="/wiki/Arnsberg_(region)" title="Arnsberg (region)">Arnsberg region</a>. It is located in the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruhr area">Ruhr area</a> and is surrounded by the cities (in clockwise direction) of <a href="/wiki/Herne,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Herne, Germany">Herne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castrop-Rauxel" title="Castrop-Rauxel">Castrop-Rauxel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dortmund" title="Dortmund">Dortmund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Witten" title="Witten">Witten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hattingen" title="Hattingen">Hattingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essen,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Essen, Germany">Essen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gelsenkirchen" title="Gelsenkirchen">Gelsenkirchen</a>. With a <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of nearly 365,000, it is the 16th most populous city in Germany. Bochum is a part of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr" title="Rhine-Ruhr">Rhine-Ruhr</a> metropolitan area.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_position">Geographical position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographical position">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city lies on the low rolling hills of Bochum land ridge (Bochumer Landrücken), part of the Ruhrhöhen (highest elevations) between the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr" title="Ruhr">Ruhr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emscher" title="Emscher">Emscher</a> rivers at the border of the southern and northern Ruhr coal region. The highest point of the city is at Kemnader Straße (Kemnader Street) in <a href="/wiki/Stiepel" title="Stiepel">Stiepel</a> at 196 metres (643&#160;ft) above sea level; the lowest point is 43 metres (141&#160;ft) at the Blumenkamp in <a href="/wiki/Hordel" title="Hordel">Hordel</a>.</p>
<p>The terrain of Bochum is characterised by rolling hills that rarely have more than three per cent graduation. Steeper graduation can be found at the Harpener Hellweg near the Berghofer Holz nature reserve (3.4%), at Westenfelder Straße in the <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wattenscheid" title="Wattenscheid">Wattenscheid</a> (3.47%), or at Kemnader Straße, which begins at the banks of the Ruhr in <a href="/wiki/Stiepel" title="Stiepel">Stiepel</a> (71&#160;m, 233&#160;ft), and rises to its highest point in the centre of Stiepel (196&#160;m, 643&#160;ft, a 5.1% increase).</p>
<p>The city extends north to south 13.0&#160;km (8.1&#160;mi) and 17.1&#160;km (10.6&#160;mi) east to west. The perimeter of the city limits is 67.2&#160;km (41.8&#160;mi).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>. The geological strata can be visited in the former quarry of Klosterbusch and at the Geological Gardens.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterways">Waterways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Waterways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The urban area is divided into the river <a href="/wiki/Ruhr" title="Ruhr">Ruhr</a> catchment in the south and the <a href="/wiki/Emscher" title="Emscher">Emscher</a> catchment in the north. The Ruhr's tributaries are the Oelbach (where as well a waste water treatment plant is established<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>), Gerther Mühlenbach, Harpener Bach, Langendreer Bach, <a href="/wiki/Lottenbach" title="Lottenbach">Lottenbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B6rsterholzer_Bach" title="Hörsterholzer Bach">Hörsterholzer Bach</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kn%C3%B6selbach" title="Knöselbach">Knöselbach</a>. The Ruhr in combination with upstream reservoirs is also used for drinking water abstraction. The Emscher's tributaries are Hüller Bach with Dorneburger Mühlenbach, Hofsteder Bach, Marbach, Ahbach, Kabeisemannsbach and Goldhammer Bach. The industrial developments in the region since the 19th century were leading to a kind of division of labour between the two river catchments, pumping drinking water from the Ruhr into the municipal supply system and discharging waste water mainly into the Emscher system. Today approximately 10% of the waste water in the Emscher catchment is discharged via the Hüller Bach.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and treated in the centralized waste water treatment plant of the <a href="/wiki/Emschergenossenschaft" title="Emschergenossenschaft">Emschergenossenschaft</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bottrop" title="Bottrop">Bottrop</a>. The ecological restoration of the Emscher tributaries initiated by the Emschergenossenschaft started with the <a href="/wiki/Internationale_Bauausstellung_Emscher_Park" title="Internationale Bauausstellung Emscher Park">Internationale Bauausstellung Emscher Park</a> in 1989.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetation">Vegetation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vegetation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The south of the city has woods, the best known of which are the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Weitmarer_Holz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Weitmarer Holz (page does not exist)">Weitmarer Holz</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weitmarer_Holz" class="extiw" title="de:Weitmarer Holz">de</a>)</span>. These are generally <a href="/wiki/Forest_ecology" title="Forest ecology">mixed forests</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a>. The occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Holly" title="Holly">holly</a> gives evidence of Bochum's <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate climate</a>.</p>

<p>Bochum is divided into six administrative districts with a total of 362,213 inhabitants living in an urban area of 145.4&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (56.1&#160;sq&#160;mi).</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Bochum-Mitte</b> includes <a href="/wiki/Bochum-Innenstadt" title="Bochum-Innenstadt">Innenstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bochum-Hamme" class="mw-redirect" title="Bochum-Hamme">Hamme</a> (including Goldhamme, and Stahlhausen), Hordel, Hofstede, Riemke, Grumme and Altenbochum There are 102,145 inhabitants living in an area of 32.60&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (12.59&#160;sq&#160;mi).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wattenscheid" title="Wattenscheid">Wattenscheid</a></b> includes Wattenscheid-Mitte, Leithe, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnnigfeld" title="Günnigfeld">Günnigfeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bochum-Westenfeld" title="Bochum-Westenfeld">Westenfeld</a>, Sevinghausen, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B6ntrop" title="Höntrop">Höntrop</a>, Munscheid and Eppendorf (which includes Engelsburg and Heide). There are 74,602 inhabitants living in an area of 23.87&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (9.22&#160;sq&#160;mi).</li>
<li><b>North</b> includes Bergen, <a href="/wiki/Bochum-Gerthe" class="mw-redirect" title="Bochum-Gerthe">Gerthe</a>, Harpen (including Rosenberg), Kornharpen, Hiltrop and Voede-Abzweig. There are 37,004 inhabitants living in an area of 18.86&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (7.28&#160;sq&#160;mi).</li>
<li><b>East</b> includes <a href="/wiki/Bochum-Laer" title="Bochum-Laer">Laer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bochum-Werne" title="Bochum-Werne">Werne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Langendreer" title="Langendreer">Langendreer</a> (including Ümmingen and Kaltehardt). There are 55,193 inhabitants living in an area of 23.46&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (9.06&#160;sq&#160;mi).</li>
<li><b>South</b> includes Wiemelhausen (which includes Brenschede, and Ehrenfeld), Stiepel (which includes Haar, Brockhausen and Schrick) and Querenburg (which includes Hustadt and Steinkuhl). There are 50,866 inhabitants living in an area of 27.11&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (10.47&#160;sq&#160;mi).</li>
<li><b>Southwest</b> includes Weitmar (which includes Bärendorf, Mark, and Neuling), Sundern, <a href="/wiki/Bochum-Linden" title="Bochum-Linden">Linden</a> and Dahlhausen. There are 56,510 inhabitants living in an area of 19.50&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (7.53&#160;sq&#160;mi).</li>
</ul>

<p>Bochum dates from the 9th century, when <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> set up a royal court at the junction of two important trade routes. It was first officially mentioned in 1041 as <i>Cofbuokheim</i> in a document of the archbishops of Cologne. In 1321, Count <a href="/wiki/Engelbert_II,_Count_of_Mark" class="mw-redirect" title="Engelbert II, Count of Mark">Engelbert II von der Marck</a> granted Bochum a town charter, but the town remained insignificant until the 19th century, when the <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> mining and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> industries emerged in the Ruhr area, leading to the growth of the entire region. The population of Bochum increased from about 4,500 in 1850 to 100,000 in 1904. Bochum acquired city status, incorporating neighbouring towns and villages. Additional population gains came from immigration, primarily from <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.</p>
<p>After the war, the new state of North Rhine-Westphalia was established, consisting of the Rhineland and Westphalia. Bochum is located in that state.</p>
<p>In the postwar period, Bochum began developing as a cultural centre of the Ruhr area. In 1965, the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruhr University">Ruhr University</a> was opened, the first modern <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> in the Ruhr area and the first to be founded in Germany since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Since the seventies, Bochum's industry has moved from heavy industry to the service sector. Between 1960 and 1980, the coal mines all closed. Other industries, such as <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive</a>, compensated for the loss of jobs. The <a href="/wiki/Opel_Astra" title="Opel Astra">Opel Astra</a> is assembled at the Opel Bochum <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">plant</a>; however, by 2009, the factory was in serious financial difficulties<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and in December 2012, Opel announced that Opel will stop vehicle production Bochum plant in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the course of a comprehensive community reform in 1975, <a href="/wiki/Wattenscheid" title="Wattenscheid">Wattenscheid</a>, a formerly independent city, was integrated into the city of Bochum. A local referendum against the integration failed. In 2007, the new synagogue of the Jewish community of Bochum, Herne und Hattingen was opened. In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> closed down its production plant, causing the loss of thousands of jobs, both at the plant and at local suppliers. 20,000 people showed up to protest against the closing.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Within months, the Canadian high-tech company, <a href="/wiki/Research_in_Motion" class="mw-redirect" title="Research in Motion">Research in Motion</a>, announced plans to open a research facility, its first outside Canada, adding several hundred jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Forbes_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Nazi_era_and_World_War_II">The Nazi era and World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Nazi era and World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-2008-0032,_Bochum,_Zerst%C3%B6rte_St._Marien_Kirche.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-2008-0032%2C_Bochum%2C_Zerst%C3%B6rte_St._Marien_Kirche.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-2008-0032%2C_Bochum%2C_Zerst%C3%B6rte_St._Marien_Kirche.jpg" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-2008-0032%2C_Bochum%2C_Zerst%C3%B6rte_St._Marien_Kirche.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-2008-0032%2C_Bochum%2C_Zerst%C3%B6rte_St._Marien_Kirche.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-2008-0032%2C_Bochum%2C_Zerst%C3%B6rte_St._Marien_Kirche.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-2008-0032%2C_Bochum%2C_Zerst%C3%B6rte_St._Marien_Kirche.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="575" /></a>

<p>On 9 November 1938, <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>, the Jewish citizens of Bochum were attacked. The <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> was set on fire and there was rioting against Jewish citizens. The first Jews from Bochum were deported to <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">Nazi concentration camps</a> and many Jewish institutions and homes were destroyed. Some 500 Jewish citizens are known by name to have been killed in the <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a>, including 19 who were younger than 16 years old. Joseph Klirsfeld was Bochum's rabbi at this time. He and his wife fled to Palestine. In December 1938, the Jewish elementary school teacher <a href="/wiki/Else_Hirsch" title="Else Hirsch">Else Hirsch</a> began organising groups of children and adolescents to be sent to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, sending ten groups in all. Many Jewish children and those from other persecuted groups were taken in by Dutch families and thereby saved from abduction or deportation and death.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because the Ruhr region was an area of high residential density and a centre for the manufacture of weapons, it was a major target in the war. Women with young children, school children and the homeless fled or were evacuated to safer areas, leaving cities largely deserted to the arms industry, coal mines and steel plants and those unable to leave.</p>
<p>Bochum was first bombed heavily in May and June 1943.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On 13 May 1943, the city hall was hit, destroying the top floor, and leaving the next two floors in flames. On 4 November 1944, in an attack involving 700 British bombers, the steel plant, Bochumer Verein, was hit. One of the largest steel plants in Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> more than 10,000 high-explosive and 130,000 incendiary bombs were stored there, setting off a conflagration that destroyed the surrounding neighbourhoods.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Homeless_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homeless-16">[16]</a></sup> An aerial photo shows the devastation.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The town centre of Bochum was a <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">strategic target</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Oil_Campaign_of_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil Campaign of World War II">Oil Campaign</a>. In 150 air raids on Bochum, over 1,300 bombs were dropped on Bochum and <a href="/wiki/Gelsenkirchen" title="Gelsenkirchen">Gelsenkirchen</a>. By the end of the war, 38% of Bochum had been destroyed. 70,000 citizens were homeless and at least 4,095 dead.<sup id="cite_ref-Homeless_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homeless-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Of Bochum's more than 90,000 homes, only 25,000 remained for the 170,000 citizens who survived the war, many by fleeing to other areas. Most of the remaining buildings were damaged, many with only one usable room. Only 1,000 houses in Bochum remained undamaged after the war. Only two of 122 schools remained unscathed; others were totally destroyed. Hunger was rampant. A resident of neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a> was quoted on 23 April 1945 as saying, "Today, I used up my last potato... it will be a difficult time till the new [autumn] potatoes are ready to be picked – if they're not stolen."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Allied ground advance into Germany reached Bochum in April 1945. Encountering desultory resistance, the US <a href="/wiki/79th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="79th Infantry Division (United States)">79th Infantry Division</a> captured the city on 10 April 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war, Bochum was occupied by the <a href="/wiki/British_Zone_of_Occupation" class="mw-redirect" title="British Zone of Occupation">British</a>, who established two <a href="/wiki/DP_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="DP camp">camps</a> to house people <a href="/wiki/Displaced_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Displaced person">displaced by the war</a>. The majority of them were former Polish <i>Zwangsarbeiter</i>, <a href="/wiki/Forced_labour_in_Germany_during_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced labour in Germany during World War II">forced labourers</a>, many of them from the Bochumer Verein.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>More than sixty years after the war, bombs continue to be found in the region, usually by construction workers. One found in October 2008 in Bochum town centre led to the evacuation of 400 and involved hundreds of emergency workers.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A month earlier, a buried bomb exploded in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Hattingen" title="Hattingen">Hattingen</a>, injuring 17 people.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_of_interest">Places of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Places of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<ul>
<li><b>Bochum City Hall</b> was built from 1927–1931 and was designed by architect Karl Roth as a modern office building, but in the Renaissance style, reflecting the industrial era's middle class, inventions and discoveries. There were statues of bronze and stone, and in the city council chambers, a bell tower. The ornate décor gave the Nazis an excuse to hound the then-mayor, who was of Jewish descent, driving him to suicide in 1933. Most of the bronze statues were melted down for the war effort and the stone carvings were damaged by the war, save for some small lion's heads over the entrance. Also left undamaged are two themed courtyard fountains made by August Vogel, the "Fountain of Beauty" and the "Fountain of Happiness", as well as Augusto Vasaris' florentine main entrance, which displays the motto, <i>In Labore Honos</i> (In labour lies honour). In 1951, a set of 28 chimes was installed, manufactured in Bochum. Known for their clarity of tone, they are the first cast steel chimes in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In front of the city hall is a large bell that was made by the Bochum "Verein für Bergbau und Gusstahlfabrikation AG", (Association for Mining and Cast Steel Manufacturing). Displayed at the 1867 Paris World's Fair, it has a diameter of 3.13 metres (10.3&#160;ft) and weighs 15 tonnes (15 long tons; 17 short tons). It was damaged during World War II and can no longer be rung.</li>
<li><b>Altes Brauhaus Rietkötter</b>, the Old Rietkötter Brewing House is one of the oldest houses in Bochum, dating from 1630. Originally a private home, it became a brewery in 1777. After nearly being torn down after the war, it now has preservation status and today houses a restaurant, where they still brew their own beer.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <b>Kaufhaus Kortum</b> department store dates from 1913 and was built as one of the nearly 20 regional stores owned by Alsberg Bros. (Gebr. Alsberg, AG) of Cologne. During the Nazi era, these stores were taken away from their Jewish owners and put into non-Jewish hands. Today, the "Kaufhaus Kortum" building has preservation status and houses an electronics store.</li>
<li>The <b>Friedrich Lueg Haus</b> was built in 1924–1925 as the first high-rise building in Bochum. Contracted by the Lueg Company, the seven-story building was designed by the architect Emil Pohle. It suffered a fire during a bombing raid in 1944 and was renovated after the war. Today, the upper floors are small offices and internet companies. The seven-theatre Bochum Union Cinema rents the ground floor, showing a variety of domestic and international films.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Mutter Wittig</b> is a baroque-style building in the town centre, originally opened as a bakery and inn in 1870. Damaged in World War II, its façade is protected by preservation status. It houses a restaurant and its windows are decorated with displays of old Bochum.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Sparkasse Bochum</b> (Bochum Savings Bank) is a town landmark designed by the architect Wilhelm Kreis. It opened in 1928 and was emblematic of the modern era. It was heavily damaged during the war, but was afterwards restored to its former appearance.</li>
<li>The <b>Schlegel Tower</b> is the only remaining structure of the once-important Schlegel brewery, which closed in 1980.</li>
<li>The <b>Jahrhunderthalle</b> (Hall of the Century), is the former gas and power station of a steel mill built at the turn of the 20th century. With the closing of the mill, the plant was renovated and turned into a three-hall concert and event site with an industrial ambiance.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Dahlhauser Heide</b> is an example of social welfare provided by wealthy German industrialists for their workers. Built in the early 1900s by the <a href="/wiki/Krupp" title="Krupp">Krupp</a> family for their coal mine workers, the modest and tastefully designed two-family houses were to enable self-sufficiency by providing gardens and a stall for a cow. The estate, which has the appearance of a small, rural town, gained preservation status in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Burg_Blankenstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Burg Blankenstein">Blankenstein Castle</a></b> was built in the 13th century by Count <a href="/wiki/Adolf_I_of_the_Mark" class="mw-redirect" title="Adolf I of the Mark">Adolf I of the Mark</a>. Though located in <a href="/wiki/Hattingen" title="Hattingen">Hattingen</a>, it is owned by Bochum and has a significant history. On 8 June 1321, Count <a href="/wiki/Engelbert_II_of_the_Mark" title="Engelbert II of the Mark">Engelbert II of the Mark</a> granted Bochum its town charter there. Today, only the gate and one tower remain.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Haus Kemnade</b> is a moated castle. Though located in the town of <a href="/wiki/Hattingen" title="Hattingen">Hattingen</a>, the castle is property of the city of Bochum in 1921. Documents regarding its earliest dates of construction have been lost; it is first mentioned in 1393. Parts of the castle were built during the Renaissance and baroque periods. The castle's location on the banks of the Ruhr river was changed when the flood of 1486 receded on the opposite side, cutting the castle off from the neighbouring village. The castle remained in private hands till 1921, when it was deeded to the city of Bochum. In 1961, a museum of local history was installed, including a large collection of 16th to 20th century musical instruments. A collection of East Asian objects is also now located there, as well as a satellite of the Bochum Museum and an art exhibition space. There is also a restaurant on site. Behind the castle is a <a href="/wiki/Fachwerk" class="mw-redirect" title="Fachwerk">timber-framed</a> farmhouse from 1800, now a museum exhibiting farm life from the past.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><b>Propsteikirche St. Peter und Paul</b>, is the oldest church in Bochum, built between 785–800 by <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>. It was rebuilt in the 11th century, but was severely damaged by fire in 1517. In 1547, it was again rebuilt, this time in the late Gothic style. The 68-metre (223&#160;ft) high bell tower is one of the landmarks of Bochum. The interior includes a baptismal font from 1175, the reliquary shrine of St. <a href="/wiki/Perpetua" class="mw-redirect" title="Perpetua">Perpetua</a> and her slave Felicitas, and a high altar with a crucifix from 1352.</li>
<li><b>Pauluskirche</b>, is the main Protestant church of the city. After the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>, both Catholics and Lutherans shared the Propsteikirche, often contentiously. In 1655, the Lutherans began to build their own church with the help of donations from the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Courland" title="Courland">Courland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. The church was heavily damaged in a bombing raid on 12 June 1943 and was later rebuilt after the war. Next to the church is a monument to peace. A statue of an old woman searching for a loved one, it is also a memorial to the 4 November 1944 bombing raid on Bochum. <a href="/wiki/Hans_Ehrenberg" title="Hans Ehrenberg">Hans Ehrenberg</a> served as minister here, until he was arrested and sent to <a href="/wiki/Sachsenhausen_concentration_camp" title="Sachsenhausen concentration camp">Sachsenhausen</a> by the Nazis.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <b>Christuskirche</b>, built in the neo-Gothic style, opened in 1879 and was among the most beautiful churches in Europe. In 1931, the room in the steeple was extended to a <a href="/wiki/Cenotaph" title="Cenotaph">cenotaph</a> for those killed in World War I. During an air raid in 1943, the church was destroyed, except for the steeple. After the war, the ruins were integrated into a new, modern structure and the steeple became a memorial dedicated to peace and understanding among nations.</li>
<li>The neo-Gothic <b>Marienkirche</b>, built between 1868–1872, was heavily damaged in World War II (see photo above), but was rebuilt after the war. It is now closed and scheduled for demolition. The stained glass windows have been removed and it has fallen victim to vandalism.</li>
<li><b>Stiepeler Dorfkirche</b> is over 1000 years old and was commemorated by a stamp in 2008. A small church consisting of one room was built by Countess Imma von <a href="/wiki/Stiepel" title="Stiepel">Stiepel</a>. Between 1130 and 1170, the old church was replaced by a Romanesque basilica. Today, the steeple and transept remain. Between 1150 and 1200, the interior walls and ceiling were decorated with a number of Romanesque paintings.</li>
<li>The <b>new synagogue</b>, which opened in 2007, consists of a white cube and stands in contrast to the round shape of the planetarium next door. The façade shows overall a variation on the Solomon's Seal achieved by relocated brickstones. The interior is graced with a gold-coloured canopy.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks_and_Gardens">Parks and Gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Parks and Gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbcaption">

Chinese garden at the <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_der_Ruhr-Universit%C3%A4t_Bochum" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanischer Garten der Ruhr-Universität Bochum">Ruhr University Botanical Gardens</a></div>

<p>Bochum has a municipal zoo, a large municipal park and a number of other gardens and parks. The <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_der_Ruhr-Universit%C3%A4t_Bochum" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanischer Garten der Ruhr-Universität Bochum">Ruhr University Botanical Gardens</a> has thousands of plants from all over the world.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Among others there is a tropical garden, a cactus garden, and a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_garden" title="Chinese garden">Chinese garden</a> designed in the southern Chinese style, the only one of its kind in Germany.</p>
<p>The Geological Garden was the first of its kind in Germany. The nearly 4-acre (16,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) park is the site of an old coal mine, the Zeche Friederika, which operated from 1750 to 1907. In 1962, the property came under environmental protection and a decade later was turned into a geological garden.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other scenic areas include the West Park, Lake Kemnade, Lake Ümmingen and the municipal forest, Weitmarer Holz.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leisure_and_entertainment">Leisure and entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Leisure and entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bochum is a cultural centre of the Ruhr region. There is a municipal theatre, the <a href="/wiki/Schauspielhaus_Bochum" title="Schauspielhaus Bochum">Schauspielhaus Bochum</a>, and about 20 smaller theatres and stages. The musical <a href="/wiki/Starlight_Express#Bochum.2C_Germany" title="Starlight Express">Starlight Express</a>, which opened in 1988, is the longest-running musical in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bermudadreieck">Bermudadreieck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bermudadreieck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bermudadreieck" title="Bermudadreieck">Bermudadreieck</a> (Bermuda Triangle), in the city center of Bochum, functions as the town's nightlife hub. Around sixty different bars and restaurants are located there, serving multicultural cuisine such as Japanese, Chinese, Indian, Italian, Spanish and German gastronomic specialties. Close to the Bermudadreieck opened in 2016 the new venue for classical music the <a href="/wiki/Anneliese_Brost_Musikforum_Ruhr" title="Anneliese Brost Musikforum Ruhr">Anneliese Brost Musikforum Ruhr</a>.</p>

<ul>
<li>Richard Serra's sculpture, "Terminal" is located in the town centre, near the central station. It consists of four 12-metre (over 39 feet) tall steel plates.</li>
<li>Ulrich Rückriem's sculpture, "Ohne Titel" (titled "Untitled"), is at the intersection of Kortumstrasse and Huestrasse. (It is currently in storage because of construction.)</li>
<li>Memorial of the herdsman at Massenberg-Boulevard: shown is the historical person "Fritz Kortebusch", the last herdsman of the town. In 1870 he guided the cattle of the citizens for the last time to the "Vöde", a grassland outside the town limits, which is today the municipal park.</li>
<li>Engelbert fountain at Kortumstrasse, in memory of Earl Engelbert II, who granted a town charter to Bochum in 1321–1324.</li>
<li>Jobsiade-fountain at Husemann-Square. Shown is a scene of the examination of Hieronymus Jobs, the main character of the "Jobsiade", a comical poem of the poet Carl Armold Kortum.</li>

<li>Collection of sculptures inside the municipal park.</li>
<li>The bell in front of the city hall serves as a reminder of the improvement of steel-casting in Bochum. The bell was built in 1867 for the Paris World's Fair.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stolpersteine" class="mw-redirect" title="Stolpersteine">Stolpersteine</a>, (literally, "stumbling stones") are small, cobblestone-sized, brass <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaques" class="mw-redirect" title="Commemorative plaques">commemorative plaques</a> which are set in sidewalks <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_by_country_that_have_Stolpersteine" class="mw-redirect" title="List of cities by country that have Stolpersteine">all over Europe</a>, marking the homes or work places of Jews and others who were arrested and murdered during the Nazi era. There are 38 stolpersteine in Bochum.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cenotaph for the victims of the mine disaster at "Vereinigte Präsident" in 1936. The sculpture was created by Wilhelm Wulff. Strict guidelines for artwork were in effect during the Nazi dictatorship, yet the sculpture follows only a few of them. The inscription also avoids typical Nazi phraseology.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bochum is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> network by the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_40" title="Bundesautobahn 40">A 40</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_43" title="Bundesautobahn 43">A 43</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_44" title="Bundesautobahn 44">A 44</a> autobahns. In addition, Bochum has a ring road, built to expressway standards, consisting of four segments; the Donezk, Oviedo, Nordhausen and Sheffield-Ring roads. It serves as a three-quarter loop around central Bochum and begins and ends at Autobahn A40. <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_University_Bochum" title="Ruhr University Bochum">Ruhr University Bochum</a> is also served by an expressway running from the Nordhausen-Ring to Autobahn A43. Until 2012, a new interchange (Dreieck Bochum-West) between the Donezk-Ring and Autobahn A40 is being constructed within tight parameters due to the existence of a nearby factory.</p>
<p>Apart from the autobahns and expressways, there is also a small ring road around the centre of Bochum, where most roads radiating out of Bochum begin. Most main roads in Bochum are multi-lane roads with traffic lights. Bochum is also served by the <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_51" title="Bundesstraße 51">Bundesstraße 51</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_226&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bundesstraße 226 (page does not exist)">Bundesstraße 226</a>. B51 runs to Herne and Hattingen, and B226 runs to Gelsenkirchen and Witten.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bochum has a <a href="/wiki/Bochum_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bochum Hauptbahnhof">central station</a> situated on the <a href="/wiki/Witten/Dortmund%E2%80%93Oberhausen/Duisburg_railway" title="Witten/Dortmund–Oberhausen/Duisburg railway">line from Duisburg to Dortmund</a>, connecting the city to the long-distance network of <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a> as well as to the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Ruhr" title="Rhine-Ruhr">Rhine-Ruhr</a> <a href="/wiki/S-Bahn" class="mw-redirect" title="S-Bahn">S-Bahn</a> network.</p>
<h3><span id="Bus,_Tram,_Underground"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus.2C_Tram.2C_Underground">Bus, Tram, Underground</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Bus, Tram, Underground">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Local service is supplied mainly by <a href="/wiki/BOGESTRA" title="BOGESTRA">BOGESTRA</a>, a joint venture handling transportation between the cities of Bochum and Gelsenkirchen. The <a href="/wiki/Bochum_Stadtbahn" title="Bochum Stadtbahn">Bochum Stadtbahn</a> is a single underground line connecting the University of Bochum to <a href="/wiki/Herne,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Herne, Germany">Herne</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Bochum/Gelsenkirchen" title="Trams in Bochum/Gelsenkirchen">Bochum/Gelsenkirchen tramway network</a> is made up of several lines, partially underground, connecting to Gelsenkirchen, Hattingen and <a href="/wiki/Witten" title="Witten">Witten</a>. Public transport in the city is priced according to the fare system of the <a href="/wiki/Verkehrsverbund_Rhein-Ruhr" title="Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Ruhr">VRR</a> transport association.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterways_2">Waterways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Waterways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As one of the few <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruhr area">Ruhr area</a> cities, Bochum is not directly connected with the German waterway net; the closest link is in the more northern located <a href="/wiki/Herne_Common" title="Herne Common">Herne</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Herne_Canal" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine-Herne Canal">Rhine-Herne Canal</a>. In the south the border of Bochum is marked by the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr" title="Ruhr">Ruhr</a>. Up to the first half of the 19th century it was one of the most-travelled rivers in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and was mainly used for coal departure. Despite of tour ships, the navigation time ended long ago.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air">Air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bochum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The closest airports are <a href="/wiki/Essen/M%C3%BClheim_Airport" title="Essen/Mülheim Airport">Essen/Mülheim Airport</a> (27&#160;km), <a href="/wiki/Dortmund_Airport" title="Dortmund Airport">Dortmund Airport</a> (31&#160;km) and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf_Airport" title="Düsseldorf Airport">Düsseldorf Airport</a> (47&#160;km). To reach the airport in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>, there are <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">ICE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intercity_(Deutsche_Bahn)" title="Intercity (Deutsche Bahn)">InterCity</a>, RE and S railway lines. Other reachable airports are the <a href="/wiki/Cologne_Bonn_Airport" title="Cologne Bonn Airport">Cologne Bonn Airport</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Weeze_Airport" title="Weeze Airport">Weeze Airport</a>, the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Osnabr%C3%BCck_International_Airport" title="Münster Osnabrück International Airport">Münster Osnabrück International Airport</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Paderborn_Lippstadt_Airport" title="Paderborn Lippstadt Airport">Paderborn Lippstadt Airport</a>.</p>

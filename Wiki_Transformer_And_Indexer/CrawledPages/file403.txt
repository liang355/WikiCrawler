<p><b>Magdeburg</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈmakdəbʊɐ̯k]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Magdeburg.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1b/Magdeburg.ogg" class="internal" title="Magdeburg.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low Saxon</a>: <i>Meideborg</i>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈmaˑɪdebɔɐ̯x]</a></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital city</a> and the second largest city of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Magdeburg is situated on the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> River and was one of the most important medieval cities of Europe.</p>
<p>Emperor <a href="/wiki/Otto_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto I, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto I</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>, founder of the archbishopric of Magdeburg, was buried in the town's cathedral after his death. Magdeburg's version of <a href="/wiki/German_town_law" title="German town law">German town law</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg_rights" title="Magdeburg rights">Magdeburg rights</a>, spread throughout <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>. The city is also well known for the 1631 <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Magdeburg" title="Sack of Magdeburg">Sack of Magdeburg</a>, which hardened <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> resistance during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>. Prior to it Magdeburg was one of the largest German cities and a notable member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>. Magdeburg was destroyed twice in its history.</p>
<p>Magdeburg is the site of two universities, the <a href="/wiki/Otto-von-Guericke_University_Magdeburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg">Otto-von-Guericke University</a> and the Magdeburg-Stendal University of Applied Sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nowadays Magdeburg is a traffic junction as well as an <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> and trading centre. The production of chemical products, steel, paper and textiles are of particular economic significance, along with <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a> and plant engineering, ecotechnology and life-cycle management, health management and logistics.</p>
<p>In 2005 Magdeburg celebrated its 1200th anniversary. In June 2013 Magdeburg was hit by <a href="/wiki/2013_European_floods" title="2013 European floods">record breaking flooding</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magdeburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Founded by <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> in 805 as Magadoburg (probably from <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>magado</i> for <i>big</i>, <i>mighty</i> and <i>burga</i> for <i>fortress</i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>), the town was fortified in 919 by King <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Fowler" title="Henry the Fowler">Henry I the Fowler</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Hungarian people">Magyars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic peoples">Slavs</a>. In 929 the city went to <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Elder" title="Edward the Elder">Edward the Elder</a>'s daughter <a href="/wiki/Eadgyth" title="Eadgyth">Edith</a>, through her marriage to Henry's son <a href="/wiki/Otto_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto I, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto I</a>, as a <i><a href="/wiki/Dower" title="Dower">Morgengabe</a></i> — a Germanic customary gift received by the new bride from the groom and his family after the wedding night. Edith loved the town and often lived there;<sup id="cite_ref-Catholic_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catholic-5">[5]</a></sup> at her death she was buried in the crypt of the <a href="/wiki/Benedictine" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> abbey of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Maurice" title="Saint Maurice">Saint Maurice</a>, later rebuilt as the cathedral. In 937, Magdeburg was the seat of a royal assembly. Otto I repeatedly visited Magdeburg and was also buried in the cathedral. He granted the abbey the right to income from various tithes and to <a href="/wiki/Corv%C3%A9e" title="Corvée">corvée</a> labour from the surrounding countryside.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Magdeburg" title="Archbishopric of Magdeburg">Archbishopric of Magdeburg</a> was founded in 968 at the <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">Ravenna</a>; <a href="/wiki/Adalbert_(Archbishop_of_Magdeburg)" class="mw-redirect" title="Adalbert (Archbishop of Magdeburg)">Adalbert of Magdeburg</a> was consecrated as its first archbishop. The archbishopric under Adalbert included the bishoprics of <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Havelberg" title="Bishopric of Havelberg">Havelberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Brandenburg" title="Bishopric of Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Merseburg" title="Bishopric of Merseburg">Merseburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Dresden-Meissen" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Dresden-Meissen">Meissen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Naumburg-Zeitz" title="Bishopric of Naumburg-Zeitz">Naumburg-Zeitz</a>. The archbishops played a prominent role in the <a href="/wiki/Ostsiedlung" title="Ostsiedlung">German colonisation</a> of the Slavic lands east of the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> river.</p>
<p>In 1035 Magdeburg received a patent giving the city the right to hold trade exhibitions and conventions, which form the basis of the later family of <a href="/wiki/German_town_law" title="German town law">city laws</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg_rights" title="Magdeburg rights">Magdeburg rights</a>. These laws were adopted and modified throughout Central and Eastern Europe. Visitors from many countries began to trade with Magdeburg.</p>

<p>In the 13th century, Magdeburg became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>. With more than 20,000 inhabitants Magdeburg was one of the largest cities in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. The town had an active maritime commerce on the west (towards <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>), with the countries of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, and maintained traffic and communication with the interior (for example <a href="/wiki/Braunschweig" title="Braunschweig">Brunswick</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Catholic_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catholic-5">[5]</a></sup> The citizens constantly struggled against the archbishop, becoming nearly independent from him by the end of the 15th century.</p>
<p>In about Easter 1497, the then twelve-year-old <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> attended school in Magdeburg, where he was exposed to the teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Brethren_of_the_Common_Life" title="Brethren of the Common Life">Brethren of the Common Life</a>. In 1524, he was called to Magdeburg, where he preached and caused the city's defection from <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholicism</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> had quickly found adherents in the city, where Luther had been a schoolboy. Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> repeatedly outlawed the unruly town, which had joined the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alliance_of_Torgau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alliance of Torgau (page does not exist)">Alliance of Torgau</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Schmalkaldic_League" title="Schmalkaldic League">Schmalkaldic League</a>. Because it had not accepted the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Interim" title="Augsburg Interim">Augsburg Interim</a> (1548), the city, by the emperor's commands, was besieged (1550–1551) by <a href="/wiki/Maurice,_Elector_of_Saxony" title="Maurice, Elector of Saxony">Maurice, Elector of Saxony</a>, but it retained its independence. The rule of the archbishop was replaced by that of various administrators belonging to Protestant dynasties. In the following years Magdeburg gained a reputation as a stronghold of Protestantism and became the first major city to publish the writings of Luther. In Magdeburg, <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Flacius" title="Matthias Flacius">Matthias Flacius</a> and his companions wrote their anti-Catholic pamphlets and the <i><a href="/wiki/Magdeburg_Centuries" title="Magdeburg Centuries">Magdeburg Centuries</a></i>, in which they argued that the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> had become the kingdom of the <a href="/wiki/Antichrist" title="Antichrist">Antichrist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Catholic_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catholic-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1631, during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">imperial</a> troops under <a href="/wiki/Johann_Tserclaes,_Count_of_Tilly" title="Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly">Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly</a>, stormed the city and committed a massacre, killing about 20,000 inhabitants and burning the town in the <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Magdeburg" title="Sack of Magdeburg">sack of Magdeburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The city had withstood a first siege in 1629 by <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_von_Wallenstein" title="Albrecht von Wallenstein">Albrecht von Wallenstein</a>. After the war, a population of only 4,000 remained. Under the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> (1648), Magdeburg was to be assigned to <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Prussia" title="Brandenburg-Prussia">Brandenburg-Prussia</a> after the death of the administrator <a href="/wiki/August,_Duke_of_Saxe-Weissenfels" class="mw-redirect" title="August, Duke of Saxe-Weissenfels">August of Saxe-Weissenfels</a>, as the semi-autonomous <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Magdeburg" title="Duchy of Magdeburg">Duchy of Magdeburg</a>. This occurred in 1680.</p>

<p>In the course of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, the fortress surrendered to <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">French</a> troops in 1806. The city was annexed to the French-controlled <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">Kingdom of Westphalia</a> in the 1807 <a href="/wiki/Treaties_of_Tilsit" title="Treaties of Tilsit">Treaty of Tilsit</a>. King <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bonaparte" title="Jérôme Bonaparte">Jérôme</a> appointed <a href="/w/index.php?title=Count_Heinrich_von_Blumenthal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Count Heinrich von Blumenthal (page does not exist)">Count Heinrich von Blumenthal</a> as mayor. In 1815, after the Napoleonic Wars, Magdeburg was made the capital of the new <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussian</a> <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Saxony" title="Province of Saxony">Province of Saxony</a>. In 1912, the old fortress was dismantled, and in 1908, the municipality <a href="/wiki/Ohrekreis" title="Ohrekreis">Rothensee</a> became part of Magdeburg.</p>

</div>
<p>Magdeburg was heavily bombed by the British and American air forces during the Second World War. The RAF bombing raid on the night of 16 January 1945, destroyed much of the city. The death toll is estimated at 2000-2500.</p>
<p>Near the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the city of about 340,000 became capital of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Magdeburg" title="Province of Magdeburg">Province of Magdeburg</a>. <a href="/wiki/Brabag" title="Brabag">Brabag</a>'s Magdeburg/Rothensee plant that produced <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">synthetic oil</a> from <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> coal was a target of the <a href="/wiki/Oil_Campaign_of_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil Campaign of World War II">Oil Campaign of World War II</a>. The impressive <i><a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%BCnderzeit" title="Gründerzeit">Gründerzeit</a></i> suburbs north of the city, called the Nordfront, were destroyed as well as the city's main street with its Baroque buildings. It was occupied by <a href="/wiki/Ninth_United_States_Army" title="Ninth United States Army">9th US Army</a> troops on 19 April 1945 and was left to <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> on 1 July 1945. <a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">Post-war</a> the area was part of the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Occupation_Zones_in_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Allied Occupation Zones in Germany">Soviet Zone of Occupation</a> and many of the remaining pre-World War II city buildings were destroyed, with only a few buildings near the cathedral and in the southern part of the old city being restored to their pre-war state. Before the <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">reunification of Germany</a>, many surviving <i>Gründerzeit</i> buildings were left uninhabited and, after years of degradation, waiting for demolition. From 1949 on until German reunification on 3 October 1990, Magdeburg belonged to the <a href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="German Democratic Republic">German Democratic Republic</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_German_reunification">Since German reunification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magdeburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Since German reunification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990 Magdeburg became the capital of the new state of <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a> within reunified Germany. Huge parts of the city and its centre were also rebuilt in a modern style. Its economy is one of the fastest-growing in the former East German states.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005 Magdeburg celebrated its 1200th anniversary.</p>
<p>The city was hit by <a href="/wiki/2013_European_floods" title="2013 European floods">2013 European floods</a>. Authorities declared a state of emergency and said they expected the Elbe river to rise higher than in 2002. In Magdeburg, with water levels of five metres (16&#160;ft) above normal, about 23,000 residents had to leave their homes on 9 June.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cathedral">Cathedral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magdeburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cathedral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Magdeburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral of Magdeburg">Cathedral of Magdeburg</a></div>
<p>One of Magdeburg's most impressive buildings is the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Germany">Lutheran</a> Cathedral of Saints <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Alexandria" title="Catherine of Alexandria">Catherine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Maurice" title="Saint Maurice">Maurice</a> with a height of 104&#160;m (341.21&#160;ft), making it the tallest church building of eastern Germany. It is notable for its beautiful and unique sculptures, especially the "Twelve Virgins" at the Northern Gate, the depictions of <a href="/wiki/Otto_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto I, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto I the Great</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Eadgyth" title="Eadgyth">Editha</a> as well as the statues of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Maurice" title="Saint Maurice">St Maurice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Alexandria" title="Catherine of Alexandria">St Catherine</a>. The predecessor of the cathedral was a church built in 937 within an abbey, called St. Maurice. Emperor Otto I the Great was buried here beside his wife in 973. St. Maurice burnt to ashes in 1207. The exact location of that church remained unknown for a long time. The foundations were rediscovered in May 2003, revealing a building 80&#160;m (262.47&#160;ft) long and 41&#160;m (134.51&#160;ft) wide.</p>
<p>The construction of the new church lasted 300 years. The cathedral of Saints Catherine and Maurice was the first <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> church building in Germany. The building of the steeples was completed as late as 1520.</p>
<p>While the cathedral was virtually the only building to survive the massacres of the Thirty Years' War, it suffered damage in World War II. It was soon rebuilt and completed in 1955.</p>
<p>The square in front of the cathedral (also called the <i>Neuer Markt</i>, or "new marketplace") was occupied by an imperial palace (<i>Kaiserpfalz</i>), which was destroyed in the fire of 1207. The stones from the ruin were used for the building of the cathedral. The presumed remains of the palace were excavated in the 1960s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sights">Other sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magdeburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Unser Lieben Frauen</i> Monastery (Our Lady), 11th century, containing the church of St. Mary. Today a museum for Modern Art. Home of the National Collection of Small Art Statues of the GDR (Nationale Sammlung Kleinkunstplastiken der DDR).</li>
<li>The <i>Magdeburger Reiter</i> ("Magdeburg Rider", 1240), the first free-standing equestrian sculpture north of the Alps. It probably depicts the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Otto_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto I, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto I</a>.</li>
<li>Town hall (1698). This building had stood on the market place since the 13th century, but it was destroyed in the Thirty Years' War; the new town hall was built in a <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> style influenced by Dutch architecture. It was renovated and re-opened in Oct 2005.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Saxony-Anhalt" title="Landtag of Saxony-Anhalt">Landtag</a>; the seat of the government of Saxony-Anhalt with its <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> façade built in 1724.</li>
<li>monuments depicting <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Guericke" title="Otto von Guericke">Otto von Guericke</a> (1907), <a href="/wiki/Eike_von_Repgow" class="mw-redirect" title="Eike von Repgow">Eike von Repkow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_von_Steuben" title="Friedrich Wilhelm von Steuben">Friedrich Wilhelm von Steuben</a>.</li>
<li>Ruins of the greatest fortress of the former <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magdeburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Otto-von-Guericke_University_Magdeburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg">Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Otto-von-Guericke_University_Magdeburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg">Otto-von-Guericke University Magdeburg</a> (German: Otto-von-Guericke-Universität Magdeburg) was founded in 1993 and is one of the youngest universities in Germany. The university in Magdeburg has about 13,000 students in nine faculties. There are 11,700 papers published in international journals from this institute.</p>
<p>The Magdeburg-Stendal University of Applied Sciences was founded in 1991. There are 30 direct study programs in five departments in Magdeburg and two departments in Stendal. The university has more than 130 professors and approximately 4,500 students at Magdeburg and 1,900 at Stendal.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_sports">Culture and sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magdeburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Culture and sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Weihnachtsmarkt_MD.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Weihnachtsmarkt_MD.jpg/220px-Weihnachtsmarkt_MD.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Weihnachtsmarkt_MD.jpg/330px-Weihnachtsmarkt_MD.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Weihnachtsmarkt_MD.jpg/440px-Weihnachtsmarkt_MD.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Weihnachtsmarkt_MD.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Magdeburg Christmas market</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Magdeburg has a municipal theatre, <a href="/wiki/Theater_Magdeburg" title="Theater Magdeburg">Theater Magdeburg</a>.</p>
<p>Magdeburg has a proud history of sports teams, with football proving the most popular. <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Magdeburg" title="1. FC Magdeburg">1. FC Magdeburg</a> currently play in the <a href="/wiki/3._Liga" title="3. Liga">3. Liga</a>. The now defunct clubs <a href="/wiki/SV_Victoria_96_Magdeburg" title="SV Victoria 96 Magdeburg">SV Victoria 96 Magdeburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cricket_Viktoria_Magdeburg" title="Cricket Viktoria Magdeburg">Cricket Viktoria Magdeburg</a> were among the first football clubs in Germany. <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Magdeburg" title="1. FC Magdeburg">1. FC Magdeburg</a> is the only East German football club to have won a European club football competition. There is also the very successful handball team, <a href="/wiki/SC_Magdeburg" title="SC Magdeburg">SC Magdeburg</a> who are the first German team to win the <a href="/wiki/EHF_Champions_League" title="EHF Champions League">EHF Champions League</a>.</p>
<p>The city is portrayed as a rebel castle on the strategy map of <i><a href="/wiki/Medieval_II:_Total_War" title="Medieval II: Total War">Medieval II: Total War</a></i>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Discus_throw" title="Discus throw">discus</a> was re-discovered in Magdeburg in the 1870s by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Georg_Kohlrausch" title="Christian Georg Kohlrausch">Christian Georg Kohlrausch</a>, a gymnastics teacher.</p>
<p>Magdeburg is well known for its <a href="/wiki/Christmas_market" title="Christmas market">Christmas market</a>, which is an attraction for 1.5 million visitors every year. Other events are the <i>Stadtfest</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Street_Day" title="Christopher Street Day">Christopher Street Day</a></i>, <i>Elbe in Flames</i> and the <i>Europafest Magdeburg</i></p>

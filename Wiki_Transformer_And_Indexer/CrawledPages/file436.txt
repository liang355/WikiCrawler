<p><b>Potsdam</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈpɔtsdam]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Potsdam.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d4/De-Potsdam.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Potsdam.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is the capital and largest city of the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">federal state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>. It directly borders the German capital <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> and is part of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin/Brandenburg_Metropolitan_Region" title="Berlin/Brandenburg Metropolitan Region">Berlin/Brandenburg Metropolitan Region</a>. It is situated on the <a href="/wiki/Havel" title="Havel">River Havel</a>, 24 kilometres (15 miles) southwest of Berlin's city centre.</p>
<p>Potsdam was a residence of the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> kings and the <a href="/wiki/German_Emperor" title="German Emperor">German Kaiser</a>, until 1918. Its planning embodied ideas of <a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Enlightenment" class="mw-redirect" title="The Age of Enlightenment">The Age of Enlightenment</a>: through a careful balance of architecture and landscape Potsdam was intended as "a picturesque, pastoral dream" which reminded its residents of their relationship with nature and reason.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around the city there are a series of interconnected lakes and cultural landmarks, in particular the parks and palaces of <a href="/wiki/Sanssouci" title="Sanssouci">Sanssouci</a>, the largest <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in Germany. The <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Conference" title="Potsdam Conference">Potsdam Conference</a> in 1945 was held at the palace <a href="/wiki/Cecilienhof" title="Cecilienhof">Cecilienhof</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Potsdam-Babelsberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsdam-Babelsberg">Babelsberg</a>, in the south-eastern part of Potsdam, was a major film production studio before the 1930s and has enjoyed success as a major center of European film production since the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Studio_Babelsberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Studio Babelsberg">Filmstudio Babelsberg</a> is the oldest large-scale film studio in the world.</p>
<p>Potsdam developed into a centre of science in Germany in the 19th century. Today, there are three public colleges, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Potsdam" title="University of Potsdam">University of Potsdam</a>, and more than 30 <a href="/wiki/Research_institute" title="Research institute">research institutes</a> in the city.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbcaption">

Templiner See south of Potsdam</div>

<p>The area was formed from a series of large <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> left after the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>. Today, the city is three-quarters green space, with just a quarter as urban area.</p>
<p>There are about 20 lakes and rivers in and around Potsdam, such as the <a href="/wiki/Havel" title="Havel">Havel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Griebnitzsee" title="Griebnitzsee">Griebnitzsee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Templiner_See" title="Templiner See">Templiner See</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiefer_See" title="Tiefer See">Tiefer See</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jungfernsee" title="Jungfernsee">Jungfernsee</a>, Teltowkanal, <a href="/wiki/Heiliger_See" title="Heiliger See">Heiliger See</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sacrower_See" title="Sacrower See">Sacrower See</a>. The highest point is the 114-metre (374&#160;ft) high <i>Kleiner Ravensberg</i>.</p>
<p>Potsdam is divided into seven historic city districts and nine new <i>Ortsteile</i> (villages), which joined the city in 2003. The appearances of the city districts are quite different. The districts in the north and in the centre consist mainly of historical buildings, the south of the city is dominated by larger areas of newer buildings.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Potsdam has an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">Oceanic climate</a>, with cool, snowy winters and relatively cool summers. The average winter high temperature is 3.5&#160;°C (38.3&#160;°F), with a low of −1.7&#160;°C (28.9&#160;°F). Snow is common in the winter. Spring and autumn are short. Summers are mild, with a high of 23.6&#160;°C (74.5&#160;°F) and a low of 12.7&#160;°C (54.9&#160;°F).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name "Potsdam" originally seems to have been <i><a href="/wiki/Potsdam#History" title="Potsdam">Poztupimi</a></i>. A common theory is that it derives from an old <a href="/wiki/West_Slavic_languages" title="West Slavic languages">West Slavonic</a> term meaning "beneath the oaks",<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> i.e., the corrupted <i>pod dubmi/dubimi</i> (<i>pod</i> "beneath, <i>dub</i> "oak"). However some question this explanation.<sup id="cite_ref-kopish_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kopish-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The area around Potsdam shows occupancy since the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> and was part of <i><a href="/wiki/Magna_Germania" class="mw-redirect" title="Magna Germania">Magna Germania</a></i> as described by <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">great migrations</a> of the Germanic peoples, <a href="/wiki/Slav" class="mw-redirect" title="Slav">Slavs</a> moved in and Potsdam was probably founded after the 7th century as a settlement of the <a href="/wiki/Hevelli" title="Hevelli">Hevelli</a> tribe centred on a castle. It was first mentioned in a document in 993 AD as <i>Poztupimi</i>, when <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Otto_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Otto III">Emperor Otto III</a> gifted the territory to the <a href="/wiki/Quedlinburg_Abbey" title="Quedlinburg Abbey">Quedlinburg Abbey</a>, then led by his aunt <a href="/wiki/Matilda,_Abbess_of_Quedlinburg" title="Matilda, Abbess of Quedlinburg">Matilda</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kopish_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kopish-5">[5]</a></sup> By 1317, it was mentioned as a small town. It gained its <a href="/wiki/Town_charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Town charter">town charter</a> in 1345. In 1573, it was still a small <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a> of 2,000 inhabitants. Potsdam lost nearly half of its population due to the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> (1618–1648).</p>
<p>A continuous <a href="/wiki/Hohenzollern" class="mw-redirect" title="Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a> possession since 1415, Potsdam became prominent, when it was chosen in 1660 as the hunting residence of <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_I_of_Brandenburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Friedrich Wilhelm I of Brandenburg">Frederick William I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Elector</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Prussia" title="Brandenburg-Prussia">Brandenburg</a>, the core of the powerful state that later became the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a>. It also housed <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> <a href="/wiki/Barracks" title="Barracks">barracks</a>.</p>

<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Potsdam" title="Edict of Potsdam">Edict of Potsdam</a> in 1685, Potsdam became a centre of European immigration. Its religious freedom attracted people from <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (<a href="/wiki/Huguenot" class="mw-redirect" title="Huguenot">Huguenots</a>), <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>. The edict accelerated population growth and economic recovery.</p>
<p>Later, the city became a full residence of the <a href="/wiki/Prussian" class="mw-redirect" title="Prussian">Prussian</a> royal family. The buildings of the royal residences were built mainly during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick II of Prussia">Frederick the Great</a>. One of these is the <a href="/wiki/Sanssouci" title="Sanssouci">Sanssouci Palace</a> (French: "without cares", by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wenzeslaus_von_Knobelsdorff" title="Georg Wenzeslaus von Knobelsdorff">Georg Wenzeslaus von Knobelsdorff</a>, 1744), famed for its formal gardens and <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> interiors. Other royal residences include the <a href="/wiki/New_Palace_(Potsdam)" title="New Palace (Potsdam)">New Palace</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Orangery_Palace" title="Orangery Palace">Orangery</a>.</p>
<p>In 1815, at the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Brandenburg" title="Province of Brandenburg">Province of Brandenburg</a>, Potsdam became the provincial capital until 1918, however, interrupted and succeeded by Berlin as provincial capital between 1827 and 1843, and after 1918. The province comprised two governorates named after their capitals Potsdam and <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_(Oder)" title="Frankfurt (Oder)">Frankfurt (Oder)</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governorate_of_Potsdam">Governorate of Potsdam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Governorate of Potsdam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Between 1815 and 1945 the city of Potsdam served as capital of the governorate of Potsdam (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regierungsbezirk_Potsdam" class="extiw" title="de:Regierungsbezirk Potsdam">Regierungsbezirk Potsdam</a></span>). The <i><a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">Regierungsbezirk</a></i> encompassed the former districts of <a href="/wiki/Uckermark" title="Uckermark">Uckermark</a>, the Mark of Priegnitz, and the greater part of the <a href="/wiki/Kurmark" title="Kurmark">Middle March</a>. It was situated between <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pomerania_(1815%E2%80%931945)" title="Province of Pomerania (1815–1945)">Province of Pomerania</a> on the north, and the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Saxony" title="Province of Saxony">Province of Saxony</a> on the south and west (<a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, with a small surrounding district, was an urban governorate and enclave within the governorate of Potsdam between 1815 and 1822, then it merged as urban district into the governorate only to be disentangled again from Potsdam governorate in 1875, becoming an own distinct province-like entity on 1 April 1881). Towards the north west the governorate was bounded by the rivers <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> and the Havel, and on the north east by the <a href="/wiki/Oder" title="Oder">Oder</a>. The south eastern boundary was to the neighbouring governorate of Frankfurt (Oder). About 500,000 inhabitants lived in the Potsdam governorate, which covered an area of about 20,700 square kilometres (7,992&#160;sq&#160;mi), divided into thirteen rural <a href="/wiki/Kreis_in_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Kreis in Prussia">districts</a>, partially named after their capitals:<sup id="cite_ref-Curtis-1839_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Curtis-1839-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> was the official capital of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> and later of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>, but the court remained in Potsdam, where many government officials settled. In 1914, the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_II_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelm II of Germany">Wilhelm II</a> signed the Declaration of War in the Neues Palais. The city lost its status as a second capital in 1918, when <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_II_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelm II of Germany">Wilhelm II</a> abdicated at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>
<p>At the start of the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> in 1933 there was a ceremonial handshake between <a href="/wiki/Reichspr%C3%A4sident" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichspräsident">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_von_Hindenburg" title="Paul von Hindenburg">Paul von Hindenburg</a> and the new <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany_(German_Reich)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chancellor of Germany (German Reich)">Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> on 21 March 1933 in Potsdam's <a href="/wiki/Garrison_Church_(Potsdam)" title="Garrison Church (Potsdam)">Garrison Church</a> in what became known as the "Day of Potsdam". This symbolised a coalition of the military (<i><a href="/wiki/Reichswehr" title="Reichswehr">Reichswehr</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazism</a>. Potsdam was severely damaged in bombing raids during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cecilienhof" title="Cecilienhof">Cecilienhof Palace</a> was the scene of the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Conference" title="Potsdam Conference">Potsdam Conference</a> from 17 July, to 2 August 1945, at which the victorious <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> leaders (<a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a>; <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> and his successor, <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>) met to decide the future of Germany and postwar Europe in general. The conference ended with the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Agreement" title="Potsdam Agreement">Potsdam Agreement</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Declaration" title="Potsdam Declaration">Potsdam Declaration</a>.</p>

<p>The government of <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> (formally known as the <a href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="German Democratic Republic">German Democratic Republic</a> (German: Deutsche Demokratische Republik, DDR)) tried to remove symbols of Prussian <a href="/wiki/Militarism" title="Militarism">militarism</a>. Many historic buildings, some of them badly damaged in the war, were demolished.</p>
<p>When in 1946 the remainder of the Province of Brandenburg west of the <a href="/wiki/Oder-Nei%C3%9Fe_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Oder-Neiße line">Oder-Neiße line</a> was constituted as the state of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, Potsdam became its capital. In 1952 the GDR disestablished its federative states and replaced them by smaller new <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_East_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Subdivisions of East Germany">East German administrative districts</a>. Potsdam became the capital of the new <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_(Bezirk)" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsdam (Bezirk)">Bezirk Potsdam</a> until 1990.</p>
<p>Potsdam, south-west of Berlin, lay just outside <a href="/wiki/West_Berlin" title="West Berlin">West Berlin</a> after the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a>. The walling off of West Berlin not only isolated Potsdam from West Berlin, but also doubled commuting times to <a href="/wiki/East_Berlin" title="East Berlin">East Berlin</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Glienicke_Bridge" title="Glienicke Bridge">Glienicke Bridge</a> across the Havel connected the city to West Berlin and was the scene of some <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> exchanges of <a href="/wiki/Spy" class="mw-redirect" title="Spy">spies</a>.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a>, Potsdam became the capital of the newly re-established state of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>. There are many ideas and efforts to reconstruct the original appearance of the city, most remarkably the <a href="/wiki/Stadtschloss,_Potsdam" class="mw-redirect" title="Stadtschloss, Potsdam">Potsdam City Palace</a> and the Garrison Church.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 2000 Potsdam is one of the fastest growing cities in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City">City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Potsdam has had a <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> (<i>Bürgermeister</i>) and <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city council</a> since the 15th century. From 1809 the city council was elected, with a mayor (<i>Oberbürgermeister</i>) at its head. During the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> the mayor was selected by the <a href="/wiki/NSDAP" class="mw-redirect" title="NSDAP">NSDAP</a> and the city council was dissolved; it was reconstituted in token form after 1945, but free elections did not take place until after <a href="/wiki/Reunification" class="mw-redirect" title="Reunification">reunification</a>.</p>
<p>Today, the city council is the city's central administrative authority. Local elections took place on 26 October 2003 and again in 2008. Between 1990 and 1999, the Chairman of the City Council was known as the "Town President" but today the post is the "Chairman of the City Council". The mayor is elected directly by the population.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_State">Federal State</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Federal State">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Landtag_Brandenburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Landtag Brandenburg">Landtag Brandenburg</a>, the parliament of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">federal state</a> of Brandenburg is in Potsdam. It is housed in the <a href="/wiki/Stadtschloss,_Potsdam" class="mw-redirect" title="Stadtschloss, Potsdam">Potsdam City Palace</a> since 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Potsdam, included in the fare zone "C" (<i>Tarifbereich C</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>'s public transport area and fare zones A and B of its own public transport area, is served by the <a href="/wiki/S7_(Berlin)" title="S7 (Berlin)">S7</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Berlin_S-Bahn" title="Berlin S-Bahn">S-Bahn</a></i> line. The stations served are <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Griebnitzsee_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsdam Griebnitzsee railway station">Griebnitzsee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potsdam-Babelsberg_station" title="Potsdam-Babelsberg station">Babelsberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Hauptbahnhof" title="Potsdam Hauptbahnhof">Central Station</a> (<i>Hauptbahnhof</i>), the main and long-distance station of the city. Other <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">DB</a> stations in Potsdam are Charlottenhof, <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Park_Sanssouci_railway_station" title="Potsdam Park Sanssouci railway station">Park Sanssouci</a> (including the monumental <i><a href="/wiki/Kaiserbahnhof_Potsdam" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaiserbahnhof Potsdam">Kaiserbahnhof</a></i>), Medienstadt Babelsberg, Rehbrücke and <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Pirschheide_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsdam Pirschheide railway station">Pirschheide</a>. The city also possesses a 27&#160;km-long <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Tramway" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsdam Tramway">tramway network</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_transport">Road transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Road transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Potsdam is served by several <a href="/wiki/German_Autobahnen" class="mw-redirect" title="German Autobahnen">motorways</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_10" title="Bundesautobahn 10">A 10</a>, a beltway better known as <i>Berliner Ring</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_115" title="Bundesautobahn 115">A 115</a> (using part of the <a href="/wiki/AVUS" title="AVUS">AVUS</a>) and is closely linked to the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_2" title="Bundesautobahn 2">A 2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_9" title="Bundesautobahn 9">A 9</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_1" title="Bundesstraße 1">B 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_2" title="Bundesstraße 2">B 2</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_federal_highways_in_Germany" title="List of federal highways in Germany">federal roads</a> cross the city. Potsdam counts a network of urban and suburban buses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_research">Education and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Education and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Potsdam is a <a href="/wiki/College_town" title="College town">university town</a>. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Potsdam" title="University of Potsdam">University of Potsdam</a> was founded in 1991 as a university of the State of Brandenburg. Its predecessor was the <i>Akademie für Staats- und Rechtswissenschaften der DDR "<a href="/wiki/Walter_Ulbricht" title="Walter Ulbricht">Walter Ulbricht</a>"</i>, a <a href="/wiki/College_of_education" class="mw-redirect" title="College of education">college of education</a> founded in 1948 which was one of the GDR's most important colleges. There are about 20,000 students enrolled at the university.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Einsteinturm_7443.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Einsteinturm_7443.jpg/220px-Einsteinturm_7443.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Einsteinturm_7443.jpg/330px-Einsteinturm_7443.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Einsteinturm_7443.jpg/440px-Einsteinturm_7443.jpg 2x" data-file-width="819" data-file-height="614" /></a>

The <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Tower" title="Einstein Tower">Einstein Tower</a> was built in 1921 to house research on the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a></div>

<p>In 1991 the <a href="/wiki/Vocational_university" title="Vocational university">Fachhochschule</a> was founded as the second college; it now has 2,400 students.</p>
<p>In addition there is a College of Film and Television (<i>Hochschule für Film und Fernsehen "<a href="/wiki/Konrad_Wolf" title="Konrad Wolf">Konrad Wolf</a>" HFF</i>), founded in 1954 in <a href="/wiki/Babelsberg" title="Babelsberg">Babelsberg</a>, the foremost centre of the German film industry since its birth, with currently 600 students.</p>
<p>There are also several research foundations, including <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraunhofer Institute">Fraunhofer Institutes</a> for <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.pioneers-in-polymers.com/index.html">Applied Polymer Research</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ibmt.fhg.de/fhg/ibmt_en/profile/locations/_index_potsdam_golm.jsp">Biomedical Engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Gravitational_Physics" title="Max Planck Institute for Gravitational Physics">Max Planck Institute for Gravitational Physics</a> (<i>Albert Einstein Institute</i>), <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Max Planck Institute">Max Planck Institute</a> of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.mpikg.mpg.de/en/">Colloids and Interfaces</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Max Planck Institute">Max Planck Institute</a> for <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.mpimp-golm.mpg.de/">Molecular Plant Physiology</a>, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090913060315/http://www.gfz-potsdam.de/portal/-">GFZ German Research Centre for Geosciences</a>, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.aip.de/">Potsdam Astrophysical Institute</a>, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.iass-potsdam.de/">Institute for Advanced Sustainability Studies</a> and the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.pik-potsdam.de/">Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research</a>, which employs 340 people in researching <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.</p>
<p>As well as universities, Potsdam is home to reputable <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">secondary schools</a>. <a href="/wiki/Montessori_Gesamtschule_Potsdam" class="mw-redirect" title="Montessori Gesamtschule Potsdam">Montessori Gesamtschule Potsdam</a>, in western Potsdam, attracts 400 students from the Brandenburg and Berlin region.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potsdam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_sights_of_Potsdam" title="List of sights of Potsdam">List of sights of Potsdam</a></div>

<p>Potsdam was historically a centre of European immigration. Its religious tolerance attracted people from France, Russia, the Netherlands and Bohemia. This is still visible in the culture and architecture of the city.</p>
<p>The most popular attraction in Potsdam is <a href="/wiki/Sanssouci_Park" title="Sanssouci Park">Sanssouci Park</a>, 2&#160;km (1&#160;mi) west of the city centre. In 1744 <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick II of Prussia">King Frederick the Great</a> ordered the construction of a residence here, where he could live <i>sans souci</i> ("without worries", in the French spoken at the court). The park hosts a <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a> (<a href="/wiki/Botanical_Garden,_Potsdam" title="Botanical Garden, Potsdam">Botanical Garden, Potsdam</a>) and many buildings:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Sanssouci" title="Sanssouci">Sanssouci Palace</a> (<i>Schloss Sanssouci</i>), a relatively modest palace of the Prussian royal and German imperial family</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Orangery_Palace" title="Orangery Palace">Orangery Palace</a> (<i>Orangerieschloss</i>), former palace for foreign royal guests</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/New_Palace_(Potsdam)" title="New Palace (Potsdam)">New Palace</a> (<i>Neues Palais</i>), built between 1763 and 1769 to celebrate the end of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> celebrated its victory in holding off the combined attacks of <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and Russia. A century later in 1866 in the Seven Weeks War Prussia defeated Austria and ended three centuries of Habsburg dominance in Germany. It is a much larger and grander palace than Sanssouci, having over 200 rooms and 400 statues as decoration. It served as a guest house for numerous royal visitors. It is now housing parts of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Potsdam" title="University of Potsdam">University of Potsdam</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Charlottenhof_Palace" title="Charlottenhof Palace">Charlottenhof Palace</a> (<i>Schloss Charlottenhof</i>), a Neoclassical palace by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Schinkel" title="Karl Friedrich Schinkel">Karl Friedrich Schinkel</a> built in 1826</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Baths_(Potsdam)" title="Roman Baths (Potsdam)">Roman Baths</a> (<i>Römische Bäder</i>), built by Karl Friedrich Schinkel and Ludwig Persius in 1829–1840. It is a complex of buildings including a tea pavilion, a Renaissance-style villa, and a Roman bathhouse (from which the whole complex takes its name).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_House_(Potsdam)" title="Chinese House (Potsdam)">Chinese Tea House</a> (<i>Chinesisches Teehaus</i>), an 18th-century pavilion built in a <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> style, the fashion of the time.</li>
</ul>
<p>Three gates from the original city wall remain today. The oldest is the Hunters' Gate (<i>Jägertor</i>), built in 1733. The <a href="/wiki/Nauener_Tor" title="Nauener Tor">Nauener Tor</a> was built in 1755 and close to the historic Dutch Quarter. The ornate <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg_Gate_(Potsdam)" title="Brandenburg Gate (Potsdam)">Brandenburg Gate</a> (built in 1770, not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg_Gate" title="Brandenburg Gate">Brandenburg Gate</a> in Berlin) is situated on the Luisenplatz at the western entrance to the old town.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Market_Square,_Potsdam" title="Old Market Square, Potsdam">Old Market Square</a> (<i>Alter Markt</i>) is Potsdam's historical city centre. For three centuries this was the site of the <a href="/wiki/Stadtschloss,_Potsdam" class="mw-redirect" title="Stadtschloss, Potsdam">City Palace</a> (<i>Stadtschloß</i>), a royal palace built in 1662. Under <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick II of Prussia">Frederick the Great</a>, the palace became the winter residence of the Prussian kings. The palace was severely damaged by bombing in 1945 and demolished in 1961 by the <a href="/wiki/Communist" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist">Communist</a> authorities. In 2002 the Fortuna Gate (<i>Fortunaportal</i>) was rebuilt in its original historic position which was followed by a complete reconstruction of the palace as the <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Brandenburg" title="Landtag of Brandenburg">Brandenburg Landtag</a> building inaugurated in 2014. Nearby the square in the Humboldtstraße block, which also was demolished after getting damaged in 1945, reconstruction of several representative residential palaces including Palazzo Pompei and Palazzo Barberini <a href="/wiki/Museum_Barberini" title="Museum Barberini">housing an arts museum</a> were completed in 2016-2017 alongside with buildings with modernized facades to restore the historical proportions of the block.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Potsdam_Brandenburger_Str.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Potsdam_Brandenburger_Str.jpg/170px-Potsdam_Brandenburger_Str.jpg" width="170" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Potsdam_Brandenburger_Str.jpg/255px-Potsdam_Brandenburger_Str.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Potsdam_Brandenburger_Str.jpg/340px-Potsdam_Brandenburger_Str.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1323" data-file-height="1728" /></a>

<p>The Old Market Square is dominated today by the dome of <a href="/wiki/St._Nicholas%27_Church,_Potsdam" title="St. Nicholas' Church, Potsdam">St. Nicholas' Church</a> (<i>Nikolaikirche</i>), built in 1837 in the classical style. It was the last work of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Schinkel" title="Karl Friedrich Schinkel">Karl Friedrich Schinkel</a>, who designed the building but did not live to see its completion. It was finished by his disciples <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_August_St%C3%BCler" title="Friedrich August Stüler">Friedrich August Stüler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Persius" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwig Persius">Ludwig Persius</a>. The eastern side of the Market Square is dominated by the Old City Hall (<i>Altes Rathaus</i>), built in 1755 by the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> architect Jan Bouman (1706–1776). It has a characteristic circular tower, crowned with a gilded <a href="/wiki/Atlas" title="Atlas">Atlas</a> bearing the world on his shoulders.</p>

<p>North of the Old Market Square is the oval French Church (<i>Französische Kirche</i>), erected in the 1750s by Boumann for the <a href="/wiki/Huguenot" class="mw-redirect" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a> community. Also to the south lies the <a href="/wiki/Museum_Barberini" title="Museum Barberini">Museum Barberini</a>, a copy of the previous building, the Barberini Palace. The museum was funded by the German billionaire <a href="/wiki/Hasso_Plattner" title="Hasso Plattner">Hasso Plattner</a>. The former <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque</a> building was built by <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Gontard" title="Carl von Gontard">Carl von Gontard</a> in 1771–1772, inspired by the Roman-style Renaissance palace <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Barberini" title="Palazzo Barberini">Palazzo Barberini</a>. The newly built museum will open in spring 2017.</p>
<p>Another landmark of Potsdam is the two-street <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Quarter" title="Dutch Quarter">Dutch Quarter</a> (<i>Holländisches Viertel</i>), an ensemble of buildings that is unique in Europe, with about 150 houses built of red bricks in the Dutch style. It was built between 1734 and 1742 under the direction of Jan Bouman to be used by Dutch artisans and craftsmen who had been invited to settle here by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Wilhelm_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick Wilhelm I">King Frederick Wilhelm I</a>. Today, this area is one of Potsdam's most visited districts.</p>
<p>North of the city centre is the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> colony of Alexandrowka, a small enclave of Russian architecture (including an <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Orthodox">Orthodox</a> chapel) built in 1825 for a group of Russian immigrants. Since 1999, the colony has been a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.</p>
<p>East of the Alexandrowka colony is a large park, the <a href="/wiki/New_Garden_(Potsdam)" class="mw-redirect" title="New Garden (Potsdam)">New Garden</a> (<i>Neuer Garten</i>), which was laid out from 1786 in the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> style. The site contains two palaces; one of them, the <a href="/wiki/Cecilienhof" title="Cecilienhof">Cecilienhof</a>, was where the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Conference" title="Potsdam Conference">Potsdam Conference</a> was held in July and August 1945. The <i><a href="/wiki/Marmorpalais" title="Marmorpalais">Marmorpalais</a></i> (Marble Palace) was built in 1789 in the style of <a href="/wiki/Classicism" title="Classicism">classicism</a>. Nearby is the <a href="/wiki/Biosph%C3%A4re_Potsdam" title="Biosphäre Potsdam">Biosphäre Potsdam</a>, a tropical <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a>.</p>
<p>Another district of Potsdam is <a href="/wiki/Potsdam-Babelsberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsdam-Babelsberg">Babelsberg</a>, a quarter south-east of the centre, housing the <a href="/wiki/Universum_Film_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Universum Film AG">UFA</a> film studios (<a href="/wiki/Babelsberg_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Babelsberg Studios">Babelsberg Studios</a>), and an extensive <a href="/wiki/Babelsberg_Park" title="Babelsberg Park">park</a> with some historical buildings, including the Babelsberg Palace (Schloß Babelsberg, a <a href="/wiki/Neo-Gothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Gothic">neo-Gothic</a> palace designed by Schinkel).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Tower" title="Einstein Tower">Einstein Tower</a> is located within the <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein_Science_Park" title="Albert Einstein Science Park">Albert Einstein Science Park</a>, which is on the top of the <i>Telegraphenberg</i> within an astronomy compound.</p>
<p>Potsdam also includes a memorial centre in the former <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> prison in Leistikowstraße. In the Volkspark in the north, there is one of the last monuments dedicated to Lenin in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

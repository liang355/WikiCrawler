<p><b>Jena</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈjeːna]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Jena.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f8/Jena.ogg" class="internal" title="Jena.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a German university city and the second largest city in <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>. Together with the nearby cities of <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a>, it forms the central metropolitan area of Thuringia with approximately 500,000 inhabitants, while the city itself has a population of nearly 110,000. Jena is a centre of education and research; the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jena" title="University of Jena">Friedrich Schiller University</a> was founded in 1558 and has 21,000 students today and the Ernst-Abbe-Fachhochschule Jena counts another 5,000 students. Furthermore, there are many institutes of the leading German research societies.</p>
<p>Jena was first mentioned in 1182 and stayed a small town until the 19th century, when industry developed. For most of the 20th century, Jena was a world centre of the optical industry around companies like <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">Carl Zeiss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schott_AG" title="Schott AG">Schott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jenoptik" title="Jenoptik">Jenoptik</a> (since 1990). As one of only a few medium-sized cities in Germany, it has some high-rise buildings in the city centre, like the <a href="/wiki/Jen_Tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Jen Tower">Jen Tower</a>. These also have their origin in the former Carl Zeiss factory. Between 1790 and 1850, Jena was a focal point of the German <a href="/wiki/Vorm%C3%A4rz" title="Vormärz">Vormärz</a> as well as of the student liberal and <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification</a> movement and <a href="/wiki/German_Romanticism" title="German Romanticism">German Romanticism</a>. Notable persons of this period in Jena were <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Friedrich Schiller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottlieb_Fichte" title="Johann Gottlieb Fichte">Johann Gottlieb Fichte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novalis" title="Novalis">Novalis</a> and <a href="/wiki/August_Wilhelm_Schlegel" title="August Wilhelm Schlegel">August Wilhelm Schlegel</a>.</p>
<p>The city's economy is based on the high-technology industry and research. The optical and precision industry is the leading branch to date, while software engineering, other digital businesses and biotechnology are of growing importance. Furthermore, Jena is also a service hub for the surrounding regions.</p>
<p>Jena lies in a hilly landscape in the east of Thuringia, within the wide valley of the <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> river.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lobdeburg_near_Jena_by_Night.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Lobdeburg_near_Jena_by_Night.jpg/220px-Lobdeburg_near_Jena_by_Night.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Lobdeburg_near_Jena_by_Night.jpg/330px-Lobdeburg_near_Jena_by_Night.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Lobdeburg_near_Jena_by_Night.jpg/440px-Lobdeburg_near_Jena_by_Night.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3471" data-file-height="2311" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lobdeburg_near_Jena_by_Night.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lobdeburg castle above Lobeda district</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Until the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> was the border between <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> regions in the west and <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavic</a> regions in the east. Owing to its function as a river crossing, Jena was conveniently located. Nevertheless, there were also some more important Saale crossings like the nearby cities of <a href="/wiki/Naumburg_(Saale)" class="mw-redirect" title="Naumburg (Saale)">Naumburg</a> to the north and <a href="/wiki/Saalfeld" title="Saalfeld">Saalfeld</a> to the south, so that the relevance of Jena was more local during the Middle Ages. The first unequivocal mention of Jena was in an 1182 document. The first local rulers of the region were the Lords of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lobdeburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lobdeburg (page does not exist)">Lobdeburg</a> with their eponymous castle near <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lobeda&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lobeda (page does not exist)">Lobeda</a>, roughly 6&#160;km (4&#160;mi) south of the city centre on the eastern hillside of the Saale valley.</p>
<p>In the 13th century, the Lords of Lobdeburg founded two towns in the valley: Jena on the west bank and Lobeda&#160;– which is one of Jena's constituent communities today&#160;– 4&#160;km (2&#160;mi) to the south on the east bank. Around 1230, Jena received town rights and a regular city grid was established between today's Fürstengraben, Löbdergraben, Teichgraben and Leutragraben. The city got a marketplace, main church, town hall, council and city walls during the late 13th and early 14th centuries making it into a fully fledged town. In this time, the city's economy was based mainly on wine production on the warm and sunny hillsides of the Saale valley. The two monasteries of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a> (1286) and the <a href="/wiki/Cistercian" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian">Cistercians</a> (1301) rounded out Jena's medieval appearance.</p>
<p>As the political circumstances in Thuringia changed in the middle of the 14th century, the weakened Lords of Lobdeburg sold Jena to the aspiring <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wettin" title="House of Wettin">Wettins</a> in 1331. Jena obtained the <a href="/wiki/Gotha" title="Gotha">Gotha</a> municipal law and the citizens strengthened their rights and wealth during the 14th and 15th centuries. Moreover, the Wettins were more interested in their residence in the nearby city of <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a>, and so Jena could develop itself relatively autonomously.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jena_(Merian).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Jena_%28Merian%29.jpg/220px-Jena_%28Merian%29.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Jena_%28Merian%29.jpg/330px-Jena_%28Merian%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Jena_%28Merian%29.jpg/440px-Jena_%28Merian%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1350" data-file-height="864" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jena_(Merian).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Jena in 1650</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> was brought to the city in 1523. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> visited the town to reorganize the clerical relations and Jena became an early centre of his doctrine. In the following years, the Dominican and the <a href="/wiki/Carmelites" title="Carmelites">Carmelite</a> convents were attacked by the townsmen and abolished in 1525 (Carmelite) and 1548 (Dominican).</p>
<p>An important step in Jena's history was the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jena" title="University of Jena">university</a> in 1558. Ernestine Elector <a href="/wiki/John_Frederick,_Elector_of_Saxony" class="mw-redirect" title="John Frederick, Elector of Saxony">John Frederick the Magnanimous</a> founded it, because he had lost his old university in <a href="/wiki/Wittenberg" title="Wittenberg">Wittenberg</a> to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wettin" title="House of Wettin">Albertines</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Schmalkaldic_War" title="Schmalkaldic War">Schmalkaldic War</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">wine-growing</a> declined in the 17th century, so that the new university became one of the most important sources of income for the city. The same century brought a boom in printing business caused by the rising importance of books (and the population's ability to read) in the Lutheran doctrine, and Jena was the second-largest printing location in Germany after <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>.</p>
<p>Beginning in the 16th century, the Ernestine dynasty saw many territorial partitions. Initially, Jena remained a part of <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Weimar" title="Saxe-Weimar">Saxe-Weimar</a>, but in 1672 it became the capital of its own small duchy (<a href="/wiki/Saxe-Jena" title="Saxe-Jena">Saxe-Jena</a>). In 1692, after two dukes (<a href="/wiki/Bernhard_II,_Duke_of_Saxe-Jena" title="Bernhard II, Duke of Saxe-Jena">Bernhard II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wilhelm,_Duke_of_Saxe-Jena" title="Johann Wilhelm, Duke of Saxe-Jena">Johann Wilhelm</a>), the dukes of Saxe-Jena died out and the duchy became part of <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Eisenach" title="Saxe-Eisenach">Saxe-Eisenach</a> and, in 1741, of the Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxe-Weimar" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Saxe-Weimar">Saxe-Weimar</a>, to which it belonged until 1809. From 1809 to 1918, Jena was part of the Duchy (from 1815 Grand Duchy) of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duchy of Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach">Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Napoleon.Jena.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Napoleon.Jena.jpg/220px-Napoleon.Jena.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Napoleon.Jena.jpg/330px-Napoleon.Jena.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Napoleon.Jena.jpg/440px-Napoleon.Jena.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1612" /></a>

<p>Around 1790, the university became the largest and most famous one among the German states and made Jena the centre of idealist philosophy (with professors like <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottlieb_Fichte" title="Johann Gottlieb Fichte">Johann Gottlieb Fichte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Friedrich Schiller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Joseph_Schelling" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling">Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling</a>) and of the early <a href="/wiki/German_Romanticism" title="German Romanticism">Romanticism</a> (with poets like Novalis, the brothers <a href="/wiki/August_Wilhelm_Schlegel" title="August Wilhelm Schlegel">August</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Schlegel" title="Karl Wilhelm Friedrich Schlegel">Friedrich Schlegel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Tieck" title="Ludwig Tieck">Ludwig Tieck</a>). In 1794, the poets <a href="/wiki/Goethe" class="mw-redirect" title="Goethe">Goethe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schiller" class="mw-redirect" title="Schiller">Schiller</a> met at the university and established a long lasting friendship. Consequently, the reputation of the University and the Duchy of Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach as particularly liberal and open-minded was enhanced.</p>
<p>On 14&#160;October 1806, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a> fought and defeated the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> army here in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jena-Auerstedt" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Jena-Auerstedt">Battle of Jena-Auerstedt</a>, near the district of Vierzehnheiligen. Resistance against the French occupation was strong, especially among the students. Many of the students fought in the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCtzow_Free_Corps" title="Lützow Free Corps">Lützow Free Corps</a> in 1813. Two years later, the <a href="/wiki/Urburschenschaft" title="Urburschenschaft">Urburschenschaft</a> fraternity was founded in the city.</p>
<p>During the later 19th century, the famous biologist <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel" title="Ernst Haeckel">Ernst Haeckel</a> was professor at the university. The expansion of science and medicine faculties was closely linked to the industrial boom that Jena saw after 1871. The initial spark of industrialization in Jena was the (relatively late) connection to the railway. The <a href="/wiki/Saal_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Saal Railway">Saal Railway</a> (<i>Saalbahn</i>, opened in 1874) was the connection from <a href="/wiki/Halle_(Saale)" title="Halle (Saale)">Halle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> along the Saale valley to <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Weimar%E2%80%93Gera_railway" title="Weimar–Gera railway">Weimar–Gera railway</a> (opened 1876) connected Jena with <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> in the west as well as <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gera" title="Gera">Gera</a> in the east. Famous pioneers of industry were <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss" title="Carl Zeiss">Carl Zeiss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Abbe" title="Ernst Abbe">Ernst Abbe</a> (with their <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">Carl Zeiss AG</a>) as well as <a href="/wiki/Otto_Schott" title="Otto Schott">Otto Schott</a> (<a href="/wiki/Schott_AG" title="Schott AG">Schott AG</a>). Since that time, production of optical items, precision machinery and laboratory glassware have been the main branches of Jena's economy. Zeiss, Abbe and Schott worked also as social reformers who wanted to improve the living conditions of their workers and the local wealth in general. When Zeiss died in 1889, his company passed to the <a href="/wiki/Carl-Zeiss-Stiftung" title="Carl-Zeiss-Stiftung">Carl-Zeiss-Stiftung</a>, which uses great amounts of the company's profits for social benefits like research projects at universities etc. This model became an example for other German companies (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bosch_Stiftung" title="Robert Bosch Stiftung">Robert Bosch Stiftung</a>).</p>

<p>Industrialization fundamentally changed the social structure of Jena. The former academic town became a working-class city; the population rose from 8,000 around 1870 up to 71,000 at the beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The city expanded along the Saale valley to the north and the south and its side valleys to the east and the west. In 1901, the <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">tram</a> system started its operation and the university got a new main building (established between 1906 and 1908 on the former castle's site). After the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> in 1920, Jena was one of the three biggest cities (together with <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gera" title="Gera">Gera</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> remained part of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>) and became an <a href="/wiki/Independent_city" title="Independent city">independent city</a> in 1922. The modern optical and glass industry kept booming and the city grew further during <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar times</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi</a> period, conflicts deepened in Jena between the influential left-wing milieus (communists and social democrats) and the right-wing Nazi milieus. On the one hand, the university suffered from new restrictions against its independence, but on the other hand, it consolidated the Nazi ideology, for example with a professorship of social anthropology (which sought to scientifically legitimize the <a href="/wiki/Racial_policy_of_Nazi_Germany" title="Racial policy of Nazi Germany">Racial policy of Nazi Germany</a>). <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a> in 1938 led to more discrimination against <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jews</a> in Jena, many of whom either emigrated or were arrested and murdered by the German government. This weakened the academic milieu, because many academics were Jews (especially in medicine). In 1945, towards the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Jena was heavily bombed by the American and British Allies. 709 people were killed, 2,000 injured and most of the medieval town centre was destroyed, but in parts restored after the end of the war. Nevertheless, Jena was the Thuringian city whose level of destruction was exceeded only by <a href="/wiki/Nordhausen" title="Nordhausen">Nordhausen</a>, whose destruction was utter. It was occupied by American troops on 13 April 1945 and was left to Red Army on 1 July 1945.</p>
<p>In 1945, Jena fell within the Soviet zone of occupation in post-World War II Germany. In 1949, it became part of the new <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">German Democratic Republic</a> (GDR). The Soviets dismantled great parts of the Zeiss and Schott factories and took them to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. On the other hand, the GDR government founded a new <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry" title="Pharmaceutical industry">pharmaceutical</a> factory in 1950, <a href="/wiki/Jenapharm" title="Jenapharm">Jenapharm</a>, which is part of <a href="/wiki/Bayer" title="Bayer">Bayer</a> today. In 1953, Jena was a centre of the <a href="/wiki/Uprising_of_1953_in_East_Germany" title="Uprising of 1953 in East Germany">East German Uprising</a> against GDR policy. The protests with 30,000 participants drew fire from Soviet <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tanks</a>.</p>
<p>The following decades brought some radical shifts in city planning. During the 1960s, another part of the historic city centre was demolished to build the <a href="/wiki/Jen_Tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Jen Tower">Jen Tower</a>. The Eichplatz in front of the tower is still unbuilt and its future is still the subject of ongoing heated discussion. Big <a href="/wiki/Plattenbau" title="Plattenbau">Plattenbau</a> settlements were developed in the 1970s and 1980s, because the population was still rising and the housing shortage remained a perpetual problem. New districts established in the north (near Rautal) and in the south (around Winzerla and Lobeda). The opposition against the GDR government was reinforced during the late 1980s in Jena, fed by academic and clerical circles. In autumn 1989, the city saw the largest protests in its history before the GDR government was dissolved.</p>
<p>After 1990, Jena became part of the refounded state of <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>. Industry came into a heavy crisis during the 1990s, but finally it managed the transition to the <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market economy</a> and today, it is one of the leading economic centres of eastern Germany. Furthermore, the university was enlarged and many new research institutes were founded.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_demographics">Geography and demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geography and demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Burgauer_Br%C3%BCcke.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Burgauer_Br%C3%BCcke.jpg/220px-Burgauer_Br%C3%BCcke.jpg" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Burgauer_Br%C3%BCcke.jpg/330px-Burgauer_Br%C3%BCcke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Burgauer_Br%C3%BCcke.jpg/440px-Burgauer_Br%C3%BCcke.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="788" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Burgauer_Br%C3%BCcke.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The medieval bridge across the Saale in Burgau district</div>

<p>Jena is situated in a hilly landscape in eastern Thuringia at the <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> river, between the <a href="/wiki/Harz" title="Harz">Harz</a> mountains 85&#160;km (53&#160;mi) in the north, the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Forest" title="Thuringian Forest">Thuringian Forest</a>/<a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Highland" title="Thuringian Highland">Thuringian Highland</a> 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) in the southwest and the <a href="/wiki/Ore_Mountains" title="Ore Mountains">Ore Mountains</a>, 75&#160;km (47&#160;mi) in the southeast. The municipal terrain is hilly with rugged slopes at the valley's edges. The city centre is situated at 160 m of elevation, whereas the mountains on both sides of Saale valley rise up to 400 m. On the eastern side those are (from north to south): the <i>Gleisberg</i> near Kunitz, the <i>Jenzig</i> near Wogau, the <i>Hausberg</i> near Wenigenjena, the <i>Kernberge</i> near Wöllnitz, the <i>Johannisberg</i> near Lobeda and the <i>Einsiedlerberg</i> near Drackendorf. On the western side, there are the <i>Jägersberg</i> near Zwätzen, the <i>Windknollen</i> north of the city centre, the <i>Tatzend</i> west of the city centre, the <i>Lichtenhainer Höhe</i> near Lichtenhain, the <i>Holzberg</i> near Winzerla, the <i>Jagdberg</i> near Göschwitz and the Spitzenberg near Maua. The mountains belong to the geological formation of Ilm Saale Plate (<a href="/wiki/Muschelkalk" title="Muschelkalk">Muschelkalk</a>) and are relatively flat on their peaks but steep to the valleys in between. Due to its jagged surface, the municipal territory isn't very suitable for agriculture all the more since the most flat areas along the valley were built on during the 20th century. At the mountains is some forest of different leaf trees and pines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jena has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental climate</a> (Dfb) or an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<i>Cfb</i>) according to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> system.<sup id="cite_ref-koppen_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-koppen-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Peel_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peel-3">[3]</a></sup> Summers are warm and sometimes humid; winters are relatively cold. The city's topography creates a microclimate caused through the basin position with sometimes <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> in winter (quite cold nights under −20&#160;°C (−4&#160;°F)) and heat and inadequate air circulation in summer. Annual precipitation is 585 millimeters (23.0&#160;in) with moderate rainfall throughout the year. Light snowfall mainly occurs from December through February, but snow cover does not usually remain for long. During the Middle Ages, Jena was famous for growing wine on its slopes. Nowadays, the next commercial wine-growing areas are situated 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi) down Saale river. Due to its distance to coastal areas and position in the Saale valley, wind speeds tend to be very low; predominant direction is SW.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_division">Administrative division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Administrative division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jena abuts the district of <a href="/wiki/Saale-Holzland-Kreis" title="Saale-Holzland-Kreis">Saale-Holzland</a> with the municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Lehesten,_Saale-Holzland" title="Lehesten, Saale-Holzland">Lehesten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neueng%C3%B6nna" title="Neuengönna">Neuengönna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golmsdorf" title="Golmsdorf">Golmsdorf</a> in the north, <a href="/wiki/Jenal%C3%B6bnitz" title="Jenalöbnitz">Jenalöbnitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gro%C3%9Fl%C3%B6bichau" title="Großlöbichau">Großlöbichau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schl%C3%B6ben" title="Schlöben">Schlöben</a> in the east and <a href="/wiki/Laasdorf" title="Laasdorf">Laasdorf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%B6llnitz" title="Zöllnitz">Zöllnitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulza" title="Sulza">Sulza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rothenstein,_Germany" title="Rothenstein, Germany">Rothenstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milda,_Germany" title="Milda, Germany">Milda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bucha,_Saale-Holzland" title="Bucha, Saale-Holzland">Bucha</a> in the south and the district of <a href="/wiki/Weimarer_Land" title="Weimarer Land">Weimarer Land</a> with the municipalities of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B6britschen" title="Döbritschen">Döbritschen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gro%C3%9Fschwabhausen" title="Großschwabhausen">Großschwabhausen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saaleplatte" title="Saaleplatte">Saaleplatte</a> in the west.</p>
<p>The city itself is divided in 30 districts. The inner-city districts are Zentrum, Nord, West, Süd, Wenigenjena (east of Saale, incorporated in 1909) and Kernberge, other big districts are Lobeda (incorporated in 1946) and Winzerla (incorporated in 1922) in the south with large housing complexes.</p>

<p>During the centuries, Jena had been a town of 4,000 to 5,000 inhabitants. The population growth began in the 19th century with an amount of 6,000 in 1840 and of 8,000 in 1870. Then, a demographic boom occurred with a population of 20,000 in 1900, 50,000 in 1920, 73,000 in 1940, 81,000 in 1960 and 104,000 in 1980. The peak was reached in 1988 with a population of 108,000. The bad economic situation in eastern Germany after the reunification resulted in a decline in population, which fell to 99,000 in 1998 before rising again to 107,000 in 2012.</p>
<p>The average growth of population between 2009 and 2012 was approximately 0.47% p. a, whereas the population in bordering rural regions is shrinking with accelerating tendency. Suburbanization played only a small role in Jena. It occurred after the reunification for a short time in the 1990s, but most of the suburban areas were situated within the administrative city borders.</p>
<p>The birth surplus was 62 in 2012, this is +0.6 per 1,000 inhabitants (Thuringian average: -4.5; national average: -2.4). The net migration rate was +4.0 per 1,000 inhabitants in 2012 (Thuringian average: -0.8; national average: +4.6).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The most important regions of origin of Jena migrants are rural areas of Thuringia, <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> as well as foreign countries like Poland, Russia, Ukraine, Hungary, Serbia, Romania and Bulgaria.</p>
<p>Like other eastern German cities, Jena has only a small amount of foreign population: circa 4.0% are non-Germans by citizenship and overall 6.2% are migrants (according to <a href="/wiki/2011_EU_census" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 EU census">2011 EU census</a>). Differing from the national average, the biggest groups of migrants in Jena are <a href="/wiki/Russians_in_Germany" title="Russians in Germany">Russians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people_in_Germany" title="Chinese people in Germany">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians_in_Germany" title="Ukrainians in Germany">Ukrainians</a>. During recent years, the economic situation of the city improved: the unemployment rate declined from 14% in 2005 to 7% in 2013. Due to the official atheism in former <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">GDR</a>, most of the population is non-religious. 15.9% are members of the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Central_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Central Germany">Evangelical Church in Central Germany</a> and 6.6% are Catholics (according to 2011 EU census).</p>
<h2><span id="Culture,_sights_and_cityscape"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture.2C_sights_and_cityscape">Culture, sights and cityscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture, sights and cityscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jena has a great variety of museums:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Optical_Museum_Jena" title="Optical Museum Jena">Optical Museum Jena</a></i> at Carl-Zeiß-Platz shows the history of optical instruments like glasses, microscopes, cameras and telescopes.</li>
<li>The <i>Phyletisches Museum</i> at Neutor hosts a natural history exhibition with focus on evolution and fossils.</li>
<li>The <i>Stadtmuseum &amp; Kunstsammlung</i> at Markt square shows the city history of Jena and hosts furthermore an exhibition of modern and contemporary art.</li>
<li>The <i>Botanischer Garten</i> (botanic garden) at Fürstengraben is one of the oldest botanic gardens in Germany (established in 1794) and hosts 12,000 plants from all over the world.</li>
<li>The <i>Romantikerhaus</i> at Unterm Markt street hosts an exhibition about the epoque of <a href="/wiki/Jena_romantics" class="mw-redirect" title="Jena romantics">Jena romantics</a> in German literature.</li>
<li><i>Schillers Gartenhaus</i> at Schillergässchen is the former summer house of <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Friedrich Schiller</a> and shows an exhibition of his life and his connection to Jena.</li>
<li>The <i>Goethe-Gedenkstätte</i> at Fürstengraben shows an exhibition about the links between <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</a> and Jena (only in summer).</li>
<li>The <i>Ernst-Haeckel-Haus</i> at Berggasse is the former house of biologist <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel" title="Ernst Haeckel">Ernst Haeckel</a> and hosts an exhibition about his life.</li>
<li>The <i>Schott Glasmuseum</i> at Otto-Schott-Straße shows the life of <a href="/wiki/Otto_Schott" title="Otto Schott">Otto Schott</a> and the history of his glass factory, the <a href="/wiki/Schott_AG" title="Schott AG">Schott AG</a>.</li>
<li>The <i>Museum 1806</i> at Cospeda district hosts an exhibition about the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jena%E2%80%93Auerstedt" title="Battle of Jena–Auerstedt">Battle of Jena–Auerstedt</a> during the Napoleonic wars.</li>
<li>The University of Jena hosts some important scientific collections. While the collections of antiques and minerals are public, the <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Coin_Cabinet_Jena" title="Oriental Coin Cabinet Jena">oriental coins</a> are only accessed for research.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cityscape">Cityscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cityscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The historic city centre is located inside the former wall (which is the area between Fürstengraben in the north, Löbdergraben in the east, Teichgraben in the south and Leutragraben in the west). There are only few historic building structures in this area (e. g. at Oberlauengasse), caused by large losses during World War II and the projects of the following decades. The Eichplatz, a big sub-used square covering a large amount of the centre, is not built on since the 1960s and the discussion about its future is still in process. The wall's defortification took place relatively early – in 18th century – and the first suburbs developed in front of the former city gates. In this areas, some historic building structures from 18th and early 19th century remained like in westward Bachstraße and Wagnergasse, in northward Zwätzengasse and in southward Neugasse.</p>
<p>The later 19th and early 20th century brought a construction boom to Jena and the city enlarged to the north and south along Saale valley, to the west along <i>Mühltal</i> and on the Saale's east side in former Wenigenjena. Compared with the city centre, later substantial losses were much slighter in this areas. During the interwar period, the construction of flats stayed on a high level but suitable ground got less, so that new housing complexes were set up relatively far away from the centre – a problem, that remained until today with long journeys and high rents as consequences. Today's Jena is not that compact as other cities in the region and urban planning is still a challenge.</p>
<p>A peculiarity of Jena is the presence of a second old town centre with market square, town hall, castle etc. in the former town of Lobeda, which is a district since 1946, located approximately 4&#160;km (2&#160;mi) to the south of Jena's centre.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights_and_architectural_heritage">Sights and architectural heritage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sights and architectural heritage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The main church St. Michael is one of the biggest <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> monuments in Thuringia and was built between 1422 and 1557. It has a bronze slab of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>'s tomb.</li>
<li>The St. John's Church was the church of the extinct village <i>Leutra</i> west of Jena and later used as the city's cemetery chapel. Since 1811, the Gothic building is the catholic church of Jena.</li>
<li>The Peace Church was built between 1686 and 1693 as new cemetery chapel and is a <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> evangelical church today.</li>
<li>The Schiller Church east of Saale river is the evangelical parish church of the former village and today's quarter Wenigenjena. <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Friedrich Schiller</a> married here in 1790.</li>
<li>The St. Peter's Church is the former city church of Jena's southern district Lobeda. The Gothic church was built around 1480.</li>
<li>The parish church of Vierzehnheiligen (dedicated to the <a href="/wiki/Fourteen_Holy_Helpers" title="Fourteen Holy Helpers">Fourteen Holy Helpers</a>) is a Gothic-style former pilgrimage church established during the 1460s.</li>
<li>The St. Mary's Church in Ziegenhain is a former pilgrimage church in Gothic style, built in the 15th century.</li>
</ul>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sights">Other sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The medieval city wall is preserved in parts (<i>Anatomieturm</i> and <i>Roter Turm</i>), the largest one is the complex around Johannistor and Pulverturm near Johannisplatz.</li>
<li>The town hall at Markt square was built around 1412 and is one of only few Gothic town halls in Germany. It has an astronomical clock featuring the "Snatching Hans" ("<i>Schnapphans</i>").</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Planetarium_Jena" title="Planetarium Jena">planetarium</a> opened in 1926 and was the first large planetarium in the world, with <a href="/wiki/Zeiss_projector" title="Zeiss projector">technology developed by Carl Zeiss</a>.</li>
<li>The University Main Building stands at the former castle's place and was established in 1908 in early-modern style (<a href="/wiki/Theodor_Fischer" class="mw-redirect" title="Theodor Fischer">Theodor Fischer</a>/<a href="/wiki/Bruno_Taut" title="Bruno Taut">Bruno Taut</a>).</li>
<li>The <i>Abbeanum</i> is a university building by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Neufert" title="Ernst Neufert">Ernst Neufert</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> style, built in 1930.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Jen_Tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Jen Tower">Jen Tower</a> is the city's highest skyscraper, built between 1969 and 1972, with a viewing platform and a sky restaurant.</li>
<li>The <i>Haus Auerbach</i> is the former house of physicist <a href="/wiki/Felix_Auerbach" title="Felix Auerbach">Felix Auerbach</a>, built by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gropius" title="Walter Gropius">Walter Gropius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Meyer_(architect)" title="Adolf Meyer (architect)">Adolf Meyer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> style in 1924. Near is the <i>Haus Zuckerkandl</i>, another mansion built by Gropius in 1929.</li>
<li>The former Carl Zeiss Factory in the city centre hosts interesting technical architecture from the period between 1880 and 1965, including Germany's first high-rise building, the <i>Bau 15</i> from 1915.</li>
<li>The monument to John Frederick the Magnanimous (built in 1858) at the Markt square is a landmark of Jena called "Hanfried".</li>
<li>The monument to <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Abbe" title="Ernst Abbe">Ernst Abbe</a> is a building of early-modern architecture by <a href="/wiki/Henry_van_de_Velde" title="Henry van de Velde">Henry van de Velde</a> (1910).</li>
<li>The Lobdeburg is a castle ruin above Lobeda district and the former seat of the lords of Lobdeburg, founders of Jena.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre_and_music">Theatre and music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Theatre and music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jena has its own theatre and orchestra, the <a href="/wiki/Jenaer_Philharmonie" title="Jenaer Philharmonie">Jenaer Philharmonie</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jena hosts a traditional football club, the <a href="/wiki/FC_Carl_Zeiss_Jena" title="FC Carl Zeiss Jena">FC Carl Zeiss Jena</a>. At its best during the 1970s and 1980s, the club won the national GDR championship and played in <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Cup_Winners%27_Cup" title="UEFA Cup Winners' Cup">UEFA Cup Winners' Cup</a> final of 1981, but lost against <a href="/wiki/FC_Dinamo_Tbilisi" title="FC Dinamo Tbilisi">FC Dinamo Tbilisi</a>. Compared to then, the results of FC Carl Zeiss are poor today, playing only in 4th league (<a href="/wiki/Regionalliga_Nordost" title="Regionalliga Nordost">Regionalliga Nordost</a>). In women's football, the <a href="/wiki/FF_USV_Jena" title="FF USV Jena">FF USV Jena</a> is member of the German first division. Both clubs' stadium is the <a href="/wiki/Ernst-Abbe-Sportfeld" title="Ernst-Abbe-Sportfeld">Ernst-Abbe-Sportfeld</a>. Also, the city's basketball team, <a href="/wiki/Science_City_Jena" title="Science City Jena">Science City Jena</a> played in <a href="/wiki/Basketball_Bundesliga" title="Basketball Bundesliga">Basketball Bundesliga</a> in 2007-2008 season and returned to top level in 2015-16 season. In addition, since 2000, the university of Jena has a rugby team. Since 2012, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=USV_Rugby_Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="USV Rugby Jena (page does not exist)">USV Rugby Jena</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> team has been playing in the <a href="/wiki/2._Rugby-Bundesliga" title="2. Rugby-Bundesliga">2. Rugby-Bundesliga</a>.</p>
<p>Current men's <a href="/wiki/Javelin_throw" title="Javelin throw">javelin throw</a> world record (98.48) by <a href="/wiki/Jan_%C5%BDelezn%C3%BD" title="Jan Železný">Jan Železný</a> was achieved in Jena.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_infrastructure">Economy and infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Economy and infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Agriculture,_industry_and_services"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture.2C_industry_and_services">Agriculture, industry and services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Agriculture, industry and services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Agriculture plays a small role in Jena, only 40% of the municipal territory are in use for farming (compared to over 60% in <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> and nearly 50% in <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a>). Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Muschelkalk" title="Muschelkalk">Muschelkalk</a> soil is not very fertile and is often used as pasture for cattle. The only large agricultural area is situated around Isserstedt, Cospeda and Vierzehnheiligen district in the northwest. Wine-growing was discontinued during the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> around 1800, but is now possible again due to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Nevertheless, the commercial production of wine hasn't yet resumed.</p>
<p>Industry is a great tradition in Jena, reaching back to the mid-19th century. In 2012, there were 80 companies in industrial production with more than 20 workers employing 8,300 persons and generating a turnover of more than 1,5 billion Euro.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The most important branches are precision machinery, pharmaceuticals, optics, biotechnology and software engineering. Notable companies in Jena are the traditional <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">Carl Zeiss AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schott_AG" title="Schott AG">Schott AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jenoptik" title="Jenoptik">Jenoptik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jenapharm" title="Jenapharm">Jenapharm</a> as well as new companies like <a href="/wiki/Intershop_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="Intershop Communications">Intershop Communications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analytik_Jena" title="Analytik Jena">Analytik Jena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_Meditec" title="Carl Zeiss Meditec">Carl Zeiss Meditec</a>. Jena has the most market-listed companies and is one of the most important economic centres of east Germany. The city is among Germany's 50 fastest growing regions, with many internationally renowned research institutes and companies, a comparatively low unemployment and a young population structure. Jena was awarded the title "Stadt der Wissenschaft" (city of science) by the <i>Stifterverband für die Deutsche Wissenschaft</i>, a German science association, in 2008.</p>
<p>Jena is also a hub of public and private services, specially in education, research and business services. Other important institutions are the High Court of Thuringia and Thuringia's solely university hospital. Furthermore, Jena is a regional centre in infrastructure and retail with many shopping centres.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="By_rail">By rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: By rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Jena has no central railway station with connection to all the lines at one point. What is relatively common in many countries is quite unusual for a German city and caused on the one hand by the city's difficult topography and on the other hand by the history, because the two main lines were built by two different private companies. The connection in north-south direction is the <a href="/wiki/Saal_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Saal Railway">Saal Railway</a> with <a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">ICE</a> trains running from <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> in the north to <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> in the south once an hour stopping at <a href="/wiki/Jena_Paradies_station" title="Jena Paradies station">Paradies station</a> and local trains to <a href="/wiki/Naumburg_(Saale)" class="mw-redirect" title="Naumburg (Saale)">Naumburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saalfeld" title="Saalfeld">Saalfeld</a> stopping at Zwätzen, <a href="/wiki/Jena_Saale_station" title="Jena Saale station">Saalbahnhof</a>, Paradies and <a href="/wiki/Jena-G%C3%B6schwitz_station" title="Jena-Göschwitz station">Göschwitz</a>. The connection in west-east direction is the <a href="/wiki/Weimar%E2%80%93Gera_railway" title="Weimar–Gera railway">Weimar–Gera railway</a> with regional express trains to <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen" title="Göttingen">Göttingen</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Zwickau" title="Zwickau">Zwickau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glauchau" title="Glauchau">Glauchau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altenburg" title="Altenburg">Altenburg</a> or <a href="/wiki/Greiz" title="Greiz">Greiz</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Gera" title="Gera">Gera</a>) and local trains between Weimar, Jena and Gera. The express trains stop at <a href="/wiki/Jena_West_station" title="Jena West station">West station</a> near the city centre and Göschwitz, the local trains furthermore at Neue Schenke. The junction between both lines is the <a href="/wiki/Jena-G%C3%B6schwitz_station" title="Jena-Göschwitz station">Göschwitz station</a>, approx. 5&#160;km (3&#160;mi) south of the city centre.</p>
<p>In 2017–when the new <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Erfurt_high-speed_railway" title="Nuremberg–Erfurt high-speed railway">Nuremberg–Erfurt high-speed railway</a> opens–the city will lose its connection to the long-distance train network. For compensation, there will be new regional express train services to <a href="/wiki/Halle_(Saale)" title="Halle (Saale)">Halle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> in the north and, already started, to <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> in the south.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="By_road">By road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: By road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The two <a href="/wiki/German_autobahns" class="mw-redirect" title="German autobahns">Autobahnen</a> crossing each other nearby at <i>Hermsdorf junction</i> are the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_4" title="Bundesautobahn 4">Bundesautobahn 4</a> (Frankfurt–Dresden) and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_9" title="Bundesautobahn 9">Bundesautobahn 9</a> (Berlin–Munich), which were both built during the 1930s. The A 4 runs quite next to the Lobeda housing complexes and the Leutra district. Therefore, it was rebuilt in the 2000s and got two tunnels to protect the residents and the environment against noise and air pollution. Furthermore, there are two <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe" title="Bundesstraße">Bundesstraßen</a> crossing in Jena: the <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_7" title="Bundesstraße 7">Bundesstraße 7</a> is a connection to <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a> in the west and <a href="/wiki/Gera" title="Gera">Gera</a> in the east and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_88&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bundesstraße 88 (page does not exist)">Bundesstraße 88</a> is a connection along Saale valley to <a href="/wiki/Naumburg_(Saale)" class="mw-redirect" title="Naumburg (Saale)">Naumburg</a> in the north and <a href="/wiki/Rudolstadt" title="Rudolstadt">Rudolstadt</a> in the south. Furthermore, there are some roads to <a href="/wiki/Apolda" title="Apolda">Apolda</a> via Isserstedt, <a href="/wiki/Blankenhain" title="Blankenhain">Blankenhain</a> via Ammerbach and <a href="/wiki/Stadtroda" title="Stadtroda">Stadtroda</a> via Lobeda. Most parts of city centre inside the former walls are pedestrian areas.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="By_aviation">By aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: By aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The next local airports to Jena are the <a href="/wiki/Erfurt%E2%80%93Weimar_Airport" title="Erfurt–Weimar Airport">Erfurt–Weimar Airport</a>, approx. 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) to the west and the <a href="/wiki/Leipzig/Halle_Airport" title="Leipzig/Halle Airport">Leipzig/Halle Airport</a>, approx. 80&#160;km (50&#160;mi) to the northeast, which both serve mostly for holiday flights to the Mediterranean and other touristic regions. The next major airports are <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Airport" title="Frankfurt Airport">Frankfurt Airport</a>, the upcoming <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport" title="Berlin Brandenburg Airport">Berlin Brandenburg Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich_Airport" title="Munich Airport">Munich Airport</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="By_bike">By bike</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: By bike">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Despite the hilly terrain in some parts, Jena is a cycling city, due to the many students. Cycling has become more popular in Jena since the 1990s when good quality bike paths began to be built. There are bike lanes along some main streets, though, in comparison to other cities in Germany, there are deficits.</p>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_touring" title="Bicycle touring">bicycle touring</a> there is the "Saale track" (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Saale-Radweg</span>) and the "Thuringian city string track" (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Radweg Thüringer Städtekette</span>). Both of these connect points of tourist interest: the former along the <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> valley from <a href="/wiki/Fichtel_Mountains" title="Fichtel Mountains">Fichtel Mountains</a> in Bavaria to the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> river near <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a>, while the latter follows the medieval <a href="/wiki/Via_Regia" title="Via Regia">Via Regia</a> closely and runs from <a href="/wiki/Eisenach" title="Eisenach">Eisenach</a> via <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a> and Jena to <a href="/wiki/Altenburg" title="Altenburg">Altenburg</a> via <a href="/wiki/Gera" title="Gera">Gera</a>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Jena" title="Trams in Jena">Jena tramway network</a> was established in 1901 and enlarged after the German reunification. It connects the major districts with the city centre; there are 5 ordinary lines served in different intervals between 7,5 and 20 minutes. Nevertheless, there are some old single-track segments interfering the services. Furthermore, there is an extensive network of buses, run (like the trams) by the "Jenah" organization (a pun on Jena and <i>Nahverkehr</i>, German for public transport). Buses of the JES Verkehrsgesellschaft connect Jena with cities and villages in the region.</p>

<p>After reunification, the educational system was realigned. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Jena" title="University of Jena">University of Jena</a>, established in 1558, was largely extended. Today there are approximately 21,000 students at this university. Another college is the <a href="/wiki/Ernst-Abbe-Hochschule_Jena" title="Ernst-Abbe-Hochschule Jena">Ernst-Abbe-Hochschule Jena</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">University of Applied Sciences</a> founded in 1991 which offers a combination of scientific training and its practical applications. There are also nearly 5,000 students.</p>
<p>Further there are six <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">Gymnasiums</a>, five state-owned and one Christian (ecumenical). One of the state-owned is a <i>Sportgymnasium</i>, an elite boarding school for young talents in athletics or football. Another state-owned Gymnasium (the <a href="/wiki/Carl-Zeiss-Gymnasium_Jena" title="Carl-Zeiss-Gymnasium Jena">Carl-Zeiss-Gymnasium Jena</a>) offers a focus in sciences also as an elite boarding school additionally to the common curriculum.</p>

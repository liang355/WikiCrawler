<p><b>Osnabrück</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ɔsnaˈbʁʏk]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Osnabr%C3%BCck.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f5/De-Osnabr%C3%BCck.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Osnabrück.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Westphalian_language" title="Westphalian language">Westphalian</a>: <span lang="wep" xml:lang="wep"><i>Ossenbrügge</i></span>; archaic English: Osnaburg) is a city in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">federal state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lower-Saxony" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower-Saxony">Lower-Saxony</a> in north-west <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is situated in a valley penned between the <a href="/wiki/Wiehen_Hills" title="Wiehen Hills">Wiehen Hills</a> and the northern tip of the <a href="/wiki/Teutoburg_Forest" title="Teutoburg Forest">Teutoburg Forest</a>. With a population of 168,145,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Osnabrück is one of the four largest cities in Lower Saxony.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETeam_Strategische_Stadtentwicklung_und_Statistik20131_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETeam_Strategische_Stadtentwicklung_und_Statistik20131-4">[4]</a></sup> The city is the centrepoint of the <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck_Land" title="Osnabrück Land">Osnabrück Land</a> region as well as the <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck_(district)" title="Osnabrück (district)">District of Osnabrück</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Das_Osnabr.C3.BCcker_Land_Friedensstadt_.E2.80.93_Lebendiges_Zentrum_im_Osnabr.C3.BCcker_Land_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Das_Osnabr.C3.BCcker_Land_Friedensstadt_.E2.80.93_Lebendiges_Zentrum_im_Osnabr.C3.BCcker_Land-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The founding of Osnabrück was linked to its positioning on important European trading routes. <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Great" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles the Great">Charles the Great</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Osnabr%C3%BCck" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Osnabrück">Diocese of Osnabrück</a> in 780. The city was also a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a>. Later Osnabrück became well known for its role in the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> in 1648 which brought the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%E2%80%99_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirty Years’ War">Thirty Years’ War</a> to an end; the treaty was signed both there and in nearby <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Münster">Münster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Teutoburg_Forest" title="Battle of the Teutoburg Forest">Battle of the Teutoburg Forest</a> took place in the region surrounding Osnabrück. The city is also known as the birthplace of novelist <a href="/wiki/Erich-Maria_Remarque" class="mw-redirect" title="Erich-Maria Remarque">Erich-Maria Remarque</a> (“<a href="/wiki/All_Quiet_on_the_Western_Front" title="All Quiet on the Western Front">All Quiet on the Western Front</a>”) and painter <a href="/wiki/Felix_Nussbaum" title="Felix Nussbaum">Felix Nussbaum</a>.</p>
<p>Osnabrück's role in the Treaty of Westphalia later led it to adopt the title <i>Friedensstadt</i> ("city of peace"). More recently Osnabrück has become well known for its industry. Numerous companies in the automobile, paper, steel and grocery sectors are located in the city and its surrounding area.<sup id="cite_ref-Staedtereport_Osnabrueck_okt_2009.pdf_.28application.2Fpdf-Objekt.29_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Staedtereport_Osnabrueck_okt_2009.pdf_.28application.2Fpdf-Objekt.29-7">[7]</a></sup> In spite of the massive destruction inflicted on the city during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Old_town" title="Old town">Altstadt</a> (old town) was eventually reconstructed extensively with designs loyal to the original <a href="/wiki/Medieval_architecture" title="Medieval architecture">medieval architecture</a> there. Osnabrück was also the home of the largest British garrison outside of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Osnabrück's modern, urban image is enhanced by the presence of more than 22,000 students studying at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="University of Osnabrück">University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Osnabrück University of Applied Sciences">University of Applied Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtportr.C3.A4t_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtportr.C3.A4t-9">[9]</a></sup> Although situated in the region of Lower Saxony, historically, culturally and linguistically Osnabrück is held to belong to the region of <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Names_of_European_cities_in_different_languages:_M%E2%80%93P#O" title="Names of European cities in different languages: M–P">Names of European cities in different languages: Osnabrück</a></div>
<p>The origin of the name Osnabrück is disputed. The suffix <i>-brück</i> suggests a bridge over or to something (from German <i>Brücke</i> = bridge) but the prefix <i>Osna-</i> is explained in at least two different ways: the traditional explanation is that today's name is a corruption of <i>Ochsenbrücke</i> (meaning "oxen bridge"), but others state that it is derived from the name of the <i>Hase</i> River which is arguably derived from <i>Asen</i> (<a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir" title="Æsir">Æsir</a>), thus giving Osnabrück the meaning "bridge to the gods".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The way in which the city's name is pronounced can also serve as a means of telling if the speaker is a native of Osnabrück or a visitor: most locals stress the last syllable, while those from elsewhere tend to stress the first one.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The city gave its name to the textile fabric of <a href="/wiki/Osnaburg" title="Osnaburg">osnaburg</a> (note: "-burg" means <i>borough</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval">Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Osnabrück initially developed as a marketplace next to the bishopric founded by <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>, King of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Franks" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of the Franks">Franks</a>, in 780. Some time prior to 803, the city became the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="Prince-Bishopric of Osnabrück">Prince-Bishopric of Osnabrück</a>. Although the precise date is uncertain, it is likely that Osnabrück is the oldest bishopric in Lower Saxony.</p>
<p>In the year 804 Charlemagne was said to have founded the <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_Carolinum_(Osnabr%C3%BCck)" title="Gymnasium Carolinum (Osnabrück)">Gymnasium Carolinum in Osnabrück</a>. This would make it the oldest German <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Gymnasium</a> school, but the charter date is disputed by historians, some of whom believe it could be a forgery.</p>
<p>In 889 the town was given merchant, customs, and coinage privileges by King <a href="/wiki/Arnulf_of_Carinthia" title="Arnulf of Carinthia">Arnulf</a> of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Carinthia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kingdom of Carinthia (page does not exist)">Carinthia</a>. Osnabrück was first referred to in records as a "city" in 1147. A decade later, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick Barbarossa</a> granted the city <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortification</a> privileges (<i>Befestigungsrecht</i>). Most of the towers which were part of the original fortifications are still visible in the city. Osnabrück became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> in the 12th century, as well as a member of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Westphalian_Federation_of_Cities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Westphalian Federation of Cities (page does not exist)">Westphalian Federation of Cities</a>.</p>
<p>The history of the town in the later Middle Ages was recorded in a chronicle by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Suho" title="Albert Suho">Albert Suho</a>, one of Osnabrück's most important clerics in the 15th century.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_age">Early Modern age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early Modern age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1561 to 1639 there was a considerable amount of social unrest and tension in Osnabrück due to the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> and also witch hunting. In 1582, during the rule of Mayor Hammacher (1565–1588), 163 women were executed as alleged witches; most of them were burned alive. In total, 276 women were executed, along with 2 men who had been charged with wizardry.</p>
<p>The first Lutheran services were held in Osnabrück in 1543. Over the next century, Lutheranism expanded in the city and several Protestant bishops were elected. However, the Catholic churches continued to operate, and the city never became completely Lutheran. After the Thirty Years' War broke out, a Catholic bishop was elected in 1623, and the city was occupied by troops of the Catholic League in 1628.<sup id="cite_ref-greengrass2014_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greengrass2014-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_Carolinum_(Osnabr%C3%BCck)" title="Gymnasium Carolinum (Osnabrück)">Gymnasium Carolinum</a> was upgraded to a <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> university in 1632, but the university was closed a year later when the city was <a href="/wiki/Swedish_intervention_in_the_Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Swedish intervention in the Thirty Years' War">taken by Swedish troops</a> and restored to Protestant control.</p>
<p>Peace negotiations took place in Osnabrück and the nearby city of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Münster">Münster</a> from 1643 to 1648. The twin Treaties of Osnabrück and Münster, collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a>, ended the Thirty Years' War. Osnabrück was officially recognized as bi-confessional Catholic and Lutheran. The <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="Prince-Bishopric of Osnabrück">prince-bishopric</a> would be held alternately by a Catholic bishop and a Lutheran bishop. The Protestant bishop would be selected from the descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Duchy of Brunswick-Lüneburg">Dukes of Brunswick-Lüneburg</a>, with priority given to the <a href="/wiki/Cadet_(genealogy)" title="Cadet (genealogy)">cadets</a> of what became the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hanover" title="House of Hanover">House of Hanover</a>.</p>
<p>In the early 18th century, renowned local jurist and social theorist <a href="/wiki/Justus_M%C3%B6ser" title="Justus Möser">Justus Möser</a> wrote a highly influential constitutional history of the town, the <i>Osnabrücker Geschichte</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200715-16_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200715-16-12">[12]</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>, the town's population fell below 6,000, however an economic revival linked to the <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> industries caused it to rise again from the 1780s onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200715_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200715-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a> brought <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> troops into the city in 1795, followed by the French in 1803.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716-14">[14]</a></sup> As a result, the town's population was kept below 10,000 for the whole first decade of the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716-14">[14]</a></sup> The Napoleonic period saw possession of the city change hands several times. Control of Osnabrück passed to the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Hanover" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of Hanover">Electorate of Hanover</a> in 1803 during the <a href="/wiki/German_Mediatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="German Mediatisation">German Mediatisation</a>, and then briefly to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kingdom of Prussia</a> in 1806. From 1807 to 1810 the city was part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">Kingdom of Westphalia</a>, after which it passed to the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">First French Empire</a>. After 1815, it became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Kingdom of Hanover</a>.</p>

<p>The town's first railway line was built in 1855, connecting it with <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B6hne" title="Löhne">Löhne</a>. Further rail connections appeared over the following decades, connecting Osnabrück with <a href="/wiki/Emden" title="Emden">Emden</a> from 1856, <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> from 1871 and <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> from 1874.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716-17_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716-17-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1866, Osnabrück was annexed by Prussia after the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> and administered as part of the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hanover" title="Province of Hanover">Province of Hanover</a>. Growth of the local economy and population was fuelled by expansion in the engineering and textile industries, with the Hammersen Weaving Mill established in 1869 and the <i>Osnabrücker Kupfer- und Drahtwerk</i> metallurgical firm following in 1873.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716-14">[14]</a></sup> The later 19th century also saw growth in the number of schools and the arrival of electricity and modern sanitation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200717_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200717-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1914, Osnabrück had over 70,000 inhabitants.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200716-14">[14]</a></sup> The outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> necessitated food rationing; the Allied blockade and a harsh winter in 1917 led to further shortages.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200717_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200717-16">[16]</a></sup> Following Germany's defeat in 1918, a council made up of workers and soldiers took control during the <a href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%9319" title="German Revolution of 1918–19">November Revolution</a>, but were replaced by the new <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a> the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200717-18_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200717-18-17">[17]</a></sup> Similarly to many other German cities, Osnabrück experienced considerable inflation and unemployment in the 1920s, with over 2,000 out of work by 1923 and nearly 14,000 receiving some form of government assistance by 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200718_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200718-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Politically, Osnabrück in the 1920s was a stronghold of support for the Social Democrats and the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Centre_Party_(Germany)" title="Centre Party (Germany)">Centre Party</a>. However, in the Reichstag elections of September 1930, the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> received the greatest percentage of votes in the city (nearly 28%) - a more than seven-fold increase from their electoral performance in Osnabrück two years prior.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200737_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200737-19">[19]</a></sup> During the campaigns prior to the two federal elections in 1932, both <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a> made well-attended speeches in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200744_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200744-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>Following the Nazis' seizure of power in January 1933, Osnabrück was subjected to the implementation of National Socialist economic, political, and social programmes. These resulted in economic growth for ethnic Germans who did not run afoul of the new regime, and the town went from having over 10,000 unemployed in early 1933 to actually having a labour shortage five years later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200755_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200755-21">[21]</a></sup> However, dissenters, supporters of opposition parties and <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> did not share in this growth and found themselves discriminated against, imprisoned or forced to close their businesses and leave town.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi200723-24.2C81.2C_186-200_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi200723-24.2C81.2C_186-200-22">[22]</a></sup> During the war, both Jews and <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romany</a> were deported to <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">concentration camps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extermination_camp" title="Extermination camp">extermination camps</a> en masse.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007197-98.2C211_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007197-98.2C211-23">[23]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> the city was bombed extensively, requiring major reconstructive programmes following the war's end.</p>
<p>The war ended for Osnabrück on 4 April 1945, when the <a href="/wiki/VIII_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="VIII Corps (United Kingdom)">XVII Corps</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Montgomery,_1st_Viscount_Montgomery_of_Alamein" class="mw-redirect" title="Bernard Montgomery, 1st Viscount Montgomery of Alamein">Montgomery's</a> <a href="/wiki/Second_Army_(United_Kingdom)" title="Second Army (United Kingdom)">Second Army</a> entered the city with little resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007137_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007137-24">[24]</a></sup> Leading Nazis fled the city and the British appointed a new mayor, Johannes Petermann. However, power remained chiefly with the occupiers, represented locally by the military governor, Colonel Geoffrey Day.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007135.2C137_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007135.2C137-25">[25]</a></sup> Relations between the occupiers and the citizens of Osnabrück were generally peaceful, though tensions existed; some minor fights broke out between British soldiers and local youths and some Osnabrückers resented the relationships that developed between the occupiers and local women.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007136-37_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007136-37-26">[26]</a></sup> Additionally, the British took over more than seventy homes for their own use by the middle of 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007150-51_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007150-51-27">[27]</a></sup> Amidst shortages, the black market thrived and became one of the main focuses of police activity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007153-56_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPanayi2007153-56-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>After World War II <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> realigned its states; Osnabrück became part of the new state of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a> in 1946. The British continued to maintain <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck_Garrison" title="Osnabrück Garrison">Osnabrück Garrison</a>, a garrison near the city, which at one point was the largest British <a href="/wiki/Garrison" title="Garrison">garrison</a> in the world, housing some 4,000 troops and employing around 500 local civilians.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> It was the target of a <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">PIRA</a> <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck_mortar_attack" title="Osnabrück mortar attack">attack in 1996</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Due to budget cuts, the troops were withdrawn in 2008 and the property returned to the local government.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>After three centuries, the city finally obtained its university when the government of Lower Saxony established the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="University of Osnabrück">University of Osnabrück</a> in 1974.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Famous_people">Famous people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Famous people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Famous Osnabrück personalities include the writer <a href="/wiki/Erich_Maria_Remarque" title="Erich Maria Remarque">Erich Maria Remarque</a> and painters <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Vordemberge-Gildewart" title="Friedrich Vordemberge-Gildewart">Friedrich Vordemberge-Gildewart</a> (1899-1962) and <a href="/wiki/Felix_Nussbaum" title="Felix Nussbaum">Felix Nussbaum</a>. Nussbaum has been honoured by Osnabrück in the form of a museum designed by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Libeskind" title="Daniel Libeskind">Daniel Libeskind</a> which opened in 1998; it was designed as a scaled-down version of Libeskind's own <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Museum_Berlin" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish Museum Berlin">Jewish Museum</a> in Berlin. The poet and scholar <a href="/wiki/Johann_Ernst_Hanxleden" title="Johann Ernst Hanxleden">Johann Ernst Hanxleden</a> was born in Osnabrück, as were reggae musician <a href="/wiki/Gentleman_(musician)" title="Gentleman (musician)">Gentleman</a> and DJ <a href="/wiki/Robin_Schulz" title="Robin Schulz">Robin Schulz</a>. <a href="/wiki/Victory_Records" title="Victory Records">Victory Records</a> and recording artists <a href="/wiki/Waterdown" title="Waterdown">Waterdown</a> are also based in Osnabrück.</p>
<p>Actress <a href="/wiki/Birgitta_Tolksdorf" title="Birgitta Tolksdorf">Birgitta Tolksdorf</a>, who became a well-known figure in American television in the 1970s, as well as stage and screen actor <a href="/wiki/Mathias_Wieman" title="Mathias Wieman">Mathias Wieman</a> (the 1958 recipient of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Justus-M%C3%B6ser-Medaille&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Justus-Möser-Medaille (page does not exist)">Justus-Möser-Medaille</a>) (see German article <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justus-M%C3%B6ser-Medaille" class="extiw" title="de:Justus-Möser-Medaille">Justus-Möser-Medaille</a>) were also born in the city. Peter van Pels, the love interest of world-famous diarist <a href="/wiki/Anne_Frank" title="Anne Frank">Anne Frank</a>, and his parents Auguste van Pels and Hermann van Pels, all hailed from Osnabrück.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Clemens_Gerke" title="Friedrich Clemens Gerke">Friedrich Clemens Gerke</a>, writer, <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>, musician and pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraph">telegraphy</a> who revised the <a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a> in 1848, is another of the city's famous sons. (Gerke's notation is still used today.)</p>
<p>Further notable Osnabrückers are <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Abeken" title="Heinrich Abeken">Heinrich Abeken</a>, theologian and Prussian Privy Legation Councillor in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in Berlin; <a href="/wiki/Justus_Moser" class="mw-redirect" title="Justus Moser">Justus Moser</a>, jurist and historian of the city; and <a href="/wiki/Hans-Gert_P%C3%B6ttering" title="Hans-Gert Pöttering">Hans-Gert Pöttering</a>, former <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_European_Parliament" title="President of the European Parliament">President of the European Parliament</a>. One of the best-known Osnabrückers in recent times is <a href="/wiki/Christian_Wulff" title="Christian Wulff">Christian Wulff</a>, Prime Minister of Lower Saxony (2003–2010) and German President (2010–2012).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hellmann_Worldwide_Logistics" title="Hellmann Worldwide Logistics">Hellmann Worldwide Logistics</a> has its headquarters in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two higher education institutions in Osnabrück, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="University of Osnabrück">University of Osnabrück</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="Osnabrück University of Applied Sciences">Osnabrück University of Applied Sciences</a> with more than 25000 students. All of the types of German grammar schools are represented in the city, including seven <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Gymnasien</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_Carolinum_(Osnabr%C3%BCck)" title="Gymnasium Carolinum (Osnabrück)">Gymnasium Carolinum</a> claims to be the oldest still existing school in Germany. Another well-known Gymnasium is the Ursulaschule, a private school, located directly opposite the Carolinum.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city of Osnabrück is connected by road to the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_1" title="Bundesautobahn 1">A1</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_30" title="Bundesautobahn 30">A30</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_33" title="Bundesautobahn 33">A33</a>. It shares its <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Osnabr%C3%BCck_International_Airport" title="Münster Osnabrück International Airport">airport</a> with <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Münster">Münster</a>.</p>
<p>The "<a href="/wiki/Osnabr%C3%BCck_Hauptbahnhof" title="Osnabrück Hauptbahnhof">Hauptbahnhof</a>" (main railway station) of Osnabrück is an important rail travel hub. Travellers from the Netherlands heading to either <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, Denmark, or Eastern Europe often have to change here.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>An extensive <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a> service operated by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stadtwerke_Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stadtwerke Osnabrück (page does not exist)">Stadtwerke Osnabrück</a> provides public transportation within Osnabrück and the surrounding region.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.stadtwerke-osnabrueck.de/66.htm">[3]</a> The central bus stop is located at the Neumarkt shopping area, a short distance from the railway station.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twinning_with_Derby">Twinning with Derby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osnabr%C3%BCck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Twinning with Derby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Osnabrück is twinned with the city of <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> in England.</p>
<p>Previously Osnabrück had made contact with the British authorities as early as 1948, hoping to find an English twin town and therefore achieve greater understanding with their former enemies in the Second World War. Unfortunately, this attempt was unsuccessful and Osnabrück did not actively consider the idea again for another quarter-century. The twinning agreement with Derby was signed on 17 February 1976 in the historic Friedenssaal (Hall of Peace) in Osnabrück's town hall. Every year since then the two towns have exchanged envoys. Derby also has a square named after Osnabrück in honour of the twinning arrangement; this features an obelisk among other things.</p>
<p>Osnabrück now has eleven twin and friendship cities: Derby (England), <a href="/wiki/Angers" title="Angers">Angers</a> (France), <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a> (Netherlands), <a href="/wiki/%C3%87anakkale" title="Çanakkale">Çanakkale</a> (Turkey), <a href="/wiki/Tver" title="Tver">Tver</a> (Russia), <a href="/wiki/Greifswald" title="Greifswald">Greifswald</a> (Germany), <a href="/wiki/Vila_Real,_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="Vila Real, Portugal">Vila Real</a> (Portugal), <a href="/wiki/Hefei" title="Hefei">Hefei</a> (China), <a href="/wiki/Evansville" class="mw-redirect" title="Evansville">Evansville</a> (USA), <a href="/wiki/Gm%C3%BCnd,_Carinthia" title="Gmünd, Carinthia">Gmünd</a> (Austria), <a href="/wiki/Gwangmyeong" title="Gwangmyeong">Gwangmyeong</a> (Korea) and there are five envoys working at the twinning office in Osnabrück, who represent Derby, Angers, Haarlem, Tver and Çanakkale.</p>
<p>Every year, Derby and Osnabrück each appoint an envoy who spends twelve months in their respective twin city. The envoy's role is varied, but primarily focuses on promoting the exchange of ideas between the two cities, as well as acting as an educational and general information officer to promote awareness of the twinning scheme. The envoy's specific duties are numerous: translating, giving talks and presentations to local societies and schools, finding pen friends and short-term host families during work placements, working in day-to-day contact to assist groups who want to get involved in twinning by identifying and approaching possible counterparts, planning the Derby Day at the annual Maiwoche (May Week) festival, and many more.</p>
<p>The exchange of envoys between two cities is very unusual. The team of envoys in Osnabrück changes every year and Osnabrück also sends envoys to Derby, Angers and Çanakkale. No other city in Germany participates in this exchange of envoys, and in Britain, only one other city, Wigan, receives and sends an envoy.</p>
<p>The twinning gives the inhabitants of both places the opportunity to interact with their international neighbours. Town twinning aims to enhance international understanding and break down social barriers.</p>

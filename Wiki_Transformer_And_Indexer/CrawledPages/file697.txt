<p>The <b>Harvard–Yenching Library</b> is the primary location for East Asia-related collections at the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard University Library">Harvard University Library</a>. In addition to <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western</a> <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a>, it houses collections in <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Tibetan languages">Tibetan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchu_language" title="Manchu language">Manchu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a>, totaling more than 1 million volumes.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The library is located at 2 Divinity Avenue on the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a> campus of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harvard%E2%80%93Yenching_Library&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1879, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ko_K%27un-hua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ko K'un-hua (page does not exist)">Ko K'un-hua</a>, a scholar from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, was engaged to give a course in the Chinese language. The small collection of books that was bought for this course became Harvard College's first acquisitions in any <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="East Asian language">East Asian language</a>. In 1914, two <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> professors came from <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Imperial_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Tokyo Imperial University">Tokyo Imperial University</a> to lecture at Harvard; and they donated several important sets of Japanese publications on <a href="/wiki/Sinology" title="Sinology">Sinology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> to the Harvard College Library, thus launching Harvard's Japanese collection. These two collections became the nucleus of Harvard's East Asian library. In 1927, <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Cary_Coolidge" title="Archibald Cary Coolidge">Archibald Cary Coolidge</a>, head of Harvard's libraries, asked <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Kaiming_Chiu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alfred Kaiming Chiu (page does not exist)">Alfred Kaiming Chiu</a>, then a graduate student at Harvard, to organize and catalog these collections.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The library was formally founded in 1928 as the Chinese-Japanese Library of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Yenching_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Yenching Institute">Harvard-Yenching Institute</a>, the library took its present name in 1965 to reflect the expansion of its collections beyond Chinese and Japanese. In 1976, management of the library shifted from the independent Harvard-Yenching Institute to the Harvard College Library.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A. Kaiming Chiu served as head of the library until his retirement (1964). He was succeeded by Eugene Wu (1965) who was succeeded by James Cheng (1998).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 75th Anniversary of the Library was celebrated in 2003 with a <a href="/wiki/Symposium" title="Symposium">symposium</a> and the publication of a volume of scholarly articles on the history of the library and its collections. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2009, the library announced a six-year, multimillion-dollar project to <a href="/wiki/Digitize" class="mw-redirect" title="Digitize">digitize</a> major sections of its rare books collection in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_China" title="National Library of China">National Library of China</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
